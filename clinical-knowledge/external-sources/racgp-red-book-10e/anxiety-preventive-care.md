# Anxiety Disorders — Preventive Activities in General Practice

Source: RACGP Red Book 10th Edition (2024) — new chapter not present in 9th edition.

## Prevalence and Burden

Anxiety disorders affect approximately 17% of Australians in any 12-month period, making them the most common mental health condition seen in general practice. They include:
- Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)
- Social anxiety disorder (social phobia)
- Panic disorder (with or without agoraphobia)
- Specific phobias
- Separation anxiety disorder

Anxiety disorders are frequently comorbid with depression; combined presentations are more functionally disabling than either condition alone.

## Preventive and Early Intervention Activities

### Case-finding (opportunistic screening)
The Red Book recommends opportunistic screening for anxiety in the following high-risk groups during routine consultations:
- Patients with chronic physical health conditions (cardiovascular disease, respiratory disease, chronic pain, diabetes)
- Patients presenting with medically unexplained symptoms
- Patients with current or past depression
- Patients using substances (alcohol, stimulants, cannabis)
- Patients with recent significant life stressors or adverse life events
- Adolescents (15–17 years) as part of well-adolescent checks

### Validated Screening Tools
- **GAD-7** (Generalised Anxiety Disorder 7): Score ≥10 = moderate anxiety (sensitivity 89%, specificity 82% for GAD)
- **DASS-21** (Depression Anxiety Stress Scale): Validated for both depression and anxiety; free for non-commercial use
- **PHQ-4**: Ultra-brief 4-item screener for anxiety and depression combined; useful time-pressed settings

### Psychoeducation and Brief Intervention
For patients who screen positive or report anxiety symptoms:
- Explain the diagnosis and normalise the experience
- Provide information about anxiety management strategies (lifestyle, relaxation, cognitive techniques)
- Recommend evidence-based apps or online programs (This Way Up, MindSpot — both Australian, free)
- Discuss sleep hygiene, caffeine reduction, physical activity

### Stepped Care Model
The RACGP Red Book endorses a stepped care approach:
1. **Step 1:** Watchful waiting + psychoeducation + self-help resources (mild symptoms)
2. **Step 2:** Low-intensity psychological interventions — structured guided self-help (CBT-based), online programs
3. **Step 3:** High-intensity psychological interventions — individual CBT with a psychologist (Mental Health Treatment Plan, up to 20 sessions per calendar year)
4. **Step 4:** Medication ± specialist referral (if steps 1–3 inadequate, or severe/comorbid presentations)

## Pharmacological Management (When Required)

First-line for GAD, social anxiety, and panic disorder:
- **SSRI:** Sertraline 50–200 mg/day or escitalopram 10–20 mg/day
- **SNRI:** Venlafaxine 75–225 mg/day (particularly for GAD)
- Allow 4–8 weeks for therapeutic effect at adequate dose

Avoid benzodiazepines as long-term treatment (dependence risk; do not improve long-term outcomes). Short-term use (2–4 weeks maximum) may be appropriate for severe acute anxiety pending psychological/SSRI therapy.

## Safety Netting and Follow-up

- Review within 2–4 weeks of initiating any treatment
- Assess suicide risk at every contact (anxiety disorders carry elevated suicide risk, especially with comorbid depression)
- Provide emergency contacts: Lifeline 13 11 14; Beyond Blue 1300 22 4636; emergency 000
