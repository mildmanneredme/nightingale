# Sepsis Clinical Care Standard — Summary for GP Telehealth Context

The ACSQHC Sepsis Clinical Care Standard (2022) applies to all Australian healthcare settings. For telehealth, the relevant application is recognition, communication, and immediate referral — not in-person treatment.

## Recognition: Identifying Possible Sepsis by Telephone/Telehealth

Sepsis must be considered when a patient has **signs of infection** combined with **physiological deterioration**. In a telehealth setting, identification relies on patient-reported symptoms.

### Symptoms the patient may report
- Fever or chills (or feeling very hot/very cold)
- Rigors (uncontrollable shaking)
- Rapid breathing (breathing faster than usual, feeling breathless at rest)
- Confusion or unusual behaviour reported by patient or carer
- Extreme fatigue, difficulty standing or walking
- Skin changes: new rash, skin very pale or mottled, cold/clammy skin

### High-risk groups requiring heightened suspicion
- Age ≥65 years
- Age ≤1 year
- Immunocompromised (chemotherapy, corticosteroids, biologics, post-transplant, asplenia, HIV)
- Recent surgery or invasive procedure (within 6 weeks)
- Indwelling device (urinary catheter, central line, dialysis access)
- Diabetes mellitus
- Chronic organ dysfunction (renal, hepatic, cardiac failure)
- Recent childbirth, miscarriage, or termination

## Sepsis Red Flags Requiring Immediate 000 / Emergency Referral

Any patient with suspected infection who reports ANY of the following should be directed to call 000 immediately:

1. **Altered consciousness:** New confusion, agitation, extreme drowsiness, or unresponsive
2. **Respiratory distress:** Breathing very fast, unable to speak in full sentences, blue lips
3. **Circulatory failure:** Extremely pale, mottled, cold, clammy skin; feeling faint; very low urine output
4. **Rapid deterioration:** Worsening significantly over hours despite simple measures
5. **Non-blanching rash:** Petechiae or purpura (meningococcal/meningitis concern — 000 immediately)

## Escalation Pathway for Telehealth

Telehealth is **not appropriate** for a patient with suspected sepsis. The correct pathway:

1. Patient reports infection + physiological deterioration → ask escalation questions above
2. If ANY red flag present → advise call 000 immediately; do not complete consultation
3. If uncertain → default to in-person assessment; advise patient to present to ED or call 000
4. Document escalation in consultation notes

## Important Note

Sepsis is a medical emergency requiring in-person assessment, IV antibiotics, and resuscitation monitoring. Telephone/telehealth assessment cannot safely rule out sepsis. **Err on the side of escalation in all cases of clinical doubt.**
