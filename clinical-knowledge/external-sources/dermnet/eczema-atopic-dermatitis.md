# Atopic Dermatitis (Eczema)

Atopic dermatitis (AD) is a chronic, relapsing inflammatory skin condition affecting ~15% of Australian children and 3–5% of adults. It is strongly associated with atopy (personal or family history of asthma, allergic rhinitis, or food allergy).

## Clinical Features

### Infant (2 months – 2 years)
- Red, weeping, crusted plaques on cheeks, forehead, scalp
- Involvement of trunk and outer surfaces of limbs
- Nappy area typically spared

### Childhood (2–12 years)
- Flexural involvement: antecubital fossa, popliteal fossa, wrists, ankles
- Lichenification from chronic scratching
- Sleep disturbance due to itch common

### Adult
- Flexural pattern common; may also involve face, neck, chest
- Chronic lichenified or nodular lesions
- Increased risk of Staphylococcus aureus colonisation → infected eczema

## Diagnosis

Clinical diagnosis based on characteristic morphology and distribution, chronic relapsing course, and atopic personal/family history.

Hanifin and Rajka criteria (simplified):
- Pruritus (essential)
- Typical age-related morphology and distribution
- Chronic relapsing course
- Personal or family history of atopy

SCORAD or IGA scores used to classify severity (mild, moderate, severe) for treatment decisions.

## Management

### Emollients (all severities — cornerstone of treatment)
- Apply liberally at least twice daily, including to unaffected skin
- Choose fragrance-free, preservative-free products
- Apply immediately after bathing to seal in moisture
- Avoid soap — use soap substitute (aqueous cream, QV wash) or gentle syndets

### Topical Corticosteroids (TCS) — for active inflammation
Stepwise potency by site and age:

| Site | First choice | Alternative |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| Face, eyelids, flexures, genitalia | Hydrocortisone 1% (mild) | Desonide 0.05% |
| Trunk, limbs | Betamethasone valerate 0.02–0.05% (moderate) | Methylprednisolone aceponate 0.1% |
| Palms, soles, thick lichenified | Betamethasone dipropionate 0.05% (potent) | Mometasone furoate 0.1% |

Apply once daily to affected areas until lesions resolve (usually 3–14 days). Avoid prolonged use on face/flexures.

### Topical Calcineurin Inhibitors (TCI)
- Pimecrolimus 1% cream (mild-moderate) or tacrolimus 0.03%–0.1% ointment (moderate-severe)
- Steroid-sparing; particularly useful for face, eyelids, flexures
- Not PBS-listed in all circumstances; check current PBS criteria

### Antibiotics
- Active infection (honey-coloured crusting, rapid worsening, systemic symptoms): cefalexin 500 mg QID for 7 days (treat empirically for S. aureus)
- Avoid topical antibiotics except fusidic acid for small areas; resistance risk
- Bleach baths (sodium hypochlorite 0.005%) reduce S. aureus colonisation; 5 mL liquid bleach per 10 L water, twice weekly

### Systemic therapies (moderate-severe, specialist-initiated)
- Dupilumab (IL-4/IL-13 inhibitor): PBS-listed for adults ≥18 with severe AD failing conventional therapy; also adolescents 12–17 years
- Tralokinumab: alternative IL-13 inhibitor; PBS listing under review
- Cyclosporine (short-term bridge): PBS-listed; monitor BP and renal function
- Methotrexate, mycophenolate: off-label; specialist use

### Antihistamines
Sedating antihistamines (cetirizine, loratadine) have limited evidence for itch reduction in AD. May help with sleep. Non-sedating antihistamines are not recommended for AD-related itch.

## Patient Education

- Itch-scratch cycle — avoid triggers (heat, sweating, wool, fragrances, house dust mite)
- Wet-wrap therapy for severe flares in children (specialist guidance)
- Written eczema action plan (Eczema Australia resources)
- Eczema and food allergy: food allergy testing warranted only if allergic reaction noted or severe uncontrolled AD in infant — not routine
