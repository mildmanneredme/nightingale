# Tinea (Dermatophyte Infections)

Tinea refers to a group of fungal infections of the skin, hair, and nails caused by dermatophytes (Trichophyton, Microsporum, and Epidermophyton species). Common forms include tinea pedis (athlete's foot), tinea corporis (ringworm), tinea cruris (groin), tinea capitis (scalp), and tinea unguium (onychomycosis).

## Clinical Presentations

### Tinea Pedis (Athlete's Foot)

Most common dermatophyte infection. Three patterns:
- **Interdigital:** Scaling, maceration, fissuring between toes (4th/5th most common). Itchy. May have secondary bacterial superinfection.
- **Moccasin/hyperkeratotic:** Diffuse dry scaling involving sole and lateral foot. Often bilateral. Minimal itch. Frequently coexists with onychomycosis.
- **Vesicular/bullous:** Acute vesicles or bullae on instep/sole. Intensely pruritic. Less common.

### Tinea Corporis (Ringworm)

- Annular scaly plaques with central clearing and raised, advancing active border
- Pruritic; may have pustules at the edge
- Can occur anywhere on body; common in skin folds and areas of sweating
- Differential: eczema (not annular), pityriasis rosea (herald patch, widespread), granuloma annulare (non-scaly)

### Tinea Cruris (Jock Itch)

- Erythematous, scaly plaques in groin, inner thighs; spares scrotum (unlike Candida)
- More common in males; associated with tinea pedis and obesity

### Tinea Capitis (Scalp)

- Scaly alopecia patches in children; may have kerion (boggy inflammatory mass)
- Requires systemic antifungal (topicals do not penetrate hair follicle)
- Common organisms in Australia: Trichophyton tonsurans (endothrix)

### Tinea Unguium (Onychomycosis)

- Distal lateral subungual: most common; yellow-white discolouration, subungual hyperkeratosis, onycholysis
- Superficial white: chalky white surface deposits
- Proximal subungual: rare; associated with immunosuppression
- Differential: psoriasis, trauma, lichen planus

## Diagnosis

- Clinical diagnosis usually sufficient for typical presentations
- Skin scraping for microscopy + culture if diagnosis uncertain or systemic therapy planned
- Wood's lamp: Microsporum species fluoresce green (Trichophyton tonsurans does not)
- PCR: increasingly available; rapid species identification; useful for onychomycosis to guide treatment duration

## Treatment

### Tinea Pedis, Corporis, Cruris
**Topical antifungals (4–6 weeks for pedis/cruris; 2–4 weeks for corporis):**
- Terbinafine 1% cream once daily (fungicidal — preferred)
- Clotrimazole 1% cream twice daily (fungistatic — alternative)
- Bifonazole 1% cream once daily

**Systemic antifungals (reserved for extensive, resistant, or follicular disease):**
- Terbinafine 250 mg daily for 2–4 weeks

### Tinea Capitis
- Griseofulvin 10–15 mg/kg/day for 6–8 weeks (traditional first-line in children)
- Terbinafine is an effective alternative for Trichophyton species (check PBS listing)

### Tinea Unguium
- Terbinafine 250 mg daily: 6 weeks (fingernails), 12 weeks (toenails)
- Confirm diagnosis with microscopy ± culture before systemic therapy
- Topical therapies (amorolfine nail lacquer) for superficial white or limited distal disease only; poor cure rates for established onychomycosis

## Prevention Advice for Patients

- Dry feet thoroughly after showering, especially between toes
- Wear breathable footwear; avoid sharing towels or footwear
- Treat tinea pedis early to prevent nail involvement
- Launder socks and underwear at high temperature
