## Herpes Simplex Assessment (RACGP)

Herpes simplex virus (HSV) infection is among the most common viral infections worldwide and in Australia. HSV-1 predominantly causes orolabial herpes (cold sores) and increasingly genital herpes (through oral-genital contact); HSV-2 predominantly causes genital herpes. Seroprevalence in Australia: HSV-1 approximately 67% of adults; HSV-2 approximately 12%. Most people with HSV are unaware of their infection — the majority of transmission occurs from asymptomatic shedding. GPs play a central role in: diagnosing primary and recurrent herpes, initiating antiviral therapy, providing counselling about transmission and disclosure, prescribing suppressive therapy, and managing special situations (pregnancy, immunocompromised, neonatal herpes). Neonatal herpes is a medical emergency. Telehealth is suitable for recurrent herpes management; primary episode and uncertain diagnosis require in-person assessment.

## Clinical Presentation

**Orolabial herpes (cold sores — predominantly HSV-1):**
- Primary infection: may be subclinical or cause gingivostomatitis (children — painful ulcers throughout mouth, high fever, drooling, poor oral intake)
- Recurrent: prodrome (tingling, burning at lip) → vesicles → crusting → resolution over 7–10 days; triggered by sun, stress, febrile illness, trauma
- Location: mucocutaneous junction of lips; occasionally intraoral or chin

**Genital herpes (HSV-1 or HSV-2):**
- Primary (first episode): most severe; multiple painful vesicles/ulcers on genitalia, perianal area, thighs; inguinal lymphadenopathy; dysuria (urethral involvement — urinary retention); systemic features (fever, myalgia, headache); duration 2–3 weeks without treatment
- Non-primary first episode: first symptomatic episode but seropositive — previous asymptomatic infection; generally milder than true primary
- Recurrent: shorter, milder (1–4 days); prodrome; usually unilateral; no systemic features; may have only subtle symptoms (fissure, tingling)
- Asymptomatic shedding: occurs in 80% of HSV-2+ patients; main source of transmission

**Special situations:**
- Immunocompromised: severe, atypical, prolonged ulcers; may disseminate (HSV encephalitis, hepatitis, pneumonitis)
- Pregnancy: primary genital herpes in 3rd trimester → neonatal herpes risk; ACICLOVIR suppression from 36 weeks; C-section if active lesions at delivery
- Neonatal herpes: can be fatal or cause severe CNS damage; appears within 2 weeks of birth; 000 — IV aciclovir urgently

## Assessment Approach

**Clinical diagnosis in typical presentations:**
- Characteristic location, morphology, and recurrent pattern usually sufficient
- Confirm diagnosis with swab for certainty, medico-legal reasons, or uncertain presentation

**Laboratory diagnosis:**
- PCR swab of lesion (from vesicle base or ulcer): gold standard; high sensitivity; HSV type identification (HSV-1 vs HSV-2); request HSV PCR specifically
- Sample collection: swab vesicle base or ulcer — not surface crust (higher viral load in base); send in viral transport medium
- HSV IgG serology (type-specific): detects seropositive individuals (past infection); NOT useful in primary infection diagnosis (delayed seroconversion); useful for partner risk assessment; HSV-2 serology indicates genital herpes history; HSV-1 serology common (non-specific for orolabial vs genital)
- Tzanck smear: historical; not recommended; low sensitivity and no type differentiation

**Investigations for genital herpes (first episode):**
- HSV swab from lesion: confirm diagnosis and type
- Concurrent STI screen: chlamydia NAAT, gonorrhoea NAAT, syphilis serology, HIV — routine with genital herpes diagnosis
- Urinalysis: if dysuria — urinary involvement

## Key Differentials

- Aphthous ulcers: mouth only; not vesicular; no fever; recurrent; often oval with white base and erythematous rim; NOT infectious
- Syphilitic chancre (primary): painless solitary genital ulcer; indurated; inguinal LAD; RPR/TPPA positive
- Chancroid (Haemophilus ducreyi): rare in Australia; painful genital ulcer; suppurative LAD; tropical exposure
- Impetigo: can affect perioral region; crusted honey-coloured lesion; Staphylococcal or Streptococcal; responds to antibiotics
- Herpes zoster: dermatomal; older age; HSV PCR and VZV PCR differentiate
- Candidal vulvovaginitis: different morphology; discharge; swab
- Stevens-Johnson syndrome: severe mucosal lesions + skin; systemic illness; drug reaction or HSV-triggered (in immunocompromised)

## Management Principles

**Antiviral therapy:**

Primary genital herpes (first recognised episode):
- Aciclovir 400 mg TDS × 7–10 days (or 200 mg 5× daily × 7–10 days): standard; PBS-listed for primary genital herpes
- Valaciclovir 500 mg BD × 7–10 days: preferred (fewer doses, better bioavailability); PBS-listed
- Famciclovir 250 mg TDS × 7–10 days: alternative; less commonly used
- Start treatment regardless of duration of symptoms — still beneficial even after >48 hours for primary episode
- Analgesia: paracetamol, NSAIDs; local anaesthetic gel (xylocaine 5% gel) for pain relief
- Sitz baths: warm salt water; comfort for genital ulcers; prevent secondary infection
- Urinary retention: severe urethral/vulvar involvement; may require catheterisation; hospital if acute retention

Recurrent genital herpes (episodic therapy):
- Valaciclovir 500 mg BD × 3 days or 1 g once daily × 5 days: episodic treatment; significantly shortens episode duration
- Aciclovir 800 mg TDS × 2 days: ultra-short course; evidence supports; PBS-listed
- Patient-initiated therapy: provide prescription in advance; patient initiates at prodrome or first symptom; most effective if started within 24 hours
- Reassure: recurrences typically decrease in frequency and severity over time

Suppressive therapy (daily antiviral):
- Indication: frequent recurrences (≥6/year); psychosocial impact; partner protection
- Valaciclovir 500 mg once daily: most evidence; PBS-listed for suppression
- Aciclovir 400 mg BD: equally effective; cheaper
- Reduces recurrence frequency by 70–80%; reduces asymptomatic shedding by 50%
- Duration: review need annually; trial cessation after 12 months
- Reduces (does NOT eliminate) transmission risk to sexual partners

Orolabial herpes (cold sores):
- Mild/intermittent: lip balm with SPF; over-the-counter docosanol 10% cream (Abreva) or aciclovir 5% cream; started at prodrome; modest benefit
- Severe recurrent (≥6/year, extensive): valaciclovir 500 mg BD × 5 days episodic OR valaciclovir 500 mg daily suppression
- Triggers: identify and address — sun (use SPF 50+ lip protection), stress, fatigue

**Counselling (essential component of management):**
- Explain: lifelong infection; periodic recurrences; transmission possible even without visible lesions (asymptomatic shedding)
- Disclosure: encourage disclosure to sexual partners; patient decision but recommended; legal obligation in some contexts
- Condom use: reduces but does not eliminate transmission; condoms do not cover all infected skin
- Suppressive therapy: reduces shedding and transmission; offer to patients with partners who are HSV-negative
- Psychological support: genital herpes diagnosis often has significant psychological impact; HERPES Helpline (Australian): 1300 293 000; Positive Life NSW; Beyond Blue for anxiety/depression

**Pregnancy:**
- Primary or non-primary first episode in 3rd trimester: HIGH risk of neonatal herpes; consult obstetrics + infectious disease urgently; aciclovir suppression from 36 weeks; elective C-section if active lesions at or near term
- Recurrent herpes in pregnancy: lower neonatal risk; aciclovir suppression from 36 weeks if frequent recurrences or patient requests; C-section only if active lesions at labour onset
- Neonatal herpes: ANY neonate with fever/vesicles/neurological symptoms within 4 weeks of birth → 000; IV aciclovir urgently; ophthalmology; neurology

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Valaciclovir (Valtrex) 500 mg** (PBS restricted: genital herpes — PBS Authority required for genital herpes; treatment episodes 500 mg BD × 5 days; suppression 500 mg daily; better bioavailability than aciclovir; significantly fewer daily doses)
- **Aciclovir 200/400/800 mg** (PBS restricted: genital herpes episodic and suppression; 400 mg BD for suppression; cheaper than valaciclovir; require more frequent dosing; also PBS for HSV in immunocompromised)
- **Aciclovir 5% cream**: OTC; cold sores; modest efficacy; apply 5× daily; start at prodrome; reasonable OTC option for mild orolabial herpes
- **Note:** PBS Authority for genital herpes: requires written diagnosis of genital herpes; primary episode OR frequent recurrences (≥6/year) OR suppression for partner protection; document diagnosis clearly in medical record

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / hospital:**
- Neonatal herpes (infant <4 weeks with fever, vesicles, or neurological symptoms): IV aciclovir immediately; life-threatening
- HSV encephalitis: fever + severe headache + altered consciousness + focal neurology — MRI, LP, IV aciclovir; 000
- Severe primary genital herpes with urinary retention: catheterisation; hospital

**Urgent specialist:**
- Pregnancy + primary genital herpes 3rd trimester: obstetric + infectious disease same-day consultation; C-section planning; aciclovir suppression
- Disseminated HSV in immunocompromised: ophthalmology (herpetic keratitis), dermatology, infectious disease

**Non-urgent:**
- Refractory genital herpes not responding to valaciclovir: check adherence; viral resistance (rare); infectious disease referral for alternate antiviral (foscarnet)
- Sexual health clinic: STI screen; partner counselling; complex psychosocial management

## Telehealth Suitability

Herpes simplex ongoing management is well-suited to telehealth; initial assessment requires in-person.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Recurrent genital/orolabial herpes review and prescription renewal
- Suppressive therapy initiation and monitoring
- Pregnancy suppression planning (with obstetric team)
- STI result review
- Psychological counselling and support resource provision

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial diagnosis (swab from active lesion — PCR)
- Primary episode assessment (severity, complications, urinary involvement)
- Concurrent STI screen (blood tests, swabs)
- Neonatal herpes assessment (urgent ED)
