## Wound Infection Assessment (RACGP)

Wound infections are among the most common presentations to Australian general practice, including surgical site infections (SSIs), traumatic wound infections, chronic wound infections (leg ulcers, diabetic foot wounds), bite wounds, and skin abscesses. Staphylococcus aureus (including community-acquired MRSA — CA-MRSA) and Streptococcus pyogenes (Group A strep) are the most common pathogens in Australian community wound infections. Correct antibiotic selection, appropriate wound management, and timely recognition of serious infections (necrotising fasciitis, sepsis) are essential GP skills. GPs manage the majority of uncomplicated wound infections; hospital referral is required for serious infections requiring IV antibiotics, surgical debridement, or systemic sepsis. Telehealth has limited utility for wound assessment — in-person examination is essential.

## Clinical Presentation

**Signs of wound infection (SIRS local criteria):**
- Local warmth (calor), redness (rubor), swelling (tumor), pain (dolor), purulent discharge, wound breakdown
- Odour: anaerobes, Pseudomonas (sweet/fruity), Proteus (ammonia)
- Fever and systemic illness: suggests spreading infection or early sepsis
- Cellulitis: spreading erythema beyond wound — indicates invasive soft tissue infection

**Wound infection types:**

Superficial wound infection:
- Affects skin only; localised erythema, warmth, purulent discharge; no systemic features; manageable in primary care

Deep wound / surgical site infection (SSI):
- Deeper than skin/subcutaneous; fever; wound dehiscence; purulent drainage from deep wound; post-surgical; requires exploration and management

Abscess:
- Localised collection of pus; fluctuant, tender, warm swelling; may have pointing (white/yellow head); incision and drainage curative; no antibiotics unless surrounding cellulitis or systemic features

Skin and soft tissue infection (SSTI):
- Cellulitis: acute spreading dermal/subcutaneous infection; erythema, warmth, swelling, tenderness; non-purulent; Streptococcus most common cause
- Infected traumatic wound: contaminated laceration, bite wound, abrasion

**High-risk wound types requiring specific management:**
- Human bites: highly contaminated; Eikenella corrodens + oral streptococci + anaerobes; amoxicillin-clavulanate first-line; low threshold for prophylaxis
- Dog/cat bites: Pasteurella multocida (cat — rapid onset 12–24 hours); Capnocytophaga canimorsus (dog — immunocompromised); amoxicillin-clavulanate; tetanus and rabies prophylaxis if applicable
- Diabetic foot wound: polymicrobial; S. aureus, Streptococci, Gram-negatives, anaerobes; podiatry + physician + vascular surgery input; see `type-2-diabetes` entry
- Chronic leg ulcers: mixed flora including Pseudomonas; treat clinically infected wounds only (wound colonisation does NOT require antibiotics)
- Puncture wounds of foot: Pseudomonas aeruginosa in rubber-soled shoe punctures

**Necrotising soft tissue infection (NSTI — necrotising fasciitis) — red flags:**
- Rapidly spreading infection; pain disproportionate to appearance (early); then paradoxical absence of pain (late — necrosis of nerves)
- Skin changes: greyish discolouration, bullae, crepitus (gas production by anaerobes), skin necrosis
- Systemic toxicity: high fever, tachycardia, hypotension, confusion
- Risk factors: diabetes, immunocompromise, IV drug use, surgical wounds
- THIS IS A SURGICAL EMERGENCY — 000; immediate surgical debridement reduces mortality

## Assessment Approach

**History:**
- Wound type and mechanism: traumatic, surgical, bite, chronic
- Duration of wound and infection
- Systemic features: fever, rigors, malaise
- Diabetes, immunosuppression, PVD: risk factors for serious infection
- Tetanus immunisation status
- Allergies

**Physical examination:**
- Wound: size, depth, wound edges (approximated vs gaping), exudate type (serous, purulent, haemoserous)
- Surrounding skin: extent of cellulitis (mark margins with pen for progression monitoring); warmth, induration, tenderness
- Crepitus: palpate firmly — gas in soft tissue suggests necrotising infection
- Lymphadenopathy: regional nodes — spreading infection
- Vital signs: temperature, HR, BP, RR — sepsis assessment

**Investigations:**
- Wound swab for C&S: collect before antibiotics; essential for bite wounds, deep infections, failure to respond to empirical treatment; wet swab after debriding surface to deep tissue; not routine for superficial clean infected wounds if treating empirically
- FBC, CRP, ESR: if systemic features or diabetic foot; WBC elevated in serious infection
- Blood cultures: if fever + tachycardia; concern for sepsis; before IV antibiotics
- X-ray: if gas in soft tissue suspected (crepitus) — excludes gas gangrene; foreign body; osteomyelitis (late feature)
- CT: suspected deep tissue infection, necrotising fasciitis workup — air tracking along fascial planes
- HbA1c: if diabetic status unknown and significant wound infection

## Key Differentials

- Necrotising fasciitis: see clinical features above — surgical emergency; CT if suspected
- Osteomyelitis: bone involvement in diabetic foot or chronic wound; X-ray changes late; MRI gold standard; IV antibiotics; orthopaedic surgery
- Deep vein thrombosis: leg swelling and erythema; but no wound, no purulent discharge; duplex ultrasound
- Contact dermatitis: erythema at wound dressing application site (dressing allergy); no infection features; change dressing
- Gout: acute joint swelling and erythema may resemble infected wound over joint; urate crystals; no fever of infection
- Gas gangrene (Clostridium): crepitus; very unwell; fulminant — 000

## Management Principles

**Wound management principles:**
- Wound cleaning: irrigate thoroughly with normal saline; remove devitalised tissue (debridement); wound exploration for foreign bodies (especially glass/wood — difficult to feel)
- Debridement: essential for infected wounds; remove necrotic tissue; probes to assess depth; surgical debridement if extensive necrosis
- Wound dressings: appropriate to exudate level and wound type; hydrocolloid for low exudate; absorbent foam for high exudate; silver-containing dressings for infected chronic wounds; avoid occlusive dressings over infected tissue
- Abscess: incision and drainage (I&D) — definitive treatment; anaesthesia with lignocaine; incise over point of fluctuance; drain pus; break up loculations; irrigate; pack with gauze if large cavity; review 48 hours

**Antibiotic therapy — Australian community wound infections:**

Uncomplicated wound infection (superficial; no systemic features):
- Flucloxacillin 500 mg QID × 5–7 days: first-line for S. aureus; covers most community streptococcal and staphylococcal infections; give on empty stomach (impaired absorption with food)
- Cefalexin 500 mg BD–TDS × 5–7 days: alternative; PBS unrestricted; less GI intolerance; also covers strep and staph; take with or without food

Bite wounds (all bite wound types — higher contamination risk):
- Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg BD × 5–7 days (prophylaxis × 3–5 days; treatment if infected × 5–7 days): covers polymicrobial including Pasteurella, Eikenella, anaerobes
- If penicillin allergy: doxycycline 100 mg BD + metronidazole 400 mg BD
- Note: hand bites (particularly human) — aggressive management, orthopedic surgery involvement if near joint/tendon

CA-MRSA (community-acquired MRSA):
- If suspected (failure of flucloxacillin/cefalexin, recurrent skin abscesses, household MRSA contact):
- Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (Bactrim) 160/800 mg BD × 5–7 days: effective against CA-MRSA (NOT MRSA from hospital strains)
- Doxycycline 100 mg BD × 5–7 days: alternative for CA-MRSA
- Linezolid or vancomycin IV: serious MRSA; hospital

Diabetic foot infection:
- Mild/moderate (no systemic features, wound not deep): amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg BD × 7–10 days; podiatry, wound care, offloading
- Severe or deep: hospital IV antibiotics (piperacillin-tazobactam or ceftriaxone + metronidazole); vascular surgery, orthopaedic surgery input

**Tetanus prophylaxis:**
- Update tetanus status in all wound presentations
- Tetanus booster (dTpa or tetanus toxoid): if last booster >5 years ago for dirty wound; or >10 years for clean wound
- Tetanus immunoglobulin: if tetanus-prone wound (contaminated, deep, >6 hours old, devitalised tissue) AND incomplete immunisation (<3 doses)

**Antibiotic prophylaxis (prevention of infection):**
- Bite wounds: start amoxicillin-clavulanate prophylactically for high-risk bites (hand, face, deep puncture, immunocompromised patient); commence within 8 hours of bite
- Clean surgical wound: antibiotic prophylaxis not routinely required by GP for clean lacerations; exceptions: contaminated wounds, immunocompromised, valvular heart disease, prosthetic implants

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Flucloxacillin 500 mg QID** (PBS unrestricted: staphylococcal infection; take on EMPTY stomach; 30–60 minutes before food or 2 hours after; failure to take correctly is very common — educate patients; reduces absorption by up to 50% if taken with food)
- **Cefalexin 500 mg BD–TDS** (PBS unrestricted: skin and soft tissue infections; with or without food; alternative to flucloxacillin; may be preferred for patient compliance)
- **Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg BD** (PBS unrestricted: bite wounds and mixed infections; diarrhoea common — advise; take with food to reduce GI side effects; penicillin allergy caution)
- **Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (Bactrim DS) 160/800 mg BD** (PBS unrestricted: CA-MRSA; also UTI; renal dose adjustment if eGFR <30; sulfa allergy contraindication; avoid in late pregnancy)
- **dTpa vaccine (Boostrix, Adacel)** (PBS unrestricted or NIP-funded: tetanus booster; administer in practice; update patient immunisation record on Australian Immunisation Register)
- **Note:** Wound colonisation (organisms present without infection) does NOT require antibiotics; treat only clinically infected wounds (pus, spreading erythema, systemic features); routine swabbing of chronic wounds is often misleading

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000:**
- Necrotising fasciitis / gas gangrene: crepitus, rapidly spreading, systemic toxicity, skin changes — surgical emergency; mortality 20–80%; immediate surgery
- Sepsis from wound infection: fever + tachycardia + hypotension + altered consciousness — IV antibiotics, resuscitation, ICU

**Urgent hospital (IV antibiotics + surgical review):**
- Deep tissue infection with systemic features unresponsive to oral antibiotics
- Osteomyelitis (diabetic foot, penetrating injury): MRI; IV antibiotics; orthopaedic surgery
- Rapidly spreading cellulitis with systemic toxicity
- Orbital cellulitis from facial wound: urgent ophthalmology + ENT

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Podiatrist: diabetic foot wound management; MBS-funded Enhanced Primary Care; regular foot care
- Vascular surgery: peripheral arterial disease as contributor to non-healing wound; ABI assessment
- Wound care nurse: complex chronic wounds; appropriate dressing selection; wound management plan

## Telehealth Suitability

Wound infection management requires in-person assessment; follow-up may be supported by telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Phone follow-up after antibiotic initiation (improvement assessment, safety netting)
- Wound swab result discussion and antibiotic modification
- Simple wound care education (dressing change technique)
- Diabetic foot wound monitoring (if patient has a remote monitoring setup)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- ALL wound infections: physical examination, wound assessment, pus collection, wound measurement
- Abscess incision and drainage
- Wound debridement
- Crepitus assessment (cannot assess via telehealth)
- Tetanus vaccination administration
