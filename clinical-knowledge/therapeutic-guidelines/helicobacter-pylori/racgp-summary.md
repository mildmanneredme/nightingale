## H. pylori Assessment and Eradication (RACGP)

Helicobacter pylori is a gram-negative bacterium that colonises the gastric mucosa of approximately 30% of Australians (higher prevalence in immigrants from endemic regions — Southeast Asia, South America, Africa). H. pylori is causally associated with: peptic ulcer disease (gastric and duodenal), gastric adenocarcinoma (2–6× increased risk), and MALT lymphoma. Eradication of H. pylori heals peptic ulcers, prevents recurrence, and reduces gastric cancer risk. Testing for H. pylori is recommended in appropriate clinical contexts; test-and-treat strategies are supported without endoscopy for low-risk dyspepsia presentations. Antibiotic resistance is an increasing concern — follow-up test-of-cure is essential. Telehealth is well-suited for H. pylori management.

## Clinical Presentation

Most H. pylori infection is asymptomatic. When symptomatic:
- Dyspepsia: epigastric pain or discomfort, bloating, early satiety, nausea — often indistinguishable from functional dyspepsia
- Peptic ulcer disease: epigastric pain; hunger pain relieved by food (duodenal ulcer); pain worsened by food (gastric ulcer); nausea, waterbrash; complications include bleeding (haematemesis, melaena) and perforation
- Gastric cancer (uncommon — but H. pylori is the primary risk factor for non-cardia gastric adenocarcinoma)

**Indications for H. pylori testing:**
- Uninvestigated dyspepsia in patient aged <45 years without alarm features — test-and-treat acceptable (avoid endoscopy if possible)
- Peptic ulcer disease (confirmed or suspected)
- MALT lymphoma
- Unexplained iron deficiency anaemia (after appropriate investigation)
- Chronic ITP (immune thrombocytopenic purpura) — H. pylori eradication may improve platelet count
- First-degree relative with gastric cancer
- Patient request (informed discussion of risks and benefits)

**Alarm features requiring urgent gastroscopy (not test-and-treat):**
- Age ≥45 with new dyspepsia
- Dysphagia
- Unintentional weight loss
- Vomiting
- Evidence of GI bleeding (haematemesis, melaena, iron deficiency anaemia)
- Epigastric mass
- Previous gastric cancer or surgery

## Assessment Approach

**Testing methods:**

Non-endoscopic (preferred for test-and-treat and test-of-cure):
- Urea breath test (UBT): most accurate non-invasive test; sensitivity/specificity >95%; patient swallows labelled urea; H. pylori urease cleaves urea, releasing labelled CO2 detected in breath; requires fasting 4+ hours; MUST stop PPIs ≥2 weeks prior and antibiotics ≥4 weeks prior (or false negative); arranged via nuclear medicine or respiratory lab
- Stool antigen test (SAT): highly accurate; non-invasive; good alternative to UBT; less availability in Australia; same medication restrictions apply
- Serology (H. pylori IgG): does NOT confirm active infection; cannot be used for test-of-cure (antibodies persist years after eradication); only useful in specific low-prevalence testing scenarios — NOT recommended for routine testing

Endoscopic (required if alarm features or endoscopy otherwise indicated):
- Rapid urease test (CLO test): biopsy from antrum + corpus; rapid result; highly accurate
- Histology: identifies H. pylori on biopsy; also assesses mucosal damage
- Culture and sensitivity: gold standard for antibiotic resistance pattern; important if considering rescue therapy

**Test-of-cure (mandatory after eradication):**
- Perform UBT or stool antigen test ≥4 weeks after completing antibiotic course AND ≥2 weeks after stopping PPI
- Essential to confirm eradication — rising antibiotic resistance means first-line failure rates approaching 20–25% in some populations
- Serology cannot be used for test-of-cure

## Key Differentials

- Functional dyspepsia: H. pylori excluded; symptom-based management; PPI, prokinetics
- Gastro-oesophageal reflux (GORD): heartburn, regurgitation; PPI therapy; see `gord` entry
- Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID)-induced ulcer: NSAID use common cause of peptic ulcer — stop NSAID, use PPI; H. pylori can coexist
- Gastric cancer: alarm features; urgent gastroscopy
- Pancreatic pathology: epigastric pain radiating to back; lipase/amylase; CT scan

## Management Principles

**First-line eradication therapy (standard triple therapy — Australian guidelines):**

Clarithromycin-based triple therapy (if clarithromycin resistance low in local population):
- PPI (omeprazole 20 mg or pantoprazole 40 mg) + amoxicillin 1 g + clarithromycin 500 mg — all twice daily for 14 days (14 days superior to 7 days — higher eradication rates)
- PPI choice: omeprazole or pantoprazole equivalent; esomeprazole or rabeprazole also used

Bismuth-based quadruple therapy (Pylera) — alternative or if clarithromycin resistance suspected/known:
- Bismuth subcitrate 140 mg + metronidazole 125 mg + tetracycline 125 mg (combined in Pylera capsule) 3 capsules four times daily + PPI (pantoprazole 40 mg twice daily) for 10 days
- PBS-restricted; used increasingly due to clarithromycin resistance concerns
- More complex regimen but excellent eradication rates even with resistance

Metronidazole-based triple therapy (if penicillin allergy):
- PPI + clarithromycin 500 mg + metronidazole 400 mg — twice daily for 14 days
- Metronidazole resistance is common (>40% in some regions) — lower success rates

**Second-line (rescue) therapy — after failed first course:**
- Use a different regimen from first-line; check for drug use in first course and avoid repeating
- Bismuth quadruple therapy (Pylera) if not used as first-line
- Levofloxacin-based triple therapy: PPI + amoxicillin + levofloxacin — used in Australia if first-line failure; levofloxacin resistance increasing; culture and sensitivity may guide choice
- Refer to gastroenterology if second-line failure

**PPI use:**
- Continue PPI for 4–8 weeks after eradication for peptic ulcer disease (to allow mucosal healing)
- Can cease PPI once ulcer healed and H. pylori eradicated if no ongoing indication (NSAID, high GI risk)

**Adherence:** Stress importance of completing full course; incomplete courses are a major cause of treatment failure and resistance

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Pylera** (bismuth subcitrate / metronidazole / tetracycline — PBS restricted: H. pylori eradication in peptic ulcer disease; 3 capsules four times daily for 10 days with pantoprazole)
- **Amoxicillin** (PBS restricted: H. pylori eradication as part of triple therapy; 1 g twice daily)
- **Clarithromycin** (PBS restricted: H. pylori eradication; 500 mg twice daily)
- **Metronidazole** (PBS unrestricted — H. pylori regimen; 400 mg twice daily in triple therapy)
- **Pantoprazole / omeprazole** (PBS restricted: peptic ulcer disease; 20–40 mg twice daily during eradication; continue for 4–8 weeks post-eradication for ulcer healing)
- **Note:** Must advise patient on medication interactions — clarithromycin interacts with statins (hold simvastatin during course), metronidazole with alcohol (disulfiram reaction), and some cardiac medications. Avoid alcohol during metronidazole course.

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Haematemesis (vomiting blood) or melaena (black tarry stool) — upper GI bleeding; gastroscopy and resuscitation urgently
- Perforation: acute onset severe abdominal pain, peritonism, shock — surgical emergency

**Urgent gastroscopy (within days):**
- Alarm features present (as listed under assessment approach)
- Confirmed or suspected bleeding peptic ulcer (haemodynamic stability — urgent ED or endoscopy unit)
- Unexplained significant anaemia in older patient — gastroscopy urgently

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Gastroenterology: failed second-line eradication; suspected MALT lymphoma; complex peptic ulcer disease; gastric cancer surveillance (atrophic gastritis on histology)

## Telehealth Suitability

H. pylori management is well suited to telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Test-and-treat prescription arrangement (order UBT via pathology, prescribe eradication therapy when positive)
- Eradication therapy prescribing with patient education
- Test-of-cure UBT arrangement and result review
- Dyspepsia assessment in patients without alarm features
- Second-line eradication prescribing if first-line failed

**Requires in-person assessment or arrangement:**
- UBT and stool antigen testing — pathology/nuclear medicine arrangement
- Alarm features requiring urgent endoscopy referral
- Clinical examination if mass, significant bleeding, or severe symptoms suspected
