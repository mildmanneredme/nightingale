## Inflammatory Bowel Disease Assessment (RACGP)

Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) encompasses Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis (UC), characterised by chronic relapsing-remitting gastrointestinal inflammation with systemic manifestations. IBD affects approximately 100,000 Australians, with incidence rising — Australia has one of the highest rates globally. Crohn's disease can affect any part of the GI tract (mouth to anus); UC is limited to the colon and rectum. Both cause significant morbidity: diarrhoea, abdominal pain, rectal bleeding, weight loss, anaemia, and extra-intestinal manifestations. Gastroenterologists manage IBD; the GP plays a key role in early recognition and referral, ongoing monitoring, co-management of complications and comorbidities, immunisation, medication side effect monitoring, and mental health support. Telehealth is suitable for stable IBD monitoring.

## Clinical Presentation

**Ulcerative Colitis (UC):**
- Rectal bleeding (bloody diarrhoea — hallmark feature): blood and mucus in stool
- Urgency, tenesmus (feeling of incomplete evacuation)
- Severity range: mild (≤4 bloody stools/day, well patient) to fulminant colitis (>10 stools, fever, tachycardia, anaemia — medical emergency)
- Disease extent: proctitis, left-sided colitis, extensive/pancolitis — influences treatment and surveillance
- Colitis complications: toxic megacolon, perforation (acute severe colitis — hospital emergency)

**Crohn's Disease:**
- Abdominal pain (right iliac fossa — ileocolic disease most common), diarrhoea (may be non-bloody), weight loss, fatigue, mouth ulcers
- Perianal disease: fistulae, abscesses, skin tags, fissures — in up to 30–40% of Crohn's patients
- Small bowel disease: malabsorption, B12 deficiency, fat-soluble vitamin deficiencies; bile acid malabsorption (right colon / ileocolic resection) causing diarrhoea
- Stricturing complications: intestinal obstruction (colicky pain, distension); surgical referral
- Penetrating complications: fistulae, abscesses

**Extra-intestinal manifestations (both UC and Crohn's):**
- Musculoskeletal: peripheral arthritis (correlates with gut activity); axial spondyloarthropathy (independent of gut)
- Skin: erythema nodosum, pyoderma gangrenosum (urgent dermatology — may require immunosuppression)
- Eyes: episcleritis (correlates with gut), uveitis (independent — urgent ophthalmology)
- Hepatobiliary: primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC) — strongly associated with UC; elevated ALP; gastroenterology/hepatology referral; increased colorectal cancer risk

**Symptoms requiring urgent assessment:**
- Severe diarrhoea with blood and systemic unwell (acute severe UC — hospital)
- High fever, abdominal tenderness, peritonism — toxic megacolon, abscess, perforation — 000
- Perianal abscess — urgent surgical drainage

## Assessment Approach

**Initial diagnosis (GP referral to gastroenterology):**
- Stool: MCS and Clostridioides difficile PCR (exclude infective cause); faecal calprotectin (elevated >150 µg/g — sensitive marker of mucosal inflammation; useful to differentiate IBD from IBS)
- FBC: anaemia (iron deficiency or chronic disease), elevated WBC in acute flare
- CRP, ESR: elevated in active disease
- Iron studies, B12, folate, vitamin D: nutritional deficiencies common, especially Crohn's
- LFTs: exclude PSC (elevated ALP); immunosuppressant monitoring
- Colonoscopy (with biopsies): definitive diagnosis — gastroenterologist arranges
- Small bowel imaging (MRI enterography or CT enterography): Crohn's small bowel extent and complications — specialist arranges

**Ongoing GP monitoring (established IBD):**
- Disease activity: patient-reported symptom score; IBD-related hospitalisations; flare frequency
- Routine bloods (every 3–6 months on immunosuppression):
  - FBC: thiopurine-related cytopenias (azathioprine, 6-mercaptopurine); leucopenia risk
  - LFTs: thiopurine hepatotoxicity; methotrexate hepatotoxicity
  - eGFR: baseline; relevant for NSAID use (avoid in IBD)
  - CRP: disease activity marker
  - Vitamin B12 (annual if ileal disease or ileocolic resection)
  - Iron studies (FBC), zinc, vitamin D: nutritional monitoring
- Faecal calprotectin: non-invasive mucosal inflammation marker; used by gastroenterologists to guide scope timing
- Colorectal cancer surveillance: UC with extensive colitis or Crohn's colitis >8 years — annual/biennial colonoscopy (gastroenterologist arranges per GESA surveillance guidelines)
- Immunisation review: IBD patients on immunosuppression — see immunisation section
- Mental health: anxiety and depression highly prevalent in IBD; PHQ-9, GAD-7; GP Mental Health Care Plan

## Key Differentials

- Infectious gastroenteritis: acute onset; travel history; stool MCS; Clostridioides difficile (antibiotic-related)
- Irritable bowel syndrome: no bleeding, no elevated CRP; faecal calprotectin normal; diagnosis of exclusion
- Colorectal cancer: rectal bleeding, change in bowel habit, weight loss in older patients; colonoscopy
- Coeliac disease: diarrhoea, malabsorption, bloating; anti-tTG IgA on gluten-containing diet
- Ischaemic colitis: sudden onset rectal bleeding in elderly with CVD risk; CT; urgent assessment
- NSAID-induced enteropathy: small bowel or colonic lesions; medication history critical
- Endometriosis: cyclical bowel symptoms in women; gynaecology referral

## Management Principles

**Gastroenterologist-led — GP supports monitoring and co-management:**

Ulcerative Colitis maintenance:

Mild-moderate UC (proctitis/left-sided):
- 5-aminosalicylate (mesalazine) — first-line; oral AND topical (suppository for proctitis, enema for left-sided) combination more effective than either alone; Pentasa, Asacol, Mezavant

Moderate-severe or steroid-dependent UC:
- Azathioprine or 6-mercaptopurine: thiopurine immunomodulators; effective for maintenance; 6–12 weeks to onset; TPMT enzyme activity testing before initiating (high-activity variants = risk of toxicity)
- Biologics: infliximab (Remicade, biosimilars), adalimumab, vedolizumab, ustekinumab — PBS-listed for moderate-severe UC; specialist prescribing; infection screening before initiation
- Tofacitinib: JAK inhibitor; oral; PBS-listed for moderate-severe UC; specialist initiation; VTE and infection risk

Crohn's Disease maintenance:
- Azathioprine / 6-MP: first-line for mild-moderate disease
- Methotrexate: SC injection; used where azathioprine not tolerated; folate supplementation; teratogenic
- Biologics: adalimumab, infliximab, ustekinumab, vedolizumab — PBS-listed for moderate-severe Crohn's; specialist prescribing
- Elemental / polymeric enteral nutrition: effective for induction in paediatric Crohn's; steroid-sparing

Flare management (GP role):
- Mild UC flare: oral mesalazine dose increase + rectal mesalazine enema; contact gastroenterologist
- Acute flare not responding to 5-ASA escalation: prednisolone 40–60 mg daily (short course, taper over 6–8 weeks); budesonide MMX (Cortiment) — topically acting corticosteroid with less systemic effect; do NOT use steroids long-term (not disease-modifying)
- Any severe flare — hospital admission with IV corticosteroids; rescue therapy (infliximab or ciclosporin) if failure

**Nutrition:**
- Iron deficiency: IV iron preferred in IBD (oral iron may worsen GI symptoms and disease activity)
- B12: supplement if ileal disease (sublingual or IM injection if malabsorption)
- Vitamin D: supplement to normal levels; immunomodulatory effect suggested in IBD

**Immunisation — critical in IBD patients on immunosuppression:**
- Influenza vaccine: annual; inactivated; safe on immunosuppression
- Pneumococcal vaccine: Prevenar 13 + Pneumovax 23; 5-yearly booster if on immunosuppression
- Hepatitis B: baseline serology; vaccinate if non-immune
- Varicella zoster (Shingrix — inactivated): recommended on immunosuppression; 2 doses (avoid live zoster vaccine if on immunosuppression)
- COVID-19: booster 6-monthly if on immunosuppression per ATAGI recommendations
- Live vaccines (MMR, varicella, BCG, yellow fever, oral typhoid): CONTRAINDICATED on immunosuppression; administer before commencing if possible

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Mesalazine (Pentasa, Asacol, Mezavant)** (PBS restricted: UC; authority required; oral ± topical; suppositories and enemas available)
- **Azathioprine** (PBS restricted: IBD; authority; check TPMT activity before initiating; FBC and LFT monitoring monthly then 3-monthly)
- **Prednisolone** (PBS unrestricted: IBD flare induction; short course only; not maintenance; calcium + vitamin D + osteoporosis monitoring if recurrent)
- **Budesonide MMX (Cortiment)** (PBS restricted: UC; topically acting corticosteroid; less systemic effect than prednisolone)
- **Adalimumab (Humira, biosimilars)** (PBS restricted: moderate-severe Crohn's or UC; TB screening (IGRA) and hepatitis B serology required; specialist initiation)
- **Infliximab (Remicade, biosimilars)** (PBS restricted: IBD; IV infusion; hepatitis B and TB screening mandatory; infection risk)
- **Note:** Vedolizumab (Entyvio) and ustekinumab (Stelara) are PBS-listed for IBD; all biologics require specialist initiation and ongoing monitoring; GP should never initiate or modify biologics independently

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Acute severe colitis: >6 bloody stools/day, fever >38°C, tachycardia, systemic unwell — IV corticosteroids in hospital; ICU if deteriorating
- Toxic megacolon: dilated colon on AXR, fever, peritonism — surgical emergency
- Perforation or peritonitis — 000 immediately
- Perianal abscess: severe pain, fluctuance — surgical drainage

**Urgent gastroenterology review:**
- Flare with significant rectal bleeding not responding to oral therapy
- Suspected stricture or bowel obstruction
- New perianal disease (fistula, abscess)
- Elevated faecal calprotectin suggesting mucosal relapse despite apparent clinical remission
- Pyoderma gangrenosum or other severe extra-intestinal manifestation

**Urgent ophthalmology:**
- Acute painful red eye in IBD patient — uveitis (independent of gut disease activity; vision threatening)

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Gastroenterologist: routine surveillance colonoscopy; biologic initiation; management review
- Dietitian: nutritional assessment; elemental diet; ostomy support
- Stoma nurse: patients post-colectomy with stoma
- Psychologist: anxiety, depression, IBD-related distress
- Crohn's and Colitis Australia: patient support and education

## Telehealth Suitability

IBD monitoring is well suited to telehealth for stable patients in remission.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Disease activity review and symptom monitoring
- Blood result interpretation (FBC, LFTs, CRP, B12, iron, vitamin D)
- Medication adherence review
- Immunisation review and catch-up planning
- Mental health review (PHQ-9, GAD-7) and GP Mental Health Care Plan
- Nutritional supplement prescribing
- Referral coordination

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Physical examination during flare
- Blood tests (pathology arranged)
- Colonoscopy — gastroenterology referral
- Perianal disease assessment
- Infusion monitoring (infliximab — specialist setting)
