## Cellulitis Assessment (RACGP)

Cellulitis is a bacterial infection of the dermis and subcutaneous tissue, most commonly caused by Streptococcus pyogenes (Group A Streptococcus) and Staphylococcus aureus. It is a common GP presentation, particularly affecting the lower limbs. Most cases of uncomplicated cellulitis can be managed with oral antibiotics in the community. Distinguishing cellulitis from mimics (especially bilateral lower limb oedema, lipodermatosclerosis, and stasis dermatitis) is critical — the majority of suspected bilateral leg cellulitis is actually a mimic. Telehealth with photo assessment is moderately suitable for cellulitis; severity assessment and identification of complications may require in-person examination.

## Clinical Presentation

Classic features: erythema, warmth, swelling, and tenderness of the skin, typically with a spreading border. Usually unilateral. Systemic features (fever, rigors, tachycardia) indicate more severe infection.

**Lower limb cellulitis:** Most common site; often associated with entry points — tinea pedis (athlete's foot) between toes, leg ulcers, minor trauma, insect bites, or chronic oedema.

**Periorbital and orbital cellulitis:** Periorbital (preseptal) — eyelid swelling without proptosis or vision change; managed with oral antibiotics. Orbital — proptosis, reduced vision, pain on eye movement; requires IV antibiotics and urgent ophthalmology/ENT.

**Facial cellulitis:** Following dental procedures, trauma, or odontogenic source. Beware Ludwig's angina (floor of mouth infection — airway emergency).

**Severity grading (Eron classification for lower limb cellulitis):**
- Class I: no systemic signs; healthy patient
- Class II: systemic features (fever, tachycardia) OR significant local complications (lymphangitis)
- Class III: systemically unwell (hypotension) OR significant limb involvement
- Class IV: sepsis, life-threatening infection

## Assessment Approach

Photo assessment via telehealth: photograph should capture erythema, borders (mark borders with pen if possible to track spread), any bullae, necrosis, or entry point. Compare with subsequent photos to assess spread.

Key assessments:
- Mark the border of erythema with a skin pen at initial presentation (patient or carer can do this at home) — allows monitoring of spread
- Temperature at home (fever suggests systemic involvement)
- Lymphangitis (red streaks extending proximally) — indicates severe infection
- Regional lymphadenopathy
- Underlying entry point: examine between toes for tinea pedis (common overlooked source)
- Assess bilateral vs unilateral — bilateral lower limb oedema and erythema almost never cellulitis; strongly suggests mimic

Investigations for typical uncomplicated cellulitis: none required (blood cultures, swabs are low yield in uncomplicated cellulitis and not routinely recommended). FBC, CRP, and blood cultures appropriate for severe or hospitalised cases.

Consider MRSA: skin trauma, recurrent infections, prior MRSA, healthcare exposure, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander populations (higher MRSA prevalence), presence of purulent material (more likely S. aureus).

## Key Differentials

- Stasis dermatitis / venous eczema — bilateral lower limb erythema, chronic, hyperpigmented, varicose veins present; not infection; treat with compression and emollients
- Lipodermatosclerosis — chronic fibrotic skin change from venous insufficiency; indurated lower limb skin; bilateral; chronic history
- Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) — unilateral calf swelling, warmth, tenderness without obvious skin erythema; risk factors; D-dimer, Doppler ultrasound required
- Contact dermatitis — well-demarcated, itchy, exposure history
- Erysipelas — superficial dermis infection (vs cellulitis in deeper dermis); well-demarcated raised border; bright red; usually Group A Streptococcus; treat similarly
- Necrotising fasciitis — severe pain (disproportionate to appearance), rapid spread, crepitus, skin necrosis, systemic toxicity; surgical emergency — immediate hospital transfer
- Abscess — fluctuant, purulent collection; incision and drainage required; oral antibiotics alone insufficient

## Management Principles

**Mild cellulitis (Eron Class I — no systemic features, healthy patient):**
Oral antibiotics for 5–7 days. Elevation of affected limb reduces oedema and speeds recovery. Rest. Mark border and reassess in 24–48 hours.

**Antibiotic choice for uncomplicated lower limb cellulitis (non-purulent, no MRSA risk):**
- Cefalexin 500 mg four times daily — first-line (covers Group A Streptococcus and most S. aureus; well tolerated)
- Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg twice daily — if mixed flora suspected (bites, aquatic exposure, diabetic foot)
- Flucloxacillin 500 mg four times daily — for S. aureus predominant (purulent) infections (fasting required — take 30 min before food)

**Penicillin allergy:**
- Doxycycline 100 mg twice daily — covers Strep and MSSA; excellent oral bioavailability; preferred in many guidelines
- Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (TMP-SMX) — if MRSA suspected; note: poor streptococcal coverage if used alone

**MRSA suspicion (purulent infection, risk factors):**
- Doxycycline or TMP-SMX — community MRSA coverage
- Refer or hospitalise if severe

**Address entry point:** Treat tinea pedis with topical antifungal if present between toes — prevents recurrence.

**Moderate-to-severe cellulitis (Eron Class II–III) or failure of oral therapy:**
- Refer to hospital for IV antibiotics (flucloxacillin IV or cefazolin IV)
- IV to oral switch when systemically improved (usually 48–72 hours)

**Recurrent cellulitis (≥2–3 episodes):**
- Manage underlying risk factors: treat oedema (compression), treat tinea pedis, maintain skin integrity
- Consider prophylactic penicillin (phenoxymethylpenicillin 250–500 mg twice daily for 6–12 months) for patients with ≥3 episodes — reduces recurrence by ~50%

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Cefalexin** (PBS unrestricted — 500 mg four times daily for 5–7 days; first-line for non-purulent cellulitis)
- **Flucloxacillin** (PBS unrestricted — 500 mg four times daily; must take 30 min before food; more specific S. aureus coverage)
- **Amoxicillin-clavulanate** (PBS restricted: skin and soft tissue infections)
- **Doxycycline** (PBS unrestricted — penicillin allergy or MRSA risk; 100 mg twice daily)
- **Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole** (Bactrim — PBS unrestricted; MRSA-active for purulent skin infections)
- **Phenoxymethylpenicillin** (PBS unrestricted — prophylaxis for recurrent lower limb cellulitis; 250–500 mg twice daily)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Suspected necrotising fasciitis: disproportionate pain, rapid spread despite antibiotics, crepitus, skin necrosis, bullae, systemic toxicity — surgical emergency; high mortality without urgent debridement
- Sepsis: fever >38.5°C, rigors, hypotension, tachycardia, altered consciousness from cellulitis
- Ludwig's angina: floor of mouth swelling, stridor, drooling, trismus — airway emergency
- Orbital cellulitis: proptosis, restricted eye movement, vision change

**Urgent in-person or hospital review:**
- Class II–III cellulitis not responding to oral antibiotics after 48 hours
- Rapidly spreading erythema despite treatment (extend border mark)
- High fever, significant lymphangitis, or haemodynamic instability
- Facial cellulitis not responding to first-line oral antibiotics
- Immunocompromised patient (diabetes, immunosuppression) — lower threshold for IV antibiotics
- Suspected DVT underlying the presentation — D-dimer, Doppler ultrasound

**Non-urgent review:**
- Recurrent cellulitis — address contributing factors and consider prophylaxis

## Telehealth Suitability

Cellulitis is moderately suitable for telehealth with photo assessment.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Photo assessment to characterise erythema extent, borders, features
- Mild uncomplicated cellulitis with no systemic features — oral antibiotic prescribing
- Safety netting instructions (border marking, symptom monitoring, return criteria)
- 24–48 hour review via telehealth + photo to assess treatment response

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Palpation for fluctuance (abscess requiring drainage)
- Assessment for crepitus (necrotising fasciitis)
- Any systemic features or failure to improve — in-person or ED
- Periorbital and facial cellulitis (risk of orbital or intracranial extension)
