## Cancer Survivorship Assessment (RACGP)

There are approximately 1.2 million cancer survivors in Australia, a number increasing as survival rates improve. Cancer survivorship encompasses the phase of care from completion of active cancer treatment through ongoing follow-up for late effects, secondary cancers, psychosocial wellbeing, and return to function. Survivorship is a distinct phase requiring proactive, holistic care beyond purely oncological follow-up. The GP plays a central and growing role in cancer survivorship — coordinating primary care needs, managing treatment late effects, monitoring for recurrence signs, delivering preventive care, addressing mental health and sexual health, supporting return to work, and maintaining overall health while the oncologist focuses on oncology-specific surveillance. A formal Survivorship Care Plan (SCP) — provided by the oncology team — should guide GP involvement. Telehealth is highly suitable for survivorship reviews.

## Clinical Presentation

**Who are cancer survivors:**
- Anyone who has completed curative treatment for cancer (surgery, chemotherapy, radiotherapy, or combination), is in remission, and is now receiving follow-up care
- Approximately 68% of Australians diagnosed with cancer survive ≥5 years (varies by cancer type)
- Common cancers entering survivorship phase: breast (9-year median survival), prostate, colorectal, melanoma, thyroid, lymphoma, head and neck

**Late effects of cancer treatment (vary by cancer type and treatment received):**

Chemotherapy late effects:
- Peripheral neuropathy: numbness, tingling, pain in hands/feet; platinum agents (cisplatin, oxaliplatin), taxanes, vinca alkaloids; may be permanent; see `peripheral-neuropathy` entry
- Cardiotoxicity: anthracyclines (doxorubicin) cause cardiomyopathy; risk accumulates with dose; echocardiogram monitoring
- Cognitive impairment ("chemo brain"): concentration, memory, processing speed deficits; common; usually improves over months-years
- Fatigue: most common late effect; multifactorial; can persist years
- Bone marrow suppression: increased infection risk; FBC monitoring in first year
- Premature ovarian insufficiency / menopause: chemotherapy, particularly alkylating agents, in premenopausal women
- Secondary malignancy: risk elevated for specific regimens; blood cancers (myelodysplasia, AML) with certain chemotherapy

Radiotherapy late effects:
- Breast: lymphoedema (arm); radiation fibrosis; skin changes; cardiac late effects (left breast radiation)
- Head and neck: xerostomia (dry mouth), dysphagia, dental decay, osteoradionecrosis of jaw, hypothyroidism (neck RT)
- Pelvic: bowel and bladder changes (rectal bleeding, urgency, incontinence); sexual dysfunction; vaginal stenosis
- Lung: radiation pneumonitis; pulmonary fibrosis
- Second malignancy: radiation-induced cancers (decades later)

Surgery late effects:
- Colostomy/stoma: see `inflammatory-bowel-disease` and bowel cancer entries; stomal therapy nurse referral
- Lymphoedema: breast surgery + axillary dissection; pelvic surgery; see `lymphoedema` entry
- Body image: mastectomy, stoma, hair loss, scarring — significant psychological impact

Hormonal therapy late effects:
- Breast cancer (aromatase inhibitors): joint pain (arthralgias), bone density loss, menopausal symptoms
- Prostate cancer (ADT): hot flushes, osteoporosis, metabolic syndrome, cognitive changes, sexual dysfunction

## Assessment Approach

**Survivorship Care Plan (SCP):**
- Provided by treating oncology team at transition to survivorship
- Contains: diagnosis summary, treatments received, follow-up schedule, late effect monitoring, who to see for what
- GP should request SCP from oncology team if not received; guides shared care

**Annual GP survivorship review (integrate into health assessment):**

Recurrence surveillance (per SCP — oncology determines schedule):
- Cancer-specific surveillance: mammography (breast), CEA (colorectal), PSA (prostate), CT scan schedule — per oncology team; GP may order and discuss
- Symptoms of concern (refer urgently if present): new pain, unexplained weight loss, unusual lumps, persistent fatigue, cough, bone pain

Late effects monitoring:
- Cardiovascular risk: anthracycline and left chest radiation increase long-term cardiac risk; echocardiogram at 5 years if high-dose anthracycline; annual BP, lipids, glucose
- Bone density (DEXA): aromatase inhibitors, ADT for prostate, premature menopause all cause bone loss; annual DEXA; bisphosphonate if osteoporosis
- Thyroid function: neck radiotherapy causes hypothyroidism; annual TSH
- Peripheral neuropathy: symptom assessment; neuropathic pain management
- Fatigue: assess contributing causes (anaemia, thyroid, depression, deconditioning); structured exercise (most effective intervention)
- Cognitive assessment: if chemo brain significant; neuropsychological testing if severe

Mental health:
- Depression and anxiety: extremely common in survivors (30–40%); PHQ-9, GAD-7 at each annual review
- Fear of cancer recurrence: significant in many survivors; CBT-based interventions; psychologist referral
- Adjustment and meaning-making: many survivors experience positive growth AND persistent distress; normalise both

Sexual health:
- Sexual dysfunction extremely common and underaddressed: vaginal atrophy (aromatase inhibitors, radiation, early menopause); erectile dysfunction (prostate treatment); reduced libido (fatigue, ADT)
- Pelvic floor physiotherapy, vaginal oestrogen (where safe — discuss with oncologist), PDE5 inhibitors
- Open, non-judgmental discussion; referral to sexual health specialist if needed

Fertility:
- For survivors of reproductive age: fertility preservation (cryopreservation) should have been discussed pre-treatment; if not, refer to fertility specialist for post-treatment options
- Pregnancy after cancer: generally safe after 2 years from diagnosis (varies by cancer type); oncology clearance first

Return to work:
- 50–80% of survivors return to work; fatigue, neuropathy, cognitive changes may be barriers
- Occupational physician referral; MBS-funded Chronic Disease Management plan supports allied health

Lifestyle:
- Exercise: most evidence-based intervention for improving fatigue, cognition, mood, and reducing recurrence risk in breast, colorectal, prostate; 150 min/week moderate-intensity + resistance training
- Weight: obesity increases cancer recurrence and all-cause mortality; weight management support
- Alcohol: reduces — alcohol increases risk of recurrence in breast cancer; limit per NHMRC guidelines
- Smoking: cessation reduces cancer recurrence and treatment side effects; see `smoking-cessation` entry
- Diet: Mediterranean diet; reduce processed meat and alcohol

## Key Differentials

- Cancer recurrence vs late treatment effects: new symptoms always require assessment relative to recurrence risk; contact oncology team for guidance
- New primary cancer vs recurrence: survivors have increased risk of second primary cancers; maintain age-appropriate cancer screening (bowel, cervical, skin) — see relevant entries
- Psychological late effects masking physical symptoms: depression/anxiety/PTSD can present somatically; screen at every visit
- Treatment-related fatigue vs new anaemia or hypothyroidism: FBC, TSH, iron studies in all fatigued survivors

## Management Principles

**GP shared care model:**
- Clear delineation of responsibilities: oncology follows specific oncology endpoints; GP provides primary and preventive care
- Communicate proactively with oncology team: report new symptoms; request updated SCP
- Medications: check for drug interactions (tamoxifen and antidepressants; aromatase inhibitors and bone agents; ADT and cardiovascular medications)
- MBS items: Chronic Disease Management plan (MBS 721, 723, 729) — coordinates allied health; GP Management Plan allows up to 5 allied health sessions per year under Chronic Disease Management

**Common medication management in survivors:**

Breast cancer (aromatase inhibitors — anastrozole, letrozole, exemestane):
- Arthralgia: NSAIDs, exercise; switch to different AI if severe
- Bone density: calcium + vitamin D + DEXA annually; bisphosphonate (alendronate/zoledronic acid) if osteoporosis or at risk
- Menopausal symptoms: systemic HRT generally avoided in hormone-receptor-positive cancer; non-hormonal options (SSRIs, SNRIs, gabapentin for hot flushes); vaginal oestrogen generally considered safe — oncology confirmation

Prostate cancer (ADT — leuprorelin, goserelin):
- Bone density: calcium + vitamin D; denosumab or bisphosphonate
- Hot flushes: venlafaxine or SSRIs
- Metabolic syndrome: exercise, diet, statin if indicated
- Testosterone may return after ADT cessation — discuss timing with oncologist

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Aromatase inhibitors (anastrozole, letrozole, exemestane)** (PBS restricted: hormone-receptor-positive breast cancer; oncology-initiated; GP may continue and manage side effects)
- **Denosumab (Prolia 60 mg — 6-monthly)** (PBS restricted: osteoporosis in cancer survivors; DEXA-confirmed; 6-monthly SC injection; dental assessment before starting; osteonecrosis of jaw risk; do NOT stop abruptly — rebound fracture risk)
- **Tamoxifen** (PBS restricted: breast cancer; note: multiple drug interactions including CYP2D6 inhibitors — avoid paroxetine, fluoxetine; prefer SSRIs with less CYP2D6 inhibition: sertraline, venlafaxine, escitalopram)
- **Venlafaxine, SSRIs** (PBS restricted: depression/anxiety; also used for hot flushes in breast/prostate cancer survivors; avoid paroxetine and fluoxetine in breast cancer on tamoxifen)
- **Note:** Exercise prescription is the most evidence-based intervention in cancer survivorship; refer to exercise physiologist under Chronic Disease Management plan

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / urgent oncology:**
- Suspected cancer recurrence: new unexplained symptoms, worsening existing symptoms, new abnormal tumour marker — contact oncology team immediately
- Febrile neutropenia (fever >38°C in patient <12 months post-chemotherapy): 000; IV antibiotics; high mortality
- Spinal cord compression (back pain + new neurological deficit in cancer survivor): 000; dexamethasone; oncology emergency

**Urgent referral:**
- Suspected recurrence or new primary cancer: urgent oncology or relevant specialist referral
- Severe depression with suicidal ideation: same-day psychiatric care
- Lymphoedema: physiotherapist specialising in lymphoedema urgently (early treatment prevents progression)
- Cardiomyopathy or arrhythmia in anthracycline-treated patient: cardiology

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Exercise physiologist: cancer-specific exercise rehabilitation program (Chronic Disease Management plan)
- Psychologist: cancer anxiety, depression, fear of recurrence (GP Mental Health Care Plan MBS 2713)
- Stomal therapy nurse / wound care: stoma management
- Sexual health specialist: persistent sexual dysfunction
- Fertility specialist: post-treatment fertility concerns

## Telehealth Suitability

Cancer survivorship reviews are highly suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Annual survivorship review: mental health, late effects review, lifestyle counselling
- Blood result discussion (tumour markers, FBC, thyroid, lipids)
- Medication review and side effect management
- Return-to-work and lifestyle counselling
- Referral coordination (exercise physiologist, psychologist, physiotherapist)
- GP Management Plan review (MBS 721/723)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Physical examination for recurrence concerns or new symptoms
- Blood tests — pathology arranged
- DEXA and imaging — radiology
- Stoma care and wound review
