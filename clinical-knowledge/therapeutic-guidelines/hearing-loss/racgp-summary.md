## Hearing Loss Assessment (RACGP)

Hearing loss is the most common sensory impairment in Australia, affecting approximately 3.6 million Australians (1 in 6 people) and increasing significantly with age (1 in 3 over 65; 1 in 2 over 75). It is highly undertreated — average time from first noticing hearing loss to seeking help is 10 years. Untreated hearing loss is associated with social isolation, depression, cognitive decline, and dementia. Age-related hearing loss (presbycusis) is the most common cause in adults. GPs play a central role in: detection (often incidentally or through family prompting), initial assessment, otoscopic examination, cerumen management, referring for audiological assessment, and facilitating access to Hearing Australia (federally funded hearing services). All Australians aged ≥26 who are eligible under the Hearing Services Program (OHS) can receive government-subsidised hearing services. Telehealth has limited utility for hearing assessment but is suitable for counselling and follow-up.

## Clinical Presentation

**Types of hearing loss:**

Sensorineural hearing loss (SNHL — most common):
- Damaged cochlear hair cells or auditory nerve
- Presbycusis (age-related): gradual high-frequency loss; symmetrical; greatest at 4000–8000 Hz (difficulty with high-pitched consonants: s, f, th); background noise most problematic; very common from 60+ years
- Noise-induced: unilateral or bilateral; 4000 Hz notch on audiogram; occupational or recreational noise exposure
- Sudden SNHL: sudden onset (within 72 hours); unilateral; hearing loss ≥30 dB in ≥3 frequencies; medical emergency — urgent ENT referral for corticosteroid treatment within 14 days to maximise outcome
- Ototoxic medications: aminoglycosides (gentamicin), cisplatin, loop diuretics (furosemide at high doses); monitor at-risk patients
- Acoustic neuroma (vestibular schwannoma): unilateral SNHL ± tinnitus ± vertigo; asymmetric SNHL requires MRI internal auditory meatus

Conductive hearing loss:
- Sound blocked from reaching inner ear
- Cerumen impaction (most common, most treatable): wax blocking external canal; remove — significant and immediate improvement
- Otitis media with effusion ("glue ear"): common in children; fluid in middle ear; conductive loss; see `childhood-otitis` entry
- Otosclerosis: progressive conductive loss in young-middle aged adults; autosomal dominant; stapedectomy surgery effective

Mixed hearing loss: combination of sensorineural and conductive components

**Symptoms prompting hearing loss evaluation:**
- Difficulty understanding speech, especially in background noise
- Asking people to repeat themselves frequently
- Turning up TV/radio volume
- Missing parts of conversations
- Difficulty hearing on the phone
- Tinnitus (ringing in ears — commonly accompanies SNHL; see `tinnitus` entry)
- Aural fullness or blocked sensation

**Patient questionnaire tools:**
- HHIE-S (Hearing Handicap Inventory for the Elderly — Screening): 10-item; scores ≥10 = significant handicap; useful for older patients
- HHQ (Hearing Handicap Questionnaire): validated; aids referral decision

## Assessment Approach

**GP assessment:**

Otoscopy (essential):
- Inspect external canal: cerumen impaction (waxy dark material), foreign body, otitis externa, exostoses (surfer's ear)
- Tympanic membrane: intact vs perforated; mobility with pneumatic otoscopy; dull, retracted, or fluid level (OME); injected, erythematous (AOM)

Tuning fork tests (512 Hz):
- Weber test: place on forehead or vertex; normally heard centrally; lateralises to better ear in SNHL; lateralises to worse ear in conductive loss
- Rinne test: compare air conduction (AC — tuning fork in front of ear) vs bone conduction (BC — tuning fork on mastoid); normally AC>BC (Rinne positive); Rinne negative (BC>AC) = conductive loss in that ear

**Audiological referral:**
- Audiogram: pure tone audiometry — quantifies degree and type of hearing loss; speech audiometry
- Refer to: Hearing Australia (government-subsidised for eligible patients), private audiologist, or hospital audiology
- Urgent audiologist if sudden SNHL suspected

**Referral to ENT:**
- Asymmetric SNHL: requires MRI internal auditory meatus to exclude acoustic neuroma
- Sudden SNHL: urgent within 72 hours for oral high-dose corticosteroids (prednisolone 1 mg/kg/day × 7 days, taper)
- Otosclerosis: suspected (progressive bilateral low-frequency conductive loss); surgical option
- Chronic middle ear disease: chronic perforation, cholesteatoma, recurrent infections

**Hearing Australia (government-funded hearing services — OHS):**
- All Australians ≥26 years who hold a Medicare card and eligible concession card (Pensioner Concession, DVA Gold/White Card, Healthcare Card)
- Also funded: children (paediatric program), Indigenous Australians (ACHIA)
- Services: hearing assessment, hearing aid fitting (subsidised), rehabilitation, counselling
- Hearing Australia locations and online: hearing.com.au
- No GP referral required; self-referral possible; GP referral letter helpful for clinical context

## Key Differentials

- Cerumen impaction: sudden apparent hearing loss; otoscopy confirms; remove cerumen — hearing restores immediately
- Otitis media with effusion: children most common; flat tympanogram type B; conductive loss; most self-resolving
- Presbycusis: gradual bilateral symmetrical SNHL; age-related; audiogram confirms
- Acoustic neuroma (vestibular schwannoma): asymmetric SNHL; MRI; rare but important; treated with microsurgery, radiosurgery, or monitoring
- Sudden SNHL: urgent — 72-hour window for treatment; MRI to exclude retrocochlear cause
- Autoimmune inner ear disease: rapidly progressive bilateral SNHL; responds to immunosuppression; rare; audiology and rheumatology
- Menière's disease: episodic unilateral SNHL + tinnitus + vertigo + aural fullness; see `dizziness-vertigo` entry

## Management Principles

**Cerumen removal:**
- Softening drops (1–2 weeks before irrigation preferred): olive oil, bicarbonate ear drops, hydrogen peroxide; 3–5 drops twice daily
- Ear syringing or irrigation: effective for impacted wax; warm water (body temperature to avoid vertigo); not if TM perforation or history of ear surgery — refer to ENT
- Microsuction: preferred by ENT/audiologists; safe; no water; preferred if perforation history

**Hearing aids:**
- Most effective treatment for SNHL; significant quality of life and cognitive decline benefit
- Subsidy: Hearing Australia program for eligible patients — see above; government rebate for aids
- Range: behind-the-ear (BTE), in-the-ear (ITE), receiver-in-canal (RIC), completely-in-canal (CIC); audiologist determines appropriate style
- Cochlear implants: severe-profound bilateral SNHL; Medicare-funded for adults in Australia if meeting criteria; specialist centre assessment

**Communication strategies (adjunct to hearing aids):**
- Face the patient when speaking; reduce background noise; speak clearly (not louder)
- Captioned telephones; hearing loops; TV captioning (Australian Broadcasting Corporation provides captioning)
- Communication Partner Training: family and carers; Hearing Australia resources

**Children (paediatric hearing):**
- Universal Newborn Hearing Screening (UNHS): automated auditory brainstem response (AABR) in hospital before discharge; identifies congenital hearing loss
- Refer all children with failed newborn screen to paediatric audiologist within 2 weeks
- Ongoing school-age screening: audiology if teacher/parent concerns
- Hearing loss impacts speech and language development — early intervention is critical

**Workplace noise-induced hearing loss:**
- WorkSafe/SafeWork obligations: employers must provide hearing protection in noisy environments
- Audiometric monitoring: required in many industries; GP may receive reports
- Compensation claims: SafeWork and state workers compensation if occupational noise exposure

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Sodium bicarbonate 5% ear drops** (PBS unrestricted: cerumen softening; or OTC olive oil, hydrogen peroxide preparations)
- **Prednisolone** (PBS unrestricted: sudden SNHL — 1 mg/kg/day × 7 days, taper over 7 days; must start within 72 hours of onset for maximum benefit)
- **Hearing aids**: Hearing Australia subsidy for eligible patients; not PBS-funded directly
- **Note:** Avoid ototoxic medications where alternatives exist: gentamicin in systemic use; cisplatin (oncology context); monitor patients on loop diuretics at high doses for hearing changes

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent ENT (within 24–72 hours):**
- Sudden SNHL: unilateral hearing loss over ≤72 hours; requires urgent oral high-dose corticosteroids; window for treatment rapidly closes; refer to ENT or ED if ENT not available within 24 hours

**Non-urgent ENT:**
- Asymmetric SNHL: requires MRI IAM to exclude acoustic neuroma
- Cholesteatoma: destructive middle ear disease; pearly mass or keratin debris visible behind TM; surgical removal required
- Chronic perforation not healing: may require myringoplasty
- Otosclerosis: confirmed or suspected; surgical or hearing aid options

**Non-urgent audiology:**
- All new significant hearing loss: audiogram for diagnosis and degree
- Hearing aid fitting and rehabilitation
- Annual audiogram for patients on ototoxic medications (cisplatin, aminoglycosides)

## Telehealth Suitability

Hearing loss management is partially suitable for telehealth; assessment is predominantly in-person.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Counselling about hearing loss, hearing aid options, and Hearing Australia access
- Follow-up after audiological assessment and hearing aid fitting
- Communication strategy counselling
- Ototoxic medication monitoring counselling
- Referral coordination

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Otoscopy and tympanoscopy
- Tuning fork examination
- Pure tone audiometry (audiologist — in-person)
- Cerumen removal (ear irrigation or microsuction)
- ENT examination
