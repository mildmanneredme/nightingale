## Herpes Zoster (Shingles) Assessment (RACGP)

Herpes zoster (shingles) is caused by reactivation of latent varicella-zoster virus (VZV) from dorsal root ganglia, decades after primary varicella (chickenpox) infection. It affects approximately 1 in 3 Australians over their lifetime, with incidence increasing significantly after age 50 and in immunocompromised individuals. The most important complication is post-herpetic neuralgia (PHN) — persistent neuropathic pain lasting months to years after rash resolution — which causes substantial morbidity. Antiviral therapy within 72 hours of rash onset reduces severity and duration and lowers PHN risk. The recombinant zoster vaccine (Shingrix) significantly reduces zoster incidence and PHN. Telehealth is suitable for uncomplicated herpes zoster assessment and antiviral prescribing.

## Clinical Presentation

**Prodrome (1–5 days before rash):**
- Burning, stinging, or shooting pain; itching or tingling in a dermatomal distribution
- May cause diagnostic confusion before rash — consider zoster in any unexplained unilateral dermatomal pain

**Rash phase:**
- Dermatomal distribution: unilateral; does NOT cross midline (distinguishes from other rashes)
- Erythematous macules → vesicles (grouped on erythematous base — "dewdrops on a rose petal") → pustules → crusting
- Full crusting usually by 7–10 days; rash typically resolves in 2–4 weeks
- Most common dermatomes: thoracic (T3–T10 — 55%); trigeminal (V1 — ophthalmic branch — 10–20%); cervical; lumbar; sacral

**Complications:**
- Post-herpetic neuralgia (PHN): pain persisting >90 days after rash onset; affects up to 20–30% of untreated patients; risk increases with age, severity, and immunosuppression
- Herpes zoster ophthalmicus (HZO): V1 (ophthalmic) branch involvement; rash on forehead, tip of nose (Hutchinson's sign — indicates nasociliary branch involvement, high risk of eye involvement); risk of keratitis, uveitis, episcleritis, postherpetic neuralgia, and vision loss; URGENT ophthalmology referral
- Ramsay Hunt syndrome: geniculate ganglion involvement; facial nerve palsy + ear vesicles + ear pain; tinnitus, deafness; urgent antiviral + corticosteroid; ENT referral
- Disseminated zoster: >3 dermatomes; immunocompromised; systemic spread to organs; hospital admission

## Assessment Approach

**Diagnosis is clinical** in typical dermatomal vesicular rash: prodromal pain + unilateral dermatomal rash = herpes zoster until proven otherwise.

**When in doubt:**
- VZV PCR swab of vesicle fluid: most sensitive; available via pathology; useful if atypical presentation, immunocompromised, or for ophthalmic zoster
- DFA (direct fluorescent antibody): older test; less sensitive than PCR
- Serology: not useful for acute diagnosis

**Red flags requiring urgent assessment:**
- Ophthalmic involvement: V1 dermatome rash ± tip of nose rash → same-day ophthalmology
- Ramsay Hunt: unilateral facial palsy + ear rash + hearing symptoms
- Immunocompromised patient: lower antiviral threshold; risk of dissemination
- Disseminated rash (>3 dermatomes): hospital assessment

**Assessment for antiviral candidacy:**
- Ideal: within 72 hours of rash onset — highest efficacy
- May benefit even beyond 72 hours if: new lesions still forming, significant pain, ophthalmic involvement, immunocompromised, Ramsay Hunt
- Not indicated: >72 hours in immunocompetent patient with only mild symptoms and no new lesions forming

## Key Differentials

- Herpes simplex (HSV): dermatomal pain + vesicles but usually smaller area; recurrent; HSV PCR or DFA to differentiate
- Contact dermatitis: may be unilateral; vesicular; allergen exposure; DOES cross midline; not dermatomal
- Cellulitis: warm, erythematous skin; no vesicles initially; may be confused in early pre-vesicular stage; systemically unwell features
- Varicella (chickenpox): diffuse, multi-stage rash; no dermatomal pattern; not limited to one side

## Management Principles

**Antiviral therapy (early treatment — within 72 hours, ideally):**

First-line:
- Valaciclovir 1 g three times daily for 7 days: preferred (superior bioavailability vs aciclovir; convenient three-times daily vs five times); most commonly used; PBS-listed for zoster
- Famciclovir 500 mg three times daily for 7 days: alternative; similar efficacy; PBS-listed

If valaciclovir/famciclovir unavailable:
- Aciclovir 800 mg five times daily for 7 days (every 4 hours while awake): older agent; effective but less convenient

Immunocompromised patients: higher dose antivirals; often IV aciclovir (hospital); specialist guidance.

**Pain management:**
- Regular paracetamol 1 g four times daily
- NSAIDs: add if paracetamol insufficient; caution in elderly
- Neuropathic pain adjuncts for significant pain (particularly if PHN risk):
  - Pregabalin 75–150 mg twice daily: reduces acute zoster pain and may reduce PHN risk; PBS-restricted for neuropathic pain; start early if significant pain
  - Amitriptyline 10–25 mg nocte: evidence for PHN prevention; start during acute phase in patients at high risk of PHN
- Topical lidocaine gel or patches (PHN): for chronic post-herpetic pain

**Oral corticosteroids:**
- Prednisolone 40 mg daily for 1 week then taper: used in Ramsay Hunt (evidence supports facial nerve recovery); limited evidence for PHN prevention in uncomplicated zoster; may be considered in severe acute pain in immunocompetent older adults

**Post-herpetic neuralgia (PHN) management:**
- Pregabalin, gabapentin (neuropathic agents): first-line for PHN
- Amitriptyline or nortriptyline: TCA; evidence for PHN
- Topical capsaicin (high-dose 8% patch — Qutenza): specialist-supervised; effective for focal PHN
- See `chronic-pain` entry for full neuropathic pain management

**Wound care:**
- Keep rash clean and dry
- Non-adherent dressings if oozing
- Avoid contact with pregnant women, neonates, immunocompromised individuals, and people who have not had chickenpox — until all lesions crusted

**Vaccination (prevention — critical discussion at every zoster presentation and preventive health check):**

Recombinant zoster vaccine (Shingrix, RZV):
- 2-dose course; 2–6 months apart; intramuscular
- Efficacy: 97% for zoster prevention in 50–69 age group; 91% in ≥70; significantly reduces PHN if zoster does occur
- Recommended: all adults ≥50 years; immunocompromised from age 18 years
- NIP-funded: from 2023 for all adults ≥65 years; from 2024 for additional immunocompromised groups — check current NIP schedule
- Can be given after acute zoster episode (wait ≥6–12 months after resolution)
- Supersedes older live attenuated zoster vaccine (Zostavax — still used in some circumstances; contraindicated in severely immunocompromised)

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Valaciclovir** (PBS restricted: herpes zoster; 1 g three times daily for 7 days; start within 72 hours of rash onset; dose reduce in renal impairment: eGFR 30–49 → 1 g twice daily; eGFR 10–29 → 1 g once daily)
- **Famciclovir** (PBS restricted: herpes zoster; 500 mg three times daily for 7 days)
- **Aciclovir** (PBS restricted: herpes zoster; 800 mg five times daily for 7 days; less convenient; used if valaciclovir unavailable)
- **Pregabalin** (PBS restricted: neuropathic pain including post-herpetic neuralgia; 75–300 mg/day)
- **Amitriptyline** (PBS unrestricted — off-label for neuropathic pain and PHN prevention; 10–75 mg nocte)
- **Shingrix** (recombinant zoster vaccine — NIP-funded for ≥65 years from 2023; private purchase for 50–64 years or immunocompromised; GP clinic administration; 2 doses 2–6 months apart)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Disseminated zoster with systemic toxicity (immunocompromised) — IV aciclovir
- Encephalitis: confusion, headache, neck stiffness with zoster — neurological emergency

**Same-day urgent ophthalmology referral:**
- Herpes zoster ophthalmicus (HZO): V1 dermatome rash; Hutchinson's sign (tip of nose involvement); any eye symptoms — keratitis, red eye, decreased vision — start antivirals immediately; ophthalmology review urgently

**Urgent ENT and/or ID referral:**
- Ramsay Hunt syndrome: facial palsy + ear vesicles + ear symptoms — antiviral + corticosteroid; audiological and ENT assessment

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Pain specialist / GP procedural: persistent PHN (>3 months) not responding to neuropathic pharmacotherapy — high-dose capsaicin, nerve blocks, neurostimulation

## Telehealth Suitability

Herpes zoster management is suitable for telehealth with photo assessment.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Clinical diagnosis from photo — dermatomal vesicular rash on erythematous base is highly characteristic
- Antiviral prescribing within 72 hours (valaciclovir, famciclovir)
- Pain management and neuropathic agent prescribing
- Patient education on transmission precautions
- Shingrix vaccination counselling and script
- PHN monitoring and management

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Ophthalmic zoster — ophthalmology referral urgently (not managed remotely)
- Ramsay Hunt syndrome — in-person ENT assessment
- Immunocompromised patient with severe or disseminated zoster
- Swab collection if PCR confirmation needed
