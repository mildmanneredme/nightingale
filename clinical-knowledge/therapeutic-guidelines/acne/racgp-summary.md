## Acne Assessment (RACGP)

Acne vulgaris is the most common skin condition in Australia, affecting up to 85% of Australians aged 15–24 years and persisting or presenting for the first time in a significant proportion of adults. It results from follicular plugging, increased sebum production, proliferation of Cutibacterium acnes, and inflammation. Acne causes significant psychological burden — anxiety, depression, and reduced self-esteem are common comorbidities. Telehealth is moderately suitable for acne assessment via photo and video; severity grading guides treatment decisions and can often be performed remotely.

## Clinical Presentation

**Non-inflammatory lesions:** Open comedones (blackheads) and closed comedones (whiteheads) — caused by follicular plugging.

**Inflammatory lesions:** Papules (raised red lesions <5 mm), pustules (pus-filled), nodules (deep, painful, ≥5 mm), and cysts (fluctuant, nodular). Nodules and cysts carry highest scarring risk.

**Acne severity grading:**
- Mild: predominantly comedones; few papules/pustules; no nodules
- Moderate: more widespread papules and pustules; may include occasional nodules; limited scarring risk
- Severe: extensive papules, pustules, and/or nodules; significant scarring risk or existing scarring; significant psychological impact

**Distribution:** Face (most common — forehead, nose, cheeks, chin), upper chest, back, and shoulders.

**Adult acne:** Women more commonly affected than men in adults >25 years; consider hormonal causes (PCOS, hyperandrogenism) in women with late-onset, cyclical, or predominantly jawline/chin acne.

**Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH):** More prominent in patients with darker skin tones; persistent dark marks after inflammatory lesions resolve.

## Assessment Approach

Photo assessment via telehealth: adequate for severity grading; patient should photograph affected areas in good lighting. Video consultation allows real-time assessment of skin texture and distribution.

Assess:
- Duration, distribution, and severity of acne
- Prior treatment history (products used, duration, response, adverse effects)
- Medications that may cause or worsen acne: corticosteroids, lithium, anticonvulsants, androgen-containing preparations, high-dose vitamin B12
- Hormonal history in females: menstrual regularity (irregular cycles suggest PCOS), oral contraceptive history, hirsutism, androgenetic alopecia
- Psychological impact — PHQ-9 and GAD-7 if depression or anxiety suspected

Investigations for hormonal acne in women: testosterone (total and free), SHBG, DHEAS, prolactin, LH/FSH (if PCOS suspected); fasting glucose and lipids before isotretinoin; pregnancy test before isotretinoin initiation.

## Key Differentials

- Rosacea — predominantly facial flushing and erythema; papulopustular lesions but no comedones; centrofacial distribution; triggered by heat, alcohol, spicy food; typically in adults >30 years
- Perioral dermatitis — small papulopustules around mouth and nose; often related to topical corticosteroid use
- Folliculitis — infected hair follicles; may occur on chest, back, scalp; bacterial or fungal (Malassezia — pityrosporum folliculitis)
- Milia — white keratin cysts; not comedones; commonly around eyes
- Drug-induced acne — monomorphic lesions (all at same stage); temporal relationship to medication

## Management Principles

**Patient education:** Acne is not caused by poor hygiene or diet (though high GI foods and dairy may exacerbate in some individuals). Avoid picking or squeezing lesions (increases scarring risk). Consistent treatment for 6–8 weeks before assessing response — patients often abandon effective treatments prematurely.

**Mild acne — topical treatments:**
- Benzoyl peroxide (BPO) 2.5–5%: OTC; first-line for comedonal and mild inflammatory acne; effective, no resistance risk. Start low to reduce irritation.
- Topical retinoids (adapalene, tretinoin): comedolytic and anti-inflammatory; require prescription. Adapalene 0.1% (Differin) available OTC. Apply at night. Avoid in pregnancy.
- Topical antibiotics (clindamycin, erythromycin) + BPO: combination reduces resistance risk. Clindamycin/BPO gel (PBS-listed).
- Azelaic acid: alternative with some anti-inflammatory and pigmentation benefit; suitable in pregnancy.

**Moderate acne — add systemic antibiotics (3–6 months maximum):**
- Doxycycline 50–100 mg daily — first-line systemic antibiotic; avoid prolonged use to prevent resistance; always combine with topical BPO.
- Minocycline — alternative; higher side effect profile; less preferred.
- Do NOT use topical and systemic antibiotics of different classes simultaneously — increases resistance risk.
- Always co-prescribe topical BPO when prescribing systemic antibiotics for acne.

**Hormonal therapy for women:**
- Combined oral contraceptive pill — reduces androgenic drive; effective for hormonal acne; several formulations with anti-androgenic progestogens (cyproterone acetate in Diane-35 — PBS-listed for severe acne in women).
- Spironolactone 25–100 mg daily — off-label but widely used for adult female acne; blocks androgen receptors; effective.

**Severe / nodulo-cystic acne — isotretinoin:**
- Highly effective; the only treatment that induces long-term remission (often permanent).
- Refer to dermatologist for initiation in most cases; can be initiated by trained GPs with awareness of monitoring requirements.
- Mandatory pregnancy prevention program — two forms of effective contraception, monthly pregnancy tests; dispensed via specialist.
- Monitor: FBC, LFTs, fasting lipids (often elevated on isotretinoin), mood (depression is a known association — screen at each visit).

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Adapalene 0.1% gel** (Differin — now available OTC in Australia; PBS-listed as restricted benefit for acne)
- **Clindamycin 1% + benzoyl peroxide 5% gel** (Duac, BenzaClin — PBS restricted: acne; maximum 3 months continuous use)
- **Doxycycline 50 mg or 100 mg** (PBS unrestricted — tetracycline antibiotic; note: avoid in pregnancy and children <12 years)
- **Cyproterone acetate 2 mg / ethinyl estradiol 35 mcg** (Diane-35 — PBS restricted: severe acne in women not responding to antibiotics)
- **Spironolactone** (PBS unrestricted for indications; off-label for acne — BP monitoring required at higher doses)
- **Isotretinoin** (Roaccutane, Oratane — PBS restricted: severe nodulo-cystic or conglobate acne or acne causing scarring; specialist authority prescription required in most states; mandatory Pregnancy Prevention Programme)
- **Benzoyl peroxide** (OTC — widely available; not PBS-listed)

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent in-person dermatology:**
- Acne fulminans — sudden severe inflammatory acne with systemic features (fever, joint pain, weight loss) — rare but requires urgent dermatology review and systemic corticosteroids before isotretinoin
- Severe scarring acne requiring urgent intervention

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Dermatology: nodulo-cystic or scarring acne for isotretinoin initiation; treatment-resistant moderate acne; significant scarring requiring laser/procedure
- Endocrinology / gynaecology: suspected PCOS or significant hyperandrogenism in women with acne

**Safety monitoring (not emergencies but require in-person visits):**
- Isotretinoin monthly monitoring: pregnancy test (mandatory), LFTs, fasting lipids, mood assessment

## Telehealth Suitability

Acne assessment and management is moderately suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Initial assessment via photo/video — severity grading, distribution assessment
- Initiation of topical treatments and counselling on correct use
- Oral antibiotic prescription for moderate acne based on photo assessment
- COC and spironolactone initiation and review
- Response assessment at 6–8 weeks via photo comparison

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Isotretinoin initiation (mandatory blood tests, pregnancy test, prescribing program)
- Acne scarring assessment and procedural management (laser, chemical peel)
- Assessment of suspected acne fulminans or systemic features
