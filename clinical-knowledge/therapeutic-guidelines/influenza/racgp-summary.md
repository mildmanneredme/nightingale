## Influenza Assessment (RACGP)

Influenza (seasonal flu) is an acute viral respiratory illness caused by influenza A or B viruses. In Australia, the influenza season typically peaks between June and September. Influenza causes approximately 3,000–3,500 deaths annually in Australia, predominantly in older adults and people with chronic conditions. GPs play a central role in influenza diagnosis, management of uncomplicated cases, identification of high-risk patients who benefit from antiviral therapy, and vaccination promotion. Telehealth is well-suited for uncomplicated influenza management and antivirals prescribing.

## Clinical Presentation

**Classic influenza:**
- Abrupt onset (hallmark): rapid onset over hours, distinguishes from slower-onset URTI
- Systemic features: high fever (38–40°C), rigors, severe myalgia, headache, marked malaise and fatigue (prostration — "hit by a truck")
- Respiratory: dry cough, sore throat, nasal congestion or discharge
- Duration: acute illness 5–7 days; fatigue may persist 2–3 weeks

**Complications:**
- Primary influenza pneumonia: viral pneumonia; progressive dyspnoea and hypoxia; high-risk groups
- Secondary bacterial pneumonia: S. aureus (MRSA), S. pneumoniae superinfection after influenza; fever returns after initial improvement; purulent sputum; requires antibiotics
- Exacerbation of underlying chronic disease: COPD exacerbation, heart failure decompensation, diabetes destabilisation
- Myocarditis, encephalitis, febrile seizures (children) — rare
- Influenza-related complications are the main source of mortality and hospitalisation

**High-risk groups for severe influenza and antiviral benefit:**
- Adults ≥65 years
- Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples ≥6 months
- Pregnant women (all trimesters) and up to 2 weeks post-partum
- Children 6 months to 5 years
- People with chronic medical conditions: cardiac disease, respiratory disease (including asthma), diabetes, chronic renal disease, immunocompromised states (HIV, immunosuppressive therapy), haematological malignancy, morbid obesity (BMI ≥40)
- Residents of aged care facilities

## Assessment Approach

**Clinical diagnosis:** In a patient with classic influenza features during influenza season — abrupt onset + fever + myalgia + cough = 70–85% probability of influenza; clinical diagnosis is often sufficient.

**Influenza rapid antigen test (RIAT):** Point-of-care nasal swab; sensitivity 50–70% (lower than PCR); can confirm influenza but negative result does not exclude; useful to guide antiviral prescribing decisions in high-risk patients.

**Influenza PCR:** Most accurate; nasal/nasopharyngeal swab; available via pathology; useful for severe cases, hospital admission, or outbreak investigation; results available within 1–4 hours at some laboratories.

**Assessment of severity:**
- Vital signs: SpO2 (pulse oximetry), RR, HR, BP, temperature
- Signs of pneumonia: unilateral or bilateral consolidation
- High-risk group identification: age, pregnancy, chronic disease
- Social factors: access to follow-up, ability to self-manage

**Investigations:**
- Not required for uncomplicated influenza in otherwise healthy adults
- SpO2: if dyspnoea, respiratory symptoms
- CXR: if pneumonia suspected
- FBC, CRP: if bacterial superinfection suspected

## Key Differentials

- COVID-19: clinically indistinguishable; RAT for COVID-19 and influenza (combined tests available); SARS-CoV-2 PCR; management principles similar for mild cases
- Other viral URTI (rhinovirus, adenovirus): less systemic features; less abrupt onset; less myalgia; no antiviral available
- Bacterial pneumonia: requires antibiotics; CXR consolidation
- Streptococcal pharyngitis: predominantly sore throat without systemic features; throat swab; see URTI entry
- Heat stroke / fever of other aetiology: non-respiratory; appropriate history and examination

## Management Principles

**Uncomplicated influenza in low-risk patients:**
- Supportive care: rest, adequate fluid intake, symptomatic management
- Paracetamol or ibuprofen: fever and myalgia relief; avoid aspirin in children (Reye's syndrome)
- Avoid antibiotics: for uncomplicated viral influenza; antibiotics only if bacterial superinfection suspected
- Work/school exclusion: until afebrile for ≥24 hours without antipyretics; reduce community transmission

**Antiviral therapy (oseltamivir):**

Indications for oseltamivir (most benefit if started within 48 hours of symptom onset):
- All high-risk patients with influenza or clinical suspicion of influenza (see high-risk groups above)
- Hospitalised patients
- Severe influenza (hypoxia, haemodynamic compromise)
- May consider in otherwise healthy adults with severe symptoms if within 24–48 hours onset

Oseltamivir (Tamiflu):
- Adult: 75 mg twice daily for 5 days; start as early as possible — within 48 hours; benefit decreases after 48 hours but still potentially worthwhile in high-risk or severe cases
- Children: weight-based dosing; doses from 30–75 mg twice daily based on weight; PBS-listed
- Reduces duration of illness by ~1 day in uncomplicated influenza; more important benefit is prevention of hospitalisation and death in high-risk patients
- Post-exposure prophylaxis: 75 mg once daily for 10 days if high-risk close contact of confirmed influenza case; PBS-listed for household contacts

Side effects: nausea and vomiting (take with food to minimise); rarely neuropsychiatric events (agitation, abnormal behaviour) — particularly in paediatric use.

Zanamivir (Relenza — inhaler): alternative; less commonly used; suitable if oseltamivir contraindicated; not for patients with airways disease (bronchospasm risk).

**Influenza vaccination (prevention — discuss at every relevant consultation):**
- Annual vaccination recommended: all Australians ≥6 months; particularly high-risk groups
- Free under National Immunisation Program (NIP): ≥65 years, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander ≥6 months, pregnant women, individuals with medical risk factors; children 6 months to <5 years (from 2024 NIP expansion)
- Quadrivalent vaccines (two influenza A + two B strains) recommended; high-dose QIV and adjuvanted formulations for adults ≥65
- Timing: ideally before influenza season (April–May in Australia)
- Effectiveness: 40–60% in most seasons; significantly reduces hospitalisation and death in high-risk groups

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Oseltamivir** (Tamiflu — PBS restricted: treatment of influenza; 75 mg twice daily for 5 days; authority required; must be in high-risk group or PBS criteria met; also available under PBS for post-exposure prophylaxis; 75 mg once daily for 10 days)
- **Zanamivir** (Relenza — PBS restricted: influenza treatment; inhaler 2 × 5 mg puffs twice daily for 5 days; avoid in airways disease)
- **Influenza vaccines** (NIP-funded for eligible groups — not PBS; prescription not required for NIP vaccines; GPs can administer and claim via AIR — Australian Immunisation Register)
- **Paracetamol, ibuprofen** (PBS unrestricted — symptomatic management)
- **Note:** Antivirals have greatest impact when started within 48 hours of symptom onset; do not delay prescribing in high-risk patients waiting for test confirmation

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Severe hypoxia (SpO2 <93%) or respiratory distress
- Haemodynamic instability (hypotension, tachycardia)
- Altered consciousness, severe headache/neck stiffness (encephalitis)
- Suspected influenza pneumonia with haemoptysis or bilateral signs

**Urgent hospital referral:**
- High-risk patient with worsening symptoms despite 48 hours antiviral therapy
- Any pregnant patient with influenza + dyspnoea, hypoxia, or failure to improve — obstetric and respiratory team involvement

**Non-urgent follow-up:**
- High-risk patients on antiviral therapy — telehealth review at 48–72 hours
- Patients with underlying COPD or asthma exacerbation triggered by influenza — review and possible COPD/asthma treatment escalation

## Telehealth Suitability

Influenza management is well suited to telehealth for uncomplicated presentations.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Clinical diagnosis based on classic history during influenza season
- Oseltamivir prescribing for high-risk patients within 48 hours of symptom onset
- Symptomatic management advice
- Influenza vaccination counselling and prescription
- 48–72 hour follow-up assessment

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- SpO2 measurement for dyspnoea (patient-owned oximeter or in-person)
- Moderate or severe presentations — CXR, clinical examination
- Any signs of haemodynamic compromise or hypoxia
