## Pre-Travel Consultation Assessment (RACGP)

Pre-travel health consultations are an important component of preventive GP care, enabling risk assessment, vaccination, malaria prophylaxis, and health preparation for Australians travelling overseas. Over 11 million Australians travel internationally each year. Travel-associated infectious disease, injury, and illness are common; malaria, travellers' diarrhoea, hepatitis A, typhoid, and sexually transmitted infections are the most common health problems affecting travellers to medium- and high-risk destinations. The pre-travel consultation should ideally occur at least 4–6 weeks before departure to allow time for vaccines to take effect, though last-minute travellers still benefit from consultation. GPs are the most common providers of travel health consultations in Australia. Telehealth is suitable for pre-travel counselling but vaccine administration requires in-person attendance.

## Clinical Presentation

**Risk assessment — traveller and destination factors:**

Destination:
- High-risk regions: sub-Saharan Africa, South and Southeast Asia, Central and South America, Pacific Islands — for tropical diseases, gastrointestinal illness, and vector-borne infections
- Food and water safety: low-income countries with poor sanitation infrastructure
- Malaria transmission: sub-Saharan Africa (highest risk), South/Southeast Asia, Central America — species-specific (P. falciparum most dangerous; Africa and some SE Asia; P. vivax — South Asia and Americas)

Traveller factors:
- Duration: longer travel = higher cumulative risk
- Activities: rural/adventure travel, sexual activity with new partners, extreme sports, healthcare volunteering, animal exposure
- Health status: underlying conditions (immunocompromised, pregnancy, chronic disease) change risk and travel suitability
- Previous vaccinations: check AIR for existing vaccine history
- Pregnancy: many vaccines and antimalarials contraindicated
- Infants and young children: special considerations for vaccines, malaria prophylaxis, dehydration risk

**Common travel health problems:**
- Travellers' diarrhoea: most common; E. coli, Campylobacter, Salmonella; usually self-limiting 3–5 days
- Malaria: potentially fatal; P. falciparum causes cerebral malaria; chemoprophylaxis essential for endemic areas
- Dengue fever: mosquito-borne (Aedes); no prophylaxis; prevention = mosquito avoidance; see `dengue-fever` entry
- Hepatitis A: food/water-borne; vaccine highly effective
- Typhoid: food/water-borne; vaccine recommended for developing countries
- Yellow fever: mosquito-borne; endemic in parts of Africa and South America; some countries require valid yellow fever certificate for entry

## Assessment Approach

**Pre-travel consultation framework:**

Itinerary review:
- Specific countries and regions (not just country — malaria risk varies markedly within countries)
- Activities planned: urban vs rural, accommodation type, water sports, animal exposure, sexual activity
- Travel dates and duration

Vaccination review:
- Check AIR: identify current vaccination status
- Routine vaccines up to date: influenza (especially for long-haul or immunocompromised), MMR (measles risk remains in developing countries), dTpa (pertussis protection for travellers to high-risk areas)

Travel-specific vaccines (not NIP-funded — private cost to patient unless eligible):

Hepatitis A (Havrix, Avaxim):
- Recommended for all travel to developing countries; highly effective (protective within 2 weeks of first dose)
- 2-dose schedule (0 and 6–12 months); single dose provides protection for most healthy adults
- Twinrix: combined HAV + HBV vaccine (3-dose: 0, 1, 6 months)

Typhoid (Typhim Vi — injectable; Vivotif — oral):
- Recommended for travel to South and Southeast Asia, Africa, Central/South America
- Parenteral: single dose; lasts 3 years
- Oral live attenuated (Vivotif): 4 capsules on alternate days; do NOT give if immunocompromised; lasts 3–5 years; do NOT give with antibiotics or antimalarials

Yellow fever (Stamaril — live attenuated, given at authorised centres only):
- Mandatory for entry to certain countries (Ecuador, Ghana, some others)
- Recommended for travel to sub-Saharan Africa and tropical South America
- Single dose; lifelong protection in most; booster may be needed for specific high-risk populations
- Must be given by authorised yellow fever vaccination centre (list at DFAT); recorded in International Certificate of Vaccination
- Contraindicated: immunocompromised, infants <6 months, egg allergy (severe), thymus disease; careful risk-benefit in >60 years

Japanese encephalitis (IXIARO — inactivated):
- Recommended for: prolonged travel (>1 month) to rural Asia; short-term travellers to high-risk activities (rice farming areas, outdoor night exposure)
- 2-dose schedule (0 and 28 days); booster at 12–24 months if ongoing risk

Meningococcal ACWY (Nimenrix, Menveo):
- Required for Hajj/Umrah (Saudi Arabia requirement — certificate needed)
- Recommended for: travel to sub-Saharan Africa meningitis belt (dry season); immunocompromised travellers

Rabies (pre-exposure prophylaxis — Rabipur):
- Recommended for: travel to endemic areas with high-risk activities (caves with bats, animal handlers, long-stay travel to remote areas far from medical care)
- 3-dose schedule (days 0, 7, 21–28); does NOT eliminate need for post-exposure treatment — reduces urgency and simplifies treatment if exposed

Cholera (Dukoral oral vaccine):
- Recommended for aid workers and travellers to active outbreak areas
- 2 doses 1–6 weeks apart; partial cross-protection against E. coli travellers' diarrhoea (ETEC); limited overall evidence for routine use

**Malaria prophylaxis (see `malaria-prophylaxis` entry for full detail):**
- Assess destination-specific malaria risk and species; choose appropriate prophylaxis
- Atovaquone/proguanil (Malarone): most well-tolerated; start 1–2 days before; continue 7 days after
- Doxycycline: cost-effective; good broad-spectrum; sun sensitivity; start 1–2 days before; continue 4 weeks after
- Mefloquine (Lariam): weekly; neuropsychiatric side effects; avoid in mental health history
- Chloroquine: resistance widespread in most of Africa; only for limited specific areas

## Key Differentials (post-travel illness)

- Post-travel fever: most important differential is malaria (can be rapidly fatal); ALWAYS do urgent malaria film + PCR if fever <28 days after any malaria-endemic area travel; typhoid; dengue; chikungunya; rickettsia
- Travellers' diarrhoea vs giardia vs amoebiasis: stool MCS and ova/parasite examination; duration guides investigation
- Schistosomiasis: freshwater exposure Africa/Asia; eosinophilia; serology 6–8 weeks post-travel
- Strongyloides: persistent eosinophilia; serology; important to treat before immunosuppression
- Hepatitis A, E: jaundice post-travel; serology

## Management Principles

**Travellers' diarrhoea prevention and self-treatment:**

Prevention:
- "Boil it, cook it, peel it or forget it" — food safety
- Avoid ice, raw salads, uncooked shellfish, street food in high-risk areas
- Hand hygiene: soap and water; alcohol gel after toilet

Self-treatment:
- Oral rehydration: oral rehydration salts (ORS); Gastrolyte; prioritise hydration especially in children and elderly
- Loperamide (Imodium): symptomatic; reduces frequency; NOT if dysentery (blood/mucus — risk of prolonged illness)
- Antibiotics (provide as standby treatment for severe travellers' diarrhoea):
  - Azithromycin 500 mg daily × 1–3 days: first-line (resistance to fluoroquinolones increasing in Asia)
  - Norfloxacin 800 mg stat or ciprofloxacin 500 mg BD × 3 days: alternative; fluoroquinolone resistance in SE Asia
  - NOT for children <1 year; azithromycin preferred for paediatric use

**Sun, heat, and altitude:**
- Sunscreen SPF 50+; protective clothing; acclimatise to heat gradually
- Altitude sickness: acetazolamide (Diamox) for prevention ≥2500 m — 125–250 mg BD starting 24 hours before ascent; continue 2 days at altitude; side effects: paraesthesiae, polyuria; contraindicated: sulfa allergy

**Deep vein thrombosis — long-haul flights:**
- Hydration; leg exercises; compression stockings (grade 2) for high-risk patients
- LMWH (enoxaparin): only for very high-risk travellers with prior DVT, thrombophilia, recent surgery — see `thrombosis-dvt` entry

**Personal safety and sexual health:**
- Condoms: carry and use; STI and HIV risk in new sexual encounters
- Safe sex counselling; hepatitis B vaccination if not immune
- Female travellers: emergency contraception; tampon availability

**Altitude sickness:**
- Acetazolamide (Diamox) 125–250 mg BD: start 24 hours before ascent to ≥2500 m; continue 2 days at altitude; paraesthesiae and polyuria are expected side effects

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Atovaquone/proguanil (Malarone)** (PBS restricted: malaria prevention; private prescription for most travellers at patient cost; start 1–2 days before, continue 7 days after leaving endemic area)
- **Doxycycline 100 mg** (PBS restricted: antimalarial and antibiotic; sun sensitivity; start 1–2 days before; continue 4 weeks after; avoid in pregnancy and children <8 years)
- **Mefloquine (Lariam)** (PBS restricted: malaria prophylaxis; weekly; neuropsychiatric screening before prescribing; contraindicated in psychiatric history, epilepsy)
- **Azithromycin** (PBS unrestricted: provide as standby antibiotic for travellers' diarrhoea; 500 mg daily × 3 days)
- **Acetazolamide (Diamox)** (PBS unrestricted: altitude sickness prevention; sulfa allergy contraindication; 125–250 mg BD)
- **Travel vaccines**: not PBS-funded (private cost); HAV, HBV, typhoid, meningococcal, Japanese encephalitis, rabies; yellow fever — authorised centres only; check with DFAT for country-specific entry requirements

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED (post-travel):**
- Fever within 28 days of return from malaria-endemic area: URGENT malaria film and PCR — cannot exclude; can be fatal within hours; do not delay
- Severe dehydration or septic shock from travellers' diarrhoea — IV resuscitation
- Suspected dengue haemorrhagic fever: thrombocytopenia, haematuria, bleeding — 000

**Urgent review post-travel:**
- Fever without clear diagnosis: malaria, typhoid, dengue — same-day blood tests; malaria film mandatory
- Persistent diarrhoea >2 weeks: stool MCS and parasite examination
- Unexplained eosinophilia on post-travel bloods: consider schistosomiasis, strongyloides, filariasis

**Non-urgent:**
- Infectious disease specialist: complex post-travel illness; tropical disease (schistosomiasis, filariasis, leishmaniasis)
- Travel medicine clinic: specialist advice for complex itineraries or immunocompromised travellers

## Telehealth Suitability

Pre-travel consultation counselling is suitable for telehealth; vaccines require in-person.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Itinerary review and risk assessment
- Malaria prophylaxis prescribing and counselling
- Travellers' diarrhoea standby antibiotic prescribing
- Altitude sickness prevention prescribing
- Vaccine planning and scheduling discussion
- Safe sex and personal safety counselling

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Vaccine administration (hepatitis A, B, typhoid, meningococcal, Japanese encephalitis, rabies)
- Yellow fever vaccination — authorised centres only; must be in person
- Blood tests if pre-travel health screen required (e.g., HIV for long-stay volunteering)
