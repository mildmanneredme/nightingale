## Breast Symptoms Assessment (RACGP)

Breast symptoms are among the most anxiety-provoking presentations in GP practice. Breast cancer is the most common cancer in Australian women (incidence approximately 20,000 per year) and the second leading cause of cancer death. The GP's primary role is to: systematically assess breast symptoms, apply clinical decision rules to stratify urgency, initiate appropriate imaging, and ensure timely referral to breast surgical services. Most breast symptoms (>90%) have benign causes, but the clinical approach must exclude malignancy first. BreastScreen Australia provides free 2-yearly mammography screening for women aged 50–74 (women 40–49 and 75+ can also self-refer). Triple assessment (clinical, imaging, pathology) is the diagnostic gold standard for breast lumps. The Breast Cancer Network Australia (BCNA) provides patient support and resources. Telehealth is not appropriate for initial assessment of breast symptoms; imaging and examination are required.

## Clinical Presentation

**Breast lump:**
- Most common symptom prompting GP presentation; 80–85% are benign
- Benign causes: fibroadenoma (young women, rubbery, mobile, well-defined, no skin changes), fibrocystic change (cyclical with menses, tender, multiple areas), breast cyst (sudden onset, smooth, well-defined, often cyclical)
- Malignant features (RED FLAGS): hard, irregular, fixed, non-tender mass; skin tethering or dimpling; nipple retraction; ipsilateral lymphadenopathy (axillary)
- Age: fibroadenoma common in under-30s; carcinoma increasingly common after age 40; postmenopausal new lump is carcinoma until proven otherwise

**Breast pain (mastalgia):**
- Cyclical mastalgia: most common; bilateral; worse premenstrually; fibrocystic change; usually benign; confirm non-cyclical pain is not from lump
- Non-cyclical mastalgia: non-hormonal; localised or diffuse; important to exclude underlying lump with examination and imaging
- Costochondritis / musculoskeletal: chest wall tenderness; reproducible on palpation of costochondral junction; Tietze syndrome
- Referred pain: cervical radiculopathy, cardiac (angina in women may present as left breast pain)
- Breast pain alone (without mass) rarely presents as malignancy; however imaging is still indicated if focal and unexplained

**Nipple discharge:**
- Physiological: bilateral, milky, expressed (not spontaneous); rule out galactorrhoea (prolactin — medications, prolactinoma)
- Concerning features (need assessment): spontaneous, unilateral, from single duct, bloodstained or clear — excludes infection vs intraductal papilloma vs carcinoma
- Galactorrhoea: bilateral, milky, spontaneous — serum prolactin; medications (metoclopramide, antipsychotics, OCP)

**Skin and nipple changes:**
- Paget's disease of nipple: eczema-like rash on nipple/areola + underlying DCIS or carcinoma; does NOT respond to topical steroids; biopsy required
- Skin dimpling / peau d'orange (orange peel appearance): breast lymphoedema from tumour infiltration of dermal lymphatics; malignancy until proven otherwise
- Inflammatory breast cancer: rare; rapid onset breast erythema, warmth, skin thickening — may be mistaken for mastitis; no fever; NOT responding to antibiotics; urgent imaging + biopsy

**Mastitis and breast abscess:**
- Lactational mastitis: erythema, warmth, tenderness, fever; Staphylococcus aureus; continue breastfeeding or pumping (reduces ductal obstruction); flucloxacillin 500 mg QID × 10 days
- Non-lactational mastitis: periductal mastitis (central duct inflammation); smoking association; treat with antibiotics; mammography after treatment
- Breast abscess: fluctuant collection; ultrasound-guided aspiration or incision/drainage

## Assessment Approach

**Clinical examination (all patients with breast symptoms):**
- Bilateral breast examination: supine and upright; inspection (asymmetry, skin changes, nipple retraction) then systematic palpation of all quadrants including axillary tail
- Lymph node examination: axillary, supraclavicular — ipsilateral lymphadenopathy with breast lump = concerning
- Characterise any lump: size, consistency (soft, firm, hard), shape (regular, irregular), borders (well-defined, indistinct), mobility, skin or deep fixation, nipple retraction
- Note: normal breast tissue is nodular and the upper outer quadrant is most prominent; compare left and right systematically

**Triple assessment (gold standard for breast lump):**
1. Clinical examination (GP or surgeon)
2. Imaging: ultrasound (primary for <40 years and lactating women); mammography (≥40 years or post-menopausal); both may be combined
3. Tissue sampling: fine needle aspiration cytology (FNAC) or core needle biopsy (CNB — preferred; histological diagnosis) — performed by radiologist or surgeon

**Investigations:**
- Breast ultrasound: first-line for women under 40 years (denser breast tissue; mammogram less useful); cyst vs solid differentiation; guide biopsy
- Mammography: first-line for women ≥40 years; two-view; detects calcifications; limits in dense breasts
- MRI breast: supplementary; dense breasts; BRCA1/2 carriers (annual MRI from 30); staging; implant assessment
- Core needle biopsy: for solid masses on ultrasound; histological diagnosis; superior to FNAC
- Serum prolactin: if galactorrhoea
- hCG: if galactorrhoea in women of reproductive age (exclude pregnancy)
- Genetic testing: BRCA1/2 testing via genetics referral for strong family history (Familial Cancer Clinic — state cancer services)

## Key Differentials

- Fibroadenoma: young woman; rubbery, mobile, well-defined; benign; confirm with ultrasound + biopsy
- Breast cyst: smooth, well-defined; may be tender; ultrasound distinguishes solid vs cystic; aspiration if symptomatic
- Fibrocystic change: bilateral, cyclic, tender; often multiple areas; confirm with imaging
- Invasive ductal / lobular carcinoma: hard, irregular, fixed; skin changes; axillary nodes; urgent referral
- Mastitis: lactating woman; fever; tender erythema; antibiotics
- Inflammatory breast cancer: rapidly progressive erythema + oedema; no fever; does NOT respond to antibiotics; urgent imaging + biopsy
- Fat necrosis: post-traumatic or post-surgical; can mimic carcinoma on imaging; biopsy required
- Gynaecomastia (men): bilateral or unilateral; medications; liver disease; hypogonadism; exclude malignancy

## Management Principles

**Breast lump — urgent referral pathway:**
- Any new breast lump in a woman ≥40 years: urgent referral to breast surgeon or breast assessment clinic within 2 weeks
- Any suspicious clinical features regardless of age: urgent referral (hard, irregular, fixed, skin changes, nipple retraction, axillary nodes)
- Fibroadenoma in women <35 years with characteristic clinical and imaging features: routine referral or watchful waiting with annual imaging (BreastScreen if 40+)
- Postmenopausal new lump: urgent referral regardless of examination findings
- CRITICAL: document "suspected breast cancer" in referral letter to trigger urgent pathway

**Breast pain management:**
- Cyclical mastalgia (benign): reassurance; well-fitting bra; evening primrose oil (1 g TDS — limited evidence, safe); avoid caffeine; NSAIDs or topical diclofenac for acute flares
- Severe refractory cyclical mastalgia: danazol (limited use; androgenic side effects); tamoxifen 10 mg daily (breast surgeon or gynaecologist); bromocriptine (rarely)
- Non-cyclical mastalgia: analgesia; anti-inflammatory; treat underlying cause

**Mastitis:**
- Lactational mastitis: continue breastfeeding or pumping; warm compress; flucloxacillin 500 mg QID × 10–14 days (S. aureus); or dicloxacillin or cefalexin if penicillin allergy; complete full course to prevent abscess formation
- Breast abscess: ultrasound-guided aspiration (preferred over incision if available); flucloxacillin; surgical drainage if not responding

**BreastScreen Australia and family history:**
- BreastScreen Australia: free 2-yearly mammography for women 50–74 (0800 878 278)
- Women 40–49 and 75+: can self-refer; not routinely invited but eligible
- Familial Cancer Clinic referral: ≥2 first-degree relatives with breast/ovarian cancer; young-onset (<40); bilateral; male breast cancer; BRCA1/2 known — Familial Cancer Centre (state services)

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Flucloxacillin 500 mg QID** (PBS restricted: staphylococcal infections; on EMPTY STOMACH — 50% absorption reduction with food; 10–14 days for mastitis; watch for jaundice in elderly — flucloxacillin hepatitis)
- **Cefalexin 500 mg QID** (PBS restricted: cellulitis and skin infections; penicillin allergy alternative; with food OK; equivalent efficacy for lactational mastitis)
- **Ibuprofen 400 mg TDS** (PBS unrestricted: adjunct analgesia for mastalgia; NSAIDs reduce cyclical breast pain; take with food; PPI cover if risk factors)
- **Note:** Tamoxifen and danazol for mastalgia are second-line and generally managed by specialist (breast surgeon or gynaecologist); they are not routinely initiated in primary care for breast pain alone.

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent (same-day or within 2 weeks):**
- Any new lump ≥40 years or any suspicious features at any age: urgent breast surgeon referral; "suspected breast cancer" documented
- Postmenopausal new breast lump: urgent
- Inflammatory breast cancer features (erythema + oedema not responding to antibiotics): urgent referral
- Paget's disease of nipple: urgent surgical referral; biopsy required
- Spontaneous unilateral nipple discharge (especially bloodstained): urgent imaging and referral

**Urgent but not emergency:**
- Breast abscess requiring drainage
- Mastitis not responding to 48 hours of antibiotics (or if systemically unwell)

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Fibroadenoma confirmed on triple assessment in women <35 years: routine breast surgical review
- Familial risk assessment: Familial Cancer Clinic referral
- Galactorrhoea: endocrinology (if prolactinoma suspected)

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Review of imaging and biopsy results; explanation of findings
- Mastitis follow-up if clinically improving on antibiotics
- Cyclic mastalgia counselling and reassurance
- BreastScreen referral and family history risk assessment discussion
- Post-operative follow-up after breast surgery

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- ALL initial breast symptom presentations: clinical breast examination and lymph node assessment are mandatory
- Mastitis (assess for abscess formation); any suspected malignant lump
