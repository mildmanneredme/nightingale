## Lateral Epicondylitis (Tennis Elbow) Assessment (RACGP)

Lateral epicondylitis (tennis elbow) is a common overuse tendinopathy affecting the common extensor tendon origin at the lateral epicondyle of the humerus, most commonly involving extensor carpi radialis brevis (ECRB). It affects approximately 1–3% of adults, predominantly aged 35–54, and is strongly associated with repetitive gripping, forearm extension, and lifting activities — seen in manual workers and sports players. Despite the name, only 5% of cases occur in tennis players. The pathology is predominantly degenerative tendinosis (not acute inflammation), with angiofibroblastic degeneration of the ECRB origin. This distinction has therapeutic implications: corticosteroid injections provide short-term relief but are associated with worse long-term outcomes than physiotherapy, and have fallen out of favour in current guidelines. Physiotherapy with progressive loading (eccentric exercises) is the cornerstone of long-term management. Most cases resolve within 12–18 months with conservative treatment. Telehealth is suitable for management after initial diagnosis.

## Clinical Presentation

**Typical presentation:**
- Lateral elbow pain: insidious onset; localised to lateral epicondyle; may radiate down dorsal forearm
- Pain on gripping, lifting, or twisting forearm (turning a door handle, lifting a cup, typing)
- Morning stiffness; pain with resisted wrist extension
- Occupational or sporting history: repetitive wrist extension; gripping; typing; manual work; tennis or racquet sport

**Physical examination findings:**
- Tenderness on palpation: lateral epicondyle; 1–2 cm distal and anterior to the epicondyle (ECRB attachment)
- Cozen's test (resisted wrist extension with elbow straight and fist clenched): reproduces lateral elbow pain — highly sensitive
- Mill's test (passive wrist flexion with elbow extended): stretches ECRB; reproduces pain
- Maudsley's test (resisted extension of middle finger at MCP joint): isolates ECRB; positive in lateral epicondylitis
- Grip strength: reduced on affected side; grip dynamometer comparison
- Elbow range of motion: usually preserved (unlike arthritis or fracture)

**Red flags to exclude serious pathology:**
- Locking or clicking: intra-articular pathology (loose body, osteochondral lesion)
- Rest pain or night pain out of proportion: tumour, infection
- Neurological symptoms: paraesthesia or weakness in hand — posterior interosseous nerve entrapment (radial tunnel syndrome) in differential
- Systemic features: fever, weight loss

## Assessment Approach

**Clinical diagnosis in typical presentation:**
- Characteristic history + localised lateral epicondyle tenderness + positive Cozen's test = clinical diagnosis
- No investigations required for typical presentation
- Duration of symptoms: recent (<3 months) vs chronic (>6 months) influences prognosis and management

**Investigations (if diagnosis uncertain or atypical):**
- X-ray elbow: exclude bony pathology (loose body, osteoarthritis, calcification); normal in lateral epicondylitis (may show soft tissue calcification in chronic cases)
- Ultrasound: confirms tendinosis (thickened, hypoechoic tendon); calcification; neovascularisation; can guide injections; identifies partial or full-thickness tears
- MRI: second-line; if ultrasound inconclusive; pre-surgical planning

**Differential considerations:**
- Elbow X-ray: if acute injury mechanism, locking, or significantly reduced ROM
- Nerve conduction studies: if significant neurological symptoms — exclude posterior interosseous nerve entrapment

## Key Differentials

- Radial tunnel syndrome (posterior interosseous nerve entrapment): similar location; pain maximum at radial tunnel (4–5 cm distal to lateral epicondyle); night pain; Maudsley's and middle finger extension most positive; EMG differentiates
- Elbow osteoarthritis: diffuse pain; loss of extension; X-ray changes; older patients
- Lateral collateral ligament injury: acute mechanism; instability; valgus stress test; MRI
- Olecranon bursitis: posterior elbow; fluctuant swelling; gout or infection
- Referred pain: cervical radiculopathy (C5–C6); shoulder pathology; neural tension signs
- Plica syndrome: lateral elbow; snapping or catching sensation; arthroscopy
- Intra-articular loose body: locking; clicking; X-ray or MRI

## Management Principles

**Education and activity modification (first-line):**
- Explain: tendinopathy (not acute inflammation); natural history is recovery over 12–18 months
- Relative rest: avoid activities that aggravate pain; modify technique (ergonomics, grip, stroke mechanics in tennis)
- Ergonomic review: workstation assessment; keyboard position; tool grip size
- Counterforce brace (epicondyle strap): reduces tension at ECRB origin; may reduce pain with activity; OTC; variable evidence but widely used; does not replace physiotherapy

**Physiotherapy (cornerstone — superior long-term outcomes to corticosteroid injection):**
- Eccentric and progressive loading exercises: Tyler Twist (eccentric rubber bar twist); wrist extension eccentric loading; progressive resistance
- Manual therapy: joint mobilisation; soft tissue therapy; improves ROM and pain
- Dry needling: trigger point needling; some evidence; adjunct to exercise
- Ultrasound therapy: limited evidence; not routinely recommended
- Programme duration: 6–12 weeks minimum for significant improvement

**Analgesia:**
- Paracetamol 1 g QID: regular for acute pain control
- Topical diclofenac gel (Voltaren): topical NSAID; applied to lateral epicondyle BD–TDS; local effect; minimal systemic absorption; PBS-listed; good evidence for lateral epicondylitis; preferred over oral NSAIDs for local musculoskeletal pain
- Oral NSAIDs (ibuprofen 400 mg TDS or naproxen 500 mg BD): short-term (<2 weeks); cover with PPI if risk factors; limited role in tendinopathy (degenerative not inflammatory)

**Corticosteroid injection (SHORT-TERM ONLY — worse long-term outcomes):**
- Evidence: provides best short-term pain relief at 4–8 weeks; however, recurrence rates are higher and long-term outcomes at 12 months are WORSE than physiotherapy alone (Coombes et al. JAMA 2013)
- RACGP and SMA guidelines: corticosteroid injection NOT recommended as routine first-line treatment
- Consider: for severe acute pain preventing participation in physiotherapy; maximum 2 injections at least 6 weeks apart; inform patient of short-term vs long-term outcome data
- Technique: 1 mL methylprednisolone 40 mg + 1 mL 1% lignocaine; ultrasound-guided superior to blind injection; inject around but not into tendon
- Do NOT inject into tendon substance: risk of tendon rupture

**Platelet-rich plasma (PRP) injection:**
- Evidence: growing evidence for moderate benefit in tendinopathy; inferior to progressive loading exercise alone in some trials
- Not PBS-listed; private cost; specialist sports medicine or orthopaedics referral typically required
- Consider in chronic refractory lateral epicondylitis (>6 months) failing physiotherapy

**Surgical management:**
- Chronic refractory cases (>12–18 months) failing all conservative measures: open or arthroscopic ECRB debridement
- Outcomes: 80–90% improvement; recovery 3–6 months post-operatively
- Specialist (orthopaedics or sports medicine) referral for surgical consideration

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Topical diclofenac 1% gel (Voltaren Emulgel)** (PBS restricted: local musculoskeletal pain including lateral epicondylitis; apply BD–TDS; rub in for 60 seconds; avoid broken skin; OTC versions available at lower concentration)
- **Ibuprofen 400 mg TDS** (PBS unrestricted: OTC NSAID; short-term adjunct; GI protection with PPI if risk factors; avoid in renal impairment, heart failure)
- **Methylprednisolone acetate 40 mg/mL (Depo-Medrol)** (PBS restricted: intra-articular/periarticular injection; short-term pain relief only; maximum 2 injections; document shared decision-making about long-term outcome evidence)
- **Note:** Physiotherapy referral should be the primary recommendation for lateral epicondylitis. Corticosteroid injections should be used judiciously, with patients informed that short-term pain relief is the only benefit and long-term outcomes are not improved.

## Escalation Triggers

**Non-urgent orthopaedic/sports medicine referral:**
- Chronic lateral epicondylitis (>12 months) failing physiotherapy and conservative management: surgical evaluation; PRP injection consideration
- Radial tunnel syndrome (posterior interosseous nerve entrapment) confirmed: neurosurgical or orthopaedic referral
- Significant tendon tear on ultrasound: orthopaedic review

**Physiotherapy:**
- All patients with lateral epicondylitis should be referred for physiotherapy if symptoms persist beyond 4–6 weeks of conservative management; eccentric loading programme is superior to injection or analgesics alone

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Initial prescription of analgesia once clinical diagnosis established
- Physiotherapy referral and exercise instruction (progressive loading)
- Brace recommendation and ergonomic advice
- Review of symptoms and progression
- Corticosteroid injection post-procedure follow-up

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial diagnosis: palpation, provocative testing (Cozen's, Mill's, Maudsley's)
- Corticosteroid or PRP injection procedure
- Suspected intra-articular pathology or neurological compromise
