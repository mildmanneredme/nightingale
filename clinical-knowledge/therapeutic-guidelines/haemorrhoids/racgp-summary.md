## Haemorrhoids Assessment (RACGP)

Haemorrhoids (piles) are among the most common anorectal conditions managed in Australian general practice, affecting approximately 75% of people at some point in their lifetime. They are vascular cushions of the anal canal that become symptomatic when enlarged, prolapsed, or thrombosed. Internal haemorrhoids arise above the dentate line (not pain-sensitive); external haemorrhoids arise below the dentate line (pain-sensitive). Most haemorrhoids can be managed conservatively in general practice; surgical referral is required for persistent Grade III–IV prolapse or complications. GPs play a central role in: initial diagnosis, excluding sinister causes of rectal bleeding, conservative management, and timely referral for procedures (rubber band ligation, haemorrhoidectomy). Telehealth is suitable for follow-up; initial assessment and proctoscopy require in-person attendance.

## Clinical Presentation

**Internal haemorrhoids (grading system — guides management):**
- Grade I: bulge into canal lumen; no prolapse; painless bright red rectal bleeding (BRBPR) per rectum
- Grade II: prolapse on straining, spontaneously reduces; bleeding and mucus discharge
- Grade III: prolapse on straining, requires manual reduction; bleeding, soiling, pruritus ani
- Grade IV: permanently prolapsed; cannot be reduced; may thrombose and become acutely painful

**External haemorrhoids:**
- Visible at anal margin; covered by squamous epithelium (pain-sensitive)
- Thrombosed external haemorrhoid: sudden onset severe perianal pain; firm, tender, purple-blue subcutaneous mass at anal margin; no rectal bleeding; very painful — peak at 48–72 hours then gradually resolves over 7–10 days
- Skin tags: residual after resolved thrombosed external haemorrhoid; not haemorrhoids per se

**Symptoms:**
- Rectal bleeding: bright red, coating stool or on paper; per rectum or dripping into toilet; painless (internal); NOT mixed with stool (if mixed — more proximal cause, investigate)
- Prolapse: tissue protruding from anus on defecation
- Mucus discharge and soiling: perianal wetness, pruritus ani
- Discomfort: fullness, incomplete emptying sensation; aching (not severe unless thrombosed)
- Acute severe pain: suggests thrombosis or complication (fissure, abscess)

**Risk factors:**
- Chronic constipation and straining at stool
- Low-fibre diet
- Prolonged sitting (particularly on toilet)
- Pregnancy and postpartum (increased intra-abdominal pressure)
- Chronic diarrhoea (frequent anal irritation)
- Portal hypertension (rarely causes haemorrhoids via anorectal varices — important distinction)

## Assessment Approach

**History:**
- Characterise bleeding: fresh red vs dark; coating vs mixed with stool; volume; duration
- Bowel habits: constipation, diarrhoea, straining
- Prolapse: degree; reducible or irreducible
- Pain: location, severity, onset — acute severe pain suggests thrombosis, fissure, abscess
- Risk factors: diet, fluid intake, pregnancy
- Red flag symptoms: change in bowel habit, weight loss, iron deficiency, bleeding mixed with stool, age >50 with new rectal bleeding, family history of colorectal cancer — these require colonoscopy/sigmoidoscopy

**Physical examination:**
- Perianal inspection: external haemorrhoids, skin tags, fissures, fistula openings, prolapse
- Proctoscopy / digital rectal examination (DRE): performed by GP or surgeon; identifies internal haemorrhoids, fissure, mass; bimanual DRE for rectal cancer screening
- Proctoscopy: visualises anal canal directly; grades haemorrhoids; identifies haemorrhoid site

**Investigations:**
- FBC: if significant bleeding — iron deficiency anaemia
- Colonoscopy: required if red flag features (age ≥50, change in bowel habit, unexplained iron deficiency, family history CRC); rectal bleeding requires exclusion of colorectal cancer regardless of obvious haemorrhoids
- Flexible sigmoidoscopy: alternative to colonoscopy for lower rectal pathology

## Key Differentials

- Colorectal cancer: any rectal bleeding requires consideration; red flag features mandate colonoscopy; do not attribute bleeding to haemorrhoids without adequate exclusion in older patients
- Anal fissure: acute severe perianal pain with defecation; bright red bleeding; painful DRE; visible fissure at anal margin (usually posterior midline); see `anal-fissure` management principles
- Perianal abscess: severe constant throbbing perianal pain; fever; tender fluctuant mass — requires incision and drainage (surgical emergency)
- Rectal prolapse: full-thickness mucosa protrudes through anus; concentric rings visible (vs radial haemorrhoid folds); requires surgical referral
- Anorectal varices: in portal hypertension — cirrhosis; blue tortuous veins; banding contraindicated; treat underlying liver disease
- Pruritus ani: perianal itching — may be from haemorrhoid mucus but also eczema, psoriasis, pinworms, contact dermatitis; examine carefully

## Management Principles

**Conservative management (first-line for all grades):**

Dietary and lifestyle modification (cornerstone):
- High-fibre diet: 25–30 g/day; fruits, vegetables, legumes, wholegrain; reduces straining
- Adequate fluid intake: 2 L/day; essential with increased fibre
- Psyllium husk supplement: 1–2 teaspoons in water daily; softens stool; reduces bleeding and straining
- Avoid prolonged sitting on toilet: no phone use on toilet — reduces straining time
- Sitz baths: warm water baths 10–15 minutes, 2–3 times daily — soothes thrombosed haemorrhoids

Topical preparations:
- Proctosedyl, Anusol, Preparation H: local anaesthetic ± corticosteroid ± vasoconstrictor; symptomatic relief of pruritus and irritation; short-term use only (≤7 days corticosteroid-containing); NOT disease-modifying
- Hydrocortisone cream 1%: reduces inflammation; short-term perianal application

**Office-based procedures (by GP or surgeon):**
- Rubber band ligation (RBL): most effective non-surgical treatment for Grade I–III internal haemorrhoids; elastic band applied to haemorrhoid base; causes ischaemia and fibrous fixation; in-office procedure; best referral option for persistent bleeding from internal haemorrhoids
- Injection sclerotherapy: phenol in oil injected into haemorrhoid base; older technique; less effective than RBL

**Thrombosed external haemorrhoid:**
- Conservative (>72 hours since onset): sitz baths, analgesia (paracetamol + ibuprofen), high-fibre diet; resolves over 7–10 days; pain peaks at 48–72 hours
- Surgical excision (within 72 hours of onset): local anaesthetic; excision of thrombus and haemorrhoid; significant pain relief; performed by GP with surgical skills or surgical referral; discuss risk-benefit with patient

**Surgical management (colorectal surgeon):**
- Haemorrhoidectomy: Grade III–IV or failed office procedures; Milligan-Morgan excision; most effective long-term; significant post-operative pain; 2–4 weeks recovery
- Stapled haemorrhoidopexy (PPH): less post-operative pain; higher recurrence; specialist decision
- HALO (Haemorrhoid Artery Ligation and Anopexy): ultrasound-guided; minimally invasive; emerging

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Psyllium husk (Metamucil)** (PBS unrestricted: constipation/fibre supplementation; 1–2 teaspoons in water daily; available OTC)
- **Docusate sodium (Coloxyl)** (PBS unrestricted: stool softener; 120 mg twice daily; adjunct to fibre)
- **Proctosedyl ointment / suppository** (PBS unrestricted: local anaesthetic + corticosteroid; short-term symptomatic relief ≤7 days; contains framycetin, hydrocortisone, cinchocaine)
- **Hydrocortisone 1% cream** (PBS unrestricted: perianal inflammation; ≤7 days; avoid long-term — perianal skin atrophy)
- **Ibuprofen / paracetamol** (PBS unrestricted: analgesia for thrombosed external haemorrhoid; ibuprofen 400–600 mg TDS preferred for anti-inflammatory effect)
- **Note:** Topical haemorrhoid preparations provide symptomatic relief only; dietary modification and RBL (for internal haemorrhoids) are the primary effective treatments; exclude colorectal malignancy before attributing rectal bleeding to haemorrhoids

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate ED / urgent surgical:**
- Perianal abscess: severe constant pain, fever, fluctuant mass — requires incision and drainage; do not delay
- Gangrenous or strangulated haemorrhoid: irreducible prolapse with ischaemia, severe pain, necrosis — surgical emergency
- Significant acute haemorrhage with haemodynamic compromise: rare; 000

**Urgent colorectal surgery:**
- Grade IV haemorrhoids: permanently prolapsed, irreducible; require haemorrhoidectomy
- Failure of conservative treatment and RBL for Grade II–III haemorrhoids

**Urgent colonoscopy (2-week wait):**
- Any red flag features: change in bowel habit, weight loss, iron deficiency anaemia, rectal bleeding mixed with stool, age ≥50 years with new-onset rectal bleeding, family history colorectal cancer
- Do NOT attribute rectal bleeding to haemorrhoids without adequate investigation in at-risk patients

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Colorectal surgeon: Grade III–IV haemorrhoids, or Grade I–II not responding to conservative management + dietary modification
- Gastroenterologist: colonoscopy for appropriate risk stratification

## Telehealth Suitability

Haemorrhoid follow-up is suitable for telehealth; diagnosis and procedures require in-person.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Dietary and lifestyle counselling
- Medication review (topical preparations, fibre supplements)
- Post-procedure follow-up after RBL or haemorrhoidectomy
- Colonoscopy result discussion
- Red flag symptom triage

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Proctoscopy and DRE
- Perianal examination
- Rubber band ligation procedure
- Thrombosed haemorrhoid assessment and potential excision
