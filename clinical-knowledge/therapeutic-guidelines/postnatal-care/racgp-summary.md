## Postnatal Care Assessment (RACGP)

Postnatal care encompasses the physical, emotional, and social health of mother and infant in the weeks to months following birth. The 6-week postnatal check with the GP is a pivotal consultation mandated in Australian maternity care pathways, but postnatal care extends well beyond this. GPs manage: physical recovery from birth (perineal trauma, caesarean section recovery, musculoskeletal issues), breastfeeding support, contraception, mental health screening (Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale — EPDS), infant health, immunisation, and return-to-work planning. Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander women, women with complex social circumstances, and those with pregnancy complications require enhanced postnatal support. Telehealth is highly suitable for many postnatal consultations.

## Clinical Presentation

**Physical recovery — key issues by timeframe:**

Immediate (0–6 weeks):
- Perineal wound healing: episiotomy or laceration repair; infection signs (increased pain, discharge, fever); wound breakdown; refer to obstetrician if breakdown or infection
- Caesarean section recovery: wound infection, haematoma, wound dehiscence; wound check at 1–2 weeks; pain management; activity restriction (no driving 4–6 weeks); abdominal wall muscle recovery
- Lochia: normal bloody discharge weeks 1–2, reducing over 6 weeks; heavy fresh bleeding, large clots, offensive smell — retained products of conception or endometritis; urgent assessment
- Urinary: urinary incontinence (common post-vaginal delivery); urinary retention (post-catheter); UTI risk postpartum
- Postpartum thyroiditis: hyperthyroid phase at 2–3 months; hypothyroid phase at 4–6 months; check TSH at 6 weeks and 6 months in at-risk women (previous postpartum thyroiditis, family history thyroid disease)
- Anaemia: postpartum iron deficiency; FBC and iron studies if symptomatic (fatigue, pallor, palpitations)

6 weeks to 6 months:
- Breastfeeding: mastitis, nipple thrush, low supply; Australian Breastfeeding Association resources; IBCLC lactation consultant referral if needed
- Pelvic floor recovery: urinary and bowel incontinence; pelvic organ prolapse; referral to women's health physiotherapist recommended for all women post-vaginal delivery with incontinence
- Diastasis recti: abdominal separation; normal up to 2 cm; severe cases — physiotherapy
- Sexual health: resumption of sexual activity (no absolute timeline but usually after 6 weeks and perineal healing); dyspareunia (postpartum vulvovaginal atrophy from oestrogen deficiency in breastfeeding women); local oestrogen if needed
- Hair loss: postpartum alopecia (effluvium) at 3–4 months; reassure — resolves by 6–12 months; check iron and thyroid if severe

**Mental health — universal screening mandatory:**
- EPDS at: 6–8 weeks (minimum); 3–4 months; repeat if symptoms or risk factors
- See `postnatal-depression` entry for full PND/PNA management
- Baby blues: extremely common (50–80%); onset day 3–5; self-limiting; reassure
- PND: EPDS ≥13 or ≥10 with clinical concern — see PND entry
- Postpartum psychosis: rare (1:1000); rapid onset; psychiatric emergency; 000

**Breastfeeding issues:**
- Mastitis: hot, red, painful breast segment; fever, myalgia; continue breastfeeding (do NOT stop); dicloxacillin or flucloxacillin 500 mg QID × 10–14 days; cefalexin if penicillin allergy; referral to IBCLC; abscess — ultrasound, surgical or radiology drainage
- Nipple thrush: burning nipple pain; white patches on nipple/areola; treat with miconazole topical to nipple; treat infant oral thrush concurrently (nystatin suspension); fluconazole oral if severe

## Assessment Approach

**6-week postnatal check — comprehensive:**

Mother:
- EPDS — mandatory screening
- Blood pressure (pre-eclampsia follow-up if raised during pregnancy)
- BMI and weight review
- Cervical screening: if due (2-yearly from age 25 in Australia)
- Contraception: discuss and initiate (see `contraception` entry)
- Wound assessment: perineal, caesarean if applicable
- Pelvic floor: urinary and bowel incontinence assessment
- Breastfeeding: review if continuing; troubleshoot issues
- FBC, iron studies: if symptomatic anaemia
- TSH: postpartum thyroiditis check (selective — higher risk women)
- Domestic violence: screen routinely (RACGP recommendation) using validated tool (Routine Assessment for Current Health — REACH, or similar)
- Social support: isolation; partner support; financial stress; Cultural safety for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander women

Infant:
- Growth: weight, length, head circumference plotted on growth chart
- Feeding: breastfeeding or formula; adequate weight gain (regain birthweight by 2 weeks; 150–200 g/week gain in first months)
- Developmental milestones: visual tracking; social smile (6 weeks); hearing (audiological screening — Universal Newborn Hearing Screening completed by now)
- Immunisations: 6-week vaccines due — Infanrix hexa (diphtheria, tetanus, pertussis, Hib, HBV, IPV) + Prevenar 13 (pneumococcal) + RotaTeq (rotavirus oral) — all NIP-funded
- Jaundice: neonatal jaundice resolves by 2 weeks; prolonged jaundice (>2 weeks) — check bilirubin, direct/indirect split, LFTs; exclude biliary atresia (direct hyperbilirubinaemia — urgent paediatric surgery referral within 2 weeks of diagnosis — time-critical)

## Key Differentials

- Endometritis: postpartum uterine infection; fever, uterine tenderness, offensive lochia; IV antibiotics in hospital
- Retained products of conception (RPOC): heavy ongoing bleeding; ultrasound; surgical or medical management
- Postpartum thyroiditis: hyperthyroid or hypothyroid phase; TSH; often resolves; 25% develop permanent hypothyroidism
- Mastitis vs breast abscess: localised fluctuant mass, systemic unwell — abscess requires drainage
- Deep vein thrombosis: postpartum is highest risk period for VTE; leg pain, swelling; Doppler ultrasound
- Pulmonary embolism: chest pain, dyspnoea, tachycardia postpartum — always consider PE; 000 if suspected
- Wound infection (perineal or caesarean): increased pain, wound erythema, purulent discharge, fever
- Postpartum haemorrhage (late): abnormal uterine bleeding >24 hours post-delivery up to 12 weeks; exclude RPOC and endometritis; urgent assessment

## Management Principles

**Contraception initiation at postnatal check:**
- Breastfeeding women: progestogen-only pill (mini-pill), Mirena IUD, Implanon, Depo-Provera are all safe from 6 weeks (some from earlier); avoid combined OCP in first 6 weeks (breastfeeding mothers — oestrogen may reduce milk supply); discuss LAM (Lactational Amenorrhoea Method — only reliable if exclusively breastfeeding, no periods, <6 months)
- Non-breastfeeding women: combined OCP safe from 6 weeks; barrier methods immediately
- Copper IUD or Mirena: can be inserted at 6-week check or later
- See `contraception` entry for detailed management

**Immunisation at 6 weeks (NIP-funded):**
- Infanrix Hexa: diphtheria-tetanus-acellular pertussis-IPV-Hib-HBV
- Prevenar 13: pneumococcal conjugate vaccine
- RotaTeq: rotavirus (oral — dose 1 must be given before 14 weeks 6 days)
- Ensure maternal pertussis and influenza vaccines were given in pregnancy; if not — give postpartum

**Breastfeeding support:**
- Australian Breastfeeding Association (ABA): 1800 686 268; free helpline 24/7
- IBCLC (International Board Certified Lactation Consultant): for complex issues (poor latch, insufficient supply, pain)
- Domperidone (off-label galactagogue): some evidence for increasing supply; not first-line; cardiac risk — use cautiously; discuss risks
- Fenugreek and other herbal galactagogues: limited evidence; generally safe; inform GP of use

**Pelvic floor:**
- All women post-vaginal delivery with any incontinence: refer to women's health physiotherapist (MBS item if on Chronic Disease Management plan)
- Pelvic floor exercises: 8–12 contractions × 3/day; evidence for prevention and treatment
- Pelvic organ prolapse: symptoms of heaviness, bulge, urinary/bowel symptoms; assess; gynaecology referral if significant

**Return to exercise:**
- Low-impact exercise (walking) from 2 weeks
- Higher impact (running, jumping): after 12 weeks and only if pelvic floor fully recovered
- Women's health physiotherapy clearance recommended before high-impact return

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Iron supplements** (ferrous sulfate PBS unrestricted: iron deficiency anaemia; 325 mg daily; constipation common — take with vitamin C; switch to ferrous gluconate if intolerant)
- **Dicloxacillin / Flucloxacillin** (PBS restricted: skin and soft tissue infection; mastitis 500 mg QID × 14 days; penicillinase-resistant)
- **Cefalexin** (PBS unrestricted: infection; 500 mg QID; alternative if penicillin allergy — note cross-reactivity with cephalosporins ~1%)
- **Miconazole topical** (PBS unrestricted: fungal infection; nipple thrush; also treat infant with nystatin oral concurrently)
- **Thyroxine** (PBS restricted: hypothyroidism; if postpartum thyroiditis progresses to permanent hypothyroidism; TSH-guided dosing)
- **Note:** Many medications are compatible with breastfeeding; consult Mothersafe NSW (1800 647 848) or LactMed database for specific queries; do not unnecessarily cease breastfeeding for medications

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Heavy postpartum haemorrhage (soaking >1 pad/hour)
- Suspected pulmonary embolism (chest pain, dyspnoea, tachycardia)
- Postpartum psychosis: confusion, hallucinations, extreme agitation — psychiatric emergency
- Wound dehiscence with significant bleeding or evisceration
- Severe sepsis (fever >38°C, rigors, haemodynamic compromise)

**Urgent same-day assessment:**
- Endometritis: fever, uterine tenderness, offensive lochia — IV antibiotics required
- Retained products of conception: heavy irregular bleeding, subinvoluted uterus
- Mastitis not improving after 48 hours of antibiotics — suspect abscess; ultrasound
- Neonatal jaundice at ≥2 weeks — check direct bilirubin; biliary atresia is time-critical diagnosis
- DVT or thrombophlebitis postpartum

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Women's health physiotherapist: all women with incontinence, prolapse, diastasis recti
- IBCLC: breastfeeding difficulties
- Psychologist: postnatal depression/anxiety (GP Mental Health Care Plan MBS 2713)
- PANDA (Perinatal Anxiety and Depression Australia): 1300 726 306
- Australian Breastfeeding Association: 1800 686 268
- Gynaecology: significant pelvic organ prolapse, dyspareunia not resolving, cervical screening abnormalities

## Telehealth Suitability

Postnatal consultations are well suited to telehealth for non-physical assessments.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- EPDS administration and mental health review
- Breastfeeding counselling and support (where no examination needed)
- Contraception counselling and prescribing
- Blood result review (FBC, TSH, iron)
- Immunisation review and scheduling
- Medication queries (breastfeeding compatibility)
- Return-to-exercise and pelvic floor education
- Referral coordination (physio, IBCLC, PANDA)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- 6-week postnatal check: physical examination of mother and infant
- Wound assessment (perineal, caesarean)
- Pelvic floor examination if prolapse suspected
- Infant growth measurements
- Infant immunisations
- Blood tests — pathology arranged
