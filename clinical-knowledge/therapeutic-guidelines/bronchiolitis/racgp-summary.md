## Bronchiolitis Assessment (RACGP)

Bronchiolitis is an acute lower respiratory tract infection predominantly caused by Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV) in infants under 2 years of age. It is the most common cause of hospitalisation in infants in Australia, with peak incidence in winter. Clinical features: coryzal prodrome (2–3 days) → cough → wheeze/crackles → respiratory distress of variable severity. Bronchiolitis is a viral disease — antibiotics, salbutamol, steroids, and nebulised adrenaline are NOT routinely recommended in standard management and do not alter the disease course. Management is supportive: oxygen for hypoxia, nasal suction, adequate hydration (nasogastric or IV if poor oral intake). The GP's role is to assess severity, provide appropriate home management with clear safety-netting advice, and identify infants requiring hospital admission. CRITICAL thresholds for hospital admission: SpO₂ <92%, respiratory distress with feeding difficulty, or respiratory rate >70 bpm. Telehealth is NOT appropriate for initial assessment of a breathless infant.

## Clinical Presentation

**Typical course:**
- Age: <24 months; highest severity in infants <6 weeks; premature infants at greatest risk
- Day 1–3: viral prodrome; coryza; low-grade fever; cough begins
- Day 3–5: wheeze; subcostal and intercostal recession; crackles on auscultation; feeding difficulty
- Day 5–7: typically the worst; peak respiratory distress; SpO₂ may fall
- Resolution: gradual over 10–14 days; cough may persist 3–4 weeks

**Severity assessment:**

Mild (manage at home):
- Alert and well
- SpO₂ ≥95% on room air
- Respiratory rate <50 bpm in infants
- Normal or mildly increased work of breathing; no significant recession
- Adequate oral feeds (>50% of usual intake)
- No apnoeas

Moderate:
- SpO₂ 92–95% on room air
- Respiratory rate 50–70 bpm
- Moderate respiratory distress; subcostal/intercostal recession
- Feeding difficulty (taking <50% of usual intake)
- Needs close monitoring; hospital assessment

Severe (immediate hospital):
- SpO₂ <92% on room air
- Respiratory rate >70 bpm
- Significant respiratory distress; severe recession; nasal flaring; grunting
- Cyanosis
- Unable to feed; severely dehydrated
- Apnoeas (pauses in breathing) — CALL 000

**High-risk groups:**
- Age <6 weeks (highest risk of apnoea and respiratory failure)
- Prematurity (<35 weeks gestation at birth)
- Congenital heart disease (haemodynamically significant)
- Chronic lung disease (bronchopulmonary dysplasia)
- Immunodeficiency
- Neuromuscular disease

## Assessment Approach

**Clinical assessment:**
- Observation (before touching infant): breathing rate, work of breathing, colour, alertness
- Respiratory rate (count over 60 seconds): >60 bpm = concern; >70 bpm = severe
- SpO₂ probe: waveform quality important; infant SpO₂ probe; in-office pulse oximetry essential for all presentations
- Auscultation: fine inspiratory crackles (mucus in small airways); expiratory wheeze; reduced air entry = severe obstruction
- Work of breathing: subcostal recession (most specific), intercostal recession, nasal flaring, grunting, head bobbing
- Hydration: anterior fontanelle (sunken if dehydrated); skin turgor; mucous membranes; last wet nappy (>8 hours without wet nappy = significant dehydration)
- Feeding: volume taken in last feed; frequency of feeds

**Investigations (rarely required):**
- SpO₂: essential at every assessment; the most important objective measurement
- CXR: NOT routinely recommended — does not change management and involves radiation; only if diagnosis uncertain or complications suspected (lobar consolidation suggesting bacterial superinfection)
- Nasopharyngeal swab for RSV: rarely necessary for management; may assist with cohorting in hospital; PCR multiplex panels
- FBC/CRP: NOT routinely indicated for viral bronchiolitis; only if bacterial superinfection suspected (high fever, focal consolidation)

## Key Differentials

- Bacterial pneumonia: high fever (>39°C); focal crackles; CXR consolidation; sick-appearing; WBC elevated
- Asthma (first presentation): >12 months; response to salbutamol; eczema, rhinitis, family history; wheeze without prodrome
- Foreign body aspiration: sudden onset; asymmetric chest signs; hyperinflation one side on CXR; urgent bronchoscopy
- Heart failure (congenital heart disease): cardiac murmur; hepatomegaly; feeding difficulty from birth; poor weight gain
- Pertussis (whooping cough): paroxysmal cough; inspiratory whoop; post-tussive vomiting; immunisation history; see `whooping-cough`
- Vascular ring: stridor; difficulty swallowing; CT chest; ENT/paediatric cardiothoracic

## Management Principles

**Supportive care (the ONLY evidence-based treatments):**

Oxygen therapy:
- Target SpO₂ ≥92% (hospital); ≥95% at home (safe threshold before hospital referral)
- Low-flow nasal cannula oxygen; flow rate 0.1–2 L/min for infants
- High-flow nasal cannula (HFNC) for moderate-severe: hospital; reduces intubation rate

Feeding support:
- Small frequent feeds: smaller volumes more frequently; reduce fatigue from feeding
- Nasogastric tube (NGT): if infant unable to take ≥50% oral feeds; less traumatic than IV; hospital
- IV fluid (0.9% NaCl): if NGT not tolerated or respiratory distress too severe to safely NG-feed; hospital

Nasal suction:
- Saline nose drops + bulb syringe: effective at clearing nasal congestion; improves feeding
- In hospital: nasopharyngeal suction for thick secretions; before feeds; before SpO₂ reassessment
- At home: saline drops and nose clearing before each feed

**What does NOT work (do NOT use routinely):**
- Salbutamol (Ventolin): multiple RCTs show NO benefit in bronchiolitis; may cause tachycardia and agitation; NOT recommended; distinguish from asthma
- Adrenaline (epinephrine) nebulised: short-term symptom improvement only; does not alter disease course or hospital admission rate; NOT recommended
- Oral or inhaled corticosteroids: NO evidence of benefit; may worsen disease
- Antibiotics: NOT indicated unless proven bacterial superinfection (rare); reduces antibiotic stewardship
- Physiotherapy: NOT recommended in acute bronchiolitis (may increase distress)
- Ipratropium, antibiotics, antifungals: NO evidence

**Home management (mild bronchiolitis):**
- Saline nose drops before each feed; small frequent feeds
- Smoke-free environment: parental smoking worsens bronchiolitis significantly
- Safety netting: critical; when to seek medical attention (see below)
- Paracetamol for fever/discomfort: safe; does NOT treat bronchiolitis but manages fever
- Follow-up: review 24 hours if borderline; "if in doubt, bring back"

**Safety-netting advice (provide written to parents):**
- Go to emergency immediately (000) if:
  - Breathing faster than normal or laboured breathing
  - Blue lips or fingernails
  - SpO₂ <92% on home pulse oximeter (if available)
  - Prolonged pause in breathing (apnoea)
  - Severely reduced feeding (less than half their usual intake for 2 consecutive feeds)
  - Deteriorating alertness
  - Any parental concern about deterioration

**Prevention:**
- Nirsevimab (Beyfortus): monoclonal antibody prophylaxis for RSV; approved in Australia for high-risk infants; single IM injection; increasingly available; not yet universally funded on NIP (check current NIP schedule)
- Palivizumab (Synagis): monthly IM injection for highest-risk infants (premature, congenital heart disease, chronic lung disease) during RSV season; available through specialist paediatric services; PBS restricted for specific high-risk criteria

## Paediatric Context and Guardian Involvement

- Weight-based oxygen flow rates: 0.1–0.5 L/kg/min (hospital); general guidance 0.1–1 L/min for small infants in primary care
- SpO₂ measurement: infant-appropriate probe (hand or foot); ensure waveform quality; warm extremity if poor signal
- Parent education: critical component; parents must understand signs of deterioration; written safety-netting plan is essential
- Breastfeeding: strongly encourage continuation — breast milk reduces RSV severity; easier to breastfeed frequently in small volumes than formula
- Smoke exposure: counsel parents strongly; passive smoke significantly worsens respiratory illness in infants; smoking cessation resources
- Parental anxiety: common; validate concern; be explicit about when to return; never dismiss parental concern in an infant with respiratory illness

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **No specific pharmacological treatment for bronchiolitis is indicated**
- **Paracetamol 15 mg/kg/dose (Panadol Children's suspension)**: PBS unrestricted; fever management; weight-based dosing critical; maximum 4 doses/24 hours; check formulation concentration (50 mg/mL vs 100 mg/mL — different volumes)
- **Saline nasal drops (0.9% NaCl)**: OTC; nasal toilet before feeds; safe; effective for congestion
- **Nirsevimab (Beyfortus)**: check current NIP funding status; for high-risk infants; administered IM; specialist initiation or primary care depending on local guidelines
- **Note:** Avoid prescribing salbutamol or oral steroids for bronchiolitis — they are ineffective and potentially harmful. If parents request medications, explain the evidence clearly and focus on supportive care education.

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / emergency:**
- Apnoea or respiratory arrest
- Cyanosis (blue lips, fingertips)
- Severe respiratory distress: grunting, severe recession, unable to feed
- Rapidly deteriorating infant

**Hospital admission (ED referral):**
- SpO₂ <92% on room air at any point
- Respiratory rate >70 bpm
- Moderate-severe respiratory distress
- Unable to maintain hydration (feeding <50% usual intake for two consecutive feeds)
- Age <6 weeks or high-risk infant (premature, congenital heart disease, chronic lung disease)
- Social circumstances precluding safe home monitoring; inability to return promptly if deteriorates

**Review within 12–24 hours:**
- Any infant assessed as mild but at the upper limit; parental concern; borderline SpO₂

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Post-discharge follow-up (confirmed improving, SpO₂ ≥95%, good oral intake)
- Parental reassurance for convalescent phase (weeks 2–3 cough)
- Discussion of prevention (nirsevimab counselling)
- Immunisation catch-up planning

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- ALL acute presentations of breathless infant: SpO₂ measurement, respiratory rate assessment, feeding evaluation — cannot be performed via telehealth
- Telehealth should NEVER be used to assess a breathless or unwell infant
