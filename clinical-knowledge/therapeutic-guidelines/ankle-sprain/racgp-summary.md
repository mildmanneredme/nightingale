## Ankle Sprain Assessment (RACGP)

Ankle sprains are among the most common musculoskeletal injuries encountered in general practice and emergency settings, accounting for approximately 5% of emergency presentations in Australia. The lateral ankle complex (anterior talofibular ligament — ATFL, calcaneofibular ligament — CFL, posterior talofibular ligament — PTFL) is injured in 85% of cases through an inversion mechanism. Medial (deltoid ligament) sprains are less common. The Ottawa Ankle Rules provide a validated, sensitive clinical decision tool to determine which patients require X-ray to exclude fracture, significantly reducing unnecessary imaging. Ankle sprains frequently become chronic and recurrent; rehabilitation addressing proprioception and neuromuscular control is critical to preventing re-injury. Physiotherapy referral is the cornerstone of management beyond the acute phase. Telehealth is suitable for follow-up once fracture is excluded.

## Clinical Presentation

**Lateral ankle sprain (ATFL most commonly injured):**
- Mechanism: inversion + plantar flexion (rolling ankle inward, often during sport or walking on uneven ground)
- Immediate pain; swelling develops over minutes to hours; ecchymosis develops over 24–48 hours
- Point tenderness: anterior talofibular ligament (anterior to lateral malleolus) — most common
- Point tenderness: calcaneofibular ligament (inferior to lateral malleolus) — grade II–III involvement

**Grading:**
- Grade I (mild): ligament stretch; no tear; mild tenderness and swelling; stable ankle; full weight-bearing possible
- Grade II (moderate): partial ligament tear; moderate swelling and ecchymosis; reduced range of motion; partial instability; painful weight-bearing
- Grade III (severe): complete ligament tear; severe swelling and ecchymosis; significant instability; unable to weight-bear; positive anterior drawer and talar tilt tests

**Medial ankle sprain (deltoid ligament):**
- Eversion mechanism; less common; associated with proximal fibular fracture (Maisonneuve fracture) — palpate full fibula length
- Stronger ligament complex; higher force required; may be associated with syndesmotic injury

**Syndesmotic (high ankle) sprain:**
- Injury to tibiofibular syndesmosis (anterior inferior tibiofibular ligament)
- Squeezing fibula against tibia (squeeze test) positive; pain with external rotation of foot (external rotation stress test)
- Slower recovery than lateral sprain; orthopedic review often needed; boot immobilisation

**Chronic ankle instability:**
- Recurrent sprains; subjective giving way; persistent weakness; impaired proprioception
- Occurs in 40–70% after lateral ankle sprain; functional instability most common type
- Requires targeted rehabilitation ± surgical reconstruction

## Assessment Approach

**Ottawa Ankle Rules (exclude fracture — >98% sensitivity for clinically significant fracture):**

X-ray indicated if ANY of:
- Bone tenderness at posterior edge or tip of lateral malleolus (distal 6 cm of fibula)
- Bone tenderness at posterior edge or tip of medial malleolus (distal 6 cm of tibia)
- Inability to weight-bear: 4 steps immediately after injury AND at time of examination
- Bone tenderness at base of 5th metatarsal (Jones fracture — inversion injury)
- Bone tenderness at navicular

**Physical examination:**
- Swelling: localisation (anterior lateral = ATFL; subtalar = CFL)
- Palpation: systematic — ATFL, CFL, PTFL, base of 5th metatarsal, navicular, full fibula (Maisonneuve), talar dome, peroneal tendons
- Anterior drawer test: grade III ATFL tear — forward displacement of talus in the mortise; positive = increased laxity compared to contralateral
- Talar tilt test: CFL tear — inversion stress with heel in neutral; positive = excessive inversion
- Squeeze test + external rotation test: syndesmotic injury
- Range of motion: dorsiflexion, plantarflexion, inversion, eversion

**Investigations:**
- X-ray ankle (AP, lateral, mortise views): if Ottawa Rules positive; exclude fracture
- MRI ankle: if diagnosis uncertain after X-ray; suspected osteochondral lesion (persistent pain after conservative management); peroneal tendon tear; pre-surgical planning
- Ultrasound: peroneal tendon tear or tendinopathy; available in general radiology

## Key Differentials

- Fracture: Ottawa Rules positive; point tenderness on bone; X-ray required
- Peroneal tendon tear/subluxation: tendon behind lateral malleolus; pain behind rather than anterior to lateral malleolus; pop felt during eversion; ultrasound
- Osteochondral talar dome lesion: persistent ankle pain months after sprain not responding to physiotherapy; MRI
- Sinus tarsi syndrome: chronic lateral pain between lateral malleolus and calcaneum; tenderness in sinus tarsi; associated with ATFL injury; CT or MRI
- Achilles tendinopathy or rupture: posterior ankle; Thompson test (Simmond's test) for rupture
- Stress fracture: overuse; athletes; fifth metatarsal stress fracture; MRI or bone scan

## Management Principles

**Acute phase (0–3 days) — POLICE principle:**
- **P**rotection: lace-up brace or ankle strap; prevent re-injury; avoid high-risk activities; NOT rigid immobilisation (delays recovery)
- **OL**ptimal **L**oading: encourage early weight-bearing as tolerated; protected mobilisation better than rest; crutches only if unable to weight-bear
- **I**ce: 15–20 minutes every 2 hours; reduces oedema and pain; ice pack with towel barrier (avoid direct skin contact — frostbite)
- **C**ompression: elasticated bandage from toes to mid-calf; reduces swelling; not too tight
- **E**levation: above heart level; reduces dependent oedema

**AVOID: HARM in acute phase:**
- **H**eat; **A**lcohol; **R**unning; **M**assage — all increase swelling in acute 48–72 hours

**Analgesia:**
- Paracetamol 1 g QID + ibuprofen 400 mg TDS (if no contraindications): regular for first 48–72 hours; reduces pain and swelling
- Topical NSAIDs (diclofenac gel): adjunct; reduces local inflammation; useful if systemic NSAID contraindicated

**Physiotherapy (cornerstone of management — prevent chronic instability):**
- Grade I–II: commence physiotherapy within 1 week; proprioception and neuromuscular control exercises key
- Grade III: physiotherapy 1–2 weeks after acute swelling settles; bracing and progressive loading
- Rehabilitation phases: acute (swelling control) → subacute (range of motion, early loading) → strength (calf, peroneals, tibialis) → proprioception (balance board, single-leg stance) → sport-specific (return to activity)
- Bracing: lace-up functional brace for return to sport (up to 12 months); reduces re-injury risk by 50%

**Return to sport / activity:**
- Grade I: 1–2 weeks; Grade II: 2–6 weeks; Grade III: 6–12 weeks or longer
- Criteria for return: full range of motion, no pain, single-leg hop test, proprioception normal

**Chronic ankle instability:**
- Structured physiotherapy: 6–12 weeks peroneals, tibialis anterior, proprioception
- Functional ankle bracing: during high-risk sports activities
- Orthopedic referral: surgical stabilisation (Brostrom procedure) if structural instability persists after full physiotherapy trial

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Paracetamol 500–1000 mg QID** (PBS unrestricted: OTC; analgesia; first-line; safe short-term)
- **Ibuprofen 400 mg TDS with food** (PBS unrestricted: OTC NSAID; reduces inflammation and pain; 7–10 day course; avoid in renal impairment, peptic ulcer, heart failure)
- **Naproxen 500 mg BD** (PBS restricted: NSAID; twice daily dosing; cover with PPI if risk factors for GI bleed)
- **Note:** Corticosteroid injection is NOT indicated for acute ankle sprain — evidence does not support use; may impair ligament healing. Physiotherapy referral provides superior long-term outcomes than any pharmacological intervention.

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate hospital:**
- Open fracture or significant fracture-dislocation: Ottawa Rules positive + deformity; 000
- Neurovascular compromise: absent pulse, compartment syndrome (pain out of proportion, tense compartment, parasthesia); urgent surgical consultation

**Urgent referral:**
- Maisonneuve fracture (medial ankle sprain + proximal fibular pain): orthopaedics; ankle mortise may be unstable
- Syndesmotic injury: significant instability; requires boot immobilisation and orthopaedic review
- Osteochondral lesion on MRI: orthopaedic/sports medicine review

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Physiotherapy: ALL significant sprains (Grade II–III) for rehabilitation; strongly reduces risk of re-injury and chronic instability
- Orthopaedics/sports medicine: chronic instability after physiotherapy; surgical stabilisation consideration
- Podiatry: foot and ankle biomechanics; custom orthotics for recurrent sprains

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Follow-up after fracture excluded; analgesia review
- Physiotherapy referral and exercise prescription advice
- Return to work and activity planning
- Chronic instability management review

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial assessment: Ottawa Rules application; palpation; X-ray if indicated
- Suspected fracture or neurovascular compromise
- Syndesmotic injury assessment
