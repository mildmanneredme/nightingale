## COPD Assessment (RACGP)

Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) is a progressive, largely irreversible airflow limitation caused by significant tobacco smoke exposure (90% of cases) or other inhaled irritants. It affects approximately 1 in 13 Australians aged ≥40 years and remains significantly underdiagnosed. COPD is characterised by airway inflammation, emphysema, and mucus hypersecretion. Management focuses on symptom relief, preventing exacerbations, slowing progression, and maintaining quality of life. Telehealth is well-suited to medication review, inhaler technique assessment via video, and monitoring of stable disease between in-person spirometry checks.

## Clinical Presentation

Typical symptoms: chronic progressive dyspnoea (initially on exertion, later at rest), chronic cough (often productive), and regular sputum production. Wheeze may be present. Symptoms develop insidiously and patients often attribute breathlessness to ageing or deconditioning.

Exacerbations: acute worsening of respiratory symptoms beyond normal day-to-day variation, requiring a change in management. Features: increased dyspnoea, increased sputum volume or purulence, wheeze, chest tightness. Trigger: most commonly viral URTIs; also bacterial infections, air pollution.

Severe COPD: significant breathlessness at rest or minimal exertion, frequent exacerbations, weight loss, cor pulmonale (peripheral oedema, raised JVP).

Risk factors: cigarette smoking (dose-response relationship — pack-years), occupational dust/chemical exposure, air pollution, biomass fuel exposure, recurrent childhood respiratory infections, alpha-1-antitrypsin deficiency (younger patient, non-smoker, lower lobe emphysema).

## Assessment Approach

Spirometry is required for diagnosis: post-bronchodilator FEV1/FVC <0.70 confirms airflow obstruction. Severity graded by FEV1% predicted (GOLD 1–4).

GOLD severity grading:
- GOLD 1 (mild): FEV1 ≥80% predicted
- GOLD 2 (moderate): FEV1 50–79%
- GOLD 3 (severe): FEV1 30–49%
- GOLD 4 (very severe): FEV1 <30%

At every review: assess symptoms using MRC dyspnoea scale or CAT (COPD Assessment Tool — 8-item score), exacerbation history (frequency and severity in past 12 months), medication adherence, inhaler technique, smoking status, oxygen use, functional status, and comorbidities.

Annual investigations: pulse oximetry (SpO₂), chest X-ray if clinically indicated (not routinely annual), FBC (polycythaemia suggests chronic hypoxia).

Assess for long-term oxygen therapy (LTOT) criteria: SpO₂ ≤88% at rest, or PaO₂ ≤55 mmHg on arterial blood gas — requires specialist review.

Screen for comorbidities: cardiovascular disease (common), depression and anxiety (up to 40%), lung cancer, osteoporosis, malnutrition.

## Key Differentials

- Asthma — younger onset, variable airflow limitation, more reversibility on spirometry; may coexist (Asthma-COPD Overlap Syndrome — ACOS)
- Cardiac failure — dyspnoea, orthopnoea, bilateral crackles, raised JVP, peripheral oedema; BNP/NT-proBNP elevated
- Bronchiectasis — productive purulent cough, recurrent infections, CT shows dilated bronchi
- Lung cancer — weight loss, haemoptysis, smoking history; LDCT screening recommended for high-risk smokers

## Management Principles

**Smoking cessation: single most effective intervention** — slows FEV1 decline significantly; all COPD patients who smoke should receive cessation support at every visit.

**Pulmonary rehabilitation:** Reduces dyspnoea, improves exercise tolerance, reduces exacerbations and hospital admissions; refer all patients with GOLD 2+ or MRC ≥2. Available via community and hospital programs.

**Pharmacological — stable COPD (COPDX stepwise approach):**

SABA/SAMA PRN (relief): salbutamol MDI or ipratropium MDI — for immediate symptom relief; all symptomatic patients.

LAMA first-line maintenance (monotherapy): tiotropium or umeclidinium — reduces dyspnoea, improves exercise tolerance, reduces exacerbations.

LABA maintenance: salmeterol, formoterol, indacaterol — alternative or additive to LAMA.

LAMA + LABA combination: superior to monotherapy in moderate-severe COPD; preferred step-up (e.g., umeclidinium/vilanterol, tiotropium + olodaterol).

Add ICS (LABA/ICS) if: exacerbations ≥2/year despite LAMA/LABA, or blood eosinophils ≥300 cells/µL (suggests steroid responsiveness). Note: ICS in COPD is associated with increased pneumonia risk.

Triple therapy (LAMA + LABA + ICS): for frequent exacerbators not controlled on dual therapy.

**Vaccination:** Annual influenza vaccination (strongly recommended — reduces exacerbations); pneumococcal vaccination (23vPPV); COVID-19 vaccination; pertussis booster.

**Nutritional support:** Undernutrition in advanced COPD — refer to dietitian; nutritional support improves outcomes.

**Exacerbation management:** Increase bronchodilator frequency; short course prednisolone 30–40 mg daily for 5 days; antibiotics if purulent sputum or clinical infection; escalate to ED if severe (see Escalation Triggers).

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Tiotropium** (Spiriva Handihaler or Respimat — PBS restricted: COPD with FEV1 ≤70% predicted) — 18 mcg daily
- **Umeclidinium** (Incruse Ellipta — PBS restricted: symptomatic COPD)
- **Salbutamol MDI** (PBS unrestricted — reliever)
- **Ipratropium MDI** (Atrovent — PBS restricted: COPD)
- **Salmeterol/fluticasone** (Seretide — PBS restricted: COPD with FEV1 ≤50% and recurrent exacerbations)
- **Budesonide/formoterol** (PBS restricted: COPD)
- **Umeclidinium/vilanterol** (Anoro Ellipta — PBS restricted: COPD)
- **Prednisolone** (PBS unrestricted — for exacerbation management; 30–40 mg daily for 5 days)
- **Roflumilast** (PBS restricted: severe COPD with chronic bronchitis and frequent exacerbations; specialist prescription or GP with authority)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED — severe acute exacerbation:**
- Severe dyspnoea at rest, accessory muscle use, tracheal tug
- Cyanosis or SpO₂ <88%
- Altered consciousness, confusion
- Haemoptysis
- Inability to complete sentences due to breathlessness
- Not responding to increased bronchodilators

**Urgent in-person GP or ED within hours:**
- Moderate exacerbation — increased dyspnoea not responding to reliever, increased purulent sputum
- SpO₂ 88–92% in a patient not usually on home oxygen
- Signs of cor pulmonale: progressive peripheral oedema

**Non-urgent specialist referral:**
- Respiratory physician: initial GOLD 2+ diagnosis, frequent exacerbators (≥2/year), consideration of LTOT, diagnostic uncertainty, suspected alpha-1-antitrypsin deficiency
- Palliative care: advanced COPD with severe functional limitation and high symptom burden — early palliative input improves quality of life

## Telehealth Suitability

Stable COPD management is well-suited to telehealth. In-person spirometry is required periodically.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Routine symptom review (MRC or CAT score)
- Medication review and inhaler technique assessment via video — demonstrate device use on camera
- Smoking cessation support and referral (Quitline 13 7848)
- Pulmonary rehabilitation referral and progress review
- Exacerbation action plan education and review
- Vaccination status review and referral

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Spirometry (diagnosis and annual monitoring)
- Pulse oximetry for LTOT assessment
- Auscultation for acute exacerbation severity assessment
- Cardiac examination for cor pulmonale
