## Febrile Child Assessment (RACGP)

Fever in children (temperature ≥38°C) is one of the most common reasons for GP and emergency department presentations in Australia. The majority of fevers in children are caused by self-limiting viral infections and require only symptomatic management and reassurance. The critical challenge is identifying the minority of children with serious bacterial infection (SBI) — including meningitis, septicaemia, urinary tract infection, pneumonia, and osteomyelitis — who require urgent assessment and treatment. Fever is a normal physiological response and temperature alone is not a reliable indicator of severity; clinical assessment of the child's overall appearance and behaviour is paramount. Telehealth can support initial assessment, triage, and parental guidance, but significant limitations apply to assessment of young infants and systemically unwell children.

## Clinical Presentation

**Definition of fever:** Temperature ≥38°C (rectal, oral, axillary or tympanic — method and site should be specified; rectal most accurate in infants <3 months).

**Age-specific risk:**
- <3 months (especially <1 month): highest risk of serious bacterial infection; lower threshold for investigation and empiric treatment; many look surprisingly well despite SBI
- 3–6 months: significant risk of SBI; careful assessment required
- >6 months in well-appearing child: majority viral; risk of SBI lower but not absent
- Immunocompromised child (cancer, immunosuppressive therapy, asplenia): any fever requires urgent assessment regardless of age

**Clinical features of the febrile child:**
- Behaviour: How does the child appear between fever peaks? Playing, interacting normally = reassuring. Persistent lethargy, irritability, inconsolable crying, refusing to weight-bear = concerning
- Colour: pink/normal vs pallor, mottling, cyanosis
- Hydration: normal wet nappies, tears, moist mucous membranes = adequate; dry mouth, no tears, sunken fontanelle, significantly reduced wet nappies = dehydration
- Respiratory: comfortable breathing vs tachypnoea, recession, grunting
- Perfusion: capillary refill ≤2 seconds normal; >2 seconds concerning; abnormal skin perfusion = sepsis risk
- Rash: non-blanching purpuric rash = meningococcal septicaemia (emergency); blanching rash = viral (less concerning but not diagnostic)

**Common causes by age:**
- <3 months: GBS, E. coli (bacteraemia, UTI, meningitis), Listeria; also viral (RSV, enterovirus)
- 3 months to 5 years: viral URTI, otitis media, pharyngitis; UTI (common cause SBI); pneumococcal disease (vaccinated vs unvaccinated); meningococcal disease; roseola (rash post-fever)
- >5 years: viral URTI, streptococcal pharyngitis, influenza; EBV/glandular fever; UTI

**Temperature height and SBI:** Temperature height alone does not reliably predict SBI. A temp of 40°C in a happy, well-perfused toddler is less concerning than a temp of 38.5°C in a lethargic, mottled 6-week-old.

## Assessment Approach

**Traffic light system (NICE/PREDICT-derived — adapted for Australian practice):**

Green (low risk): Normal colour, normal activity between fever peaks, responds normally to social cues, normal respiratory rate and effort, moist mucous membranes, no rash or blanching rash only, no petechiae. → Reassurance, symptomatic management, safety netting.

Amber (intermediate risk): Pallor reported by parent, reduced activity but rousable, tachycardia (HR above age-normal limits), tachypnoea, dry mucous membranes, poor feeding (but some fluid intake), fever >5 days without localising source, swollen limbs/joints, no weight-bearing. → Investigate, consider antibiotic treatment; review within 24 hours or refer.

Red (high risk): Mottled/pallor/cyanosis, not roused by social stimuli, does not rouse, inconsolable cry, grunting/recession/nasal flaring, significantly decreased skin turgor, non-blanching rash/petechiae, fever in child <3 months, rigors, focal neurological signs. → Immediate hospital transfer (000).

**Vital sign age-appropriate norms (approximate):**
- Heart rate: <3 months >160 concerning; 3–12 months >150; 1–5 years >140; >5 years >130
- Respiratory rate: <6 months >60; 6–12 months >50; 1–5 years >40; >5 years >30

**Investigations (as clinically indicated):**
- Urine: mid-stream urine or catheter specimen in infants — UTI is the most common serious bacterial infection missed in young febrile children; dipstick + MC&S in any infant or child with unexplained fever ≥48 hours
- Blood tests (amber/red features or ill-appearing): FBC, CRP, blood culture, UEC, LFTs; procalcitonin (rising use in paediatric febrile assessment)
- Throat swab: if pharyngitis or glandular fever suspected
- Chest X-ray: if respiratory signs or fever without source
- LP (lumbar puncture): if meningitis suspected; after stabilisation
- Blood glucose: always check in unwell febrile child (hypoglycaemia possible)

## Key Differentials

- Viral URTI (most common) — rhinovirus, coronavirus, influenza, RSV; often with coryzal symptoms; self-limiting
- Urinary tract infection — most commonly missed SBI; suspect in febrile infant/toddler without clear focus; urine testing essential
- Otitis media — common in young children; examine ears
- Streptococcal pharyngitis — >5 years; tonsillar exudate, tender cervical nodes; throat swab; if positive, treat (see URTI entry for antibiotic details)
- Roseola (HHV-6) — high fever 3–5 days, then rash as fever resolves; classic in toddlers
- EBV (glandular fever) — fever, exudative tonsillitis, lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly in older children/teens; avoid amoxicillin (causes rash)
- Meningococcal disease — non-blanching petechial/purpuric rash; septic shock features; emergency
- Bacterial meningitis — stiff neck, photophobia, altered consciousness, bulging fontanelle in infant; LP for diagnosis; immediate hospitalisation
- Kawasaki disease — fever >5 days + ≥4 of: conjunctival injection, polymorphous rash, lip/oral changes, cervical lymphadenopathy, changes to hands/feet; coronary artery aneurysm risk; paediatric referral
- Septic arthritis/osteomyelitis — localised bone or joint pain, swelling, fever, refusal to weight-bear; orthopaedic emergency in child

## Management Principles

**Antipyretics (symptomatic — not required to treat fever per se, but reduce discomfort):**

Paracetamol:
- First-line for fever-associated discomfort; weight-based dosing is mandatory
- Dose: 15 mg/kg/dose (max 1 g) every 4–6 hours; maximum 4 doses in 24 hours
- Age: suitable from birth; safe in infants; available as infant drops, liquid, suppository

Ibuprofen:
- Effective antipyretic and anti-inflammatory; adds analgesic benefit; can alternate with paracetamol
- Dose: 5–10 mg/kg/dose (max 400 mg) every 6–8 hours; maximum 3 doses in 24 hours
- Age: suitable from 3 months and ≥5 kg; avoid in dehydration, renal impairment, active bleeding
- Caution in varicella infection (aspirin avoided entirely — Reye's syndrome; ibuprofen also generally avoided in varicella)

Alternating paracetamol and ibuprofen: common practice but no strong evidence for superior fever control over single agent; may reduce dose of each; acceptable if child very distressed.

**Fluids:**
- Encourage oral fluids; breastfeeding on demand; clear fluids for older children
- Ice blocks, diluted cordial, water; avoid carbonated drinks in young infants
- Signs of dehydration (see above) — seek review or hospital assessment

**When to start antibiotics empirically:**
- Confirmed or suspected SBI: bacterial UTI (positive dipstick/culture — treat empirically), bacterial meningitis, septic arthritis, pneumonia with consolidation
- Febrile infants <3 months (especially <28 days): empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics (hospital setting — IV benzylpenicillin + gentamicin) pending culture results
- Community-based antibiotic prescription in older children: only if bacterial source identified (streptococcal pharyngitis, bacterial otitis media, UTI confirmed on dipstick/culture)

**Antibiotic selection (common paediatric infections):**
- UTI: trimethoprim 4 mg/kg (max 150 mg) twice daily for 3–5 days; or cefalexin 12.5 mg/kg (max 500 mg) three times daily; urine culture to guide therapy
- Streptococcal pharyngitis: amoxicillin 50 mg/kg/day (max 1.5 g/day) in divided doses for 10 days
- Otitis media: amoxicillin first-line if antibiotic indicated (see otitis entry for criteria)

**Safety netting — parental guidance (essential):**
Return promptly if: non-blanching rash develops, child becomes difficult to rouse or inconsolable, breathing becomes difficult, child appears mottled or pale, fever >5 days without diagnosis, parent is concerned despite advice.

## Paediatric Context and Guardian Involvement

Prescribing in paediatrics is always weight-based — doses must be calculated per kg and capped at adult maximum. The consultation system supports guardian profiles; when a guardian is consulting on behalf of a child, all clinical recommendations should reference the child's weight, age, and developmental stage rather than adult defaults. Guardian engagement is essential for:
- Medication administration education (correct measuring devices, appropriate formulations — liquid not tablet for young children)
- Safety netting — parents are the most reliable observers of behavioural change
- Fever education — many parents have significant fever phobia; reassurance and education that fever is not inherently dangerous reduces unnecessary presentations
- Follow-up planning — ensure guardian has clear return-to-doctor criteria and knows when to call 000

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Paracetamol** (PBS unrestricted — weight-based dosing mandatory; Panadol Children's, Dymadon, infant drops; 15 mg/kg/dose every 4–6 hours)
- **Ibuprofen** (PBS unrestricted — weight-based; Nurofen for Children; 5–10 mg/kg/dose every 6–8 hours; avoid under 3 months, <5 kg, dehydration, renal impairment)
- **Trimethoprim** (PBS restricted: UTI; paediatric liquid formulation; 4 mg/kg twice daily)
- **Amoxicillin** (PBS restricted — various paediatric indications including otitis media, streptococcal pharyngitis; 25 mg/kg/dose twice daily)
- **Cefalexin** (PBS unrestricted — UTI, skin infections; 12.5 mg/kg three times daily)
- **Note:** Aspirin is contraindicated in children under 16 years (Reye's syndrome risk) — never prescribe for fever. Weight-based calculations essential for all paediatric prescribing.

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Non-blanching petechial or purpuric rash — assume meningococcal disease until proven otherwise
- Any child with red flag features: mottling, poor perfusion, altered consciousness, grunting, severe respiratory distress
- Febrile infant <3 months — ED assessment required; high risk SBI even if well-appearing
- Suspected meningitis: bulging fontanelle, neck stiffness, photophobia
- Febrile child with rigors and known immunocompromised state (cancer, post-transplant)
- Any seizure associated with fever in child with previous complex febrile seizures or no febrile seizure history in older child (>5 years)

**Same-day in-person assessment:**
- Febrile child 3–6 months (amber features or any parental concern)
- Fever >5 days without identified source — investigation for occult SBI required
- Amber traffic light features — reduced activity, poor feeding, dehydration
- Child not improving after 48 hours of symptomatic management
- Suspicion of otitis media, UTI, or pneumonia requiring examination and investigation
- Febrile limp or joint swelling — exclude septic arthritis (emergency)

**Telehealth suitable (with limitations):**
- Parental triage and advice for well-appearing child >6 months with fever and clear viral features
- Safety netting education and return criteria guidance
- Reassurance for typical viral illness presentation in child known to GP
- Review of positive UTI culture result and antibiotic prescription
