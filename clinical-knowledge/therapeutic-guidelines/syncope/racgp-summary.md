## Syncope and Presyncope Assessment (RACGP)

Syncope is a transient loss of consciousness due to global cerebral hypoperfusion, characterised by rapid onset, short duration, and spontaneous complete recovery. It accounts for approximately 1% of all GP presentations and 3% of ED visits. Presyncope (near-syncope) describes the prodrome or near-loss of consciousness without a complete blackout. The cause of syncope is benign in most outpatient cases (reflex/vasovagal — the most common), but cardiac syncope carries significant mortality risk (up to 20% at 1 year) and must be excluded. Risk stratification to identify low-risk (vasovagal) from high-risk (cardiac arrhythmia, structural) syncope is the primary clinical goal. Telehealth has significant limitations for syncope assessment — ECG and clinical examination are essential.

## Clinical Presentation

**Classification of syncope:**

Reflex syncope (most common — 50–60% of all syncope):
- Vasovagal syncope: prodrome of diaphoresis, nausea, pallor, tunnel vision, warmth before LOC; prolonged standing, hot environment, emotional stress, pain, medical procedure as triggers; recovery rapid with supine position; most common in young adults and adolescents
- Situational syncope: specific triggers — cough, micturition, defaecation, swallowing; parasympathetic activation; benign
- Carotid sinus syndrome: syncope triggered by carotid sinus pressure (tight collar, shaving); elderly; carotid sinus massage diagnostic (hospital setting)

Orthostatic hypotension syncope:
- BP drop >20 mmHg systolic (or >10 mmHg diastolic) within 3 minutes of standing
- Causes: dehydration, blood loss, medications (antihypertensives, alpha-blockers, diuretics, antidepressants), autonomic neuropathy (diabetes, Parkinson's), Addison's disease
- Common in elderly; often medication-related

Cardiac syncope (high risk):
- Arrhythmic: VT, AF with rapid ventricular rate, complete heart block, sick sinus syndrome, WPW with rapid conduction; may have no prodrome; occurs with exertion or at rest; often recurrent
- Structural: severe aortic stenosis (syncope on exertion), hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HOCM — exertional syncope in young athlete), massive PE, cardiac tamponade

**High-risk features (cardiac syncope more likely):**
- Syncope during exertion or supine (not standing)
- No prodrome; abrupt onset/recovery
- Prior cardiac disease, heart failure, cardiomyopathy, or known arrhythmia
- Syncope preceded by palpitations
- Family history of sudden cardiac death or channelopathy
- Abnormal ECG (QTc >500 ms, Brugada pattern, delta waves, LBBB, prior MI)
- Age >65 or significant cardiovascular comorbidity

**Low-risk features (vasovagal likely):**
- Young age
- Clear precipitating trigger (prolonged standing, emotional stress, pain, heat)
- Classic prodrome (nausea, diaphoresis, pallor)
- Rapid recovery in supine position
- Normal ECG
- No structural heart disease

## Assessment Approach

**History:**
- Detailed description of the event from patient AND witness — eyewitness account invaluable; tonic-clonic movements suggest seizure vs hypoxic myoclonus in syncope
- Prodrome: what happened in the seconds before? Lightheadedness, nausea, diaphoresis = vasovagal; palpitations = arrhythmia; chest pain = ACS; no prodrome = cardiac
- Recovery: rapid and complete = syncope; prolonged confusion = post-ictal (seizure)
- Circumstances: standing, exertion, supine, after meals (postprandial hypotension in elderly)
- Medical history: cardiac disease, diabetes (autonomic neuropathy), Parkinson's, medications
- Medication review: antihypertensives, diuretics, alpha-blockers, antidepressants — all can cause orthostatic hypotension

**Physical examination:**
- Vital signs: BP sitting and standing (postural hypotension assessment — essential)
- HR: bradycardia, irregularity
- Cardiovascular: murmur (aortic stenosis — ejection systolic, radiates to neck; HOCM); carotid bruit; signs of heart failure

**Investigations:**
- 12-lead ECG: essential in ALL cases; QTc measurement, WPW, Brugada pattern, ischaemia, AV block, bundle branch blocks
- Postural blood pressure: lying → standing; automatic high risk if definite orthostatic hypotension
- Glucose: hypoglycaemia presenting as near-syncope
- FBC: anaemia contributing to presyncope
- UEC: electrolytes, dehydration
- Troponin: if cardiac cause suspected (ECG changes, chest pain preceding)
- Holter monitor: if arrhythmic cause suspected and ECG normal
- Echocardiography: structural heart disease assessment if murmur or clinical concern
- Exercise ECG: if exertional syncope
- Tilt table test: for recurrent unexplained syncope to confirm vasovagal mechanism (specialist)
- EEG: only if seizure remains in differential (not routine in syncope)

**Risk stratification tools:**
- CSRS (Canadian Syncope Risk Score): validated in ED; predicts 30-day serious adverse event
- San Francisco Syncope Rule: history of CHF, haematocrit <30%, ECG abnormality, dyspnoea, systolic BP <90 — any = high risk
- Clinical judgement remains essential

## Key Differentials

- Seizure: tonic-clonic movements, post-ictal confusion, tongue biting, incontinence, prolonged recovery; refer neurology if suspected
- Hypoglycaemia: diabetic on insulin/sulfonylurea; check glucose; may be confused at recovery
- TIA: focal neurological deficit; no LOC typically; see `tia-followup` entry; refer to stroke team
- Falls without LOC in elderly — distinguish true syncope from mechanical fall
- Psychogenic non-epileptic attacks (PNEA) — prolonged, history of trauma/psychiatric disorder; neurology/psychology referral
- Drop attacks in elderly — sudden fall without LOC; basilar ischaemia, cervical spondylosis

## Management Principles

**Vasovagal syncope:**
- Reassurance: explain benign nature and mechanism (vasovagal — reflex-mediated)
- Physical countermeasure manoeuvres: leg crossing and muscle tensing, hand-grip manoeuvres at onset of prodrome — proven to abort vasovagal events; teach patient and carer
- Increase fluid and salt intake: 2–3 L/day fluid, added salt if BP allows
- Avoid triggers: prolonged standing, hot environments, dehydration
- Driving restriction: advise patient not to drive until syncope-free for prescribed period (state-specific driving regulations apply; patient must report to licensing authority if recurrent syncope — GP obligation to document advice)
- Pharmacotherapy (rarely needed for vasovagal): fludrocortisone 0.1 mg daily (expands plasma volume), midodrine (alpha-1 agonist, increases SVR) — specialist-supervised; rarely indicated

**Orthostatic hypotension:**
- Review and reduce/stop offending medications (antihypertensives, diuretics, alpha-blockers, antidepressants)
- Non-pharmacological: head-of-bed elevation, compression stockings, slow positional change, avoid prolonged standing
- Increase fluid and salt intake
- Fludrocortisone or midodrine: if refractory, specialist referral

**Cardiac syncope:**
- Requires urgent cardiology referral or ED assessment
- Arrhythmic: antiarrhythmic medication, pacemaker (bradycardia), ICD (VT in structural heart disease), ablation (SVT, WPW)
- Structural: aortic stenosis — cardiology for valve replacement consideration; HOCM — specialist management

**Driving and work restrictions:**
- All patients with unexplained or cardiac syncope must be advised not to drive until cleared by specialist
- Commercial drivers (truck, bus): stricter restrictions; AUSTROADS guidelines apply
- Occupational hazards: working at heights or with heavy machinery — restriction until cause identified and managed

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Fludrocortisone** (Florinef — PBS restricted: orthostatic hypotension; 0.05–0.1 mg daily; monitor BP, electrolytes, oedema)
- **Midodrine** (PBS restricted: orthostatic hypotension; 2.5–10 mg three times daily; avoid supine hypertension — do not take within 4 hours of bedtime)
- **Note:** Most vasovagal syncope is managed non-pharmacologically; pharmacotherapy has modest evidence; referral for recurrent unexplained syncope to cardiologist or electrophysiologist is preferred over empiric drug treatment

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Syncope + chest pain, dyspnoea, or palpitations preceding event — cardiac emergency
- Syncope in patient with known structural heart disease or cardiomyopathy
- Syncope with prolonged LOC or abnormal recovery — seizure or cardiac
- ECG showing VT, complete heart block, ST elevation, QTc >500 ms, Brugada pattern — emergency cardiac
- Syncope with suspected aortic dissection, massive PE, or tamponade

**Urgent same-day or next-day referral:**
- Any syncope with high-risk features (no prodrome, exertional, supine, palpitations)
- Recurrent unexplained syncope
- First syncope in patient with known cardiac disease — cardiologist

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Cardiologist/electrophysiologist: recurrent unexplained syncope with normal initial workup, suspected arrhythmic cause, structural heart disease
- Neurologist: suspected seizure disorder
- AUSTROADS driving fitness assessment documentation

## Telehealth Suitability

Syncope assessment has significant telehealth limitations.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- History taking from patient and witness after in-person assessment has excluded high-risk features
- Follow-up review of results (Holter, echo) for low-risk confirmed vasovagal syncope
- Lifestyle counselling for vasovagal syncope (physical manoeuvres, fluid/salt)
- Medication adjustment for orthostatic hypotension where diagnosis established

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- ALL new syncope presentations — postural BP, ECG, and clinical examination essential
- Any high-risk features identified in history — do not defer in-person assessment
