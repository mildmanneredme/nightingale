## School Refusal Assessment (RACGP)

School refusal (also called school avoidance or emotionally based school non-attendance) is defined as difficulty attending school resulting in prolonged absences, driven by emotional distress rather than antisocial or conduct problems. It affects approximately 1–2% of school-age children and adolescents. It is distinguished from truancy (deliberate avoidance without parental knowledge or emotional distress) and school withdrawal (parental-driven absence). School refusal is not a diagnosis but a presentation — it typically reflects underlying anxiety disorders (social anxiety, generalised anxiety, separation anxiety), depression, ASD, ADHD, learning difficulties, bullying, or school-specific stressors. It is associated with significant long-term consequences: academic failure, vocational difficulties, mental health disorders, and social isolation. Early intervention is critical: the longer the absence continues, the harder it is to return. GP Mental Health Treatment Plan (MHTP) and multidisciplinary approach (school, family, allied health) are cornerstone. Telehealth is suitable for management consultations.

## Clinical Presentation

**Core features:**
- Morning distress: refusal to get out of bed, leave the house, or enter the school building; physical symptoms (nausea, headache, abdominal pain, trembling, chest tightness) that resolve on weekends, holidays, or when staying home
- Parental compliance: absence is known to and often facilitated by parents (distinguishes from truancy)
- Emotional rather than behavioural: child is not defiant or antisocial; distress is genuine
- Duration: typically worsening; may start as occasional absence, escalate to complete non-attendance

**Associated conditions:**
- Anxiety disorders (most common): social anxiety (fear of peers, performance), separation anxiety (fear of harm to parent), generalised anxiety, panic disorder
- Depression: particularly in older adolescents; hopelessness, loss of interest; poor sleep
- ASD: sensory overwhelm at school; social demands; changes in routine
- ADHD: academic frustration; social difficulties; risk of victimisation
- Learning difficulties / specific learning disorder: embarrassment about academic struggles; avoidance of performance situations
- Bullying: peer victimisation; cyberbullying; most common precipitant identified by students
- Physical health: chronic pain, fatigue (ME/CFS), migraine — real physical symptoms that overlap with anxiety

**Somatic symptoms (common presenting complaint):**
- Headache, abdominal pain, nausea, dizziness, chest pain, fatigue — all physical symptoms without organic cause
- Resolve on weekends and holidays; worsen Monday morning
- Extensive medical investigation usually unhelpful and reinforces sick role
- GP role: rule out serious organic disease, then redirect to psychological framework

**Parental and family dynamics:**
- Parental anxiety: highly anxious parents may inadvertently reinforce school avoidance
- Family avoidance patterns: normalising avoidance of feared situations within the family
- Single parent / social stressors: complex family circumstances
- Enmeshed parent-child relationship: separation anxiety in parent and child

## Assessment Approach

**History:**
- School attendance pattern: onset; duration; frequency; what triggers absence; last day of full attendance
- Morning routine: what happens; child's emotional state; physical symptoms reported; how family responds
- School environment: specific fearful situation (classroom, sport, canteen, specific teacher, peer conflict, performance situations)
- Bullying: assess directly with child and separately with parent
- Mental health: anxiety symptoms (worry, panic attacks); depression (PHQ-A for adolescents); previous mental health diagnosis
- Learning: academic performance; any learning assessment done; parent-teacher communication

**Validated screening tools:**
- Screen for Child Anxiety Related Disorders (SCARED): parent and child version; anxiety screening
- PHQ-A (Patient Health Questionnaire for Adolescents): depression screening
- School Refusal Assessment Scale-Revised (SRAS-R): identifies function of school refusal (avoid aversive situations, escape evaluative situations, gain attention, pursue tangible rewards outside school)
- Paediatric Anxiety Rating Scale (PARS): clinician-administered

**School liaison:**
- Contact school welfare officer, school counsellor, or year coordinator with patient consent
- Obtain teacher observations: academic performance; peer relationships; observed behaviour at school
- Identify specific stressors: class, teacher, peer conflict, school layout, transitions
- School is a critical partner in the RTW plan

**Medical examination:**
- Brief physical to exclude organic cause of somatic complaints; avoid extensive investigation if symptoms are anxiety-mediated
- BP (standing: orthostatic hypotension — POTS sometimes presents in adolescents with fatigue/dizziness)

## Key Differentials

- Truancy: deliberate absence without parental awareness or distress; antisocial; conduct disorder; different management
- School withdrawal: parent keeps child home; parent-driven; parenting support needed
- Medical illness with school impact: ME/CFS, migraine, IBD — real physical conditions; distinguish from pure anxiety
- Bullying: both cause and complication of school refusal; assess directly
- ASD: sensory, social, and rigidity challenges at school; separate pathway
- Learning disorder: academic performance concerns; psychoeducational assessment
- Family crisis: bereavement, parental illness, domestic violence — primary stressor driving absence

## Management Principles

**Core principle: return to school as early as possible (evidence-based):**
- Every day out of school makes return harder; avoid prolonged validation of avoidance
- GP's role: NOT to provide ongoing medical certificates for school refusal (perpetuates avoidance); address underlying cause + coordinate return plan

**Psychoeducation (patient and family):**
- Explain anxiety cycle: avoidance → short-term relief → reinforces anxiety → increases avoidance
- Physical symptoms are REAL: anxiety genuinely causes nausea, headache, abdominal pain; NOT "faking"
- Message to child: "Your body is telling you it's scared, not that you're sick. The brave thing is to go anyway."
- Message to parents: returning to school, even with some distress, is the treatment

**GP Mental Health Treatment Plan (MHTP):**
- Initiate MHTP: enables 10 (+ up to 10 additional) Medicare-rebated psychology sessions
- Refer to clinical psychologist or mental health social worker specialising in childhood anxiety/school refusal
- CBT for anxiety: graduated exposure hierarchy to school situations; FRIENDS programme for children

**Graduated return to school:**
- Avoid "cold turkey" re-entry after prolonged absence (increases trauma and failure)
- Gradual exposure plan: developed with school, parents, and psychologist
  - Stage 1: drive past school; sit in car park
  - Stage 2: brief supervised visit to welfare officer (no class)
  - Stage 3: partial attendance (1 hour; favourite class or trusted teacher)
  - Stage 4: half days; escalate to full days
- School welfare officer and classroom teacher involvement: essential; prearranged safe space
- Modify school environment temporarily if needed: reduced transition times, small group, eating in office

**Pharmacotherapy (adjunct — not first-line):**
- SSRIs (sertraline 25–50 mg or fluoxetine 10–20 mg): for moderate-severe anxiety disorder; paediatric initiation (under GP with paediatric/psychiatric guidance); onset 4–6 weeks
- Benefit: reduced anxiety intensity allows engagement with CBT exposure; not "treating school refusal" per se
- NOTE: FDA/TGA black box warning for SSRIs in under 18s (increased suicidal ideation); close monitoring at 1–2 weeks after initiation; parent education; risk-benefit discussion documented
- Avoid: benzodiazepines (reinforces avoidance; cognitive impairment during school; dependence)

**Headspace:**
- headspace.org.au: free mental health services for young people 12–25; GP referral or self-referral; bulk-billing; psychologists, social workers, vocational support
- Schools: headspace school support programme in some states

**Complex or chronic cases:**
- Inpatient day program: for severely entrenched school refusal; paediatric hospital-based intensive CBT exposure programme
- Child psychiatry: severe anxiety; depression; comorbid conditions; medication complex
- OSHC (Out of School Hours Care) or flexible learning: for adolescents with prolonged absence; re-engagement programmes; TAFE or distance education as transitional pathway

## Paediatric Context and Guardian Involvement

- Parent coaching: psychologist coaches parents in extinction of avoidance reinforcement; parents often need their own CBT input
- School transition: Year 7 entry (primary to secondary school) is highest risk period for school refusal onset; also after holidays, illness absences, or significant life events
- Siblings: school refusal affects siblings' school experience; family systems approach
- Child's voice: explore child's perspective separately from parent; create safe space; identify specific fear; genuine collaboration with child improves engagement
- Cultural considerations: some cultural groups have higher rates of keeping children home for non-standard reasons; respectful exploration; avoid judgemental assumptions

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Sertraline 25–50 mg (Zoloft)** (PBS restricted: anxiety and depression; initiation in young people under 18 should ideally involve consultation with paediatric psychiatry or paediatrician; start low; weekly contact in first month; black box warning documentation)
- **Fluoxetine 10–20 mg (Prozac)** (PBS restricted: depression in children ≥8 years — only FDA-approved SSRI for children; long half-life reduces discontinuation syndrome; useful if adherence concern)
- **Melatonin** (for comorbid sleep difficulties in anxious children with school refusal; see `insomnia`)
- **Note:** Do NOT issue ongoing medical certificates for school refusal without addressing the underlying mental health cause. A medical certificate that legitimises absence as "medically unwell" perpetuates the avoidance cycle and worsens long-term outcomes.

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent:**
- Active suicidal ideation: common in adolescents with entrenched school refusal + depression; same-day assessment; Mental Health Act if imminent risk; 000

**Non-urgent referral (prompt — within 1–2 weeks):**
- Clinical psychologist (MHTP): all cases; CBT for anxiety or depression; graduated exposure
- headspace (12–25 years): free; rapid access; no wait for initial assessment
- School welfare officer / school counsellor: coordinate return plan; contact within the week of presentation
- Paediatric psychiatry: severe, entrenched cases; comorbid depression; medication guidance; inpatient day programme consideration

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Mental health assessment and MHTP development
- SSRI prescribing and monitoring
- Parent coaching and psychoeducation
- Coordination with school via telehealth (3-way call with consent)
- Follow-up for progress towards return to school

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial comprehensive assessment including observation of child-parent interaction
- Physical examination if somatic complaints
- High suicide risk (prefer in-person safety assessment)
