## Cardiovascular Risk Assessment (RACGP)

Cardiovascular disease (CVD) — comprising coronary artery disease, stroke, peripheral arterial disease, and heart failure — is the leading cause of death in Australia. Identifying and modifying CVD risk in primary prevention is one of the most impactful activities in general practice. Australia's approach uses absolute CVD risk assessment, which calculates the probability of a cardiovascular event (heart attack or stroke) over 5 years based on multiple risk factors combined — not individual risk factors treated in isolation. The Heart Foundation's 2023 guideline endorses the Australian absolute CVD Risk Calculator (Australian absolute CVD risk score). This replaces older cholesterol-only or Framingham-based approaches. Telehealth is well-suited to CVD risk assessment, lifestyle counselling, and pharmacotherapy management.

## Clinical Presentation

CVD risk assessment is largely pre-symptomatic — the goal is identifying and treating high-risk patients before a cardiovascular event. Common triggers for risk assessment:
- Routine health check (GP health assessment — MBS items 701, 703, 705, 715)
- Finding isolated risk factor (hypertension, hyperlipidaemia, diabetes)
- Age-based opportunistic screening (men ≥45 years, women ≥55 years, or ≥35 for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples)
- Family history of premature CVD (first-degree relative with CVD <55 years male, <65 years female)

**Major modifiable risk factors:**
- Hypertension (systolic BP ≥130 mmHg)
- Dyslipidaemia (elevated LDL-C, reduced HDL-C, elevated TG)
- Smoking (current and recent)
- Type 2 diabetes
- Obesity (BMI ≥30, waist circumference elevated)
- Physical inactivity
- Unhealthy diet (high saturated fat, low fibre)
- Excessive alcohol intake

**Non-modifiable risk factors:**
- Age, male sex, ethnicity (Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander populations — significantly higher CVD risk; risk calculator may underestimate)
- Family history of premature CVD
- Personal history of premature CVD

## Assessment Approach

**Australian Absolute CVD Risk Calculator (freely available at cvdcheck.org.au):**
Inputs: age, sex, total cholesterol, HDL-cholesterol, systolic BP, smoking status, diabetes status; outputs: 5-year risk of CVD event.

**Risk categories:**
- Low (<10%): lifestyle advice; no immediate pharmacotherapy unless lipid/BP targets not met with lifestyle alone after 3–6 months
- Intermediate (10–15%): lifestyle ± pharmacotherapy depending on individual factors; shared decision-making
- High (≥15%): pharmacotherapy recommended (statin + antihypertensive if indicated) + lifestyle

**Automatic high risk (no calculator required — treat as high risk):**
- Established CVD (previous MI, stroke, TIA, CABG/PCI, PAD)
- Familial hypercholesterolaemia (FH)
- LDL-C ≥4.9 mmol/L
- Non-diabetic CKD with eGFR <45 or albuminuria
- Diabetes: age ≥60, or ≥10 years diabetes duration, or microalbuminuria/retinopathy/neuropathy
- Systolic BP ≥180 mmHg or DBP ≥110 mmHg
- Total cholesterol >7.5 mmol/L

**Annual review includes:**
- Update risk calculation as risk factors change
- Assess lifestyle behaviour change
- Adjust pharmacotherapy if targets not met
- Screen for new comorbidities (glucose, lipids, BP, kidney function)

**Investigations:**
- Fasting lipid panel: TC, LDL-C, HDL-C, TG (required for risk calculator)
- Fasting glucose / HbA1c: diabetes screening
- UEC, creatinine, eGFR: CKD assessment
- ECG: baseline in older patients or with cardiac symptoms
- BP measurement: seated, both arms (note higher arm), two readings averaged

**Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander populations:** Higher CVD risk at younger ages; risk calculator underestimates risk; lower thresholds for treatment; begin screening at age 35 years (or younger if risk factors present); GP health assessment item 715.

## Key Differentials

- Secondary hypertension — renal artery stenosis, phaeochromocytoma, primary aldosteronism; suspect if young, refractory, or associated features
- Familial hypercholesterolaemia — markedly elevated LDL-C, family history; specialist referral
- Metabolic syndrome — cluster of CVD risk factors (central obesity + 2 of: elevated BP, raised TG, reduced HDL-C, impaired fasting glucose); high CVD and T2D risk
- Haemochromatosis — iron overload causes cardiomyopathy and diabetes; check iron studies in unexplained elevated ferritin

## Management Principles

**Lifestyle (applies to all risk categories):**
- Mediterranean diet: most evidence for CVD prevention; olive oil, fish, nuts, vegetables, legumes, whole grains; reduces CVD events 25–30% vs low-fat diet
- Physical activity: 150–300 min/week moderate or 75–150 min vigorous activity + 2 strength training sessions/week
- Smoking cessation: single most impactful intervention in a smoker; use combination NRT + bupropion or varenicline
- Alcohol: maximum 10 standard drinks/week, ≤4 per occasion (2020 NHMRC guidelines)
- Weight management: 5–10% weight loss reduces BP, lipids, glucose, inflammation

**Pharmacotherapy:**

Antihypertensive therapy — indicated when:
- Systolic BP persistently ≥140 mmHg (or ≥130 in high CVD risk)
- See `hypertension` entry for agent selection and targets

Lipid-lowering therapy — indicated when:
- High absolute CVD risk (≥15%): statin regardless of lipid levels; rosuvastatin or atorvastatin
- Intermediate risk with LDL-C >2.5 mmol/L after 3 months lifestyle
- See `hyperlipidaemia` entry for drug selection and targets

Antiplatelet therapy:
- Aspirin 100 mg daily: NOT recommended for primary prevention in low-moderate risk (bleeding risk outweighs benefit per 2023 guidelines — this reverses decades of prior practice)
- Aspirin in secondary prevention (established CVD): well-established benefit; continue indefinitely unless contraindicated
- Clopidogrel: alternative in aspirin intolerance in secondary prevention

Diabetes management:
- SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin) and GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, dulaglutide) have demonstrated cardiovascular benefit in T2D — incorporate into glucose management for high CVD risk patients with T2D (see `type-2-diabetes` entry)

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Statins** (rosuvastatin, atorvastatin — PBS restricted: absolute CVD risk ≥15% or automatic high risk; see `hyperlipidaemia` entry)
- **Antihypertensives** (ACE inhibitors, ARBs, calcium channel blockers, thiazides — see `hypertension` entry)
- **Aspirin 100 mg** (Cardiprin, generic — PBS unrestricted for secondary prevention; NOT recommended for primary prevention; over-the-counter)
- **SGLT2 inhibitors** (PBS restricted: T2D with CVD or high CVD risk — see `type-2-diabetes` entry)
- **Smoking cessation agents** (varenicline — PBS restricted; NRT — PBS unrestricted; see URTI/smoking cessation entries)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Acute coronary syndrome: chest pain, jaw pain, arm pain, diaphoresis, acute dyspnoea — call 000; aspirin 300 mg if not allergic
- Acute stroke: sudden facial droop, arm weakness, speech difficulty, visual disturbance, severe headache — call 000 (FAST mnemonic)
- Hypertensive emergency: systolic BP >180 + end-organ damage (confusion, chest pain, dyspnoea, visual change) — emergency
- Acute severe aortic dissection: sudden tearing chest/back pain

**Urgent same-day assessment:**
- Very high BP (systolic >180 mmHg) without symptoms — urgent antihypertensive review; consider same-day in-person assessment
- Suspected acute limb ischaemia — 5 Ps (pain, pallor, pulselessness, paraesthesia, paralysis) — vascular surgery urgently

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Cardiologist: established CVD for specialist secondary prevention management, complex arrhythmia, heart failure
- Endocrinologist: complex diabetes + CVD, suspected secondary hypertension
- Dietitian: structured dietary review for CVD risk reduction

## Telehealth Suitability

Cardiovascular risk assessment and management is highly suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Absolute CVD risk calculation (patient provides BP readings from home or pharmacy, bloods via pathology)
- Risk category discussion and shared decision-making
- Lifestyle counselling and behaviour change planning
- Statin and antihypertensive prescribing
- Annual cardiovascular risk review for patients with established risk
- Smoking cessation counselling and pharmacotherapy prescribing

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Blood pressure measurement (can be done at pharmacy or with validated home BP monitor)
- Physical examination for signs of established CVD
- ECG (for suspected arrhythmia, baseline in high-risk patients)
- Blood tests — pathology for lipid panel, glucose, kidney function
