## Eating Disorders Assessment (RACGP)

Eating disorders are serious, complex, and potentially life-threatening mental health and medical conditions. They affect approximately 1 million Australians, with the highest mortality rate of any mental health condition (largely from cardiac arrhythmias in anorexia nervosa and from suicide). Eating disorders are underdiagnosed and often present to GPs with medical consequences rather than the underlying disorder. GPs play a critical role in: early identification using screening tools, managing medical complications, coordinating multidisciplinary care, and early referral to specialist eating disorder services. The main eating disorders in GP practice are: anorexia nervosa (AN), bulimia nervosa (BN), and binge eating disorder (BED). The COVID-19 pandemic significantly increased eating disorder prevalence, particularly in adolescents. Telehealth is suitable for ongoing monitoring; acute medical instability requires in-person assessment.

## Clinical Presentation

**Anorexia nervosa (AN):**
- Restriction of food intake leading to significantly low body weight (BMI <18.5 kg/m²; in growing adolescents — below expected weight trajectory)
- Intense fear of gaining weight or becoming fat
- Distorted body image; denial of seriousness of low weight
- Subtypes: restricting type; binge-eating/purging type
- Physical signs: emaciation, lanugo hair, bradycardia, hypotension, hypothermia, parotid gland enlargement, calluses on dorsum of hand (Russell's sign from self-induced vomiting), dental erosion, amenorrhoea

**Bulimia nervosa (BN):**
- Recurrent episodes of binge eating (loss of control) followed by compensatory behaviours
- Compensatory behaviours: self-induced vomiting (most common), laxative/diuretic misuse, excessive exercise, fasting
- Normal or overweight BMI — BN is NOT characterised by low weight (distinguishes from AN purging type)
- Physical signs: parotid gland enlargement (bilateral), dental erosion (stomach acid), calluses on fingers (Russell's sign), hypokalaemia, electrolyte disturbances, oesophageal tears

**Binge eating disorder (BED):**
- Recurrent binge eating WITHOUT compensatory behaviours
- Associated with obesity; significant psychological distress
- Most common eating disorder; least likely to be presented to GP as eating disorder — often presents with obesity

**ARFID (Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder):**
- Restriction not driven by body image concerns; driven by sensory sensitivity, fear of choking, lack of interest in food
- Common in autism spectrum disorder; children particularly
- Weight loss or nutritional deficiency; treatment differs from AN

**Disordered eating presentations to GP:**
- Amenorrhoea (in restricting AN)
- Electrolyte abnormalities (BN purging)
- Dental erosion noted on dental review
- Request for diet advice with extreme concern about weight
- Fatigue, fainting, palpitations
- Gastrointestinal complaints — constipation, reflux
- Parental concern about child or adolescent's eating
- Athlete presenting with relative energy deficiency in sport (RED-S) — female athlete triad: low energy availability + menstrual dysfunction + low bone density

## Assessment Approach

**Screening tools:**
- SCOFF questionnaire (5 yes/no questions): validated screening tool
  1. Do you make yourself Sick because you feel uncomfortably full?
  2. Do you worry you have lost Control over how much you eat?
  3. Have you recently lost more than One stone (6 kg) in a 3-month period?
  4. Do you believe yourself to be Fat when others say you are thin?
  5. Would you say that Food dominates your life?
  Score ≥2 "yes" responses = possible eating disorder; further assessment warranted

- EDE-Q or EAT-26: longer validated questionnaires; more comprehensive

**GP assessment:**
- Weight and height: BMI calculation; weight trend over time; growth chart review for adolescents
- Dietary history: 24-hour recall; restriction patterns; binge-purge behaviours (ask sensitively)
- Menstrual history: amenorrhoea or oligomenorrhoea
- Physical activity: excessive exercise
- Body image concerns: ask directly; "How do you feel about your body?"
- Psychological: PHQ-9 (depression), anxiety assessment; trauma history
- Risk: suicidal ideation — very high in eating disorders; ask directly

**Physical examination:**
- Vital signs: HR (bradycardia in AN — <50 bpm is significant), BP (orthostatic hypotension), temperature
- BMI: weight/height²; use with clinical context (muscle mass confounders)
- Signs of purging: parotid enlargement, Russell's sign, dental erosion
- Cardiovascular: QTc on ECG (risk of fatal arrhythmia with electrolyte disturbance)
- Lanugo hair, peripheral oedema (refeeding risk)

**Medical investigations:**
- FBC: anaemia, leukopenia
- Electrolytes (Na, K, Mg, Phos): hypokalaemia (purging); hypophosphataemia (refeeding risk)
- LFTs: elevated in starvation
- BSL: hypoglycaemia in severe AN
- TSH: hypothyroidism (common comorbidity and differential)
- eGFR: renal function
- ECG: QTc prolongation (risk factor for fatal arrhythmia — especially with hypokalaemia)
- DEXA: bone density in AN — osteoporosis from malnutrition and amenorrhoea
- Phosphate: critical before refeeding — refeeding syndrome risk

**Risk stratification (hospitalisation criteria — any one indicating):**
- BMI <14 kg/m² (adult) or BMI <75% of expected median for age (adolescent)
- Rapid weight loss >1 kg/week
- HR <40 bpm or >120 bpm
- Systolic BP <80 mmHg or postural drop >20 mmHg
- Hypoglycaemia (BSL <3 mmol/L)
- QTc >450 ms or significant arrhythmia
- Syncope
- Hypokalemia, hyponatremia, hypophosphataemia: any significant electrolyte abnormality
- Muscle weakness unable to rise from chair without using arms
- Refusal to eat or drink and no outpatient support

## Key Differentials

- Depression: weight loss, poor appetite, low energy; PHQ-9; but absence of body image disturbance and no restriction to achieve thinness
- Coeliac disease: malnutrition; weight loss; GI symptoms; anti-tTG antibody; can coexist
- Hyperthyroidism: weight loss; anxiety; tachycardia; TSH
- Chronic illness causing weight loss: malignancy, IBD, chronic infection
- Type 1 diabetes: may use insulin restriction for weight control ("diabulimia") — serious medical emergency; HbA1c, glucose monitoring
- Gastroparesis: vomiting, food avoidance from nausea; no body image issues; gastric emptying study
- ARFID: restriction without body image disturbance; children, ASD; separate treatment

## Management Principles

**Multidisciplinary team (essential for all but very mild presentations):**
- GP: medical monitoring, coordination, prescribing, safety-netting
- Psychologist or psychiatrist: CBT-E (Cognitive Behavioural Therapy — Enhanced), FBT (Family-Based Treatment for adolescents)
- Dietitian: nutritional rehabilitation; meal planning; supported eating; refeeding monitoring
- Psychiatrist: moderate-severe; medication; inpatient decisions

**Medical monitoring (GP core role):**
- Regular weighing: weekly in active treatment; consistent conditions (same scale, same time, same clothing); avoid obsessive weighing at home
- Vital signs: HR, BP, orthostatic measurements
- Electrolytes: regularly in purging behaviours or during refeeding
- ECG: if bradycardia, electrolyte disturbance, or QTc concern

**Psychological treatment:**
- Anorexia nervosa: Family-Based Treatment (FBT/Maudsley method) — gold standard for adolescents; CBT-E for adults; clinical psychology or specialist eating disorder service
- Bulimia nervosa: CBT-E most effective; self-help CBT with therapist support also effective (BITE-BEAT app)
- BED: CBT-E; dialectical behaviour therapy (DBT) for emotion regulation
- NEDC treatment pathway: nedc.com.au

**Pharmacotherapy:**
- Anorexia nervosa: NO medication proven effective for core AN symptoms; olanzapine may assist with anxiety at mealtimes (specialist); not primary treatment
- Bulimia nervosa: fluoxetine 60 mg daily (higher than depression dose) — PBS-restricted; reduces binge-purge frequency; adjunct to CBT; tricyclics (older evidence, side effects)
- BED: lisdexamfetamine (Vyvanse) 50–70 mg daily: PBS-listed for moderate-severe BED in adults (Australia approved 2020); first medication specifically approved for BED; stimulant; contraindications (cardiovascular, substance misuse history)

**Refeeding syndrome (AN — medical emergency risk):**
- Risk when severely malnourished and nutritional support started too quickly
- Hypophosphataemia (key risk): causes cardiac and respiratory failure, arrhythmia
- Prevent: slow refeeding; thiamine before refeeding; phosphate monitoring; electrolyte correction
- Inpatient refeeding: required for severe AN; specialist eating disorder unit preferred; medical ward if unavailable

**Adolescent-specific considerations:**
- FBT (Maudsley): parents take charge of meals initially; most evidence for adolescent AN
- Growth monitoring: weight and height on growth charts; expected weight for age/height
- School and social functioning: coordinate with school about eating during school day
- Parents/guardians: central role; need education, support, and involvement in treatment team
- MediaEDCare: resources for parents

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Fluoxetine 60 mg** (PBS restricted: bulimia nervosa — specifically listed indication at 60 mg daily; higher dose than depression; reduces binge-purge frequency; adjunct to CBT; not effective for AN)
- **Lisdexamfetamine (Vyvanse) 50/70 mg** (PBS restricted: moderate-severe binge eating disorder in adults — specifically listed; stimulant; prescribing note: do not use in those with prior substance use disorder or significant cardiovascular disease; specialist psychiatrist initiation recommended)
- **Olanzapine**: off-label for AN anxiety at mealtimes; weight gain side effect may be used therapeutically; specialist only
- **Note:** Avoid diuretics in BN/purging — worsen electrolyte abnormalities; avoid laxatives; avoid weight loss medications in eating disorders — reinforce disordered behaviours
- **Potassium supplementation**: if hypokalaemia from purging; oral (Span-K) or IV if severe (<3.0 mmol/L); treat electrolyte abnormalities promptly

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Cardiac arrhythmia (ventricular): QTc >500 ms; sustained arrhythmia — potentially fatal
- Syncope or collapse
- Haematemesis from Mallory-Weiss tear (purging)
- Severe hypoglycaemia
- Suicidal crisis with plan

**Urgent inpatient eating disorder unit or medical hospital:**
- Any hospitalisation criteria (see assessment section): BMI <14, HR <40, BP <80, significant electrolyte disturbance, syncope, refusal to eat
- Refeeding syndrome risk: severe malnutrition; inpatient refeeding required

**Urgent eating disorder specialist:**
- Moderate-severe AN or BN unresponsive to outpatient management
- Rapid deterioration
- High psychiatric risk (suicidality, psychosis)

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Eating disorder specialist (psychologist/psychiatrist): NEDC provider finder: nedc.com.au
- Dietitian: nutritional rehabilitation; CDM plan
- Dental: dental erosion monitoring and treatment
- Endocrinology: amenorrhoea, bone density management in AN

## Telehealth Suitability

Eating disorders require a predominantly in-person approach for medical monitoring; some components suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- SCOFF screening and initial psychoeducation
- PHQ-9 and suicide risk monitoring
- Medication review (fluoxetine, lisdexamfetamine)
- Referral coordination (psychologist, dietitian, psychiatrist)
- Parent/family psychoeducation session
- Stable BED or BN monitoring

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Weight and vital sign monitoring (weight especially — cannot verify remotely)
- ECG
- Blood tests — electrolytes, glucose, FBC (pathology arranged)
- Physical examination for medical complications
- All severely unwell patients — in-person or hospital
