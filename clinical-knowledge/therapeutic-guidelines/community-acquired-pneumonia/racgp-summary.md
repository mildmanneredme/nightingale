## Community-Acquired Pneumonia Assessment (RACGP)

Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP) is an acute infection of the lower respiratory tract acquired outside a healthcare setting. It is a leading cause of death from infectious disease in Australia, responsible for approximately 80,000 hospitalisations annually. Most cases can be managed in the community with oral antibiotics; however, risk stratification to identify patients requiring hospitalisation is essential. Streptococcus pneumoniae remains the most common bacterial cause; atypical organisms (Mycoplasma pneumoniae, Legionella, Chlamydophila pneumoniae) are responsible for a significant proportion. Viral pneumonia (influenza, COVID-19, RSV) is increasingly recognised. Antibiotic stewardship — using targeted narrow-spectrum antibiotics when appropriate — is an important component of CAP management. Telehealth has moderate suitability; SpO2 measurement and auscultation require in-person assessment.

## Clinical Presentation

**Classic CAP:**
- Acute onset fever, cough (productive — purulent or rusty sputum in pneumococcal), pleuritic chest pain, dyspnoea
- Tachycardia, tachypnoea
- On examination: dullness to percussion, reduced breath sounds, bronchial breathing, increased vocal resonance over consolidation
- SpO2 may be reduced; RR >20 is an important early warning sign

**Atypical CAP (Mycoplasma, Chlamydophila, Legionella):**
- More gradual onset; dry cough; prominent extrapulmonary features — headache, malaise, myalgia; less systemic toxicity initially
- Mycoplasma pneumoniae: most common cause in young adults; community outbreaks; resistant to beta-lactams (requires macrolide or doxycycline)
- Legionella pneumophila: severe CAP; travel, water exposure, air conditioning; hyponatraemia; diarrhoea; responds to fluoroquinolone or azithromycin

**Viral pneumonia:**
- Influenza: fever, myalgia, cough, headache; see `influenza` entry; can be complicated by bacterial superinfection (S. aureus, Streptococcus)
- COVID-19: see `covid-19` entry; may cause severe pneumonia in high-risk groups
- RSV: increasingly recognised in older adults and immunocompromised

**Aspiration pneumonia:** Patients with dysphagia, reduced consciousness, oesophageal disease; right lower lobe or right middle lobe involvement; mixed anaerobic organisms; amoxicillin-clavulanate coverage.

## Assessment Approach

**Severity assessment — CURB-65 score (1 point each):**
- Confusion (new)
- Urea >7 mmol/L
- Respiratory rate ≥30/min
- Blood pressure: systolic <90 or diastolic ≤60 mmHg
- Age ≥65 years

CURB-65 interpretation:
- 0–1: low severity — community management appropriate; mortality ~0.6–2.7%
- 2: moderate severity — consider hospital referral; mortality ~6.8%
- ≥3: severe — hospital admission required; mortality ~14–27%

**PSI (Pneumonia Severity Index):** More comprehensive; used in ED; GP may use CURB-65 for practical decision-making.

**Investigations in general practice:**
- SpO2 (pulse oximetry — essential): SpO2 <93% = significant hypoxia; hospital assessment
- CXR: confirms diagnosis; identifies extent, complications (effusion, abscess, cavitation); CXR not always available in GP — clinical diagnosis acceptable if typical presentation
- FBC: elevated WBC suggests bacterial; lymphopenia in viral
- CRP: elevated; useful to guide antibiotic prescribing (normal CRP makes bacterial pneumonia less likely)
- Blood cultures: low yield in outpatient CAP; reserve for hospitalised patients
- Urine Legionella antigen: if Legionella suspected (severe, travel, water exposure)
- Sputum MC&S: low yield; reserved for hospitalised severe cases or failure to respond
- COVID-19 PCR / RAT: if COVID-19 suspected; guides management

## Key Differentials

- Acute bronchitis: cough without systemic features; normal exam; no consolidation; does not require antibiotics in most cases (see `acute-bronchitis` entry)
- Pulmonary embolism: pleuritic pain, dyspnoea, haemoptysis; Wells score; D-dimer; CT-PA
- Heart failure: bilateral lower zone crackles; leg oedema; raised JVP; CXR shows cardiomegaly and pulmonary oedema
- Lung cancer: persistent cough, haemoptysis, weight loss, smoking history; CXR or CT
- TB: prolonged cough, night sweats, weight loss, haemoptysis; high-risk exposure or endemic area; sputum for AFB; notification required

## Management Principles

**Low-severity CAP (CURB-65 0–1, SpO2 >93%, able to take oral medications, no social concerns):**

First-line outpatient oral antibiotic for typical CAP:
- Amoxicillin 1 g three times daily for 5 days — first-line for typical bacterial CAP; narrow-spectrum; excellent S. pneumoniae coverage; well tolerated; PBS-listed
- Doxycycline 100 mg twice daily for 5 days — alternative if penicillin allergy or if atypical organism suspected; covers S. pneumoniae and Mycoplasma; PBS-listed

Atypical CAP (or unable to exclude atypical organisms):
- Doxycycline 100 mg twice daily for 5 days — covers both typical and atypical; appropriate for most outpatient CAP in uncertain aetiology
- Azithromycin 500 mg once daily for 3 days — alternative for atypical; shorter course; macrolide resistance concern for S. pneumoniae in some regions

If aspiration suspected: amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg twice daily for 5 days.

**Supportive care:**
- Adequate hydration
- Analgesics for pleuritic pain (paracetamol, NSAID)
- Antipyretics
- Rest; avoid smoking
- Review at 48–72 hours to assess response — failure to improve warrants reassessment and possible hospital referral

**Clinical review at 48–72 hours:**
- If not improving — reassess diagnosis; consider hospital referral; consider alternative antibiotic (treatment failure or resistant organism); repeat CXR if available
- Resolution CXR: at 6 weeks in smokers or high-risk patients to ensure resolution and exclude underlying malignancy

**Pneumococcal vaccination:** All adults ≥65, and younger adults with chronic conditions (diabetes, heart disease, lung disease, immunosuppression) — discuss and offer 23-valent polysaccharide vaccine (23vPPV — Pneumovax) and 13-valent conjugate vaccine (13vPCV — Prevenar) where appropriate; also NIP-funded for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander adults ≥50 and younger with risk conditions.

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Amoxicillin** (PBS restricted: community-acquired pneumonia; 1 g three times daily for 5 days; first-line narrow-spectrum)
- **Doxycycline** (PBS unrestricted — respiratory infections; 100 mg twice daily for 5 days; penicillin allergy or atypical)
- **Amoxicillin-clavulanate** (PBS restricted: pneumonia; aspiration or mixed organism; 875/125 mg twice daily)
- **Azithromycin** (PBS restricted: atypical pneumonia, in specific indications; 500 mg daily for 3 days; macrolide — S. pneumoniae macrolide resistance concern)
- **Note:** Fluoroquinolones (moxifloxacin, levofloxacin) are reserved for severe CAP or penicillin-allergic patients with atypical — not first-line in mild outpatient CAP; risk of C. difficile and selection of resistant organisms
- **Antibiotic stewardship:** narrow-spectrum antibiotics preferred; avoid broad-spectrum unless clinically indicated

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- CURB-65 ≥3 — hospital admission for IV antibiotics and monitoring
- SpO2 <93% or rapid respiratory rate ≥30/min — significant hypoxia; hospital
- Haemodynamic instability: hypotension, tachycardia, altered consciousness
- Suspected Legionella, Staphylococcal, or necrotising pneumonia — severe CAP needing IV therapy
- Severe dyspnoea at rest, cyanosis
- Inability to tolerate oral medication (vomiting) or take oral fluids

**Urgent same-day hospital referral:**
- CURB-65 2 — significant risk; discuss with hospital; consider admission especially if elderly, frail, or social concerns
- Failure to respond after 48–72 hours of oral antibiotics
- Clinical deterioration on treatment
- Large parapneumonic effusion — needs aspiration assessment

**Non-urgent follow-up:**
- CXR at 6 weeks post-pneumonia in smokers or patients ≥50 years — to confirm radiological resolution and exclude underlying malignancy (carcinoma presenting as pneumonia is a known entity)

## Telehealth Suitability

CAP management via telehealth has significant limitations.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Review of mild CAP at 48–72 hours in confirmed low-severity patient responding to treatment
- Antibiotic prescribing for confirmed mild CAP when clinical assessment (including SpO2) has been done by the patient with a validated device
- Post-pneumonia follow-up review and CXR result interpretation
- Vaccination counselling and prescribing

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- New presentation of suspected pneumonia — SpO2 measurement, auscultation, clinical examination
- Any features of severity — cannot adequately exclude hypoxia or haemodynamic instability without in-person assessment
- CXR arrangement and interpretation
- Moderate or severe CAP — hospital assessment required
