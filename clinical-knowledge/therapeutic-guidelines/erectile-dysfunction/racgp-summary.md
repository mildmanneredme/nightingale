## Erectile Dysfunction Assessment (RACGP)

Erectile dysfunction (ED) is the persistent inability to attain or maintain a penile erection sufficient for satisfactory sexual performance. ED affects approximately 50% of Australian men aged 40–70 years to some degree. It is substantially underreported due to stigma. Importantly, ED is a marker of cardiovascular risk — penile arteries are small and often first affected by atherosclerosis; ED may precede coronary artery disease by 2–5 years. All men presenting with ED should have a comprehensive cardiovascular risk assessment. ED is also frequently associated with low testosterone, depression, relationship issues, and lifestyle factors. Telehealth is suitable for initial assessment and pharmacotherapy management of ED, though physical examination requires in-person assessment.

## Clinical Presentation

**Definition and classification:**
- ED: persistent (≥3 months) inability to achieve/maintain erection adequate for intercourse
- Severity: mild (occasional), moderate (frequent), severe (consistent inability)
- Psychogenic ED: typically sudden onset, situational (partner-specific or circumstantial), morning erections preserved, younger men
- Organic ED: gradual onset, consistent regardless of situation, morning erections reduced or absent, older men, cardiovascular risk factors

**Prevalence by age:** 40% of men aged 40 years; rising to 70% by age 70 years; risk increases with cardiovascular disease, diabetes, obesity, smoking, and hypogonadism.

**Relationship to cardiovascular disease:**
- ED and CVD share risk factors (hypertension, dyslipidaemia, diabetes, smoking, obesity)
- ED predicts future cardiovascular events; Princeton consensus: ED in younger men without CVD risk factors should prompt cardiovascular risk stratification

**Associated conditions:**
- Diabetes mellitus — autonomic and vascular neuropathy; ED extremely common
- Hypertension and antihypertensive medications — beta-blockers and thiazide diuretics worsen ED; ACE inhibitors and ARBs neutral/favourable; calcium channel blockers neutral
- Hypogonadism (low testosterone) — reduced libido and ED; check total testosterone
- Depression and anxiety — highly associated with ED; bidirectional
- Medications: beta-blockers, thiazides, antidepressants (SSRIs, TCAs), antipsychotics, opioids, 5-alpha reductase inhibitors (finasteride, dutasteride), anti-androgens, alcohol

## Assessment Approach

**History:**
- Sexual history: duration, severity, situational vs consistent, morning erections, libido, orgasm and ejaculation (distinguish ED from ejaculatory disorders), relationship factors, sexual partner history
- IIEF (International Index of Erectile Function) questionnaire — 5-item version (IIEF-5/SHIM) — validated severity assessment; score ≤21 = some degree of ED; ≤7 = severe
- Cardiovascular history: hypertension, dyslipidaemia, diabetes, smoking, family history of CVD, exercise tolerance, symptoms of angina
- Psychological assessment: depression (PHQ-9), anxiety (GAD-7), relationship satisfaction, performance anxiety, sexual trauma history
- Medication review: identify drugs known to impair erectile function
- Lifestyle: alcohol use (AUDIT), smoking, physical activity, obesity (BMI/waist circumference)

**Physical examination (in-person required):**
- Blood pressure, BMI, waist circumference
- Genital examination: phimosis, Peyronie's disease (penile plaques), testicular atrophy, secondary sexual characteristics
- Peripheral pulses (assessment of peripheral arterial disease)

**Investigations:**
- Fasting glucose / HbA1c — exclude undiagnosed diabetes
- Fasting lipid panel — cardiovascular risk
- Total testosterone (early morning 8–10 am fasting sample) — hypogonadism; if low, confirm with repeat + LH, FSH (to classify primary vs secondary hypogonadism)
- FBC — anaemia
- TFTs — thyroid disease
- PSA — if testosterone therapy considered (contraindicated in prostate cancer)

## Key Differentials

- Psychogenic ED — younger men, situational, morning erections preserved; psychological and relationship factors
- Hypogonadism — low testosterone; low libido prominent; treat with testosterone (specialist supervised if clinically indicated)
- Peyronie's disease — penile curvature from fibrous plaque; may cause pain and ED; urology referral
- Neurogenic ED — spinal cord injury, MS, diabetic autonomic neuropathy; specialist management
- Arterial insufficiency — pudendal artery atherosclerosis; vascular workup if PDE5Is ineffective
- Medication-induced — review and switch if possible (e.g., change beta-blocker to ACE inhibitor, SSRI to bupropion or mirtazapine)
- Venous leak — inability to maintain erection despite adequate initiation; specialist vascular assessment

## Management Principles

**Lifestyle modification (evidence-based, treat first):**
- Exercise: aerobic exercise 3–5 times/week significantly improves erectile function by improving vascular health and testosterone
- Weight loss: obesity independently causes ED via hypogonadism, vascular, and psychological mechanisms; 5–10% weight loss improves ED
- Smoking cessation: smoking causes vasoconstriction and atherosclerosis — cessation improves ED
- Alcohol reduction: heavy alcohol use worsens ED; moderate use neutral
- Treat modifiable cardiovascular risk factors: BP, lipids, glycaemic control

**Psychological and relationship therapy:**
- Psychoeducation: explain psychological component and performance anxiety cycle
- CBT, couples therapy, sex therapy: particularly effective for psychogenic ED and mixed ED
- Mindfulness: emerging evidence for sexual performance anxiety

**Pharmacotherapy — first-line:**

PDE5 inhibitors (phosphodiesterase type 5 inhibitors): block cGMP breakdown, prolong smooth muscle relaxation and vasodilation in corpus cavernosum; require sexual stimulation to be effective:
- Sildenafil (Viagra, generic): 25–100 mg taken 30–60 min before intercourse; on-demand; food reduces absorption; duration 4–6 hours; most studied
- Tadalafil (Cialis, generic): 10–20 mg on demand; or 2.5–5 mg daily (daily low-dose for spontaneity and men with BPH); duration 36 hours; not affected by food; preferred by many patients
- Vardenafil (Levitra): 10–20 mg on demand
- Avanafil (Spedra): faster onset 15–30 min; fewer food interactions

PDE5I contraindications/cautions:
- Contraindicated with nitrates (sublingual, oral, topical, inhaled — absolute contraindication; can cause severe hypotension and death)
- Caution with alpha-blockers (additive hypotension — use lower PDE5I dose and allow 4–6 hour interval from alpha-blocker dose)
- Contraindicated in severe hepatic impairment
- Caution in recent MI, stroke, unstable angina — discuss with cardiologist (Princeton Consensus III guidelines apply)
- Side effects: headache, flushing, nasal congestion, dyspepsia, visual disturbances (sildenafil — blue-tinged vision; caution in retinal pigmentosa)

**Second-line / specialist options:**
- Vacuum erection devices (VED): mechanical; very effective; no drug interactions; useful for contraindications to PDE5Is or when ED is severe
- Intracavernosal alprostadil injection: highly effective; self-injection into corpus cavernosum; urologist training required
- Intraurethral alprostadil (MUSE): pellet inserted into urethra; less effective than injection
- Testosterone replacement therapy: only if confirmed hypogonadism (low testosterone + symptoms); does not treat ED directly but improves libido and may improve PDE5I response; requires specialist assessment (PBS-restricted; contraindicated in prostate cancer)
- Penile prosthesis: surgical implant; urology referral; reserved for refractory ED

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Sildenafil** (Viagra, generic — PBS restricted: erectile dysfunction; 25 mg, 50 mg, 100 mg; on-demand; authority required for PBS subsidy; also available OTC in some states in low doses without prescription)
- **Tadalafil** (Cialis, generic — PBS restricted: erectile dysfunction; 10 mg, 20 mg on-demand; 2.5 mg, 5 mg daily; authority required)
- **Vardenafil** (Levitra — PBS restricted: erectile dysfunction; 10 mg, 20 mg; authority required)
- **Note:** PDE5 inhibitors are also available over the counter (OTC) in Australia for certain doses (sildenafil 50 mg) following 2022 TGA reclassification from S4 to S3 — patients may self-purchase from pharmacy with pharmacist counselling; prescription not required for some formulations
- **Testosterone** (PBS restricted: confirmed hypogonadism — not for ED alone; requires low testosterone + symptoms; prostate cancer exclusion; various formulations)
- **Note:** Absolute contraindication — NEVER prescribe PDE5 inhibitors concurrently with nitrates in any form

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent cardiovascular assessment:**
- New ED in men aged <40 years with cardiovascular risk factors — heightened cardiac risk; early cardiovascular risk profiling and possible cardiology referral (Princeton Consensus III: ED is a cardiac risk equivalent in young men)
- Unstable angina, recent MI, or NYHA class III–IV heart failure — PDE5Is contraindicated; cardiological clearance before ED treatment

**Non-urgent specialist referral:**
- Endocrinology/andrology: confirmed hypogonadism requiring testosterone therapy evaluation; secondary hypogonadism (low LH/FSH)
- Urology: Peyronie's disease, failed PDE5I trials, suspected arterial or neurogenic cause, consideration of surgical treatment
- Psychologist/sex therapist: predominantly psychogenic ED, performance anxiety, relationship factors
- Cardiologist: if ED presents in context of significant CVD — risk stratification before PDE5I prescribing

## Telehealth Suitability

Erectile dysfunction assessment is well suited to telehealth for many presentations.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Sexual and psychological history taking
- IIEF-5 scoring and severity classification
- PDE5 inhibitor prescribing for men without absolute contraindications
- Medication review for ED-causing drugs
- Lifestyle counselling (exercise, smoking cessation, weight loss)
- Testosterone result interpretation and referral

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Physical examination (blood pressure, genital examination, peripheral pulses)
- Blood tests (testosterone, glucose, lipids) — pathology arranged
- Intracavernosal injection training — urologist supervised
- High-risk cardiovascular patients — in-person assessment before PDE5I prescribing
