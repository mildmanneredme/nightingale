## Dengue Fever Assessment (RACGP)

Dengue fever is a mosquito-borne viral infection caused by one of four dengue virus serotypes (DENV 1–4), transmitted by Aedes aegypti and Aedes albopictus mosquitoes. It is the most rapidly spreading mosquito-borne viral disease globally — approximately 400 million infections annually. In Australia, dengue is NOT endemic (no sustained local transmission on mainland), but is endemic in Queensland's far north (Cairns, Townsville) during wet season outbreaks and is commonly imported from South-East Asia, the Pacific Islands, and South America. Dengue is notifiable in all Australian states and territories. GPs encounter dengue in: returning travellers with febrile illness, North Queensland residents during outbreaks, and occasionally recently arrived migrants. There is no specific antiviral treatment — management is supportive. Dengue haemorrhagic fever (DHF) and dengue shock syndrome (DSS) are life-threatening complications requiring hospitalisation. Telehealth has limited utility for acute febrile illness; in-person assessment is required.

## Clinical Presentation

**Classic dengue fever ("breakbone fever"):**

Incubation: 4–10 days after mosquito bite

Phase 1 — Febrile (Days 1–3):
- Sudden high fever (≥38.5°C); often up to 40°C; may be biphasic ("saddleback" pattern)
- Severe headache (retro-orbital pain — pain behind the eyes is characteristic)
- Severe myalgia and arthralgia ("breakbone fever" — bone and joint pain)
- Facial flushing, injected conjunctivae
- Nausea, vomiting, anorexia
- Early rash: erythematous or macular — may be difficult to see

Phase 2 — Critical (Days 4–6):
- Fever may defervese (drop) — this is the MOST DANGEROUS PHASE
- Risk of plasma leakage leading to haemorrhage and shock
- Warning signs (require hospitalisation): abdominal pain, persistent vomiting, rapid breathing, bleeding gums, fatigue/restlessness, liver enlargement, rapid drop in platelet count on FBC

Phase 3 — Recovery (Days 7–10):
- Reabsorption of leaked plasma — risk of fluid overload (caution with IV fluids)
- Recovery rash: maculopapular or petechial, islands of white in sea of red — characteristic
- Bradycardia, generalised itching
- Fatigue may persist weeks

**Dengue haemorrhagic fever (DHF) and dengue shock syndrome (DSS):**
- DHF: thrombocytopenia (platelets <100×10⁹/L) + haemoconcentration + haemorrhagic manifestations (petechiae, purpura, positive tourniquet test, GI bleeding)
- DSS: DHF + circulatory failure (narrow pulse pressure <20 mmHg, hypotension, rapid weak pulse) — medical emergency

**Dengue in Australian context:**
- Second dengue infection with different serotype increases risk of DHF (antibody-dependent enhancement)
- Most severe disease in secondary infections — important travel history for prior dengue

## Assessment Approach

**History (key questions in febrile returned traveller):**
- Travel history: destination, activities, dates of travel and return; any known dengue outbreak at destination
- Mosquito exposure: time of day (Aedes bites during daylight — NOT dusk/dawn like malaria mosquitoes), repellent use
- Duration of illness and symptom progression
- Warning signs: severe abdominal pain, persistent vomiting, bleeding, extreme fatigue
- Prior dengue infection — risk of severe disease with reinfection
- Other infections to exclude: malaria (000 if suspected — always urgent), typhoid, chikungunya, Zika

**Physical examination:**
- Temperature, pulse, BP, respiratory rate, oxygen saturation
- Tourniquet test (capillary fragility): inflate BP cuff to midpoint between systolic and diastolic for 5 minutes; >20 petechiae in 1-inch square area = positive; indicates dengue but not specific
- Skin: flushing, petechiae, purpura, rash
- Abdomen: hepatomegaly, right upper quadrant tenderness, ascites
- Mucosal bleeding: gums, nose
- Signs of shock: narrow pulse pressure, weak rapid pulse, cold extremities, decreased consciousness

**Investigations:**
- FBC with differential: thrombocytopenia (platelets <150 falling to <100), leukopenia (WBC <5), haemoconcentration (rising haematocrit >20% from baseline — key warning sign of plasma leakage)
- LFTs: elevated transaminases (AST commonly >ALT) — common and can be markedly elevated
- Dengue NS1 antigen: detectable days 1–5 of illness (early); ELISA or rapid test; high specificity; best in first 3–5 days
- Dengue IgM and IgG: detectable from day 4–5; IgM positive = recent primary infection; both IgM and IgG positive = secondary infection (higher severity risk)
- Dengue PCR (NAAT): most sensitive in early illness (days 1–5); gold standard; available in hospital/reference laboratories
- Note: malaria film + PCR must be performed simultaneously if any risk of malaria co-exposure — malaria cannot be excluded on clinical grounds alone

**Repeat monitoring in outpatient dengue:**
- FBC every 24–48 hours during febrile and critical phase (days 3–6): track platelet count and haematocrit
- Hospitalise if platelets fall below 50×10⁹/L or haematocrit rises >20% from baseline

## Key Differentials

- Malaria: must always be excluded in travellers with fever within 28 days of return from endemic area; do malaria film + PCR urgently alongside dengue testing — malaria can be fatal within hours
- Typhoid fever: sustained fever, relative bradycardia, abdominal pain, rose spots; blood culture; Widal test
- Chikungunya: similar presentation; severe polyarthralgia persisting months after fever resolves; same mosquito vector
- Zika virus: milder illness; rash + fever + conjunctivitis + arthralgia; same vector; critical in pregnancy (microcephaly)
- Ross River virus: Australian endemic; arthralgia-predominant; rash; see `ross-river-fever` entry
- Influenza: respiratory predominant; not associated with plasma leakage or thrombocytopenia
- Leptospirosis: animal exposure, freshwater; jaundice, renal failure; serology

## Management Principles

**No specific antiviral treatment — supportive care:**

Outpatient management (dengue without warning signs):
- Rest; adequate oral hydration — 2–3 L/day oral fluids (water, oral rehydration salts, coconut water)
- Paracetamol: 500–1000 mg 4–6 hourly for fever and pain — maximum 4 g/day; preferred analgesia
- AVOID NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen, aspirin): increase bleeding risk due to antiplatelet effect and gastric irritation; CONTRAINDICATED in dengue
- AVOID aspirin: risk of Reye's syndrome; antiplatelet effect worsens haemorrhage risk
- Safety netting: MUST provide written warning signs and instructions to return immediately

**Warning signs requiring hospitalisation (admit to hospital):**
- Abdominal pain or tenderness
- Persistent vomiting (≥3 episodes/day)
- Clinical fluid accumulation (ascites, pleural effusion)
- Mucosal bleeding
- Lethargy, restlessness, altered consciousness
- Liver enlargement (hepatomegaly >2 cm)
- Rapid rise in haematocrit with rapid fall in platelet count

**Inpatient management (hospitalisation):**
- IV fluid resuscitation: cautious hydration — titrated to clinical response; Hartmann's solution preferred; avoid over-hydration (risk of pulmonary oedema in recovery phase)
- Platelet transfusion: only for active severe bleeding with very low platelets (<10–20×10⁹/L); NOT for prophylaxis
- Avoid corticosteroids: no evidence of benefit; may worsen outcomes
- Avoid antibiotics: unless secondary bacterial infection suspected

**Notification and public health:**
- Dengue is notifiable in all Australian states and territories: report confirmed cases to local public health unit
- Queensland: contact local public health unit for outbreak investigation and vector control
- Mosquito control: Aedes aegypti control in QLD (Wolbachia program) — Australian first

**Prevention (for future travellers):**
- No approved vaccine for routine use in Australia (Dengvaxia only for previously infected individuals ≥9 years in endemic areas — not approved in Australia for travellers)
- DEET-containing repellents: 20–30% DEET; apply every 4–6 hours; reapply after swimming; most effective against Aedes
- Clothing: long-sleeved shirts, long trousers; permethrin-treated clothing
- Accommodation: air conditioning, screened windows, mosquito nets
- Aedes mosquitoes bite during DAYLIGHT hours — protection required all day

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Paracetamol** (PBS unrestricted: fever and analgesia in dengue; 500–1000 mg 4–6 hourly; ONLY safe analgesic — avoid NSAIDs and aspirin)
- **Oral rehydration salts (Gastrolyte, Hydralyte)** (PBS unrestricted: OTC; dengue oral hydration maintenance)
- **Note:** NSAIDs and aspirin are CONTRAINDICATED in dengue — increase haemorrhage risk significantly; this is a critical prescribing safety point
- **DEET repellent**: not PBS-funded; OTC; essential prevention
- **Dengvaxia (dengue vaccine)**: not approved in Australia for travellers; available in some endemic countries for seropositive individuals only

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Dengue shock syndrome: hypotension, rapid weak pulse, narrow pulse pressure, cold extremities, altered consciousness — resuscitation and ITU admission
- Active major haemorrhage: GI bleeding, severe mucosal bleeding
- Respiratory distress from pleural effusion or pulmonary oedema

**Same-day hospital admission (warning signs present):**
- Any warning signs (see management above): abdominal pain, persistent vomiting, fluid accumulation, mucosal bleeding, lethargy, rapid rise in haematocrit, rapid fall in platelets
- Platelet count <50×10⁹/L
- Haematocrit rise >20% from baseline

**Urgent investigation (24-hour review):**
- Febrile traveller with dengue risk exposure and positive NS1 or serology: daily FBC monitoring during febrile and critical phase; clear safety netting instructions
- Concurrent malaria must always be excluded urgently

**Notifiable disease reporting:**
- All confirmed dengue cases must be reported to state/territory public health unit

## Telehealth Suitability

Dengue acute illness assessment is predominantly in-person; follow-up may be telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Post-recovery follow-up (fatigue, joint pain, return to activity)
- Result discussion (serology, FBC trends) in stable outpatient
- Pre-travel dengue prevention counselling and education
- Safety netting telephone follow-up between in-person reviews

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Acute febrile illness assessment (vital signs, tourniquet test, physical examination)
- Blood tests — FBC, LFTs, dengue serology/PCR — pathology arranged
- Concurrent malaria exclusion (urgent film and PCR)
- Assessment for hospitalisation warning signs
