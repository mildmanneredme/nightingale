## Seborrhoeic Dermatitis Assessment (RACGP)

Seborrhoeic dermatitis (SD) is a chronic, relapsing inflammatory skin condition affecting sebaceous gland-rich areas (scalp, face, chest). It is caused by an abnormal immune response to Malassezia yeast (a normal commensal of human skin). SD affects approximately 1–3% of the general population and up to 10% of HIV-positive patients (severe, treatment-resistant SD is a marker of immune compromise). It presents across all age groups: infantile seborrhoeic dermatitis (cradle cap in infants) and adult seborrhoeic dermatitis. The scalp (dandruff at mild end) is most commonly affected, followed by the face (nasolabial folds, eyebrows, medial cheeks). SD is a clinical diagnosis; management centres on antifungal and anti-inflammatory agents. It is a lifelong condition requiring long-term maintenance therapy. GPs manage the majority of SD; specialist referral for refractory or atypical presentations. Telehealth is well-suited to SD management.

## Clinical Presentation

**Adult seborrhoeic dermatitis:**
- Scalp: dandruff (pityriasis capitis) — fine to coarse white/yellow greasy scales; pruritus; erythema of scalp skin between scale plaques; mild to severe
- Face: nasolabial folds (characteristic), medial cheeks, eyebrows, eyelids (blepharitis), glabella, ears (pinna and external auditory canal); erythema with overlying white-yellow greasy scale
- Chest: mid-sternal region; petaloid (petal-shaped) or pityriform patches
- Flexures: axillae, groin, submammary — intertriginous SD; non-scaly erythema in folds
- Otitis externa-like: external ear canal; scaling and pruritus

**Infantile seborrhoeic dermatitis (cradle cap):**
- Thick yellow-brown greasy adherent scale on vertex of scalp
- May spread to forehead, nappy area, body folds
- Distinct from eczema: non-pruritic; usually not distressing to infant
- Typically self-resolves within weeks to months

**Severity:**
- Mild: dandruff only; fine scale; no inflammation
- Moderate: thick scale on scalp and face; erythema; pruritus
- Severe: extensive facial involvement; immunocompromised (HIV); thick plaques; consider HIV testing

## Assessment Approach

**Clinical diagnosis — no investigations required in typical presentation:**
- Distribution: sebaceous-rich sites (scalp, face especially nasolabial folds and eyebrows, chest, ear)
- Morphology: erythematous patches with overlying white/yellow greasy scale; well-defined
- Pruritus: often present but not severe

**Investigations if atypical or severe:**
- HIV serology: severe, extensive, refractory SD — consider HIV; SD is an AIDS-defining dermatosis in advanced immunosuppression
- Skin scraping (KOH): if tinea capitis or tinea faciei in differential; rare
- Skin biopsy: only if diagnostic uncertainty persisting

## Key Differentials

- Psoriasis (scalp): thicker, silvery adherent scale; well-defined; nail pitting; extensor surfaces elsewhere; biopsy differentiates
- Atopic eczema (face): pruritic; exudate; family history atopy; children usually; spares nasolabial folds typically
- Tinea capitis: children; hair loss; fluorescence with Wood's lamp; KOH hyphae; culture
- Rosacea: central face; erythema and papules/pustules; no scale; different distribution
- Contact dermatitis: distribution matches allergen exposure; patch testing
- Pityriasis rosea: herald patch; Christmas tree distribution trunk; self-limiting; young adults
- Lupus erythematosus: butterfly distribution; no scale; ANA positive; photosensitive

## Management Principles

**Scalp seborrhoeic dermatitis:**

Mild (dandruff):
- Zinc pyrithione shampoo (Head & Shoulders, Selsun Blue): 1–2× weekly; OTC; reduces Malassezia; first-line
- Selenium sulfide 2.5% shampoo (Selsun Gold): weekly; OTC and PBS; more potent; allow 3–5 minutes contact before rinse
- Coal tar shampoo (Ionil T, Polytar): reduces scale; OTC; stains; odour; effective

Moderate-severe (thick scale, erythema):
- Ketoconazole 2% shampoo (Nizoral): 2–3× weekly initially; reduce to weekly or fortnightly maintenance; most evidence; PBS-listed for seborrhoeic dermatitis
- Topical corticosteroid + ketoconazole: betamethasone valerate scalp lotion for inflammation; use short-term (2–4 weeks) then switch to maintenance antifungal alone; avoid long-term steroids (scalp atrophy, hair loss)
- Salicylic acid shampoo: descaling (keratolytic); prepare scalp for antifungal penetration; use before ketoconazole

**Facial seborrhoeic dermatitis:**

Mild:
- Ketoconazole 2% cream (Nizoral cream): apply BD for 2–4 weeks; maintenance 1–2× weekly; PBS-listed
- Ketoconazole 2% shampoo used as face wash: leave on 3–5 minutes; very effective for facial SD

Moderate-severe:
- Ciclopirox olamine 1% cream: antifungal with anti-inflammatory properties; applied BD; PBS-listed
- Hydrocortisone 1% cream (low-potency steroid): short-term (1–2 weeks) to reduce inflammation quickly; then switch to antifungal maintenance; avoid fluorinated steroids on face (skin atrophy, perioral dermatitis)
- Pimecrolimus 1% cream (Elidel): calcineurin inhibitor; anti-inflammatory; not immunosuppressive; safe for face and sensitive areas; PBS-listed; useful for steroid-sparing maintenance; slight stinging on application initially
- Tacrolimus 0.1% ointment (Protopic): stronger; specialist-initiated for severe facial SD or refractory presentations

**Blepharitis (eyelid margin SD):**
- Warm compresses × 5 minutes daily
- Lid hygiene: baby shampoo or commercial eyelid wipes (OCuSOFT) BD
- Ketoconazole cream: very cautious periocular application; avoid eye contact
- Refer to ophthalmology/optometrist if keratitis or vision affected

**Infantile seborrhoeic dermatitis (cradle cap):**
- Gentle shampooing: daily with baby shampoo; gently loosen scale with soft brush or fingers
- Baby oil, olive oil, or mineral oil: apply to scalp before shampooing; softens and loosens adherent scale
- Ketoconazole 2% shampoo: for persistent cradle cap; safe in infants; PBS-listed; apply BD for 2 weeks
- Usually self-resolves; reassure parents

**Maintenance therapy (chronic relapsing condition):**
- Maintenance antifungal (ketoconazole shampoo weekly or fortnightly): prevents relapse; continuous use as needed; most patients require ongoing maintenance
- Avoid triggers: stress, fatigue, heat and humidity, harsh products
- Gentle skin care: fragrance-free cleanser; avoid alcohol-based products (dry and irritate)

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Ketoconazole 2% shampoo (Nizoral)** (PBS restricted: seborrhoeic dermatitis — listed indication; 2–3× weekly for treatment; 1× weekly for maintenance; allow 3–5 minutes contact time)
- **Ketoconazole 2% cream** (PBS restricted: seborrhoeic dermatitis, tinea — listed; apply BD for 2–4 weeks; maintenance 1–2× weekly for face)
- **Selenium sulfide 2.5% shampoo (Selsun)** (PBS unrestricted: dandruff/seborrhoeic dermatitis; weekly; OTC availability; less expensive)
- **Ciclopirox olamine 1% cream** (PBS restricted: seborrhoeic dermatitis — listed; anti-inflammatory + antifungal; BD application)
- **Pimecrolimus 1% cream (Elidel)** (PBS restricted: atopic dermatitis — also used off-label/on-label for facial SD; steroid-sparing; safe on face; no skin atrophy risk)
- **Note:** Avoid potent topical steroids on the face for seborrhoeic dermatitis — skin atrophy, perioral dermatitis, steroid rosacea; use low-potency hydrocortisone 1% for maximum 2 weeks, then transition to antifungal or pimecrolimus for maintenance

## Escalation Triggers

**Non-urgent dermatology:**
- Severe refractory SD not responding to ketoconazole + pimecrolimus: consider biopsy; isotretinoin (very resistant cases — specialist)
- Diagnostic uncertainty: atypical features; suspected psoriasis vs SD; skin biopsy
- HIV-related SD: infectious disease or immunology for HIV management

## Telehealth Suitability

Seborrhoeic dermatitis management is well-suited to telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Initial assessment (patient can describe/photograph distribution and morphology)
- Treatment initiation (ketoconazole shampoo/cream prescribing)
- Review of response and maintenance planning
- Cradle cap parental counselling
- Referral coordination

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Uncertain diagnosis requiring close examination or biopsy
- Severe or immunocompromised patients requiring physical examination
- HIV testing (blood test)
