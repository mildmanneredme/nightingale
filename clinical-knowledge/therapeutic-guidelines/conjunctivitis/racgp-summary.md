## Conjunctivitis Assessment (RACGP)

Conjunctivitis is inflammation of the conjunctiva presenting with red eye, discharge, and discomfort. It is one of the most common eye conditions in general practice. The three main types are viral, bacterial, and allergic. Most acute infectious conjunctivitis is viral and self-limiting; antibiotics are frequently over-prescribed. Correct identification of the type guides appropriate management and avoids unnecessary antibiotic use. Telehealth with photo assessment is moderately suitable — the key clinical task is distinguishing conjunctivitis from more serious causes of red eye that require urgent ophthalmology review.

## Clinical Presentation

**Viral conjunctivitis:** Unilateral or bilateral watery or mucopurulent discharge, conjunctival injection, follicular reaction on lower tarsal conjunctiva, preauricular lymphadenopathy. Often follows URTI. Highly contagious. Adenovirus most common. Typically self-limiting in 1–2 weeks.

**Bacterial conjunctivitis:** Mucopurulent discharge (often worse in morning), bilateral or unilateral, conjunctival injection. Common organisms: Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae. Newborns and sexually active adults: consider gonococcal and chlamydial (neonatal ophthalmia and adult inclusion conjunctivitis).

**Allergic conjunctivitis:** Bilateral itching (cardinal feature), watery discharge, chemosis (conjunctival oedema), papillary reaction on upper tarsal conjunctiva. Associated with atopy, allergic rhinitis. Seasonal (pollen) or perennial (house dust mite, cat).

**Distinguishing serious from benign red eye (MUST NOT be missed on telehealth):**
- Photophobia + reduced vision + ciliary flush = keratitis or anterior uveitis — urgent ophthalmology
- Pain + halos around lights + fixed mid-dilated pupil = acute angle-closure glaucoma — emergency
- Corneal ulcer = contact lens wearer with pain, photophobia — urgent

## Assessment Approach

Photo assessment: bilateral vs unilateral, type and colour of discharge, periorbital involvement. Ask about: contact lens use, visual acuity changes, photophobia, pain severity (mild discomfort vs significant pain).

Critically assess for red flags requiring in-person/urgent review (see Escalation Triggers): reduced visual acuity, significant photophobia, ciliary flush, corneal opacity, significant eye pain, fixed pupil.

STI-related conjunctivitis: consider in sexually active adults with purulent conjunctivitis and no other infectious contact — Chlamydia trachomatis causes chronic follicular conjunctivitis with mucopurulent discharge.

Neonatal conjunctivitis (ophthalmia neonatorum): any eye discharge in newborn <28 days — requires urgent assessment, swab for Neisseria gonorrhoeae and Chlamydia.

## Key Differentials

- Anterior uveitis (iritis) — deep aching eye pain, photophobia, ciliary flush (perilimbal injection), reduced vision; urgent ophthalmology
- Acute angle-closure glaucoma — severe eye pain, nausea, vomiting, halos around lights, fixed mid-dilated pupil, corneal haziness; ophthalmic emergency — 000
- Corneal ulcer / keratitis — photophobia, pain, discharge in contact lens wearer or after foreign body; urgent ophthalmology
- Episcleritis — localised red sector, mild discomfort, no discharge; usually benign
- Subconjunctival haemorrhage — bright red patch, no pain or discharge; benign; no treatment needed
- Blepharitis — eyelid margin inflammation with crusting; chronic; treat with lid hygiene

## Management Principles

**Viral conjunctivitis:** No antibiotic treatment indicated. Management: cool compresses, regular hand washing (highly contagious — school/work exclusion until discharge resolves), lubricating eye drops for comfort. Resolves spontaneously in 1–2 weeks.

**Bacterial conjunctivitis:** Mild cases often self-resolve in 7–10 days without antibiotics. Topical antibiotics appropriate for: moderate-severe symptoms, school exclusion concerns (reduces duration and contagion), immunocompromised patients, neonates.

Topical antibiotic options:
- Chloramphenicol 0.5% eye drops four times daily for 5–7 days — first-line; broad-spectrum; very low risk of systemic toxicity
- Chloramphenicol 1% eye ointment — useful overnight application, or for children who resist drops

**Allergic conjunctivitis:** Allergen avoidance where possible. Cold compresses for symptomatic relief. Topical antihistamine/mast cell stabiliser drops:
- Sodium cromoglicate (Opticrom — OTC) — mast cell stabiliser; preventive; must be used regularly
- Olopatadine (Patanol — OTC) — combined antihistamine/mast cell stabiliser; once or twice daily; effective

Oral antihistamines (cetirizine, loratadine): useful if comorbid allergic rhinitis — also reduce ocular symptoms.

**Chlamydial conjunctivitis:** Azithromycin 1 g single dose OR doxycycline 100 mg twice daily for 7 days (treat STI as per ASHM guidelines); partner notification required.

**Gonococcal conjunctivitis:** IV/IM ceftriaxone — requires urgent specialist management; do not manage in primary care via telehealth.

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Chloramphenicol 0.5% eye drops** (Chlorsig — PBS restricted: bacterial conjunctivitis; also available OTC in pharmacist-only formulation in some states) — 1–2 drops four times daily for 5–7 days
- **Chloramphenicol 1% eye ointment** (PBS restricted: bacterial conjunctivitis) — apply to lower fornix
- **Olopatadine 0.1% eye drops** (Patanol — OTC in Australia) — allergic conjunctivitis; once or twice daily
- **Sodium cromoglicate 2% eye drops** (Opticrom — OTC) — preventive; 1–2 drops four times daily
- **Tobramycin eye drops** (PBS restricted: bacterial eye infections) — alternative to chloramphenicol; broader spectrum
- **Azithromycin** (PBS restricted: chlamydial infection) — 1 g single dose for chlamydial conjunctivitis

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED ophthalmology:**
- Severe eye pain with reduced vision and fixed dilated pupil — acute angle-closure glaucoma
- Chemical eye injury (alkali or acid splash) — irrigate immediately and call 000

**Urgent ophthalmology (same day):**
- Reduced visual acuity — any cause of red eye with vision loss requires urgent specialist review
- Significant photophobia with ciliary flush — anterior uveitis
- Contact lens wearer with pain and photophobia — corneal ulcer until proven otherwise
- Purulent conjunctivitis in a neonate <28 days — neonatal ophthalmia; swab urgently, start IV antibiotics

**Urgent in-person GP:**
- Suspected gonococcal conjunctivitis — profuse purulent discharge; systemic treatment required
- Periorbital or orbital cellulitis (skin swelling around eye, proptosis, restricted eye movement, fever)

## Telehealth Suitability

Conjunctivitis is moderately suitable for telehealth — photo assessment aids diagnosis but red flags must be actively excluded.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Typical viral or allergic conjunctivitis — bilateral watery discharge, itching, no vision change, no photophobia
- Mild bacterial conjunctivitis in adults with intact vision and no red flags — topical chloramphenicol
- Allergic conjunctivitis management — topical antihistamine or mast cell stabiliser drops

**Requires in-person or urgent assessment:**
- Any reduced visual acuity — cannot safely assess via telehealth
- Significant photophobia or eye pain beyond mild irritation
- Contact lens wearer with eye pain
- Neonatal conjunctivitis
- Suspected corneal, uveal, or glaucoma pathology — all require slit-lamp examination
