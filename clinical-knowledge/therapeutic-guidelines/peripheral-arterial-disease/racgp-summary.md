## Peripheral Arterial Disease Assessment (RACGP)

Peripheral arterial disease (PAD) is atherosclerosis of the peripheral arteries, most commonly affecting the lower limb arteries. It affects approximately 10–15% of Australians aged ≥65 years and is a marker of systemic atherosclerosis — patients with PAD have significantly elevated risk of coronary artery disease, stroke, and cardiovascular death. Many patients are asymptomatic; symptomatic PAD typically presents as intermittent claudication (exertional leg pain). Severe PAD causes critical limb ischaemia (CLI) — rest pain, ulceration, or gangrene — which is a vascular emergency. Telehealth is moderately suitable; ABI measurement and physical examination require in-person assessment.

## Clinical Presentation

**Intermittent claudication (most common symptomatic PAD):**
- Exertional calf, thigh, or buttock pain/cramping that reliably occurs after a predictable walking distance and is relieved by rest within 5–10 minutes
- Reproducible with exercise; bilateral or unilateral depending on distribution of disease
- Level of occlusion determines symptom location: superficial femoral artery → calf claudication; aortoiliac occlusion (Leriche syndrome) → bilateral buttock/thigh claudication + erectile dysfunction
- Fontaine Classification: Stage I = asymptomatic; Stage II = claudication; Stage III = rest pain; Stage IV = tissue loss (ulceration/gangrene)

**Critical limb ischaemia (CLI — vascular emergency):**
- Rest pain (worse at night, lying; relieved by hanging leg down or sleeping sitting up)
- Non-healing ischaemic ulcers
- Gangrene (dry or wet)
- Requires urgent vascular surgery assessment — limb-threatening

**Acute limb ischaemia (ALI — surgical emergency):**
- 6 Ps: Pain, Pallor, Pulselessness, Paraesthesia, Paralysis, Poikilothermia (cold)
- Acute occlusion of artery (thrombosis or embolism); sudden onset
- Call 000 immediately — requires emergency revascularisation within hours

## Assessment Approach

**History:**
- Claudication distance and stability/progression over time
- Risk factors: smoking (most important PAD risk factor), diabetes, hypertension, hyperlipidaemia
- Current medications: antiplatelet, statin, antihypertensive
- Foot care and wound history

**Ankle-Brachial Index (ABI) — key diagnostic test:**
- Ratio of ankle to brachial systolic BP; measured with Doppler probe and BP cuff
- Normal: 0.91–1.30
- Mild PAD: 0.70–0.90
- Moderate PAD: 0.40–0.70
- Severe PAD: <0.40
- ABI >1.30: calcified vessels (non-compressible) — common in diabetes; unreliable; further investigation needed (toe-brachial index or duplex ultrasound)
- ABI performed in-person; GP training or vascular laboratory

**Physical examination:**
- Peripheral pulses: femoral, popliteal, posterior tibial, dorsalis pedis — absent or reduced in PAD
- Skin changes: pallor, cyanosis, hair loss, cool limbs, dependent rubor (red when hanging down)
- Venous filling time: >20 seconds suggests severe ischaemia
- Buerger's test: elevation pallor + dependent rubor
- Foot and skin examination: ulcers, wounds, nail changes, calluses

**Investigations:**
- ABI (as above)
- Fasting glucose, HbA1c: diabetes (major PAD risk factor and complicator)
- Lipid panel: dyslipidaemia management
- Renal function: CKD common with widespread atherosclerosis
- Duplex ultrasound: anatomical assessment of arterial occlusions; non-invasive
- CT angiography: pre-operative planning for revascularisation; specialist-arranged
- ECG: concomitant coronary artery disease common

## Key Differentials

- Spinal stenosis (neurogenic claudication): back-related leg pain; worsened by standing/walking but not quickly relieved by rest (relieved by sitting/forward flexion); may require MRI
- Venous claudication: heaviness/bursting pain in affected leg after walking; slow recovery; varicose veins or DVT history; distinguish from arterial
- Deep vein thrombosis: acute calf pain and swelling; Wells score; Doppler ultrasound
- Musculoskeletal causes: osteoarthritis, compartment syndrome, shin splints; no pulse abnormality

## Management Principles

**Cardiovascular risk factor modification (most important — reduces cardiovascular events and may slow PAD progression):**
- Smoking cessation: most impactful single intervention; halves progression risk and amputation rate
- Statin therapy: all PAD patients regardless of cholesterol level — LDL-C target <1.8 mmol/L in PAD (very high CVD risk); reduces MI, stroke, and cardiovascular death
- Antiplatelet therapy: aspirin 100 mg or clopidogrel 75 mg daily — reduces cardiovascular events in PAD; clopidogrel preferred over aspirin in PAD (CAPRIE trial)
- Blood pressure control: target <130/80 mmHg; ACE inhibitor or ARB preferred (reduce cardiovascular events in PAD); beta-blocker: not contraindicated in PAD (historical concern not supported by evidence)
- Glycaemic control in diabetes: see `type-2-diabetes` entry; reduces microvascular complications that worsen PAD
- Wound care: foot care education critical in diabetic PAD patients

**Supervised exercise therapy:**
- First-line for intermittent claudication: structured supervised walking program (walk to near-maximal pain, rest, repeat); 3 × 30 minutes/week for ≥12 weeks; improves claudication distance, functional capacity, and quality of life
- Exercise physiologist or physiotherapist referral; often as effective as angioplasty for mild-moderate claudication

**Pharmacotherapy for claudication:**
- Cilostazol (PBS-restricted): phosphodiesterase inhibitor; increases claudication distance ~40–60%; 100 mg twice daily; contraindicated in heart failure; not widely used in Australia
- Naftidrofuryl: limited PBS availability; modest evidence for claudication

**Revascularisation (vascular surgery — for CLI or severely limiting claudication):**
- Angioplasty ± stenting: endovascular; lower limb arteries; day procedure or short admission
- Bypass surgery: open surgical revascularisation; for multilevel disease
- Decision based on anatomy (lesion characteristics), patient fitness, and functional status
- All CLI patients require urgent vascular surgery assessment

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Clopidogrel** (PBS restricted: established PAD; 75 mg daily; first-line antiplatelet preferred over aspirin in PAD based on CAPRIE evidence)
- **Aspirin** (PBS unrestricted: secondary CVD prevention; 100 mg daily; acceptable alternative to clopidogrel)
- **Rosuvastatin / atorvastatin** (PBS restricted: high CVD risk — PAD qualifies as established CVD; LDL-C target <1.8 mmol/L)
- **Cilostazol** (PBS restricted: intermittent claudication — authority required; 100 mg twice daily; contraindicated in heart failure)
- **Antihypertensive agents** (ACE inhibitor or ARB first-line; see `hypertension` entry)
- **Wound dressings / podiatry**: GP can arrange podiatry referral for diabetic foot in PAD — critical for limb salvage

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Acute limb ischaemia: sudden onset 6 Ps (Pain, Pallor, Pulselessness, Paraesthesia, Paralysis, Cold) — vascular surgical emergency; limb salvageable within 4–6 hours of onset
- CLI: rest pain, ischaemic ulcer, gangrene — urgent vascular surgery referral (same day)

**Urgent vascular surgery referral:**
- Rapidly progressive claudication or new rest pain
- New ischaemic ulcer or non-healing wound in PAD
- ABI <0.40 (severe PAD)

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Vascular surgery: ABI 0.40–0.70 with functional limitation; lifestyle-limiting claudication not responding to exercise therapy; anatomy assessment for revascularisation
- Exercise physiologist: supervised exercise program for claudication
- Podiatrist: diabetic foot care, wound prevention (high-risk PAD + diabetes)
- Cardiologist: concomitant CAD assessment (very common in PAD)

## Telehealth Suitability

PAD management is moderately suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Cardiovascular risk factor review and pharmacotherapy management (statin, antiplatelet, antihypertensive prescribing)
- Smoking cessation counselling and pharmacotherapy
- Claudication progress monitoring (patient-reported walking distance)
- Lifestyle advice and exercise referral
- Diabetes and wound management review

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- ABI measurement — Doppler probe required; key diagnostic and monitoring test
- Foot and pulse examination
- Wound assessment in diabetic PAD patients
