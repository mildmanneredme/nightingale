## Carpal Tunnel Syndrome Assessment (RACGP)

Carpal tunnel syndrome (CTS) is the most common peripheral nerve entrapment neuropathy, caused by compression of the median nerve at the wrist within the carpal tunnel. It affects approximately 3–6% of the general adult population in Australia; prevalence increases with age and is higher in women (3:1). CTS is strongly associated with repetitive hand/wrist activities, pregnancy, diabetes, hypothyroidism, rheumatoid arthritis, and obesity. Clinical diagnosis is reliable in typical presentations; nerve conduction studies confirm severity. GPs play a central role in: diagnosing CTS using clinical tests (Tinel's, Phalen's), managing mild-moderate disease with night splints and corticosteroid injections, identifying secondary causes, and referring for surgical decompression in severe or refractory cases. Telehealth is suitable for initial assessment of typical presentations; physical examination and procedures require in-person attendance.

## Clinical Presentation

**Characteristic symptoms:**
- Tingling, numbness, paraesthesiae in the median nerve distribution: thumb, index, middle finger, and radial half of ring finger (NOT little finger — distinguishes CTS from ulnar neuropathy)
- Nocturnal symptoms: waking from sleep with hand tingling; "glove-wearing" sensation; characteristic — patients often hang hand out of bed or shake to relieve
- Worse with wrist flexion or extension: driving, holding phone, reading, typing
- Flicking or shaking the hand ("flick sign"): relieves symptoms temporarily — highly specific for CTS
- Grip weakness: difficulty opening jars, gripping; weak pinch grip

**Signs (advanced disease):**
- Thenar wasting: atrophy of abductor pollicis brevis (thenar eminence); indicates severe and potentially irreversible nerve damage
- Reduced pinch grip strength
- Reduced sensation in median nerve distribution
- Abductor pollicis brevis weakness: inability to abduct thumb away from palm against resistance

**Associated conditions:**
- Pregnancy: 3rd trimester; median nerve oedema from fluid retention; usually resolves postpartum; treat conservatively
- Diabetes: peripheral neuropathy predisposes
- Hypothyroidism: nerve oedema; check TSH
- Rheumatoid arthritis: tenosynovitis within carpal tunnel
- Obesity, acromegaly: structural
- Repetitive occupational use: construction, computer work, assembly line
- Post-traumatic: distal radius fracture

## Assessment Approach

**Clinical diagnosis — typical presentation:**
- Nocturnal hand tingling in median distribution + flick sign: >90% sensitivity and specificity for CTS
- Physical examination and provocative tests confirm

**Provocative tests:**
- Tinel's sign: tap over carpal tunnel at wrist crease; positive if tingling in median distribution (sensitivity ~60%, specificity ~60%)
- Phalen's test: full wrist flexion × 60 seconds; positive if tingling reproduced in median distribution (sensitivity ~75%, specificity ~75%)
- Reverse Phalen's (prayer test): full wrist extension × 60 seconds
- Durkan's test (carpal compression): direct pressure over carpal tunnel for 30 seconds; positive if median tingling (sensitivity ~87%, specificity ~90% — most accurate)
- Abductor pollicis brevis (APB) weakness: thenar abduction against resistance; weakness indicates severe CTS

**Investigations:**
- Nerve conduction studies (NCS) / electromyography (EMG): gold standard; confirms diagnosis, localises compression, grades severity (mild/moderate/severe); performed by neurologist or neurophysiologist; required before surgery; not always needed for clinical diagnosis or conservative management
- Ultrasound wrist: identifies swelling of median nerve at carpal tunnel inlet; useful when NCS unavailable; non-invasive
- Blood tests (to identify secondary causes): TSH (hypothyroidism), fasting glucose/HbA1c (diabetes), inflammatory markers, rheumatoid factor — if clinically indicated

## Key Differentials

- Ulnar neuropathy (cubital tunnel): tingling in ring and little finger (NOT thumb/index/middle); elbow flexion worsens; Tinel's at elbow; EMG/NCS differentiates
- Cervical radiculopathy (C6/C7): tingling in arm + neck pain; dermatomal distribution; exacerbated by neck movement; MRI cervical spine
- Thoracic outlet syndrome: arm tingling from neurovascular compression between clavicle and rib; positional; vascular features (pallor); specialist assessment
- Peripheral polyneuropathy: bilateral symmetrical; stocking-glove distribution; associated systemic disease (diabetes); NCS shows generalised neuropathy
- Raynaud's phenomenon: colour changes (white-blue-red) with cold; not primarily tingling; review triggers
- De Quervain's tenosynovitis: pain at radial wrist (1st dorsal compartment); Finkelstein's test positive; different from CTS
- Trigger finger: catching, locking, nodule on flexor tendon; no sensory symptoms

## Management Principles

**Conservative management (mild-moderate CTS):**

Night splint (first-line for mild-moderate):
- Neutral wrist position splint worn during sleep and/or activity: prevents wrist flexion; reduces nocturnal symptoms
- OT-made or off-the-shelf; neutral position (not hyper-extended)
- Evidence: significant symptom reduction in mild-moderate CTS; 50% achieve symptom control
- Duration: minimum 6 weeks of consistent use; may continue long-term

Activity modification:
- Reduce repetitive wrist flexion-extension activities; ergonomic assessment of workstation
- Frequent rest breaks; ergonomic keyboard and mouse positioning

Corticosteroid injection (effective second-line):
- Methylprednisolone or triamcinolone injected into carpal tunnel (not median nerve)
- Significant and rapid symptom relief; 80% response rate; lasts 1–12 months
- 1–2 injections appropriate; further injections if response good and recurrence
- Safer alternative to surgery; excellent response predicts good surgical outcome
- Technique: ultrasound-guided preferred (reduces rare nerve injection risk); blind injection acceptable by experienced clinician

Non-pharmacological:
- Hand therapy: tendon and nerve gliding exercises; occupational therapy
- Treat underlying cause: glycaemic control (diabetes), thyroxine (hypothyroidism), anti-inflammatory treatment (RA), postpartum (pregnancy CTS)

**Surgical decompression (carpal tunnel release):**
- Indications: moderate-severe NCS changes; thenar wasting; failed conservative management (splint + 1–2 injections × 6–12 months); intolerable symptoms
- Open or endoscopic carpal tunnel release: division of transverse carpal ligament
- High success rate (90%+); low complication rate
- Recovery: hand use within days; full function within 4–6 weeks
- Note: thenar atrophy may not fully recover even after surgery — early referral prevents irreversible damage
- Refer to: hand surgeon or plastic surgeon; public hospital orthopaedic hand clinic or private

**Pregnancy-related CTS:**
- Night splinting: first-line; safe; most effective
- Corticosteroid injection: small risk to fetus but generally considered safe; discuss with obstetrician if severe
- Surgical decompression rarely required — wait for postpartum resolution (most cases resolve within 3 months postpartum)

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Methylprednisolone acetate (Depo-Medrol 40 mg/mL)** or **Triamcinolone acetonide (Kenacort 40 mg/mL)**: corticosteroid for carpal tunnel injection; 20–40 mg injected into carpal tunnel; can be mixed with 1 mL lignocaine for comfort; not PBS-funded for injection (private; minimal cost)
- **Night splints**: not PBS-funded; OT supply or off-the-shelf ~$30–50; some private health funds cover OT prescription
- **NSAIDs (naproxen, ibuprofen)**: limited evidence for CTS; may provide short-term analgesic relief but do not treat nerve compression; short course only; not primary treatment
- **Note:** Oral corticosteroids are less effective than injection for CTS and have systemic side effects; local injection preferred
- **Note:** Pregabalin/gabapentin have no evidence for CTS and are NOT recommended; address underlying nerve compression rather than treat neuropathic pain

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent hand surgery:**
- Rapid thenar wasting or progressive weakness: severe or acute-onset; risk of permanent nerve damage if decompression delayed
- Acute CTS from haematoma or fracture: fracture at wrist with acute onset CTS — acute compartment syndrome of carpal tunnel; surgical emergency

**Non-urgent hand surgery:**
- Severe NCS findings (EMG): low SNAP amplitude, denervation changes — early surgical referral
- Failed conservative management (splinting + 2 injections × 6–12 months): surgical decompression
- Patient preference for definitive treatment over ongoing conservative management

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Neurologist or neurophysiologist: NCS/EMG if diagnosis uncertain or before surgery
- Occupational therapist: splint fabrication; workstation assessment; ergonomics; hand exercises
- Rheumatologist: if RA or inflammatory cause driving CTS

## Telehealth Suitability

CTS assessment is partially suitable for telehealth; initial assessment may be done remotely but examination required.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- History-taking and symptom characterisation (nocturnal tingling, flick sign, distribution)
- Ordering pathology (TSH, glucose) and NCS referral
- Treatment discussion and decision-making
- Conservative management education (splinting, activity modification)
- Post-injection or post-surgical follow-up

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Physical examination: Tinel's, Phalen's, Durkan's, thenar atrophy assessment
- Corticosteroid injection administration
- Pre-surgical assessment
