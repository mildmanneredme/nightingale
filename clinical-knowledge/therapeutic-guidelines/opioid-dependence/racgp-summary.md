## Opioid Dependence Assessment (RACGP)

Opioid use disorder (OUD) — also termed opioid dependence — is a chronic relapsing condition characterised by compulsive opioid use despite harmful consequences, with neurobiological changes in reward pathways. In Australia, OUD affects approximately 70,000 people who inject drugs (PWID), plus a significant burden from prescription opioid dependence. Prescription opioid misuse has grown substantially: oxycodone, fentanyl, and codeine (prior to up-scheduling in 2018) are common sources. GPs are central to managing OUD via opioid pharmacotherapy (OPT) — buprenorphine-naloxone (Suboxone) or methadone — which reduces mortality by 50%, prevents overdose, reduces crime, and improves social functioning. GP authorisation to prescribe OPT varies by state/territory (most require an S8 prescriber authority). Telehealth is well-suited to OPT monitoring. Naloxone prescribing for overdose prevention is a key GP responsibility.

## Clinical Presentation

**DSM-5 / ICD-11 OUD criteria (≥2 in 12 months = disorder; ≥6 = severe):**
- Taking opioids in larger amounts or over longer period than intended
- Persistent desire or unsuccessful efforts to cut down
- Significant time spent obtaining, using, or recovering from opioids
- Craving (strong desire or urge to use)
- Recurrent use resulting in failure to fulfil major role obligations
- Continued use despite social or interpersonal problems caused by use
- Activities given up or reduced because of use
- Recurrent use in physically hazardous situations
- Continued use despite persistent physical or psychological problems
- Tolerance (markedly increased dose needed for same effect)
- Withdrawal (characteristic withdrawal syndrome or using to avoid it)

**Opioid withdrawal syndrome:**
- Onset: 8–24 hours after last dose (short-acting opioids — heroin, oxycodone IR); 36–72 hours (methadone)
- Symptoms: yawning, lacrimation, rhinorrhoea, sweating, piloerection; then nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, abdominal cramps, myalgias, insomnia, anxiety, agitation
- Signs: dilated pupils, tachycardia, hypertension, diaphoresis
- Duration: 5–7 days (short-acting opioids); 2–4 weeks (methadone)
- COWS score (Clinical Opiate Withdrawal Scale): quantifies severity; guides treatment

**Types of opioid dependence in Australian primary care:**
- Heroin dependence: injecting; HIV/hepatitis C risk; chaotic lifestyle; social disadvantage
- Prescription opioid dependence: started for legitimate pain; dose escalation; doctor shopping (now largely detected via SafeScript in Victoria — national real-time prescribing monitoring)
- Codeine dependence: prior to 2018 up-scheduling; now uncommon but still presents
- Fentanyl patch dependence: increasingly seen; very high tolerance
- Iatrogenic dependence: prolonged prescription opioid use for chronic non-cancer pain; patient may not identify as "drug user"

## Assessment Approach

**Comprehensive assessment:**
- History: opioid type(s), route, frequency, quantity, last use; duration of dependence; previous treatment (OPT history); other substance use; injecting practices
- Medical history: hepatitis B/C status, HIV; chronic pain comorbidities; psychiatric comorbidities; pregnancy
- Social: housing, social supports, legal issues, employment
- Motivation: stage of change; goals (abstinence vs OPT vs harm reduction)

**Physical examination:**
- Signs of intoxication (miosis, sedation) or withdrawal (mydriasis, diaphoresis, tachycardia)
- Injection sites: track marks, thrombosis, skin infections, abscesses
- Signs of complications: endocarditis murmur, septic arthritis, DVT

**Investigations:**
- Urine drug screen (UDS): baseline; confirms opioid use; identifies polysubstance use (benzodiazepines, amphetamines, cannabis — concurrent use increases overdose risk)
- LFTs, hepatitis B surface antigen and HBsAb, hepatitis C antibody and RNA PCR: liver disease very common
- HIV serology: if PWID
- FBC, renal function, CRP
- Pregnancy test: in women of reproductive age — methadone and buprenorphine are safe in pregnancy

**Screening tools:**
- DAST-10 (Drug Abuse Screening Test): validates severity
- AUDIT: screen concurrent alcohol use
- COWS (Clinical Opiate Withdrawal Scale): severity of withdrawal — guides induction timing

## Key Differentials

- Opioid dependence vs opioid use for legitimate pain: complex overlap; patients with chronic pain may develop dependence; OPT can address both
- Acute opioid toxicity/overdose: respiratory depression, miosis, sedation, coma — see Escalation Triggers; treat with naloxone
- Benzodiazepine or alcohol dependence: concurrent in many OUD patients; assess carefully — alcohol and benzodiazepine withdrawal can be fatal (unlike opioid withdrawal which is distressing but not fatal in adults)
- Psychiatric emergency: psychosis from stimulant use (amphetamines); suicidal ideation — very common comorbidity
- Chronic non-cancer pain managed with opioids: ICD-11 distinguishes dependence from therapeutic use; COWS and loss-of-control criteria help differentiate

## Management Principles

**Opioid pharmacotherapy (OPT) — most effective treatment:**

Buprenorphine-naloxone (Suboxone, Buvidal):
- First-line for most patients; safer than methadone (ceiling effect on respiratory depression); can be prescribed by authorised GPs
- Sublingual film: 2/0.5 mg, 4/1 mg, 8/2 mg, 16/4 mg — once daily; dose range 8–32 mg/day
- Extended-release injectable buprenorphine (Buvidal — weekly or monthly): superior adherence; removes daily dispensing burden; growing evidence for patient outcomes; PBS-listed; increasingly used in GP setting
- Induction: wait for mild withdrawal (COWS ≥8) to avoid precipitated withdrawal; start 2–4 mg; titrate over days to suppress cravings and withdrawal
- State/territory authority: most states require GP to hold S8 prescriber authority or work under a licenced prescriber

Methadone:
- Oral liquid dispensed daily by pharmacy; supervised consumption
- More complex to prescribe (specialist authority usually required); higher overdose risk; QTc prolongation
- Best for severe dependence with treatment-resistant history; daily dispensing provides structure
- Dose: 30–40 mg induction; maintenance 60–120 mg/day

**Naloxone (overdose prevention):**
- Prescribe naloxone to ALL patients with OUD and their household contacts
- Nyxoid nasal spray (1.8 mg): PBS-listed; two doses in pack
- Intranasally: 1 dose, call 000, second dose if no response in 2–3 minutes
- Patient and household education on overdose recognition and naloxone use

**Psychosocial interventions (alongside OPT):**
- Counselling: individual or group; motivational interviewing; CBT
- Case management: social support, housing, Centrelink
- Alcohol and other drug (AOD) counselling: refer to community drug and alcohol services (state-funded)
- Narcotics Anonymous, SMART Recovery: peer support

**Harm reduction:**
- Needle and Syringe Programs (NSPs): free needles, safe disposal, hepatitis C testing — state-funded; no GP prescription required
- Safe injecting rooms: Victoria (North Richmond Community Health) and NSW — legal
- Hepatitis C treatment: highly effective direct-acting antivirals now available via GP with special PBS authority; cure rate >95% — see `hepatitis-c` entry

**Withdrawal management (detoxification):**
- NOT recommended as stand-alone treatment (high relapse and overdose risk after abstinence)
- Symptomatic: clonidine 50–150 µg TDS (BP monitoring); loperamide; NSAIDs; metoclopramide
- Inpatient detox: for severe withdrawal, pregnancy, or complex social circumstances — refer to addiction medicine or hospital AOD service

**Comorbidities:**
- Chronic pain: buprenorphine provides analgesia; maximise non-opioid pain management; pain clinic co-management
- Psychiatric: depression, anxiety, PTSD very common; treat alongside OPT; avoid benzodiazepines
- Hepatitis C: treat with direct-acting antivirals while on OPT — cure improves outcomes
- Pregnancy: buprenorphine or methadone is recommended throughout pregnancy; neonatal opioid withdrawal syndrome (NOWS) expected but manageable; obstetric and addiction medicine co-management

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Buprenorphine-naloxone (Suboxone)** (PBS restricted S8: opioid dependence — requires state/territory OPT prescriber authority in most jurisdictions; daily supervised dosing initially, take-away doses for stable patients)
- **Buprenorphine extended-release injection (Buvidal 8/16/24/32 mg weekly; 64/96/128 mg monthly)** (PBS restricted S8: opioid dependence; must be initiated under supervised setting; eliminates daily dispensing; superior adherence)
- **Methadone** (PBS restricted S8: opioid dependence; specialist authority usually required; daily supervised dispensing at pharmacy; QTc monitoring)
- **Naloxone (Nyxoid nasal spray 1.8 mg)** (PBS unrestricted: opioid overdose reversal; prescribe to all OUD patients and households; two-dose pack; educate on use with 000)
- **Clonidine** (PBS unrestricted: off-label for opioid withdrawal symptom management; 50–150 µg TDS; monitor BP — hypotension risk)
- **Note:** SafeScript (real-time prescription monitoring) is mandatory for all Schedule 8 prescriptions in Victoria and increasingly nationally — check before prescribing controlled substances

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000:**
- Opioid overdose: unresponsive, slow/absent breathing, cyanosis, miosis — administer naloxone (Nyxoid nasal spray), call 000, rescue breathing if trained; further naloxone dose if no response in 2–3 minutes
- Suspected endocarditis (in PWID): fever + new murmur + embolic signs — blood cultures, ED, cardiology
- Septic shock from injection site infection or septicaemia

**Urgent AOD/addiction medicine:**
- Pregnant woman with OUD: same-day obstetric and addiction medicine referral; OPT stabilisation urgent
- Suicidal ideation with plan: same-day psychiatric care
- Severe intoxication with polysubstance use (opioids + benzodiazepines + alcohol): medical emergency

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Addiction medicine specialist: complex OUD; failure to respond to buprenorphine; methadone initiation
- Hepatology/gastroenterology: hepatitis C treatment (if not managed in primary care); cirrhosis
- AOD counselling services: state and territory community AOD services for psychosocial support
- Social worker: housing, Centrelink, legal issues

## Telehealth Suitability

OUD monitoring is largely suitable for telehealth in stable patients.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Ongoing OPT monitoring (doses, side effects, cravings, functioning)
- Take-away dose assessment (stability criteria review)
- Hepatitis C and HIV result discussion
- Psychiatric comorbidity monitoring
- Naloxone counselling and education
- Social support coordination

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- OPT induction (initial assessment, UDS, COWS scoring)
- Urine drug screen collection
- Physical examination (injection sites, signs of complications)
- Blood tests — pathology arranged
- Buvidal injection administration
