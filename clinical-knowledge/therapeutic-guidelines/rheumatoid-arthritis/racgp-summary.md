## Rheumatoid Arthritis — GP Monitoring (RACGP)

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is a chronic autoimmune inflammatory arthritis characterised by symmetric polyarthritis predominantly affecting the small joints of hands and feet. It affects approximately 400,000 Australians. RA causes progressive joint destruction, disability, and is associated with significantly increased cardiovascular risk. Early diagnosis and aggressive treat-to-target management (achieving remission or low disease activity) substantially improves outcomes. Rheumatologists initiate and manage complex RA therapy; the GP plays a critical role in early recognition and referral, monitoring for disease activity and comorbidities, and managing therapy side effects and cardiovascular risk. Telehealth is suitable for routine RA monitoring visits.

## Clinical Presentation

**Early RA (must recognise and refer early — within 6 weeks of symptom onset to rheumatology):**
- Symmetric small joint synovitis: MCP joints, PIP joints, wrists — warm, swollen, tender; bilateral
- Morning stiffness ≥60 minutes: key distinguishing feature from OA (<30 min); reflects degree of inflammation
- Constitutional features: fatigue, malaise, low-grade fever, weight loss in severe cases
- Extra-articular features: rheumatoid nodules (subcutaneous, over pressure points), pleuritis, pericarditis, episcleritis, dry eyes/mouth (secondary Sjögren's)

**Established RA features (complications if inadequately treated):**
- Joint deformity: ulnar deviation of MCPs, swan-neck and boutonnière deformities, "Z-deformity" thumb
- Erosive disease on X-ray or MRI: joint erosion and joint space narrowing
- Functional impairment: fine motor tasks, grip strength
- Extra-articular disease: RA is associated with pulmonary fibrosis, pericarditis, vasculitis, amyloidosis

**Disease activity assessment:**
- DAS28 (Disease Activity Score): rheumatology tool; incorporates joint count, ESR/CRP, patient global assessment
- RAPID3 (Routine Assessment of Patient Index Data): patient self-reported; simple; usable in GP

## Assessment Approach

**Investigations for suspected RA (refer to rheumatology alongside investigations):**
- RF (Rheumatoid Factor): positive in 70–80% of RA; not specific (false positives: hepatitis, SLE, Sjögren's, healthy elderly)
- Anti-CCP (anti-cyclic citrullinated peptide antibodies): more specific than RF; 60–80% sensitivity, >95% specificity; high titer strongly predicts erosive disease; seropositive earlier than RF
- CRP, ESR: elevated in active RA; also use for monitoring
- FBC: anaemia of chronic disease in RA; also for DMARD monitoring
- LFTs, renal function: baseline before DMARD initiation; methotrexate monitoring
- X-ray hands and feet: baseline; bony erosions in established disease

**GP monitoring of established RA on DMARDs:**
- Disease activity review: joint count (tender/swollen), patient global assessment, ESR/CRP
- Methotrexate (MTX) monitoring: FBC (cytopenias — pancytopenia risk), LFTs (hepatotoxicity) — at initiation monthly, then 3-monthly when stable; dose up to 25 mg/week SC or oral
- Hydroxychloroquine: annual ophthalmology review (retinal toxicity with prolonged use — rare but cumulative dose-dependent)
- Leflunomide: LFTs, BP monitoring; teratogenic — strict pregnancy prevention
- Biologics (TNF inhibitors — adalimumab, etanercept, certolizumab, golimumab; non-TNF — tocilizumab, abatacept, rituximab): TB screening (IGRA) before initiation; skin checks for increased infection risk; annual influenza vaccine; pneumococcal vaccine
- Cardiovascular risk: RA is an independent cardiovascular risk factor (chronic inflammation); annual CVD risk assessment; statin if indicated; BP management; smoking cessation critical

## Key Differentials

- Osteoarthritis: DIP joint involvement, Heberden/Bouchard nodes, morning stiffness <30 min, normal RF/CCP, no systemic features
- Gout: acute attacks, hyperuricaemia, MSU crystals; asymmetric; see `gout` entry
- Psoriatic arthritis: associated psoriasis; asymmetric; DIP joint involvement; nail changes; enthesitis; see `psoriasis` entry
- Lupus (SLE): ANA positive; butterfly rash; multi-organ; DNA antibodies
- Reactive arthritis: asymmetric; post-infectious; STI or GI trigger; uveitis, urethritis (Reiter's triad)
- Septic arthritis: monoarticular; hot; systemically unwell; urgent aspiration

## Management Principles

**DMARD therapy (rheumatologist-initiated — GP co-manages):**

Conventional synthetic DMARDs (csDMARDs):
- Methotrexate (MTX): anchor therapy; 7.5–25 mg/week oral or SC; folic acid 5 mg weekly (day after MTX) mandatory; reduces MTX side effects (nausea, stomatitis); monitor FBC, LFTs; teratogenic — contraception required
- Hydroxychloroquine: adjunct; lower-risk; antimalarial; annual ophthalmology
- Sulfasalazine: adjunct; GI side effects common; FBC monitoring
- Leflunomide: active metabolite (teriflunomide); teratogenic — washout procedure with cholestyramine required before pregnancy

Biologic DMARDs (bDMARDs — if inadequate response to 2 csDMARDs):
- TNF inhibitors (adalimumab, etanercept, certolizumab, golimumab): SC injection; highly effective; PBS-restricted; requires pre-treatment TB screening (IGRA), hepatitis B screening; infection risk; withhold with active infection or severe illness
- Non-TNF biologics: abatacept (CTLA-4 inhibitor), tocilizumab (IL-6 inhibitor), rituximab (CD20 B-cell depletion)
- Targeted synthetic DMARDs (tsDMARDs): JAK inhibitors — baricitinib, upadacitinib, tofacitinib; oral tablets; PBS-restricted; thrombosis, infection, lipid monitoring; avoid in high CV/cancer risk

**Treat-to-target (T2T) strategy:**
- Target: remission or low disease activity within 6 months
- Assess every 1–3 months during active disease adjustment; step up DMARD if target not reached
- Once stable: 6-monthly monitoring adequate

**Symptomatic management (adjuncts — do not delay DMARD):**
- NSAIDs: reduce pain and stiffness; not disease-modifying; GI protection (PPI); limit duration
- Low-dose prednisolone (bridging): useful during DMARD initiation or disease flare; lowest effective dose; short course preferred; long-term increases infection, osteoporosis, cardiovascular risk
- Intra-articular corticosteroid: for single inflamed joint; in-person procedure

**Comorbidity management:**
- Cardiovascular risk: RA doubles CVD risk; aggressive risk factor management; statin as per absolute CVD risk
- Osteoporosis: corticosteroid use + RA inflammation increase fracture risk; calcium, vitamin D, DEXA; bisphosphonate if indicated
- Vaccination: biologic therapy — annual influenza, pneumococcal, COVID-19, zoster (Shingrix if >50 and on bDMARD); live vaccines contraindicated on biologics (Shingrix is inactivated — safe)
- Depression/anxiety: high prevalence; screen and treat

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Methotrexate** (PBS restricted: RA; requires authority; 7.5–25 mg weekly; folic acid 5 mg weekly on different day; FBC + LFT monitoring; teratogenic)
- **Hydroxychloroquine** (PBS restricted: RA; 200–400 mg daily; ophthalmology annually after 5 years)
- **Leflunomide** (PBS restricted: RA; 20 mg daily; LFTs + BP monitoring; teratogenic)
- **Adalimumab** (Humira, biosimilars — PBS restricted: RA; authority; refractory to csDMARDs; pre-treatment TB screening)
- **Etanercept** (Enbrel, biosimilars — PBS restricted: RA; as per adalimumab)
- **Prednisolone** (PBS unrestricted — short-course bridging; minimise long-term use; calcium + vitamin D if prolonged)
- **Note:** DMARDs require close monitoring; GP and rheumatologist shared care protocol; any serious infection, fever, or unusual symptom in patient on biologic — hold biologic and assess urgently

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent rheumatology or hospital assessment:**
- Suspected septic arthritis in RA patient — joint aspiration emergency; biologics must be held
- Serious infection in patient on biologic — hold biologic; urgent assessment; IV antibiotics if indicated
- Vasculitis features: digital ischaemia, skin ulcers, mononeuritis multiplex — urgent rheumatology
- High disease activity with rapid joint destruction — urgent rheumatology review and DMARD escalation

**Urgent GP or specialist review:**
- MTX-related severe cytopenias or hepatotoxicity — hold MTX; haematology or hepatology as needed
- Suspected pulmonary toxicity (MTX or RA-ILD): new dyspnoea, dry cough in RA patient — CXR, HRCT chest, pulmonology

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Physiotherapist, occupational therapist: joint protection, exercise, splints, adaptive equipment
- Podiatrist: foot care, metatarsal support for forefoot RA
- Ophthalmologist: annual for hydroxychloroquine monitoring; uveitis if suspected

## Telehealth Suitability

RA monitoring is well suited to telehealth for stable patients.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Disease activity review (patient-reported joint symptoms, stiffness duration)
- Blood result interpretation (FBC, LFTs, CRP for DMARD monitoring)
- Medication renewal and side effect assessment
- CVD risk factor monitoring
- Vaccination review and counselling
- Referral coordination (rheumatology, physio, podiatry)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Joint examination (swollen/tender joint count)
- Blood tests (pathology arranged)
- Flare assessment — clinical examination to determine if infection or disease flare
- Intra-articular corticosteroid injection — in-person procedure
