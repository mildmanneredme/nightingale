## Polycystic Ovary Syndrome Assessment (RACGP)

Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) is the most common endocrine disorder in women of reproductive age, affecting approximately 10–12% of Australian women. It is characterised by hyperandrogenism, ovulatory dysfunction, and polycystic ovarian morphology (PCOM). PCOS has significant metabolic, reproductive, and psychological implications. Australia led the development of the 2023 International PCOS Guideline (Monash University). Management is predominantly lifestyle-based, supplemented by pharmacotherapy for specific features (menstrual irregularity, hyperandrogenism, infertility, metabolic risk). Telehealth is well-suited for PCOS assessment and ongoing management.

## Clinical Presentation

**Reproductive features:**
- Menstrual irregularity: oligo-amenorrhoea (cycles >35 days or <8 per year) or amenorrhoea (>90 days) — ovulatory dysfunction; most common presenting complaint
- Subfertility: anovulatory infertility; PCOS is the most common cause of anovulatory infertility in Australia
- Miscarriage risk: slightly elevated in PCOS (particularly if obesity or insulin resistance)

**Hyperandrogenism — clinical or biochemical:**
- Hirsutism: excess terminal hair in androgen-sensitive distribution (face, neck, chest, abdomen, upper thighs); graded by modified Ferriman-Gallwey score (≥4–6 = clinically significant)
- Acne: typically hormonal pattern — jawline, chin, lower face
- Alopecia: female-pattern hair loss (androgenic)
- Biochemical: elevated total or free testosterone, elevated DHEA-S, elevated androstenedione

**Polycystic ovarian morphology (PCOM) on ultrasound:**
- ≥20 follicles per ovary OR ovarian volume >10 mL on transvaginal ultrasound (in women >8 years post-menarche)
- Not required for PCOS diagnosis if hyperandrogenism + ovulatory dysfunction present

**Metabolic features (important comorbidities):**
- Insulin resistance and compensatory hyperinsulinaemia — present in 50–70% of PCOS
- Type 2 diabetes: 3–7× increased risk; screen regularly with HbA1c
- Gestational diabetes: significantly elevated risk in pregnancy
- Obesity: 60–80% of women with PCOS are overweight or obese; though PCOS occurs across all weight ranges
- Dyslipidaemia: elevated TG, reduced HDL-C
- Cardiovascular risk: elevated in long term due to metabolic risk factors

**Psychological features:**
- Anxiety and depression: significantly higher prevalence than general population; PHQ-9 and GAD-7 at every visit
- Body image concerns and eating disorders: screen with specific validated tools
- Quality of life impact from hirsutism, acne, infertility, weight concerns

## Assessment Approach

**Diagnosis — Rotterdam criteria (2 of 3 required, after exclusion of other causes):**
1. Oligo/anovulation
2. Clinical and/or biochemical hyperandrogenism
3. Polycystic ovarian morphology on ultrasound

Must exclude other causes of hyperandrogenism and menstrual irregularity before PCOS diagnosis.

**Investigations:**
- Androgens: total testosterone (most accessible); free testosterone (preferred — reflects bioavailable androgen); DHEA-S if congenital adrenal hyperplasia suspected; LH and FSH (LH:FSH ratio elevated in classic PCOS — useful but not diagnostic)
- Thyroid function: TSH — hypothyroidism mimics some PCOS features; must exclude
- Prolactin: hyperprolactinaemia causes amenorrhoea and sometimes galactorrhoea; exclude
- 17-OH progesterone (morning, early follicular phase): if congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH) suspected (very high androgens, ambiguous features)
- Fasting glucose / HbA1c: diabetes and prediabetes screen; repeat 1–3 yearly in all PCOS
- Fasting lipid panel: dyslipidaemia screen
- Pelvic ultrasound (transvaginal preferred): PCOM assessment; rule out structural pathology; note — ultrasound alone without hyperandrogenism or anovulation is NOT sufficient for PCOS diagnosis

## Key Differentials

- Thyroid disease — hypothyroidism causes menstrual irregularity and weight gain; TSH essential
- Hyperprolactinaemia — amenorrhoea, galactorrhoea; prolactin level
- Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (non-classic) — elevated 17-OH progesterone; ACTH stimulation test; adult women; similar presentation to PCOS
- Cushing's syndrome — central obesity, easy bruising, purple striae, buffalo hump, moon face; 24-hour urinary cortisol or low-dose dexamethasone suppression test
- Androgen-secreting tumour — rapid onset virilisation, extremely high testosterone (>5 nmol/L); urgent imaging
- Primary ovarian insufficiency (POI) — elevated FSH, low oestradiol; amenorrhoea; age <40; see menopause entry

## Management Principles

**Lifestyle (first-line for all PCOS with metabolic or weight concerns):**
- Weight loss of 5–10%: restores ovulation in 50–80% of overweight/obese women; reduces androgen levels, improves insulin resistance, lowers diabetes risk
- Dietary approach: no single best diet; reduce energy intake; Mediterranean diet preferred for metabolic benefits; low glycaemic index diet reduces insulin resistance
- Exercise: 150 min/week moderate aerobic + resistance training; improves insulin sensitivity independent of weight loss
- Dietitian referral: recommended for all overweight PCOS with metabolic features

**Menstrual cycle regulation:**
- Combined oral contraceptive pill (COCP): first-line for menstrual regulation; protects endometrium (chronic anovulation — endometrial hyperplasia and cancer risk from unopposed oestrogen); also treats acne and hirsutism via anti-androgenic effect
- Progesterone withdrawal bleed: medroxyprogesterone acetate 5–10 mg for 10–12 days every 1–3 months — if COCP not appropriate; induces withdrawal bleed and prevents endometrial hyperplasia; does not treat hyperandrogenism
- Levonorgestrel IUD (Mirena): endometrial protection; suitable for women not wanting estrogen or not requiring contraception; does not treat systemic hyperandrogenism

**Hyperandrogenism (hirsutism, acne):**
- COCP first-line (anti-androgenic effect, especially with ethinylestradiol + cyproterone acetate — Diane-35, or desogestrel/drospirenone formulations)
- Spironolactone: anti-androgen; 25–100 mg daily; second-line or adjunct; not PBS-listed for hirsutism; requires contraception (teratogenic if pregnancy during use); effective for hirsutism in 6–12 months
- Eflornithine cream (Vaniqa): facial hair; reduces growth rate; topical; PBS-listed for hirsutism
- Metformin: modest androgenic benefit via insulin resistance reduction; second-line

**Insulin sensitisation (metabolic risk or infertility):**
- Metformin: 500 mg 3 times daily (titrate to minimise GI side effects); improves insulin sensitivity; reduces testosterone; restores menstruation in some women; reduces T2D risk; PBS-restricted in T2D but off-label in PCOS for insulin resistance (prescribe on private script)
- Inositol (myo-inositol): supplement, not TGA-registered; some evidence for ovulatory restoration and metabolic benefit; widely used by patients; discuss

**Infertility (fertility specialist referral — GP initiates discussion):**
- Ovulation induction: letrozole (aromatase inhibitor) — first-line over clomiphene in PCOS; endocrinologist or fertility specialist prescribes; PBS Authority required
- Weight loss improves natural conception rate
- IVF: for women not responding to ovulation induction or with other infertility factors

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Ethinylestradiol/cyproterone acetate** (Diane-35 — PBS unrestricted: moderate-to-severe acne, hirsutism related to androgen excess; also used for menstrual regulation in PCOS; anti-androgenic OCP of choice in PCOS)
- **Metformin** (PBS restricted: T2D; off-label for PCOS — private prescription; 500 mg three times daily; titrate for GI tolerability)
- **Medroxyprogesterone acetate** (Provera — PBS restricted: secondary amenorrhoea, endometrial protection; 5–10 mg for 10–12 days every 1–3 months)
- **Letrozole** (Femara — PBS restricted: breast cancer; off-label for ovulation induction in PCOS — fertility specialist)
- **Eflornithine** (Vaniqa cream — PBS restricted: facial hair in women; 11.5% cream applied twice daily)
- **Spironolactone** (PBS unrestricted: as aldosterone antagonist; off-label for hirsutism; 25–100 mg daily; contraception mandatory)

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent assessment:**
- Rapid onset severe virilisation (voice deepening, clitoromegaly): androgen-secreting tumour; urgent testosterone measurement and pelvic imaging — specialist referral

**Non-urgent specialist referral:**
- Endocrinologist: diagnostic uncertainty, suspected secondary hyperandrogenism, CAH, Cushing's, management of significant metabolic comorbidities
- Gynaecologist: complex menstrual management, endometrial assessment if prolonged amenorrhoea, surgical ovulation induction
- Fertility specialist: infertility, ovulation induction with letrozole or gonadotrophins, IVF
- Psychologist: anxiety, depression, body image concerns — very common in PCOS

## Telehealth Suitability

PCOS assessment and management is well suited to telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Symptom assessment and PCOS feature review
- Menstrual cycle assessment and regularity monitoring
- COCP prescribing for menstrual regulation and hyperandrogenism
- Metformin discussion and prescribing
- Lifestyle counselling and dietitian referral
- Psychological screening (PHQ-9, GAD-7)
- Blood test results review (androgens, HbA1c, lipids)
- Referral coordination (fertility, endocrinology)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Pelvic ultrasound — radiology referral (transvaginal preferred)
- Blood pressure measurement (important before OCP initiation)
- Hirsutism grading and clinical examination
- Blood tests — pathology referral
