## Urticaria Assessment (RACGP)

Urticaria (hives) is characterised by transient, pruritic, raised wheals (weals) that appear and resolve within 24 hours, often with surrounding erythema. Angioedema — deeper swelling of dermis, subcutaneous tissue, or mucosae — occurs in 50% of urticaria patients. Urticaria affects approximately 15–25% of people at some point in their life. Acute urticaria (<6 weeks duration) is common and often triggered by infection, medication, or allergy; most cases resolve spontaneously. Chronic urticaria (>6 weeks) affects 0.5–1% of the population and is predominantly autoimmune in origin — identifying a trigger in chronic urticaria is often unsuccessful (<50%). GPs manage most acute and many chronic urticaria cases; specialist referral for refractory chronic urticaria and for anaphylaxis assessment. The distinction between urticaria and urticarial vasculitis (wheals lasting >24 hours, bruising) is important. Telehealth is suitable for chronic urticaria review; acute urticaria with throat symptoms requires urgent in-person or emergency assessment.

## Clinical Presentation

**Urticaria morphology:**
- Wheals (weals): raised erythematous or pale oedematous plaques; pruritic; transient (individual lesions resolve within 24 hours, often within 1–2 hours); variable size (mm to cm)
- Angioedema: deeper, asymmetric, non-pitting swelling; often affects face (periorbital, lips, tongue), hands, genitalia; may be painful rather than itchy; resolves in 1–3 days
- Dermographism: wheals induced by skin stroking — common form of physical urticaria

**Classification:**
- Acute urticaria: <6 weeks; common; frequently allergic or post-infectious
- Chronic spontaneous urticaria (CSU): >6 weeks, occurring most days; no identifiable external trigger; predominantly autoimmune (autoreactive IgE or IgG antibodies against IgE receptor)
- Chronic inducible urticaria: triggered by physical stimuli — cold contact (cold urticaria), dermographism, pressure, cholinergic (exercise, sweating), solar, aquagenic

**Severity and impact:**
- Urticaria Activity Score (UAS7): patient-reported wheals + itch over 7 days; range 0–42; guides treatment decisions; validated for CSU monitoring
- Dermatology Life Quality Index (DLQI): quality of life impact

**Common triggers (acute urticaria):**
- Infection: viral URTI most common (especially children); bacterial; parasitic
- Medications: NSAIDs (most common drug trigger — aspirin, ibuprofen); antibiotics (penicillin, cephalosporins); ACE inhibitors (angioedema)
- Foods: peanuts, tree nuts, shellfish, fish, milk, eggs, wheat — IgE-mediated
- Insect stings: hymenoptera (bees, wasps); may be part of anaphylaxis
- Latex: healthcare workers, atopic individuals
- Contact allergens: nickel, preservatives

**ACE inhibitor angioedema (important specific type):**
- Bradykinin-mediated (not IgE-mediated)
- Tongue, face, laryngeal swelling — no urticaria
- Can occur months to years after starting ACE inhibitor
- Treat by ceasing ACE inhibitor; substitute ARB (lower risk); antihistamines ineffective

## Assessment Approach

**History:**
- Duration: acute vs chronic (>6 weeks)
- Distribution: localised vs generalised; face, throat involvement
- Airway symptoms: throat tightness, voice change, stridor, dysphagia — anaphylaxis concern
- Individual lesion duration: resolve within 24 hours (urticaria) vs persist >24 hours and bruise (urticarial vasculitis)
- Triggers: food timing (1–2 hours after eating), medication timing, exercise, cold, stress
- Systemic symptoms: fever, joint pain, fatigue — suggests systemic cause
- Medication review: NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, antibiotics, codeine
- Personal/family history of atopy, thyroid disease, autoimmune conditions

**Physical examination:**
- Skin: morphology, distribution, extent; note if lesions present and their exact character
- Dermographism: stroke skin with tongue depressor — wheal formation
- Angioedema: assess mouth, tongue, pharynx, larynx (stridor, voice change)
- Lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly: systemic disease

**Investigations:**
- Acute urticaria: usually none required; identify and avoid trigger
- Chronic spontaneous urticaria (>6 weeks): limited testing recommended (routine extensive allergy testing not productive):
  - FBC with differential: eosinophilia (parasites, drug reaction)
  - ESR, CRP: raised in systemic urticarial vasculitis
  - TSH: thyroid autoimmunity (Hashimoto's) associated with CSU
  - Hepatitis B and C serology: associated with urticaria
  - Anti-nuclear antibody (ANA): SLE screen if systemic features
  - Skin prick testing: for specific foods/allergens only if clear IgE-mediated trigger suspected — NOT routinely useful in CSU
  - Skin biopsy: if urticarial vasculitis suspected (lesions lasting >24 hours, purpuric)

**Anaphylaxis assessment:**
- If airway involvement (throat swelling, voice change, stridor): 000 / anaphylaxis protocol
- Post-anaphylaxis: ASCIA allergy referral; adrenaline autoinjector prescription; allergy action plan

## Key Differentials

- Anaphylaxis: urticaria + systemic reaction (airway, cardiovascular, GI) — 000; adrenaline
- Urticarial vasculitis: wheals persist >24 hours; may bruise or leave hyperpigmentation; biopsy shows leukocytoclastic vasculitis; associated with SLE, hepatitis; immunology/rheumatology referral
- Angioedema without urticaria: ACE inhibitor angioedema; hereditary angioedema (C1 esterase inhibitor deficiency — rare; recurrent; triggered by stress/minor trauma; unresponsive to antihistamines)
- Fixed drug eruption: persistent single or few lesions that recur at same site with drug re-exposure; no wheals
- Erythema multiforme: target lesions; post-infection (HSV); mucous membrane involvement
- Mastocytosis: urticaria pigmentosa; Darier's sign (scratch lesion → urticarial wheal); tryptase elevated; specialist
- Contact dermatitis: specific distribution (contact site); scale and vesicles; not purely wheals; patch testing
- Insect bite reactions: local wheals; clear history

## Management Principles

**Trigger identification and avoidance:**
- Identify and avoid triggers: key for acute urticaria; often not possible for CSU
- Stop NSAIDs: if suspected trigger; note that NSAIDs worsen 30% of CSU regardless of whether they caused it
- Cease ACE inhibitor: if angioedema — switch to ARB; angioedema can be life-threatening

**Antihistamines (first-line and maintenance):**

Non-sedating second-generation H1 antihistamines (first-line):
- Cetirizine 10 mg daily: most commonly used; non-sedating; PBS-listed OTC; can cause mild sedation
- Loratadine 10 mg daily: non-sedating; OTC; well-tolerated
- Fexofenadine 180 mg daily: truly non-sedating; PBS-listed; preferred if driving/alertness important
- Desloratadine, bilastine: alternatives; PBS-listed

Dose escalation (CSU not controlled on standard dose):
- Up to 4× standard dose: ASCIA and international guideline-recommended; cetirizine 20–40 mg daily; fexofenadine 360 mg daily; evidence supports benefit; safe
- Note: Up-dosing antihistamines is recommended before adding other agents

Sedating antihistamines (short-term only):
- Promethazine, diphenhydramine: NOT recommended for regular use in CSU; anticholinergic side effects; sedation; cognitive impairment; use only for acute severe itch at night

**Step-up therapy for refractory CSU (specialist territory):**
- Omalizumab (Xolair): anti-IgE monoclonal antibody; 300 mg SC every 4 weeks; highly effective for CSU; PBS-listed for CSU refractory to high-dose antihistamines; specialist (immunologist/dermatologist) initiation
- Short courses of prednisolone (3–7 days): for acute severe flares of CSU; avoid long-term systemic steroids
- Ciclosporin: immunosuppressive; specialist; for severe refractory CSU unresponsive to omalizumab

**Anaphylaxis management (in practice):**
- Adrenaline (epinephrine) autoinjector: prescribe if risk of severe reaction; EpiPen 0.3 mg or EpiPen Jr 0.15 mg
- ASCIA anaphylaxis action plan: complete for all patients at risk — anaphylaxis.net.au
- Patient education: carry autoinjector at all times; train use; wear MedicAlert

**Acute urticaria management:**
- Mild (no airway involvement): oral non-sedating antihistamine; identify and avoid trigger; reassure natural resolution
- Moderate: antihistamine ± short prednisolone course; review in 24–48 hours
- Severe (throat/tongue/voice): 000; adrenaline if progressing to anaphylaxis

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Cetirizine 10 mg** (PBS unrestricted: allergic conditions; once daily; mild sedation possible; standard first-line antihistamine)
- **Fexofenadine 180 mg** (PBS restricted: urticaria — allergic conditions; non-sedating; preferred for drivers; once daily)
- **Loratadine 10 mg** (PBS unrestricted: allergic conditions; once daily; OTC; well-tolerated)
- **Prednisolone** (PBS unrestricted: short course 25–50 mg × 3–7 days for acute severe urticaria flare; avoid prolonged courses)
- **Omalizumab (Xolair) 300 mg SC** (PBS restricted: CSU — must fail adequate trial of high-dose antihistamines ≥3 months; specialist (immunologist/dermatologist) initiation; 300 mg SC every 4 weeks)
- **EpiPen (adrenaline 0.3 mg autoinjector)** (PBS restricted: anaphylaxis — prescribe to all patients with severe allergic reactions or risk of anaphylaxis; patient must carry at all times)
- **Note:** Up-dosing antihistamines to 2–4× standard dose is recommended international practice before escalating to omalizumab; document trial of up-dosed antihistamines in patient record

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000:**
- Anaphylaxis: urticaria + airway compromise, hypotension, vomiting, or cardiovascular collapse — adrenaline immediately; 000
- Laryngeal angioedema: rapidly progressive tongue/throat swelling — 000; airway emergency

**Urgent allergy/immunology:**
- Post-anaphylaxis: all patients after anaphylaxis — allergy workup, EpiPen prescription, action plan; ASCIA referral guidelines
- Hereditary angioedema (suspected): recurrent angioedema without urticaria, unresponsive to antihistamines; C1 esterase inhibitor level and function

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Immunologist or dermatologist: CSU refractory to high-dose antihistamines (3 months); omalizumab initiation
- Rheumatology: suspected urticarial vasculitis (persistent lesions, SLE features)

## Telehealth Suitability

Chronic urticaria monitoring is suitable for telehealth; acute presentations with throat symptoms require urgent in-person care.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Chronic urticaria review: UAS7 scoring, medication adjustment
- Antihistamine dose escalation counselling and prescribing
- Trigger avoidance counselling
- Omalizumab response monitoring
- Referral coordination

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Any suspected anaphylaxis or throat/voice symptoms — urgent in-person or ED
- ACE inhibitor angioedema assessment
- Initial skin biopsy for urticarial vasculitis
- Skin prick testing (allergy specialist)
