## HIV Monitoring Assessment (RACGP)

HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) infection is now a manageable chronic condition due to modern antiretroviral therapy (ART). Australia has approximately 29,000 people living with HIV, with approximately 90% diagnosed. With adherent ART, people living with HIV (PLHIV) can achieve an undetectable viral load (UVL), which prevents transmission (U=U: Undetectable = Untransmittable) and allows near-normal life expectancy. GPs play an increasingly important role in HIV care — testing, linkage to care, shared management of stable patients on ART, and management of HIV-related comorbidities and side effects. Specialist HIV/sexual health physicians (s100 prescribers) initiate and manage ART; the GP provides primary care in partnership. Pre-exposure prophylaxis (PrEP) is now PBS-funded and managed by GPs and sexual health clinicians. HIV is a notifiable condition in all Australian states and territories. Telehealth is suitable for stable HIV monitoring visits.

## Clinical Presentation

**Primary HIV infection (acute seroconversion — 2–4 weeks post-exposure):**
- Flu-like illness: fever, lymphadenopathy, pharyngitis, rash (maculopapular), myalgia, arthralgia
- Oral ulcers, genital ulcers
- Aseptic meningitis (less common)
- Window period: HIV antibody test may be negative; HIV RNA (viral load) PCR positive within days of infection
- 4th-generation combination antigen/antibody test: detects p24 antigen + antibody from ~18 days post-infection; preferred test

**Established HIV infection (without treatment):**
- Asymptomatic phase: can last years; progressive CD4+ T-cell count decline
- HIV-related complications emerge as CD4 drops <200 cells/µL (AIDS-defining threshold)
- AIDS-defining conditions: PCP (Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia), CMV retinitis, MAC (Mycobacterium avium complex), Kaposi's sarcoma, toxoplasma encephalitis, cryptococcal meningitis, oesophageal candidiasis, progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy

**PLHIV on ART — what GPs monitor:**
- Adherence: missed doses lead to drug resistance and viral rebound; single missed dose often not critical but patterns matter
- Viral load: goal undetectable (<50 copies/mL); "low-level viraemia" (50–200) warrants review with HIV specialist
- CD4 count: immune reconstitution; normal >500 cells/µL; routine monitoring less critical once virologically suppressed
- ART side effects: see below
- Comorbidities: cardiovascular disease (HIV is an independent CVD risk factor), renal disease, liver disease, bone loss, malignancy (NHL, anal cancer, cervical cancer)

## Assessment Approach

**HIV testing:**
- HIV combination antigen/antibody test (4th generation): recommended as standard test; Medicare-funded
- HIV RNA PCR: detects infection earlier than antibody tests; use for acute HIV suspicion or post-exposure prophylaxis monitoring
- Offer HIV testing routinely to: all sexually active adults, MSM (men who have sex with men), people who inject drugs, sexual partners of PLHIV, people from high-prevalence countries, any patient with STI presentation
- Testing is confidential; informed consent required; use opt-out approach where possible

**Routine monitoring for PLHIV on ART (ASHM guidelines — typically 3–6 monthly):**

Virological:
- HIV viral load: 3-monthly until confirmed undetectable; 6-monthly once stable; specialist arranges
- Resistance testing: if viral load detectable; specialist

Immunological:
- CD4 count: 6-monthly if CD4 >200; annually if CD4 >500 and suppressed; specialist arranges

Routine GP monitoring:
- Renal function (eGFR, urine ACR): tenofovir-based ART (TDF particularly nephrotoxic — TDF DF/FTC/Bictegravir may be less nephrotoxic than older TAF formulations); 6-monthly
- LFTs: hepatitis B or C coinfection; ART hepatotoxicity; 6-monthly
- Fasting glucose, lipids: HIV and ART increase cardiovascular risk; annual
- FBC: anaemia (ART, CMV, MAC)
- Hepatitis A, B, C serology: if not documented; vaccinate if non-immune (Hep A+B vaccines)
- Syphilis serology: annual in sexually active PLHIV (higher prevalence)
- STI screening: MSM — 3-monthly gonorrhoea/chlamydia (urogenital, pharyngeal, rectal); syphilis
- Cervical screening: PLHIV women — 3-yearly (or annually if previously abnormal) with HPV testing per NCSP
- Anal cancer screening: MSM PLHIV — anal cytology ± HRA (high-resolution anoscopy) where available; specialist
- Bone density (DEXA): PLHIV >50 years or other osteoporosis risk factors; tenofovir reduces bone density
- Cardiovascular risk: D:A:D cardiovascular risk calculator preferred for PLHIV; statin if indicated
- Mental health: depression, anxiety, and stigma are highly prevalent; PHQ-9 at each visit

**ART side effects (common and clinically important):**
- Tenofovir disoproxil fumarate (TDF): nephrotoxicity, osteoporosis, lactic acidosis; most replaced by tenofovir alafenamide (TAF — less nephrotoxic)
- Efavirenz: neuropsychiatric (vivid dreams, depression, dizziness, suicidal ideation); take at bedtime; many switched off due to neuropsychiatric side effects
- Integrase strand transfer inhibitors (INSTI — dolutegravir, bictegravir, cabotegravir): weight gain (significant, especially with TAF); insomnia; mood changes; very high efficacy and barrier to resistance
- Protease inhibitors: dyslipidaemia, GI side effects, boosted with ritonavir or cobicistat (drug interactions)

## Key Differentials

- HIV seroconversion vs viral illness (EBV, CMV, influenza): HIV RNA PCR in acute presentations; 4th gen antigen/antibody test
- AIDS-defining illness vs other cause: requires CD4 count and specialist HIV care context
- Drug interaction mimicking new illness: INSTI + metformin (raises metformin levels); cobicistat-boosted regimens have extensive interactions; check Liverpool HIV Drug Interactions checker (hiv-druginteractions.org)

## Management Principles

**PrEP (Pre-exposure prophylaxis) — GP role:**
- PBS-funded from 2018: tenofovir disoproxil fumarate/emtricitabine (TDF/FTC — Truvada, generic)
- Indication: HIV-negative individuals at substantial risk of HIV acquisition (MSM with condomless sex, heterosexual with HIV-positive partner, people who inject drugs)
- Daily oral PrEP or event-based (2-1-1 dosing for MSM): 2 tablets 2–24 hours before sex, 1 the day after, 1 the day after that
- Pre-PrEP baseline: HIV test (must be HIV-negative), STI screen, HBsAg (TDF active against HBV — stopping PrEP can cause hepatitis B reactivation), eGFR (TDF nephrotoxicity)
- Monitoring on PrEP: 3-monthly HIV test, STI screen, eGFR; 6-monthly hepatitis B serology if non-immune
- If HBsAg positive: PrEP also treats HBV; stopping PrEP can cause HBV flare — do NOT stop without specialist advice

**PEP (Post-exposure prophylaxis) — emergency treatment:**
- Must start within 72 hours of HIV exposure (the sooner the better)
- Refer to ED or sexual health clinic for PEP initiation
- 28-day course of ART (typically TDF/FTC + raltegravir or dolutegravir)
- GP role: arrange urgent same-day referral; provide bridging prescription if experienced in PEP prescribing

**Shared care of stable PLHIV on ART:**
- Routine monitoring (as above) done by GP with HIV specialist involved for ART adjustments
- Medication renewals: most ART is on PBS s100 (through HIV specialist prescriber); GP may provide interim prescriptions
- Drug interactions: CRITICAL — check every new prescription against ART using Liverpool HIV Drug Interactions checker; rifampicin, St John's Wort, antacids, PPIs (affect some ART) are common interactions
- Vaccination: annual influenza, COVID-19 boosters per ATAGI; Pneumovax 23, Prevenar 13 (CD4 <200 → do not give live vaccines); Hep A + B if non-immune; HPV vaccine; zoster (Shingrix — inactivated — safe; live zoster vaccine contraindicated if CD4 <200)

**U=U counselling:**
- Undetectable = Untransmittable: PLHIV with sustained undetectable viral load cannot sexually transmit HIV
- Counsel patients on U=U — reduces stigma, supports relationships, informs risk disclosure decisions
- Not a reason to stop PrEP for HIV-negative partners in other contexts

**Mental health:**
- Depression, anxiety, stigma, disclosure stress highly prevalent in PLHIV
- PHQ-9 at every visit; GP Mental Health Care Plan for psychology
- AFAO (Australian Federation of AIDS Organisations), state-based PLHIV organisations provide peer support

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **TDF/FTC (Truvada, generic)** (PBS s85 PrEP listed: HIV-negative individuals at substantial risk; GP can prescribe PrEP after appropriate baseline tests; GPs must complete ASHM online PrEP prescribing training)
- **ART for HIV treatment**: PBS s100 restricted — only HIV specialist prescribers (s100 authority) can initiate; GP may provide interim supply; do not alter ART dose or combination without specialist guidance
- **Note:** Liverpool HIV Drug Interactions checker (hiv-druginteractions.org) — mandatory reference before any new co-prescription in PLHIV; many common drugs (PPIs, rifampicin, anticonvulsants, statins, antifungals) interact significantly with ART
- ASHM online PrEP prescribing training required before GP initiates PrEP — recommended for all GPs in sexual health-active practice

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Suspected AIDS-defining illness (confusion, new CNS symptoms, severe respiratory failure, progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy) — urgent hospital
- PEP required after HIV exposure: ED or sexual health clinic within 72 hours (sooner = more effective)
- Severe ART toxicity or immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome (IRIS)

**Urgent HIV specialist review:**
- Detectable viral load (>50 copies/mL) in previously suppressed patient — resistance risk; urgent
- CD4 count <200 cells/µL — OI prophylaxis needed (co-trimoxazole for PCP prophylaxis); specialist
- New HIV diagnosis: immediate linkage to HIV specialist (s100 prescriber) for ART initiation
- Suspected new OI

**Non-urgent referral:**
- HIV specialist (s100 prescriber): initial ART, ART changes, comorbidity management
- Sexual health clinic: STI management, PrEP initiation support
- ASHM clinical support (1800 ASHM AU): GP clinical advice on HIV management
- AFAO / state PLHIV organisations: peer support, disclosure support, community resources

## Telehealth Suitability

Stable HIV monitoring is well suited to telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Adherence review and ART side effect monitoring
- Blood result interpretation (viral load, CD4, eGFR, LFTs, lipids)
- PrEP counselling and prescribing renewal
- Drug interaction review
- Mental health screening and counselling
- Vaccination review and catch-up planning
- Referral coordination

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Blood tests — pathology arranged
- STI swab collection (pharyngeal, rectal, urogenital)
- Anal cytology (specialist setting)
- Physical examination if new symptoms
