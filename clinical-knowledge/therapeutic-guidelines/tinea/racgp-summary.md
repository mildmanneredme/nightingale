## Tinea Assessment (RACGP)

Tinea refers to superficial fungal infections of the skin, hair, and nails caused by dermatophyte fungi (Trichophyton, Microsporum, Epidermophyton species). Tinea is among the most common dermatological presentations in Australian general practice — tinea pedis (athlete's foot) affects up to 70% of the population at some point. The condition is highly contagious and spreads via direct contact (person-to-person), fomites (floors, showers, towels), and from animals (ringworm from cats and dogs). Correct diagnosis is essential — tinea can closely mimic eczema and psoriasis, leading to inappropriate use of topical steroids (which temporarily suppress symptoms but cause treatment failure and ringworm spread). GPs diagnose and treat most tinea with topical antifungals; systemic antifungals are required for nail and scalp tinea and extensive skin disease. Telehealth is suitable for straightforward presentations after initial in-person diagnosis.

## Clinical Presentation

**Tinea pedis (athlete's foot — most common):**
- Interdigital: maceration, scaling, fissuring between toes (especially between 4th and 5th toes); often malodorous; pruritic
- Moccasin distribution: diffuse scaling, hyperkeratosis of sole and sides of foot; less pruritic; often bilateral; associated with onychomycosis
- Vesicular: acute; vesicles and bullae on instep; intensely pruritic; often secondary bacterial infection

**Tinea unguium / onychomycosis (nail fungal infection):**
- Toenails much more common than fingernails
- Subungual: distal/lateral nail thickening, yellowing, crumbling, onycholysis (nail lifting from bed)
- Total dystrophic: entire nail plate involvement; advanced disease
- Often asymptomatic but may cause discomfort with footwear; cosmetically distressing

**Tinea corporis (ringworm — body):**
- Annular (ring-shaped) lesion with active scaling, erythematous border and central clearing
- Expanding outward; may have multiple coalescing rings
- Pruritic; any body site; may be from animal contact (cat, dog — Microsporum canis)
- Tinea incognito: ringworm treated with topical steroid — disguised, spreading, no ring shape; easy to miss

**Tinea cruris (jock itch — groin):**
- Pruritic, erythematous, scaling, well-defined border advancing outward
- Inner thighs, inguinal folds, perineum; spares scrotum/vulva (unlike candidiasis)
- Men more than women; associated with obesity, tight clothing, excessive sweating
- Often co-exists with tinea pedis (self-inoculation)

**Tinea capitis (scalp ringworm):**
- Predominantly children; rare in adults
- Scaling, alopecia (patchy hair loss with broken hairs); kerion (boggy, tender, suppurative mass) — severe form
- Requires systemic antifungal (topical insufficient for scalp); school exclusion until treatment started
- Zoophilic (from animals — Microsporum canis) or anthropophilic (human-to-human — Trichophyton tonsurans)

**Tinea versicolor (pityriasis versicolor — technically Malassezia, not a dermatophyte):**
- Hypo or hyperpigmented macules on trunk and upper arms; fine scale; becomes more visible after sun exposure
- Not contagious; not tinea in strict classification but colloquially included
- Treatment: topical ketoconazole shampoo or selenium sulfide; systemic fluconazole for extensive disease

## Assessment Approach

**Clinical diagnosis in most cases:**
- Classic presentation of tinea corporis, tinea pedis, tinea cruris: clinical diagnosis without testing
- Location, morphology, characteristic ring, scaling, and itch pattern

**Skin scraping and microscopy (KOH preparation):**
- When diagnosis uncertain: scrape active border (not centre) of lesion onto slide; KOH dissolves keratin; hyphae visible on microscopy
- Also for: treatment failure, extensive or atypical disease, nail (from subungual debris)
- Skin scraping and culture: sent to laboratory; culture takes 2–4 weeks; identifies species but rarely changes management

**Wood's lamp (UV fluorescence):**
- Microsporum canis (animal ringworm): bright green fluorescence — tinea capitis
- Tinea versicolor: golden-yellow fluorescence
- Does NOT fluoresce: Trichophyton species (most human-to-human tinea)

**Nail assessment:**
- Distinguish from: psoriatic nail changes, trauma, onychogryphosis (deformed thick nail), lichen planus
- Nail clipping + subungual debris: send for culture ± PCR to confirm before starting systemic antifungal
- DLSO score (Dermatophyte Nail Severity Score): guides prognosis for treatment

## Key Differentials

- Eczema (atopic or contact): less well-defined margins; bilateral symmetric distribution; family history atopy; no hyphae on KOH; responds to steroids; tinea incognito can disguise ringworm
- Psoriasis: well-demarcated silvery plaques; extensor surfaces; nail pitting (not thickening); scalp, elbows, knees; no hyphae
- Pityriasis rosea: herald patch + Christmas tree distribution on trunk; self-limiting; no KOH hyphae
- Candidiasis: intertriginous; moist areas; satellite pustules; affects scrotum (unlike tinea cruris); responds to antifungals
- Bacterial intertrigo: erythema, maceration, smell; Gram-negative organisms; responds to antibacterial
- Granuloma annulare: annular but non-scaling; dermal; no pruritus; no KOH hyphae

## Management Principles

**Topical antifungals (first-line for skin tinea):**

Tinea pedis, tinea corporis, tinea cruris:
- Clotrimazole 1% cream (Canesten): BD for 2–4 weeks; widely available OTC; effective
- Miconazole 2% cream (Daktarin): BD for 2–4 weeks; OTC
- Terbinafine 1% cream (Lamisil): once daily for 1–2 weeks (shorter course); fungicidal (not just fungistatic); preferred for quick cure; OTC
- Apply beyond visible lesion margin; continue for 1 week after apparent resolution to prevent recurrence

Tinea versicolor:
- Ketoconazole 2% shampoo (Nizoral): apply to trunk and allow to sit 5 minutes before rinsing; use 3× weekly for 2 weeks; or once weekly for recurrence prevention
- Selenium sulfide 2.5% shampoo: similar application; OTC
- Systemic fluconazole 300–400 mg single dose: for extensive disease; short course effective

**Systemic antifungals (required for nail, scalp, extensive skin, immunocompromised):**

Tinea unguium (onychomycosis):
- Terbinafine 250 mg daily (FIRST-LINE): toenails 12 weeks, fingernails 6 weeks; cure rate 70–80% toenails; PBS-listed for confirmed onychomycosis (skin scraping/culture often required); hepatotoxicity (rare) — LFTs if prolonged course or pre-existing liver disease; CYP2D6 inhibitor (interacts with certain antidepressants, beta-blockers)
- Itraconazole 200 mg daily × 12 weeks OR pulse dosing (400 mg daily × 1 week/month × 3 pulses): alternative to terbinafine; multiple drug interactions (CYP3A4); monitor for cardiac effects; check interactions carefully
- Topical antifungal nail lacquer (amorolfine — Loceryl): adjunct or monotherapy for mild/early disease; 1–2 × weekly for 6–12 months; poor nail penetration; no PBS listing

Tinea capitis:
- Griseofulvin: 10–20 mg/kg/day (children); 6–8 weeks; PBS-listed for tinea capitis in children; older but effective; with fatty food for absorption
- Terbinafine: effective for Trichophyton species (most common in Australia); specialist confirmation of species before use
- Adjunct: antifungal shampoo (ketoconazole, selenium sulfide) to reduce infectivity — advise family and contacts

**Adjunctive measures:**
- Hygiene: keep affected areas dry; change socks daily; moisture-wicking socks; dry between toes; separate towels; no sharing of footwear
- Treat source: treat household contacts; treat affected animals (veterinarian)
- Prevent tinea pedis: thongs in communal showers, pool surrounds; foot powder in sweaty feet
- Footwear: well-ventilated; alternate shoes daily for onychomycosis

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Terbinafine 250 mg oral** (PBS restricted: dermatomycosis — tinea unguium confirmed; 12 weeks for toenails, 6 weeks for fingernails; check for drug interactions especially with antidepressants; LFTs if liver disease risk; may need skin scraping/culture first)
- **Griseofulvin** (PBS restricted: tinea capitis especially in children; 10–20 mg/kg/day; with fatty food; 6–8 weeks; photosensitivity; avoid in pregnancy)
- **Itraconazole (Sporanox)** (PBS restricted: onychomycosis alternative; numerous drug interactions — CYP3A4; negative inotropic effect; avoid in heart failure; check interactions)
- **Topical antifungals (clotrimazole, miconazole, terbinafine cream)**: mostly OTC; PBS-listed topical terbinafine available; most tinea requires only OTC topical treatment
- **Fluconazole oral** (PBS restricted: systemic fungal infections; single dose 150–300 mg for tinea versicolor off-label; weekly 150 mg for tinea capitis alternative)
- **Note:** Always confirm clinical diagnosis before prescribing systemic antifungals — LFT risk, drug interactions, and cost; skin scrapings/nail clippings are simple and confirm diagnosis

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent review:**
- Kerion (severe scalp tinea): boggy, tender, suppurative scalp mass; start systemic antifungal immediately + prednisolone 1 mg/kg/day × 5 days to prevent scarring alopecia; dermatology or GP urgent
- Cellulitis complicating tinea: spreading erythema, warmth, fever — antibiotics (flucloxacillin, cefalexin) + antifungal

**Non-urgent dermatology:**
- Diagnostic uncertainty: biopsy if clinical and KOH inconclusive
- Treatment-resistant onychomycosis after two courses of terbinafine
- Immunocompromised patients with extensive or invasive tinea

## Telehealth Suitability

Tinea management is partially suitable for telehealth for straightforward follow-up.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Review of treatment response after initial in-person diagnosis
- Systemic antifungal initiation discussion (if diagnosis confirmed by scraping)
- Drug interaction screening (terbinafine + patient's medications)
- LFT result review
- Hygiene and prevention counselling

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial assessment: confirm diagnosis by clinical examination ± KOH scraping
- Nail clipping and culture prior to systemic treatment
- Kerion assessment
- Wood's lamp examination
