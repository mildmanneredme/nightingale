## Menstrual Disorders Assessment (RACGP)

Menstrual disorders are among the most common reasons women of reproductive age consult a GP. They encompass heavy menstrual bleeding (HMB), dysmenorrhoea (painful periods), irregular menstruation, and intermenstrual or postcoital bleeding. HMB affects approximately 1 in 5 women and has a significant impact on quality of life, work, and social functioning. Many conditions causing menstrual disorders (e.g., fibroids, endometriosis, PCOS) can be identified and initial management commenced in general practice. Telehealth is moderately suitable — history-taking, investigation interpretation, and medical management can be conducted remotely; pelvic examination and ultrasound require in-person attendance.

## Clinical Presentation

**Heavy menstrual bleeding (HMB):** Excessive blood loss during regular menstrual cycles that interferes with physical, social, or emotional quality of life. Clinically defined as >80 mL per cycle (impractical to measure); assessed by impact on daily life, flooding, clot passage, anaemia symptoms, and pad/tampon use.

**Dysmenorrhoea:** Painful menstruation. Primary dysmenorrhoea: no identifiable cause; prostaglandin-mediated uterine cramping; onset adolescence/early adulthood; cramping lower abdominal pain, often with nausea, diarrhoea, headache; typically improves with age and after pregnancy. Secondary dysmenorrhoea: associated with underlying pathology (endometriosis, fibroids, adenomyosis, pelvic inflammatory disease, IUD); onset later; progressive worsening.

**Irregular menstruation:** Cycle length <21 or >35 days; anovulatory cycles (common in PCOS, perimenopause, thyroid disease, hyperprolactinaemia, hypothalamic amenorrhoea).

**Intermenstrual bleeding (IMB):** Bleeding between regular periods — requires investigation to exclude endometrial, cervical, or ovarian pathology, STIs, or endometrial polyps.

**Postcoital bleeding:** Bleeding after intercourse — investigate for cervical pathology (ectropion, polyp, infection, malignancy) and STIs.

## Assessment Approach

Menstrual history: cycle regularity and length, duration and heaviness of flow, presence of clots, flood soaking, dysmenorrhoea severity and timing, associated symptoms (pelvic pain, bloating, dyspareunia — suggests endometriosis).

Gynaecological history: parity, contraception use (IUD, hormonal), STI history, last cervical screening (Pap/HPV test), prior gynaecological procedures.

Investigations:
- FBC (iron deficiency anaemia from HMB — check ferritin)
- Thyroid function (TSH) — hypothyroidism causes menorrhagia
- Coagulation screen if heavy from menarche onset — consider von Willebrand disease
- Prolactin, LH/FSH if irregular cycles (PCOS, hyperprolactinaemia, menopause)
- STI swabs if IMB or postcoital bleeding (chlamydia, gonorrhoea)
- Cervical screening — up to date?
- Pelvic ultrasound (in-person referral required): fibroids, polyps, adenomyosis, ovarian cysts — essential for structural cause

Endometrial biopsy (specialist): indicated for abnormal uterine bleeding in women ≥45 years, or younger with risk factors (obesity, PCOS, unopposed oestrogen, tamoxifen use) — to exclude endometrial hyperplasia/carcinoma.

## Key Differentials

- Uterine fibroids (leiomyomata) — most common structural cause of HMB; diagnosed on ultrasound; bulk symptoms if large
- Endometriosis — progressive secondary dysmenorrhoea, deep dyspareunia, pelvic pain, infertility; often underdiagnosed; laparoscopy for definitive diagnosis
- Adenomyosis — HMB and dysmenorrhoea with enlarged boggy uterus; diagnosed on pelvic MRI or ultrasound
- Endometrial polyps — IMB, HMB; diagnosed on transvaginal ultrasound or hysteroscopy
- PCOS — irregular cycles, anovulation, hyperandrogenism; ultrasound (polycystic ovaries)
- Hypothyroidism — menorrhagia, weight gain, fatigue, cold intolerance; TSH elevated
- Endometrial cancer — postmenopausal bleeding or abnormal uterine bleeding in women ≥45 years; urgent investigation

## Management Principles

**Heavy menstrual bleeding — medical management (first-line):**

Levonorgestrel-releasing intrauterine system (Mirena 52 mg IUS): most effective medical treatment for HMB — reduces blood loss by up to 97%; also provides contraception; lasts 5–8 years. PBS-listed for HMB. Insertion requires in-person appointment.

Combined oral contraceptive pill (COC): reduces blood loss by 30–50%; benefits: cycle regulation, contraception, dysmenorrhoea relief; easy to initiate in primary care.

Tranexamic acid (antifibrinolytic): reduces blood loss by ~50%; taken only during menstruation; no contraceptive effect; useful for women not wanting hormonal therapy. PBS-listed for HMB.

Norethisterone (progestogen): 5 mg twice to three times daily days 5–26 of cycle; reduces blood loss; less effective than LNG-IUS or tranexamic acid; oral contraceptive doses preferred over cyclic high-dose progestogen.

Mefenamic acid (NSAID): reduces blood loss and dysmenorrhoea; useful if both HMB and dysmenorrhoea are present.

**Dysmenorrhoea — primary:**
NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen, mefenamic acid) are first-line — start before onset of pain; continue for 2–3 days of menstruation. COC pill is effective for both contraception and dysmenorrhoea. Heat (topical heat application to lower abdomen) provides symptomatic relief.

**Dysmenorrhoea — secondary (endometriosis suspicion):**
Empirical hormonal management if endometriosis suspected (progressive dysmenorrhoea, deep dyspareunia): COC, Mirena, or progestogen (medroxyprogesterone) — if not responding to NSAIDs; refer to gynaecology for diagnosis and management.

**Surgical management** (refer to gynaecology): endometrial ablation (for HMB not wanting pregnancy), polypectomy (polyps), myomectomy (fibroids wanting fertility preservation), hysterectomy (definitive for HMB not wanting future pregnancy, or failed medical management).

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Levonorgestrel-releasing IUS** (Mirena 52 mg — PBS restricted: idiopathic HMB; requires insertion by trained clinician)
- **Tranexamic acid** (Cyklokapron — PBS restricted: menorrhagia; 1 g three times daily for up to 4 days during menstruation)
- **Mefenamic acid** (Ponstan — PBS restricted: dysmenorrhoea and menorrhagia; 500 mg three times daily during menstruation)
- **COC pills** (multiple brands — PBS unrestricted for contraception; PBS restricted for dysmenorrhoea/endometriosis under separate listings)
- **Norethisterone** (PBS restricted: menorrhagia; 5 mg twice daily days 14–26 of cycle; note: more commonly used at contraceptive progestogen doses as part of COC)
- **Medroxyprogesterone acetate** (Provera — PBS restricted: endometriosis-associated pain as continuous progestogen)
- **GnRH analogues** (goserelin, leuprorelin — PBS restricted: endometriosis; specialist-initiated; causes temporary menopause)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000:**
- Acute severe pelvic pain with haemodynamic compromise — possible ruptured ectopic pregnancy or haemorrhagic ovarian cyst; emergency

**Urgent in-person GP or ED:**
- Positive pregnancy test with pelvic pain and bleeding — ectopic pregnancy until proven otherwise
- Profuse acute haemorrhage with haemodynamic instability
- Signs of PID (fever, bilateral adnexal tenderness, purulent cervical discharge) — same-day antibiotics required

**Urgent gynaecology referral:**
- Postmenopausal bleeding (any amount) — endometrial cancer until proven otherwise
- Abnormal uterine bleeding in women ≥45 years without clear benign cause
- Suspected endometrial cancer (rapid weight loss, endometrial thickness >4 mm on ultrasound)
- Significant anaemia from HMB not responding to medical therapy

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Gynaecology: structural cause identified (fibroids, polyps, adenomyosis), medical management failure, surgical management consideration, endometriosis management, fertility assessment
- Haematology: suspected coagulation disorder

## Telehealth Suitability

Menstrual disorder assessment and initial management is moderately suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Symptom history and menstrual cycle assessment
- Investigation ordering (FBC, TSH, STI swabs, pelvic ultrasound referral)
- Medical management initiation (COC, tranexamic acid, mefenamic acid, norethisterone)
- Results review and treatment response assessment
- Referral coordination (gynaecology, ultrasound)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Pelvic examination (required before IUS insertion; assessment of pelvic tenderness or mass)
- Cervical screening (speculum examination)
- Pelvic ultrasound (radiology)
- IUS/IUD insertion and removal
