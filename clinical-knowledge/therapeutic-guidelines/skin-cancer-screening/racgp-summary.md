## Skin Cancer Screening Assessment (RACGP)

Australia and New Zealand have the highest rates of skin cancer in the world. Two in three Australians will be diagnosed with skin cancer by age 70. Skin cancer is the most common malignancy in Australia — approximately 980,000 non-melanoma skin cancers (NMSC: basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma) and 16,000 melanomas are diagnosed annually. Melanoma is the most common cancer in Australians aged 15–44. Despite this burden, there is no nationally funded population-wide skin cancer screening program (unlike bowel or cervical cancer). The GP plays a central role in: opportunistic skin cancer detection through whole-body skin examination, risk-stratified surveillance, clinical and dermoscopic diagnosis, early treatment referral, and sun protection education. GPs in Australia frequently perform skin cancer surgery — this is a significant component of Australian GP practice. Telehealth has limited utility for skin cancer assessment but is suitable for follow-up and education.

## Clinical Presentation

**Melanoma (most dangerous — early detection critical):**

ABCDE criteria for clinical assessment:
- Asymmetry: one half does not match the other
- Border: irregular, ragged, notched, or blurred edges
- Colour: variation — multiple colours within one lesion (brown, black, red, white, blue)
- Diameter: >6 mm (though early melanomas can be smaller)
- Evolution: change in size, shape, colour, or new symptom (itching, bleeding)

Additional features:
- "Ugly duckling" sign: a lesion that looks different from the patient's other moles
- Amelanotic melanoma: pink or skin-coloured; no pigment; easily missed; dermoscopy essential
- Nodular melanoma: rapidly growing; raised; may be any colour; most dangerous subtype (delays in diagnosis common)
- Lentigo maligna melanoma: slowly expanding irregular pigmented macule on sun-damaged skin (face, scalp of elderly); in-situ before invasive

**Non-melanoma skin cancer (NMSC):**

Basal cell carcinoma (BCC — most common):
- Pearly/translucent papule or nodule; rolled edge; telangiectasia; central ulceration (rodent ulcer)
- Slowly growing; local invasion; rarely metastasises
- Locations: face (nose, periocular), trunk (especially superficial BCC)
- Histological types: superficial (shave + curettage), nodular (excision), infiltrative (wider margins), morphoeic (scar-like; aggressive)

Squamous cell carcinoma (SCC):
- Keratinised, scaling nodule or plaque; induration; may ulcerate
- Faster growing than BCC; metastatic risk (2–5% overall; higher on lip, ear, immunosuppressed)
- Precursor: actinic keratosis (AK) — rough, scaly patch on sun-damaged skin; field treatment

Merkel cell carcinoma, dermatofibrosarcoma protuberans: rare; urgent dermatology/oncology

**Risk stratification:**
- High risk: personal or family history of melanoma, previous NMSC, ≥50 moles, dysplastic naevus syndrome, immunosuppression (organ transplant, haematological malignancy — SCC risk 65–250× higher), occupational sun exposure, fair skin/red/blonde hair, blue/green eyes, childhood sunburn history
- Population recommendation: Cancer Council recommends all adults have regular skin checks; no specific population-level interval; high-risk individuals every 6–12 months with GP or dermatologist

## Assessment Approach

**Whole-body skin examination:**
- Annual skin check in high-risk patients; opportunistic at any consultation
- Good lighting; systematic head-to-toe examination including scalp, between toes, nails, genitalia
- Dermoscopy: substantially improves diagnostic accuracy over naked eye alone; recommended for all GPs performing skin cancer assessment; RACGP-endorsed dermoscopy training (Skin Cancer College of Australasia courses)
- Document and photograph suspicious lesions for serial monitoring

**Dermoscopy — key features (GP learning tool):**
- Melanocytic vs non-melanocytic: determine first
- Melanoma patterns: atypical pigment network, regression structures, blue-white veil, irregular streaks, atypical vessels
- BCC patterns: leaf-like structures, spoke wheel, arborising vessels
- SCC: white structureless areas, keratin pearls, irregular vessels
- Dermatoscope: polarised (most commonly used); non-polarised with contact fluid

**Biopsy indications:**
- Any suspicious lesion not definitively diagnosed clinically should be excised or biopsied
- Incisional biopsy acceptable for large lesions where excision not feasible; avoid shave biopsy for melanoma suspicion (may result in incomplete specimen)
- Excisional biopsy (2 mm clinical margins for melanoma); histopathology is gold standard

**Follow-up intervals after treatment:**
- Melanoma in situ: annual skin check for life
- Invasive melanoma (Stage I–II): 3–6 monthly GP skin check for 5 years, then annually; specialist-driven surveillance (may include CT/PET scanning based on staging)
- BCC/SCC: 6-monthly skin check for 5 years; high-risk SCC — more frequent with specialist

## Key Differentials

- Seborrhoeic keratosis: "stuck on" appearance; rough surface; waxy; common in middle-aged and elderly; not premalignant; differentiate from melanoma with dermoscopy (milia-like cysts, comedo-like openings)
- Dermatofibroma: firm, dimples when pressed; pigmented; legs; benign
- Haemangioma: red-purple; lacunar vascular structures on dermoscopy; benign
- Blue naevus: uniform blue pigmentation; dermoscopy shows steel-blue homogeneous pattern; benign
- Pyogenic granuloma: rapidly growing red-pink vascular nodule; often post-trauma; bleeds easily; can mimic amelanotic melanoma — excise and histopathology
- Lichen planus-like keratosis: red-brown; solitary; can mimic melanoma; dermoscopy shows regression pattern

## Management Principles

**Actinic keratosis (AK) — field treatment:**
- Single AK: cryotherapy (liquid nitrogen); fluorouracil 5% cream (Efudix) — field treatment; imiquimod 5% (Aldara) — field treatment; ingenol mebutate (Picato — withdrawn 2020 due to skin cancer risk in animal models; no longer recommended)
- Field treatment (multiple AKs on sun-damaged skin): 5-FU cream 5% daily × 4 weeks; or imiquimod 5% 3×/week × 4 weeks; or photodynamic therapy (PDT) — specialist/GP with PDT equipment
- Diclofenac 3% gel: for mild, widespread AKs; slower response
- Annual skin check after field treatment — high recurrence rate

**BCC treatment:**
- Superficial BCC: imiquimod 5% cream (Aldara) — 5×/week × 6 weeks; or 5-FU cream; photodynamic therapy; suitable for low-risk locations
- Nodular/infiltrative BCC: surgical excision (4–6 mm margins); Mohs micrographic surgery for high-risk locations (face, periocular, nose, ears)
- Vismodegib (Erivedge): hedgehog pathway inhibitor; PBS-listed for locally advanced or metastatic BCC; specialist oncology
- Sonidegib: alternative hedgehog inhibitor

**SCC treatment:**
- Surgical excision: 6 mm margins for standard SCC; Mohs for high-risk locations or poorly differentiated
- Sentinel lymph node biopsy: considered for high-risk SCC (>2 cm, poorly differentiated, depth >6 mm, perineural invasion)
- Radiotherapy: elderly patients, large lesions, peri-orbital; or adjuvant post-surgery
- Cemiplimab (Libtayo): PBS-listed for locally advanced or metastatic SCC; immunotherapy; specialist

**Melanoma treatment:**
- Wide local excision: 1 cm margins (melanoma in situ or <1 mm Breslow thickness); 1–2 cm for 1–2 mm; 2 cm for >2 mm
- Sentinel lymph node biopsy: for melanoma >1 mm Breslow thickness; specialist
- Immunotherapy (nivolumab, pembrolizumab) / targeted therapy (dabrafenib + trametinib for BRAF V600 mutation): advanced or high-risk melanoma; specialist oncology
- All melanoma: multidisciplinary team management at specialist centre; GP role in primary care follow-up

**Sun protection — primary prevention:**
- Slip (protective clothing), Slop (SPF 50+ sunscreen), Slap (hat), Seek (shade), Slide (sunglasses)
- Sunscreen: SPF 50+ broad-spectrum; reapply every 2 hours and after swimming
- SunSmart app (Cancer Council): daily UV index; educate patients not to go unprotected when UV ≥3

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Imiquimod 5% cream (Aldara)** (PBS restricted: actinic keratosis, superficial BCC; 3–5 applications/week per indication)
- **Fluorouracil 5% cream (Efudix)** (PBS restricted: actinic keratosis; apply BD until brisk inflammatory reaction; 2–4 weeks field treatment)
- **Diclofenac 3% gel (Solaraze)** (PBS restricted: actinic keratosis; twice daily for 60–90 days)
- **Vismodegib (Erivedge)** (PBS restricted: locally advanced or metastatic BCC; specialist oncology initiation; teratogenic — absolute contraception required)
- **Cemiplimab (Libtayo)** (PBS restricted: locally advanced or metastatic SCC or BCC; specialist oncology)
- **Note:** Skin cancer surgery is a significant component of Australian GP billing; MBS items for skin lesion excision (items 31255–31360 depending on size and complexity); GP credentialling in skin surgery endorsed by RACGP

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent dermatology or plastic surgery:**
- Suspected invasive melanoma: refer to specialist within 2 weeks; do not delay for additional GP assessment after clinical suspicion
- High-risk SCC: lesions >2 cm, perineural invasion suspected, on lip or ear, immunosuppressed patient — dermatology or plastic surgery urgently
- Lesions suspicious for Merkel cell carcinoma: pink-red, rapidly growing, no hair follicles; urgent excision

**Urgent oncology:**
- Known melanoma with new lymph node enlargement, systemic symptoms, or distant metastasis — urgent staging and MDT review
- SCC with lymphadenopathy or suspected distant metastasis

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Dermatologist: diagnostic uncertainty, high-risk BCC/SCC, unusual presentations, complex field treatment
- Skin Cancer College of Australasia (SCCA) training: GP dermoscopy and skin cancer surgery training
- Psychologist: melanoma-related anxiety and distress (GP Mental Health Care Plan MBS 2713)

## Telehealth Suitability

Skin cancer assessment is not suitable for primary diagnosis via telehealth but is suitable for education and follow-up.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Sun protection and skin cancer prevention counselling
- Post-treatment follow-up discussion (after healing, for stable patients)
- Discussing results of histopathology
- Referral coordination
- Patient education about ABCDE criteria and self-examination

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Whole-body skin examination (in-person mandatory)
- Dermoscopy
- Lesion biopsy and excision
- Wound review post-procedure
