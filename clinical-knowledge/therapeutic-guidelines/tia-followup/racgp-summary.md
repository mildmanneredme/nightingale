## TIA Follow-up Assessment (RACGP)

A transient ischaemic attack (TIA) is a temporary episode of neurological dysfunction caused by focal ischaemia without infarction, lasting typically <1 hour and always <24 hours by definition. TIA is a medical emergency and a strong predictor of subsequent stroke — 10–15% of patients will have a stroke within 3 months, with the highest risk in the first 48 hours. The old concept of TIA as a "mini-stroke" requiring only delayed outpatient review is obsolete: TIA requires immediate investigation and urgent secondary prevention. The GP plays a critical role in: recognising TIA symptoms, triaging urgently (ideally to a specialist stroke service or TIA clinic), and ongoing long-term secondary stroke prevention. Telehealth is suitable for stable secondary prevention monitoring.

## Clinical Presentation

**TIA symptoms (focal neurological deficits — sudden onset, brief, resolving):**
- Unilateral limb weakness or numbness
- Facial droop (unilateral)
- Speech disturbance: dysphasia (language), dysarthria (articulation)
- Monocular visual loss (amaurosis fugax): curtain coming down — carotid or ophthalmic artery
- Homonymous hemianopia (visual field loss — same side both eyes)
- Diplopia + ataxia or vertigo (posterior circulation/vertebrobasilar TIA)
- Sudden, transient, focal — all features resolve completely (no permanent deficit)

**What is NOT a TIA:**
- Syncope or loss of consciousness (NOT focal)
- Limb shaking/jerking — not a TIA feature
- Confusion without other focal features
- Bilateral symptoms simultaneously (consider cardiac, metabolic, or migraine)
- Headache alone
- Dizziness or vertigo in isolation (low specificity for TIA without other focal features)

**ABCD2 score (TIA stroke risk stratification):**
- Age ≥60 (1), Blood pressure ≥140/90 (1), Clinical features: unilateral weakness (2) or speech-only (1), Duration: ≥60 min (2) or 10–59 min (1), Diabetes (1)
- Score ≥4 = high risk (48-hour stroke risk ~4%); score 0–3 = low-moderate risk
- NOTE: ABCD2 score has limitations — even low scores require urgent evaluation; DWI-MRI detects infarction in up to 40% of clinical TIA; do not use ABCD2 to defer urgent assessment

## Assessment Approach

**Immediate acute assessment (GP role — recognise and refer urgently):**
- If symptoms are ongoing when patient presents: treat as acute stroke — 000 immediately; do not delay for GP assessment
- If symptoms have fully resolved: urgent same-day assessment (ideally within 24 hours — Stroke Foundation recommendation)
- ABCD2 score: use to communicate risk; does NOT allow deferral beyond 24 hours

**Investigations initiated in ED or TIA clinic (urgent):**
- CT brain: exclude haemorrhage, tumour; cannot reliably detect early infarction
- MRI DWI (diffusion-weighted imaging): most sensitive for acute infarction; detects ~40% of clinical TIAs as true infarcts
- CT angiography (CTA) or MRA head/neck: carotid and vertebrobasilar stenosis assessment; carotid endarterectomy is time-critical if ≥50% stenosis
- 12-lead ECG: AF (AF accounts for ~20% of stroke/TIA — anticoagulation indication); other arrhythmias
- Holter monitor or prolonged cardiac monitoring: paroxysmal AF detection (14–30 days monitoring if ECG normal)
- Echocardiogram: cardiac embolism source (thrombus, PFO, valvular disease)
- Fasting glucose, HbA1c, lipids, FBC, coagulation: risk factor profiling and thrombophilia if young patient

**Secondary prevention GP monitoring:**
- BP monitoring: target <130/80 for most TIA/minor stroke patients
- Fasting glucose/HbA1c: diabetes management
- Lipid profile: LDL-C <1.8 mmol/L target post-TIA/stroke (high-intensity statin)
- Antiplatelet therapy review: adherence, side effects
- Anticoagulation (if AF): INR or DOAC adherence; renal function monitoring
- Carotid imaging follow-up: post-endarterectomy ultrasound (specialist-arranged)
- Driving: AUSTROADS regulations — cease driving immediately after TIA; 2–4 weeks before resuming (private vehicle); commercial vehicle operators — specialist clearance required; document advice

## Key Differentials

- Stroke: focal neurological deficit persisting >24 hours (or with imaging evidence of infarction); 000 immediately
- Migraine with aura: visual aura (scintillating scotoma spreading over 20–30 minutes), followed by headache; positive visual symptoms (lights, zigzags); no motor deficit typically; younger age
- Todd's paralysis: post-seizure focal weakness; history of seizure; resolves over minutes to hours
- Hypoglycaemia: focal neurological features; BGL; resolves with glucose — check BGL in any neurological emergency
- Hemiplegic migraine: motor aura; rare; family history; exclude stroke first
- Vestibular neuritis: vertigo; no other focal features; HINTS examination to distinguish central from peripheral vertigo
- Multiple sclerosis: younger patient; relapsing-remitting episodes; MRI periventricular plaques; neurology
- Peripheral nerve lesion: sensory or motor in a dermatomal/peripheral nerve distribution; not a TIA pattern

## Management Principles

**Secondary stroke prevention (GP long-term role after specialist assessment):**

Antiplatelet therapy (non-cardioembolic TIA):
- Dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT): aspirin 100 mg + clopidogrel 75 mg for first 21 days post-TIA (POINT/CHANCE trial evidence); then clopidogrel 75 mg long-term (superior to aspirin for secondary prevention in TIA/stroke)
- Aspirin monotherapy: 100 mg daily if clopidogrel contraindicated or DAPT not tolerated
- Dipyridamole modified-release + aspirin (Asasantin SR): alternative long-term option; GI side effects and headache common
- Do NOT use antiplatelet AND anticoagulant together (unless specific cardiac indication after specialist advice)

Anticoagulation (cardioembolic TIA — AF or cardiac embolism):
- Atrial fibrillation: DOAC (apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran) superior to warfarin for most AF patients; see `atrial-fibrillation` and `anticoagulation-management` entries
- Timing after TIA: early anticoagulation (within 48 hours) generally safe for TIA and minor stroke; specialist guidance for moderate/large infarcts
- Mechanical heart valve: warfarin (DOACs not safe in mechanical valves)

Blood pressure control:
- Target <130/80 mmHg post-TIA/stroke (Stroke Foundation recommendation); ACE inhibitor + thiazide combination reduces recurrent stroke by ~30%
- Perindopril + indapamide (PROGRESS trial evidence): first-line combination for BP in stroke/TIA secondary prevention

Lipid lowering:
- High-intensity statin: rosuvastatin 20–40 mg or atorvastatin 40–80 mg; target LDL <1.8 mmol/L; reduces recurrent stroke significantly
- If already on statin — maximise dose; check adherence

Carotid artery stenosis (if ≥50% symptomatic stenosis detected):
- Urgent carotid endarterectomy (CEA): ideally within 48–72 hours of TIA for symptomatic ≥70% stenosis — time-critical intervention; maximum benefit within 2 weeks; arrange urgent vascular surgery referral

Diabetes management:
- Optimise glycaemic control: HbA1c target <7% generally; avoid hypoglycaemia (impairs cerebral perfusion)

Lifestyle:
- Smoking cessation: most important modifiable risk factor; see `smoking-cessation` entry
- Alcohol: reduce to low-risk levels (≤10 drinks/week NHMRC 2020)
- Physical activity: ≥150 min/week moderate-intensity

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Aspirin 100 mg** (PBS unrestricted: TIA/stroke secondary prevention; combined antiplatelet first 21 days with clopidogrel)
- **Clopidogrel (Plavix, generic)** (PBS restricted: TIA and ischaemic stroke secondary prevention; authority required; 75 mg daily)
- **Dipyridamole MR + aspirin (Asasantin SR)** (PBS restricted: TIA/stroke prevention; twice daily; headache common initially — analgesics, resolves)
- **Perindopril + indapamide (Coversyl Plus)** (PBS restricted: hypertension; combination particularly evidence-based for post-stroke/TIA BP control)
- **Rosuvastatin 20–40 mg / Atorvastatin 40–80 mg** (PBS restricted: dyslipidaemia; target LDL <1.8 mmol/L; high-intensity statin mandatory post-TIA)
- **Apixaban / Rivaroxaban** (PBS restricted: AF with stroke risk; see anticoagulation-management entry)
- **Note:** DAPT (dual antiplatelet) is evidence-based for first 21 days post-TIA; thereafter clopidogrel monotherapy preferred

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000:**
- Any focal neurological deficit still present on GP presentation — treat as stroke emergency; thrombolysis time-window
- TIA with AF — high embolic risk; urgent same-day specialist review for anticoagulation initiation
- Suspected carotid dissection (neck pain + TIA in young patient) — MRA/CTA urgently

**Urgent same-day neurology or stroke clinic:**
- ALL patients with resolved TIA: ideally assessed within 24 hours (Stroke Foundation guideline)
- ABCD2 ≥4 or crescendo TIA (multiple episodes in 24 hours): highest priority — near-term stroke risk very high
- Symptomatic carotid stenosis detected — urgent vascular surgery referral for endarterectomy

**Non-urgent secondary prevention management (GP):**
- Long-term antiplatelet/anticoagulation monitoring
- Driving clearance reassessment (AUSTROADS standards)
- Lifestyle risk factor management
- Neurological rehabilitation if any residual deficit — physiotherapy, speech therapy, occupational therapy

## Telehealth Suitability

TIA secondary prevention monitoring is well suited to telehealth for stable patients.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Secondary prevention medication review and adherence monitoring
- Blood result interpretation (HbA1c, lipids, renal function, INR/DOAC)
- Blood pressure monitoring discussion (home BP monitoring encouraged)
- Lifestyle counselling (smoking, alcohol, exercise)
- Driving restriction documentation and review
- Referral coordination

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Acute TIA assessment — urgent in-person or ED
- Neurological examination
- Blood tests, ECG, carotid imaging (referral)
- Post-carotid endarterectomy wound review
