## Osteoporosis Assessment (RACGP)

Osteoporosis is a metabolic bone disease characterised by reduced bone mass and deterioration of bone microarchitecture, resulting in increased fracture risk. It affects approximately 1 in 3 women and 1 in 5 men over age 50 in Australia. Osteoporotic fractures — particularly hip, vertebral, and distal radius fractures — are a major cause of morbidity, disability, and death in older Australians. Despite effective treatments, osteoporosis is significantly undertreated in Australia. The GP plays a central role in fracture risk assessment, pharmacotherapy initiation, and preventive care. Telehealth is well-suited for osteoporosis management and monitoring.

## Clinical Presentation

Osteoporosis is typically asymptomatic until a fragility fracture occurs. Recognition depends on identifying at-risk patients and proactive screening.

**Fragility fractures (osteoporotic fractures):** Define clinical osteoporosis — fracture occurring from minimal trauma (fall from standing height or less) or spontaneously. Common sites:
- Vertebral compression fractures: acute back pain ± height loss; many are silent (discovered incidentally on imaging); kyphosis ("dowager's hump")
- Hip fracture: most serious — 20% mortality at 1 year; >50% lose independence
- Distal radius (Colles') fracture: fall on outstretched hand
- Proximal humerus, pelvis, ribs

**Risk factors for osteoporosis and fracture:**
- Non-modifiable: older age, female sex, previous fragility fracture, family history of hip fracture, Asian or Caucasian ethnicity
- Medical conditions: premature menopause (<45 years), prolonged amenorrhoea, male hypogonadism, corticosteroid use (>5 mg prednisolone/day for >3 months), rheumatoid arthritis, chronic kidney disease, coeliac disease, inflammatory bowel disease, type 1 diabetes, hyperthyroidism, hyperparathyroidism
- Lifestyle: smoking, excessive alcohol, low BMI (<19), sedentary lifestyle, low calcium intake, vitamin D deficiency
- Medications: long-term corticosteroids, aromatase inhibitors, androgen deprivation therapy (ADT), antiepileptics, PPIs (possibly modest effect)

## Assessment Approach

**Absolute fracture risk assessment — FRAX® and Garvan Fracture Risk Calculator:**
- FRAX® (WHO Fracture Risk Assessment Tool — frax.shef.ac.uk): calculates 10-year probability of major osteoporotic fracture and hip fracture; incorporates clinical risk factors ± BMD; Australian FRAX model available
- Garvan Fracture Risk Calculator (garvan.org.au): Australian-developed; incorporates falls history (not included in FRAX); considered more clinically useful in Australian context
- Treatment recommended if 10-year fracture probability exceeds threshold (varies by tool and risk; typically: major osteoporotic fracture ≥20%, hip fracture ≥3% on FRAX; or Garvan ≥20% for major fracture)

**Bone Mineral Density (BMD) — DEXA scan:**
- T-score (SD compared to young adult mean): normal ≥-1.0; osteopenia -1.0 to -2.5; osteoporosis ≤-2.5
- WHO criteria: osteoporosis diagnosed if T-score ≤-2.5 at lumbar spine or femoral neck (in postmenopausal women and men ≥50)
- DEXA indications: women ≥65 years (routine), postmenopausal women <65 with risk factors, men ≥70, any fragility fracture, corticosteroid use ≥3 months, hypogonadism, secondary osteoporosis causes

**Laboratory investigations (to exclude secondary osteoporosis):**
- FBC (anaemia — malignancy, myeloma)
- UEC, creatinine (CKD)
- LFTs (liver disease)
- Calcium, phosphate (primary hyperparathyroidism — elevated calcium)
- Vitamin D (25-OH vitamin D): see `vitamin-d-deficiency` entry
- TSH (hyperthyroidism)
- Testosterone (male hypogonadism)
- Serum electrophoresis (myeloma in elderly)
- 24-hour urine calcium (hypercalciuria in secondary osteoporosis)

## Key Differentials

- Osteomalacia: vitamin D deficiency with impaired bone mineralisation; bone pain; elevated ALP; low vitamin D; treat with vitamin D
- Multiple myeloma: older patient with bone pain, elevated ESR/CRP, anaemia; protein electrophoresis; urgent haematology
- Malignancy (bone metastases): known cancer history; localised bone pain; imaging
- Hyperparathyroidism: elevated calcium + PTH; renal stones; bone loss; endocrinology referral
- Paget's disease of bone: elevated ALP; asymmetric bone involvement; diagnosed on bone scan or X-ray

## Management Principles

**Non-pharmacological (all patients):**
- Calcium: 1000–1200 mg/day from dietary sources + supplements; dairy, leafy greens, fortified foods preferred over supplements (supplements alone may modestly increase cardiovascular risk when not dietary-deficient)
- Vitamin D: 800–2000 IU/day (supplement if insufficient); see `vitamin-d-deficiency` entry
- Weight-bearing exercise: resistance and balance training; reduces falls and fracture risk; physiotherapist or exercise physiologist referral
- Smoking cessation; alcohol reduction to <2 standard drinks/day
- Falls prevention: see `falls-prevention` entry; multifactorial assessment; remove home hazards; vision correction

**Pharmacotherapy (significantly reduces fracture risk):**

Bisphosphonates (first-line):
- Alendronate 70 mg weekly (oral): reduces vertebral fracture by 50%, hip fracture by 40%; must be taken fasting with a full glass of water; remain upright for 30 minutes after (oesophageal irritation risk); avoid in severe CKD (eGFR <35); rare: osteonecrosis of jaw (ONJ), atypical femoral fracture (with very long-term use)
- Risedronate 35 mg weekly or 150 mg monthly: similar efficacy; slightly less oesophageal irritation
- Zoledronic acid (Aclasta) 5 mg IV annually: intravenous; excellent for patients unable to take oral bisphosphonates; administered in infusion clinic; PBS-restricted

Denosumab (Prolia):
- 60 mg SC every 6 months; given by GP or nurse; potent anti-resorptive (RANK-L inhibitor); reduces vertebral fracture by 68%, hip fracture by 40%; safe in CKD (no renal clearance); risk of rebound bone loss if stopped abruptly (must transition to bisphosphonate on cessation); rare ONJ and atypical fracture; PBS-restricted

Hormone therapy:
- MHT in postmenopausal women: reduces bone loss; appropriate first-line option in early postmenopause with vasomotor symptoms (dual benefit); see `menopause` entry
- Testosterone in hypogonadal men: maintains bone density

Teriparatide / romosozumab (specialist-initiated):
- Anabolic agents for severe osteoporosis or failure of antiresorptive therapy
- Teriparatide (Forteo): PTH analogue; 18-month course; specialist referral; PBS-restricted for very high-risk patients
- Romosozumab (Evenity): sclerostin inhibitor; specialist only

**Bisphosphonate holiday:**
- After 5 years oral bisphosphonate or 3 years IV zoledronic acid: reassess fracture risk; low-risk patients can take a drug holiday; high-risk patients continue; discuss with specialist

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Alendronate** (Fosamax, generic — PBS restricted: confirmed osteoporosis [T-score ≤-2.5] or clinical diagnosis after fragility fracture; 70 mg once weekly; authority required)
- **Risedronate** (Actonel, generic — PBS restricted: as per alendronate; 35 mg weekly or 150 mg monthly)
- **Zoledronic acid** (Aclasta — PBS restricted: osteoporosis; 5 mg IV annually; infusion setting; requires authority)
- **Denosumab** (Prolia — PBS restricted: osteoporosis; 60 mg SC every 6 months; GP can administer; authority required)
- **Calcium supplements** (PBS unrestricted — various; 600 mg elemental calcium twice daily if dietary intake insufficient)
- **Vitamin D** (cholecalciferol — PBS unrestricted; 1000–2000 IU daily maintenance)
- **Note:** Treatment should be continued for ≥3–5 years (bisphosphonates) or ongoing (denosumab — do not stop without bisphosphonate transition); PBS requires authority for most agents

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Suspected hip fracture (fall with hip pain, unable to weight-bear, externally rotated leg)
- Vertebral fracture with neurological compromise (bowel/bladder dysfunction, bilateral leg weakness — surgical emergency)

**Urgent assessment:**
- Acute vertebral compression fracture — pain assessment, analgesia, spinal imaging; orthopaedics or geriatrics as appropriate
- Suspected secondary osteoporosis cause — hypercalcaemia (primary hyperparathyroidism), myeloma, malignancy

**Non-urgent specialist referral:**
- Endocrinologist: secondary osteoporosis, complex cases, anabolic therapy (teriparatide, romosozumab)
- Rheumatologist: inflammatory arthritis with osteoporosis, complex management
- Falls clinic (geriatrician/physiotherapist): recurrent falls with osteoporosis; multidisciplinary falls prevention

## Telehealth Suitability

Osteoporosis management is well suited to telehealth for monitoring and prescribing.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- FRAX/Garvan risk assessment using patient-reported clinical data
- DEXA result interpretation and treatment initiation
- Bisphosphonate and denosumab prescribing (patient arranges SC denosumab at GP rooms separately)
- Calcium and vitamin D supplementation review
- Lifestyle advice (exercise, diet, falls prevention)
- Blood test result review (vitamin D, calcium)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- DEXA scan — radiology referral
- Denosumab SC injection — GP clinic administration
- Physical examination for secondary causes
- Falls assessment (gait, balance, vision)
