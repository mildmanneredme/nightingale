## Polypharmacy Review Assessment (RACGP)

Polypharmacy — typically defined as concurrent use of ≥5 medications — affects approximately 40% of Australians aged ≥65 years and up to 80% of aged care residents. Problematic polypharmacy occurs when the potential harms of medication combinations outweigh benefits, increasing the risk of: adverse drug reactions (ADRs), drug-drug interactions, falls, hospitalisation, cognitive impairment, reduced quality of life, and non-adherence. Approximately 11% of hospital admissions in older Australians are medication-related. GPs are best placed to undertake structured medication review using STOPP/START criteria, reduce potentially inappropriate medications (PIMs), and manage the deprescribing process collaboratively with patients and carers. Polypharmacy review is a high-value primary care intervention. The goal is NOT to minimise medications per se, but to ensure each medication has a clear indication, the benefit outweighs harm, and the patient's goals and preferences are central. Telehealth is suitable for medication review consultations.

## Clinical Presentation

**Clinical indicators prompting polypharmacy review:**
- ≥5 medications: automatic trigger for structured review
- Frailty or functional decline: see `frailty` entry; medications are often both driver and consequence
- Falls (recurrent or severe): many PIMs increase fall risk (sedatives, antihypertensives, anticholinergics)
- Cognitive impairment or delirium: anticholinergic medications and sedatives impair cognition
- Adverse drug reactions (ADRs): any suspected ADR warrants medication review
- Hospitalisation: medication reconciliation on discharge; admission may be medication-related
- Non-adherence: patient struggling to manage complex regimen
- End-of-life or shift in goals of care: preventive medications may no longer align with goals
- Renal or hepatic impairment: dose adjustment and medication review required
- New symptom: may be ADR rather than new disease — avoid prescribing cascade
- Recent specialist visit adding new medications: duplication or interaction risk

**Prescribing cascade:**
- Symptom perceived as new disease → new medication prescribed → side effect of new medication causes further symptom → further new medication prescribed
- Common examples: NSAIDs → hypertension → CCB → ankle oedema → diuretic; antipsychotic → EPS → anticholinergic
- Prevent by: identifying medication causes of new symptoms before prescribing

**High-risk medication classes in older patients (STOPP Criteria):**
- Sedatives and hypnotics: benzodiazepines, Z-drugs (zopiclone, zolpidem), sedating antihistamines (promethazine) — fall and cognitive risk
- Anticholinergics: oxybutynin, tolterodine, tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline, nortriptyline), first-generation antihistamines, antipsychotics with anticholinergic activity — cognitive impairment, urinary retention, constipation, fall risk; anticholinergic burden score
- Antihypertensives: excessive lowering of BP in older frail patients — orthostatic hypotension, falls; diastolic <60 mmHg
- NSAIDs: GI bleeding (especially without PPI); renal impairment; fluid retention; worsening heart failure
- Sulfonylureas (glibenclamide especially): prolonged hypoglycaemia in elderly; prefer shorter-acting agents
- Opioids: constipation; sedation; falls; confusion; respiratory depression
- Digoxin >125 µg/day: toxicity in elderly with renal impairment
- PPIs: often continued indefinitely without indication; long-term risks (fracture, hypomagnesaemia, C. difficile, B12 deficiency)
- Antipsychotics in dementia: increased stroke and mortality risk; beware prescribing for non-psychiatric symptoms

## Assessment Approach

**Structured medication review (STOPP/START framework):**

Step 1 — Create accurate medication list:
- Include ALL medications: prescription, OTC, vitamins, herbal; phone pharmacy for dispensed record; check My Health Record
- Confirm doses, frequency, indication, duration, and prescribing physician

Step 2 — STOPP criteria (Screening Tool of Older Persons' Prescriptions):
- Identify potentially inappropriate medications (PIMs) to stop or reduce
- Review 114 criteria in STOPP v3 (2023); key categories: cardiovascular, renal, CNS, falls risk, anticholinergic, pain, endocrine
- For each PIM: assess whether benefit-risk balance justifies continuation for this specific patient

Step 3 — START criteria (Screening Tool to Alert to Right Treatment):
- Identify potentially missing indicated medications (omissions of care)
- Common START omissions: ACE inhibitor post-MI; statin for established CVD; anticoagulation in AF with CHA₂DS₂-VASc ≥2 (checking bleeding risk too); B12 replacement; calcium/vitamin D in osteoporosis

Step 4 — Patient and carer discussion:
- Present medication review findings in plain language
- Explain rationale for deprescribing (not removing medicines without reason)
- Elicit patient priorities, concerns, and preferences
- Agree on a deprescribing plan

Step 5 — Implementation and monitoring:
- Deprescribe one medication at a time when feasible (easier to attribute any withdrawal effects)
- Monitor for withdrawal symptoms (benzodiazepines — taper; opioids — taper; antidepressants — taper; antihypertensives — monitor BP)
- Follow-up 2–4 weeks after each change

**Anticholinergic burden assessment:**
- Anticholinergic Cognitive Burden (ACB) scale: rates each medication 0–3 for anticholinergic potential; total score ≥3 associated with cognitive impairment and falls
- Target: reduce total ACB score; minimise anticholinergic medications
- ACB calculator: anticholinergicscale.com.au (Australian version)

**Medication reconciliation (hospitalisation/care transition):**
- Highest medication error risk at care transitions: hospital admission, discharge, residential aged care entry
- RACGP: medication reconciliation is a patient safety priority at every care transition
- Refer to NIMC (National Inpatient Medication Chart) for in-hospital reconciliation

## Key Differentials

- New disease vs ADR: new symptom may represent ADR or drug interaction — always review medication list before initiating new treatment
- Under-prescribing: START criteria identify medications that should be prescribed but are omitted; older patients are often under-treated for CVD risk, osteoporosis, depression
- Therapeutic duplication: two drugs from same class; two proton pump inhibitors; two antihypertensives from same class — identify and eliminate

## Management Principles

**Deprescribing principles:**
- Collaborative: patient and carer partnership; involve in decision-making; explain rationale; address concerns
- Prioritise: start with medications most likely to cause harm with lowest benefit; use STOPP criteria
- Systematic: one medication at a time when possible (easier to attribute adverse effects to specific drug)
- Gradual tapering: for CNS depressants (benzodiazepines, opioids), antidepressants, antihypertensives
- Set realistic goals: not all medications can be deprescribed; target those with clearest harm-benefit imbalance
- Document clearly: rationale for each deprescribing decision in medical record; communicate to specialists
- Monitor: withdrawal symptoms; clinical outcomes (BP, glucose, mood after changes)

**Specific deprescribing protocols:**

Benzodiazepines and Z-drugs:
- 10–20% dose reduction every 2–4 weeks; slower if long-term use
- Switch to diazepam (longer half-life; easier to taper in small increments) before tapering
- Alternatives: CBT-I for insomnia (most effective long-term); low-dose melatonin; sleep hygiene
- Do NOT abrupt discontinue: seizure and severe withdrawal risk

PPIs:
- Step down to lowest effective dose; trial cessation; on-demand use for symptom breakthrough
- If PPI >8 weeks without ongoing indication: trial step-down; rebound acid hypersecretion expected first 2–4 weeks (warn patient)
- Ongoing indication: GORD, Barrett's, high NSAID + H. pylori, antiplatelet/anticoagulant

Antihypertensives:
- Gradual dose reduction (25% per 2–4 weeks) if BP well-controlled, frail, or new orthostatic hypotension
- Monitor BP closely; home BP monitoring before and after changes; aim for not overtreating
- In frail elderly: systolic >130 in isolation may be acceptable — over-treatment increases falls/stroke risk

Statins:
- In end-of-life or very frail elderly (limited life expectancy): primary prevention statins can be ceased
- Secondary prevention statins: continue unless life expectancy <1 year or patient declines
- Discuss individual risk-benefit with patient

Glucose-lowering agents:
- Elderly type 2 diabetes: HbA1c target can be relaxed to <8–8.5% to reduce hypoglycaemia
- Reduce/cease intensive regimens causing hypoglycaemia: glibenclamide (avoid in elderly), gliclazide dose reduction
- SGLT2 inhibitors: review volume depletion risk in frail elderly

**Aged care medication review (RACGP Residential Medication Management Review — RMMR):**
- Clinical pharmacist review: MBS-funded for residential aged care residents (RMMR); quarterly; GP reviews and accepts/rejects recommendations
- HMR (Home Medicines Review): MBS-funded for community-dwelling patients with complex medication needs; pharmacist visits home; report to GP

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Note:** Polypharmacy review is primarily about deprescribing (reducing medications) not adding new ones
- **Melatonin 2 mg MR (Circadin)** (PBS restricted: insomnia ≥55 years; can replace benzodiazepine/Z-drug for sleep in frail elderly)
- **Vitamin B12 (high-dose oral or cyanocobalamin IM)**: START criterion — ensure B12 adequacy in elderly, especially on metformin
- **Calcium + Vitamin D**: START criterion — all elderly with osteoporosis or fall risk; supplement if diet insufficient
- **ACB calculator**: anticholinergicscale.com.au — use to calculate anticholinergic burden; target reduction

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent:**
- Suspected serious ADR: acute organ failure (renal failure from NSAID/ACE inhibitor combination in dehydrated elderly), bleeding from anticoagulant, severe hypoglycaemia — hospital
- Benzodiazepine withdrawal symptoms (seizure, delirium): do NOT abruptly cease in dependent patients — hospital if withdrawal occurs

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Clinical pharmacist (HMR): complex polypharmacy; Home Medicines Review MBS item
- Geriatrician: frail elderly with complex polypharmacy and falls; comprehensive geriatric assessment
- Residential aged care clinical pharmacist: RMMR for all aged care residents

## Telehealth Suitability

Polypharmacy review is well-suited to telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Medication history review and STOPP/START analysis
- Deprescribing plan development and patient/carer education
- Medication reconciliation discussion after hospitalisation
- Monitoring following deprescribing changes (BP, blood glucose by phone/telehealth)
- HMR and RMMR follow-up discussion

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Physical examination if new symptoms suspected as ADR
- Blood tests for medication monitoring (renal function, electrolytes, glucose)
- BP and weight measurement for antihypertensive and diuretic review
