## Palpitations Assessment (RACGP)

Palpitations — the unpleasant awareness of one's own heartbeat — are a common GP presentation affecting up to 16% of the general population at some point. Most palpitations are benign (ectopic beats, sinus tachycardia, anxiety), but a significant minority represent cardiac arrhythmias requiring investigation and treatment. The key clinical task is risk-stratifying palpitations into those requiring urgent cardiac investigation from those that can be managed expectantly. Structural heart disease significantly increases the risk that palpitations represent a malignant arrhythmia. Telehealth has moderate suitability for palpitations — initial history and risk stratification can be conducted remotely, but ECG is essential for definitive assessment.

## Clinical Presentation

**Characterise the palpitation:**
- Rate: fast, slow, or normal
- Rhythm: regular or irregular
- Onset: abrupt or gradual
- Duration: seconds, minutes, or persistent
- Triggers: exertion, alcohol, caffeine, stress, change of position, meals
- Termination: spontaneous, with Valsalva, abrupt
- Associated symptoms: chest pain, dyspnoea, presyncope, syncope, diaphoresis

**Common causes:**

Benign:
- Ectopic beats (premature atrial contractions — PACs, premature ventricular contractions — PVCs): very common; patients describe a "skipped beat" or "thud"; often triggered by caffeine, stress, alcohol; benign in structurally normal heart
- Sinus tachycardia: anxiety, fever, anaemia, dehydration, thyrotoxicosis, medications, exercise
- Anxiety / panic disorder: highly prevalent cause; associated with other anxiety symptoms

Arrhythmic (require investigation):
- Atrial fibrillation (AF): irregular, variable rate; often episodic; associated with stroke risk; see `atrial-fibrillation` entry
- Supraventricular tachycardia (SVT): paroxysmal, abrupt onset and termination, rapid regular rate (150–250 bpm); may terminate with Valsalva; young patients; often recurrent
- Ventricular tachycardia (VT): wide-complex tachycardia; significant risk in structural heart disease; haemodynamic compromise; emergency
- Wolf-Parkinson-White (WPW): pre-excitation syndrome; delta waves on ECG; risk of rapid AF via accessory pathway; refer to cardiologist
- Bradyarrhythmias: sinus node disease, AV block; palpitations felt as slow or pauses

**High-risk features:**
- Palpitations + syncope or presyncope — VT until proven otherwise
- Palpitations + chest pain or dyspnoea
- Known structural heart disease (cardiomyopathy, valvular disease, prior MI)
- Family history of sudden cardiac death or inherited arrhythmia (long QT syndrome, Brugada)
- Palpitations during exercise (VT risk)

## Assessment Approach

**History (key diagnostic tool):**
- Character of palpitation (rate, rhythm, onset, termination as above)
- Circumstances: at rest vs exertion; triggers; association with meals or positional changes
- Associated symptoms (as above — especially syncope, chest pain, dyspnoea)
- Medical history: cardiac disease, thyroid disease, anaemia
- Medications: sympathomimetics (decongestants, ADHD medications), salbutamol, digoxin, antiarrhythmics, QT-prolonging drugs (antipsychotics, antihistamines, some antibiotics)
- Caffeine and alcohol intake; illicit stimulant use (cocaine, amphetamines)
- Family history: sudden cardiac death, inherited arrhythmia, cardiomyopathy

**Physical examination (in-person):**
- Vital signs: HR, BP, SpO2, temperature (fever — sinus tachycardia)
- Cardiovascular: regular vs irregular rhythm; murmur (valvular disease); signs of heart failure (JVP, peripheral oedema)
- Thyroid examination (hyperthyroidism)
- Signs of anaemia

**Investigations:**
- 12-lead ECG: essential baseline — PR interval (WPW delta wave), QTc (long QT syndrome), Brugada pattern, AF, atrial flutter, ectopics, evidence of prior MI or structural disease; normal resting ECG does not exclude paroxysmal arrhythmia
- Bloods: TFTs (thyrotoxicosis), FBC (anaemia), UEC (electrolytes — hypokalaemia/hypomagnesaemia worsen arrhythmia), glucose, iron studies
- Ambulatory cardiac monitoring (Holter monitor 24–48 hours): first-line arrhythmia capture for frequent palpitations; correlation of symptoms with recorded rhythm is diagnostic
- Event recorder (patch monitor 7–30 days or longer): for infrequent palpitations not captured on Holter; patient activates during symptom
- Echocardiography: if structural heart disease suspected (murmur, family history, exertional palpitations)
- Exercise ECG: if palpitations consistently provoked by exercise

## Key Differentials

- Ectopic beats (PAC, PVC) — very common; benign in structurally normal heart; reassurance and lifestyle modification; if highly symptomatic — beta-blocker
- Atrial fibrillation — irregular palpitations; see `atrial-fibrillation` entry; anticoagulation and rate control
- SVT (AVNRT, AVRT) — paroxysmal, abrupt onset/termination, regular; vagal manoeuvres; EP study and ablation if recurrent
- Ventricular tachycardia — must be excluded in patients with structural heart disease; urgent cardiology
- Anxiety / panic disorder — diagnosis of exclusion after ECG and cardiac workup normal; PHQ-9, GAD-7; CBT
- Hyperthyroidism — sinus tachycardia; check TFTs; treat underlying thyroid disease
- Anaemia — sinus tachycardia; FBC; iron supplements or treat underlying cause
- Drug-induced — caffeine excess, stimulants, salbutamol, decongestants; medication review

## Management Principles

**Ectopic beats (PAC/PVC) in structurally normal heart:**
- Reassurance: explain that ectopic beats are very common and not dangerous in the absence of structural heart disease
- Lifestyle: reduce caffeine, alcohol, stress; regular sleep
- If highly symptomatic: low-dose beta-blocker (metoprolol 25–50 mg daily) — reduces ectopic frequency; well tolerated
- Cardiology referral if PVC burden >10% on Holter (risk of PVC-induced cardiomyopathy), or frequent and highly symptomatic

**SVT:**
- Acute termination: vagal manoeuvres (Valsalva, carotid sinus massage, modified Valsalva — supine with legs elevated); adenosine IV (hospital setting)
- Prevention (if recurrent): beta-blocker or verapamil; radiofrequency ablation is curative — refer to cardiologist/electrophysiologist
- Patient education: Valsalva technique to self-terminate episodes

**Anxiety-related palpitations:**
- Psychoeducation: explain physiological link between anxiety and palpitations
- CBT for anxiety; see `anxiety` entry
- SSRI/SNRI if anxiety disorder confirmed
- Beta-blocker (propranolol 10–40 mg situationally) — for performance or situational anxiety-triggered palpitations

**AF:** see `atrial-fibrillation` entry for full management.

**Exercise-induced or structural heart disease palpitations:** Cardiology referral — risk of malignant arrhythmia; echocardiography; EP study.

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Metoprolol** (PBS restricted — palpitations/arrhythmia; 25–100 mg daily for ectopics or SVT prevention; also first-line for rate control in AF)
- **Atenolol** (PBS restricted — rate control; 25–100 mg daily)
- **Verapamil** (PBS restricted — SVT prevention; 120–360 mg/day; contraindicated with beta-blocker and in WPW)
- **Propranolol** (PBS unrestricted — situational anxiety-related palpitations; 10–40 mg as needed or regular)
- **Note:** QT-prolonging medications should be reviewed in any patient with palpitations and QTc >450 ms on ECG (cisapride withdrawn; metoclopramide, domperidone, some antipsychotics, some antibiotics — check against QT drug list at crediblemeds.org)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Palpitations + syncope — VT or haemodynamically significant arrhythmia; emergency
- Haemodynamic instability with arrhythmia (hypotension, severe chest pain, altered consciousness)
- Sustained rapid irregular palpitations with rate >150 — suspect rapid AF or flutter; emergency cardioversion may be required
- WPW + AF: extremely rapid conduction via accessory pathway; haemodynamic collapse risk; do not use AV-blocking drugs (digoxin, verapamil, adenosine — paradoxically worsen)

**Urgent same-day cardiology / ED referral:**
- Palpitations + presyncope in patient with structural heart disease
- New rapid regular tachycardia (likely SVT) not terminating spontaneously — consider referring to ED for IV adenosine

**Non-urgent cardiology referral:**
- ECG shows delta waves (WPW), prolonged QTc >500 ms, evidence of prior MI
- Recurrent SVT — for EP study and possible ablation
- High PVC burden on Holter (>10% of beats) — cardiomyopathy risk
- Family history of sudden cardiac death or inherited arrhythmia syndrome

## Telehealth Suitability

Palpitations assessment is moderately suited to telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- History and risk stratification (low-risk features, normal ECG on record)
- Lifestyle advice for ectopic beats (caffeine, alcohol reduction)
- Results review (Holter, event recorder, echocardiogram)
- Low-dose beta-blocker prescribing for confirmed ectopics in low-risk patient
- Anxiety management for anxiety-related palpitations

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- 12-lead ECG — essential for all new palpitation presentations
- Vital signs and cardiovascular examination
- Any patient with high-risk features (syncope, exertional, structural heart disease)
- Arrangement and review of Holter or patch monitor
