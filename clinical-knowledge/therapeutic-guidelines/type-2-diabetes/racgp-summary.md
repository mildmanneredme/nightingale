## Type 2 Diabetes Assessment (RACGP)

Type 2 diabetes (T2DM) is one of the most common chronic conditions managed in Australian general practice, affecting approximately 1.3 million Australians. Ongoing management focuses on glycaemic control, cardiovascular risk reduction, and prevention of microvascular and macrovascular complications. Telehealth is well-suited to medication review, results discussion, lifestyle counselling, and monitoring between scheduled in-person reviews.

## Clinical Presentation

Most patients present with established diagnosis seeking ongoing management. New presentations may include: incidental hyperglycaemia on routine bloods, symptoms of osmotic diuresis (polyuria, polydipsia, nocturia), fatigue, blurred vision, or recurrent infections. Many patients with T2DM are asymptomatic at diagnosis. Risk factors include obesity (BMI ≥30), family history, gestational diabetes, polycystic ovary syndrome, and cardiovascular disease.

## Assessment Approach

For ongoing management reviews, assess:
- Most recent HbA1c (target generally <53 mmol/mol / <7% for most adults; individualise for frailty, age, and hypoglycaemia risk)
- Fasting glucose and recent self-monitoring records
- Blood pressure (target <130/80 mmHg for those with CVD risk)
- Lipid profile (LDL target <1.8 mmol/L if high CVD risk)
- Renal function — eGFR and urine albumin-creatinine ratio (ACR) annually
- Weight and BMI trajectory
- Current medication adherence and tolerability
- Hypoglycaemia episodes (frequency, severity, awareness)
- Foot examination status (at least annual in-person)
- Eye review status (retinal photography every 2 years, or annually if any retinopathy)
- Mental health screening — depression is common in T2DM (PHQ-9)
- Lifestyle factors: diet quality, physical activity, smoking status, alcohol use

For telehealth consultations, focus on results interpretation, medication titration based on recent pathology, and psychosocial support.

## Key Differentials

- Type 1 diabetes — consider if young, lean, ketosis, rapid progression, or GAD/IA2 antibodies positive
- Latent Autoimmune Diabetes in Adults (LADA) — T2DM features with autoimmune markers
- Maturity-Onset Diabetes of the Young (MODY) — strong family history, young age, non-obese
- Secondary diabetes — Cushing's syndrome, haemochromatosis, pancreatic disease, glucocorticoid-induced
- Impaired fasting glucose / impaired glucose tolerance — not yet diagnostic for T2DM

## Management Principles

Lifestyle modification (dietary change and physical activity) is first-line for all patients. Pharmacotherapy is initiated when lifestyle measures are insufficient to achieve glycaemic targets.

First-line pharmacotherapy: metformin (if eGFR ≥30 mL/min/1.73m²) — start at 500 mg with meals, titrate to 1–2 g twice daily. Gastrointestinal side effects are common; extended-release formulation may improve tolerability.

For patients with established cardiovascular disease or high CVD risk:
- SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin) have demonstrated cardiovascular and renal benefits — preferred add-on therapy per RACGP guidance
- GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, dulaglutide) provide cardiovascular benefit and weight loss

For patients with heart failure or CKD: SGLT2 inhibitors preferred (cardio-renal protective).

Intensification pathway: metformin → add SGLT2i or GLP-1RA (if CVD risk present) or sulfonylurea (if cost a barrier) or DPP-4 inhibitor → consider insulin if targets not met.

Insulin: usually basal insulin (insulin glargine or degludec) added to oral agents; refer to endocrinologist or diabetes educator for initiation if unfamiliar.

Allied health referral: diabetes educator, dietitian (Medicare CDM items apply for eligible patients), podiatry for foot care.

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Metformin** (PBS unrestricted) — first-line; 500 mg, 850 mg, and 1 g formulations; XR formulation available
- **Empagliflozin** (PBS — restricted benefit: T2DM not adequately controlled on metformin ± sulfonylurea) — also listed for heart failure with reduced ejection fraction
- **Dapagliflozin** (PBS — restricted benefit: T2DM; also listed for CKD and heart failure)
- **Semaglutide** (Ozempic — PBS restricted: T2DM not adequately controlled; oral semaglutide Rybelsus also PBS-listed)
- **Dulaglutide** (PBS restricted)
- **Sitagliptin, saxagliptin** (DPP-4 inhibitors — PBS restricted)
- **Glipizide, glibenclamide, gliclazide MR** (sulfonylureas — PBS unrestricted for T2DM)
- **Insulin glargine, insulin degludec** (PBS restricted — type 2, inadequate control on oral agents)
- Authority prescriptions required for SGLT2 inhibitors and GLP-1 RAs under PBS restricted benefit criteria

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / emergency department:**
- Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) — vomiting, abdominal pain, rapid breathing, ketones on urine/blood testing, altered consciousness
- Hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS) — extreme hyperglycaemia, severe dehydration, altered consciousness
- Severe hypoglycaemia — loss of consciousness, seizure, or inability to self-treat

**Urgent in-person GP review within 24 hours:**
- Symptomatic hypoglycaemia that resolved but patient is on sulfonylurea or insulin
- Blood glucose >25 mmol/L without explanation
- New foot ulcer, wound infection, or rapidly spreading cellulitis on a diabetic foot
- Symptoms suggesting new cardiovascular event (chest pain, dyspnoea, palpitations)

**Non-urgent specialist referral:**
- Endocrinologist: poorly controlled T2DM despite optimised oral therapy, complex insulin regimens, or diagnostic uncertainty
- Ophthalmology: any diabetic retinopathy detected on retinal photography
- Nephrology: eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73m² or rapidly declining, or ACR >30 mg/mmol
- Podiatry: any diabetic foot complication, peripheral neuropathy, or high-risk foot

## Telehealth Suitability

Type 2 diabetes ongoing management is highly suitable for telehealth. Results review, medication adjustment based on recent pathology, lifestyle counselling, and psychosocial support can all be delivered via telehealth.

**Cannot be assessed via telehealth:**
- Annual foot examination (requires physical inspection and monofilament testing)
- Injection site review for insulin users
- Physical examination for new complications (e.g., suspected peripheral vascular disease)

**Best practice for telehealth diabetes reviews:**
- Patient should have recent pathology (HbA1c, eGFR, lipids, ACR) available before the call
- Confirm patient has BP monitor at home if possible
- Ensure in-person annual reviews (foot, eyes, BP, weight) remain on schedule
