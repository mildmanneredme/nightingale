## Vulvodynia Assessment (RACGP)

Vulvodynia is chronic vulval pain (>3 months duration) without an identifiable causative lesion or disorder. It affects approximately 8–15% of women during their lifetime and is significantly underdiagnosed — women often suffer for years before receiving a correct diagnosis. The International Society for the Study of Vulvovaginal Disease (ISSVD) classification: localised (vestibulodynia/provoked vestibulodynia — most common) vs generalised; provoked, spontaneous, or mixed. Provoked vestibulodynia (PVD) is the most common subtype — pain at the vulval vestibule with touch, sexual activity, tampon insertion, or gynaecological examination. Vulvodynia is a diagnosis of exclusion — all identifiable causes of vulval pain (infection, dermatoses, neurological, hormonal) must be excluded first. The pathophysiology is multifactorial: central sensitisation, peripheral sensitisation, pelvic floor hypertonicity, psychological factors. Management is multimodal: pelvic floor physiotherapy, topical treatments, psychosexual therapy, systemic medications for central sensitisation. Telehealth is suitable after initial examination; physical examination is essential at first presentation.

## Clinical Presentation

**Provoked vestibulodynia (PVD — most common):**
- Pain provoked by touch, pressure, or penetration at the vulval vestibule
- Dyspareunia (painful intercourse) is the most common presenting complaint; may prevent all sexual activity
- Pain on tampon insertion, bicycle riding, gynaecological examination
- Localised to vestibule (area around vaginal opening); no spontaneous pain between stimuli
- Erythema of vestibule on examination (variable; may be minimal despite significant pain)
- Cotton swab test (Q-tip test): provoked pain with gentle touch at vestibule — 6 o'clock, 4 o'clock, and 8 o'clock positions most sensitive; compare to labia majora (non-sensitive site); positive test confirms localised provoked pain

**Generalised vulvodynia:**
- Diffuse, constant or spontaneous burning pain across the vulva; not provoked by touch alone
- May also be provoked (mixed type)
- Less common than PVD; older women more common; may be related to central sensitisation
- Neuropathic quality: burning, stinging, rawness, irritation, aching
- Described as "like sitting on hot coals" or "skin feeling sunburned"

**Psychological and sexual impact:**
- Significant depression, anxiety, relationship distress, and fear of sexual activity
- Sexual avoidance; vaginismus (conditioned pelvic floor response to anticipated pain)
- Partner relationship strain; reduced quality of life
- Shame and embarrassment; reluctance to disclose to healthcare providers

**Associated conditions:**
- Pelvic floor hypertonicity (hypertonic pelvic floor): levator ani muscles overactive; contributes to vestibulodynia and dyspareunia; pelvic physiotherapy essential
- Interstitial cystitis / bladder pain syndrome: pelvic pain conditions frequently co-exist
- Fibromyalgia, IBS: central sensitisation overlap
- History of childhood sexual abuse, sexual trauma: not causative but increases vulnerability

## Assessment Approach

**Diagnosis of exclusion — first exclude identifiable causes:**
- Infections: candida (most common misdiagnosis; vaginal swab; does not respond to antifungals if vulvodynia), bacterial vaginosis, HSV, HPV (warts), Trichomonas
- Dermatoses: lichen sclerosus (white atrophic patches; ecchymosis; figure-8 pattern), lichen planus, lichen simplex chronicus; contact dermatitis (hygiene products, latex, lubricants); psoriasis
- Hormonal: vulvovaginal atrophy (postmenopause, post-partum, COCP-related androgen suppression)
- Neurological: pudendal neuralgia (sitting provokes; relieved by standing; specific nerve distribution); post-herpetic neuralgia (post-herpes zoster)

**Clinical assessment:**
- History: duration; nature of pain (constant, provoked, burning, stinging); relation to sexual activity; prior treatments; gynaecological history; contraception (COCP — low-dose OCP can cause hypo-oestrogenic state and vestibular atrophy); trauma or abuse history
- Vulval examination: systematic inspection under good lighting; magnifying glass useful
  - Labia majora and minora; clitoral hood; vestibule; perineum; perianal area
  - Look for: lichen sclerosus, lichen planus, erosions, ulcers, warts, discolouration, atrophy
  - Vestibule: degree of erythema; note location
- Cotton swab (Q-tip) test: apply gentle pressure to labia majora (baseline), then systematically to vestibule at multiple clock positions; pain score 0–10; confirms localised vestibulodynia
- Speculum examination: vaginal walls; discharge
- Bimanual examination: uterine/adnexal tenderness (pelvic pathology); pelvic floor tone assessment (levator ani tenderness = hypertonic pelvic floor)

**Investigations:**
- Vaginal swab: MCS + candida culture (even if prior antifungal treatment) + STI testing (chlamydia, gonorrhoea NAAT if relevant)
- pH strip: BV (pH >4.5); trichomoniasis (pH >4.5)
- Punch biopsy: if white patch (lichen sclerosus), erosion, raised lesion, or uncertain diagnosis — gynaecology
- Hormonal profile: if COCP-related suspected (Free testosterone, SHBG, oestradiol) — specialist

## Key Differentials

- Recurrent vulvovaginal candidiasis: vaginal swab essential; confirm fungal; trial antifungal only if confirmed
- Lichen sclerosus: white atrophic plaques; figure-8; biopsy; clobetasol propionate
- Lichen planus: lacy white striae; erosions; oral ulcers; biopsy; immunosuppressive treatment
- Vulvovaginal atrophy (GSM): postmenopausal; vaginal dryness; pallor; responds to topical oestrogen
- Contact dermatitis: hygiene sprays, sanitary products, latex; avoid offending product; topical hydrocortisone
- Pudendal neuralgia: specific nerve distribution; sitting provokes; specialist pain management; nerve block
- Vaginismus (genito-pelvic pain/penetration disorder): pelvic floor hypertonicity; conditioned fear response; pelvic physiotherapy + psychosexual therapy

## Management Principles

**Patient education (critical first step):**
- Validate and explain: vulvodynia is real, has a pathophysiological basis, is not "in their head," and is not caused by poor hygiene or sexually transmitted infection
- Explain: chronic pain cycle and central sensitisation; multimodal treatment required
- Vulval hygiene: avoid soap, bubble bath, feminine hygiene sprays, scented products; use water only; pat dry gently; wear loose cotton underwear

**Pelvic floor physiotherapy (cornerstone of treatment):**
- Most effective single intervention for provoked vestibulodynia; reduces hypertonic pelvic floor
- Referral to pelvic floor physiotherapist with vulvodynia experience: internal examination + biofeedback + progressive desensitisation exercises + dilators
- Aim: retrain levator ani to relax rather than contract with anticipated pain; average 12–16 weeks of treatment

**Topical treatments:**
- Topical anaesthetic (lignocaine 5% ointment): applied to vestibule 20–30 minutes before intercourse; short-term relief; may allow progressive desensitisation; do NOT apply to entire vulva (reduces normal sensation)
- Compounded preparations: topical amitriptyline 2% + baclofen 2% cream (vestibular application); compounding pharmacy; used by specialist centres; reduces peripheral sensitisation
- Topical oestrogen: if COCP-related hypo-oestrogenic atrophy or post-menopausal atrophy contributing; vaginal oestradiol cream or pessary; often underutilised even in women on COCP
- Oral contraceptive pill: if COCP-associated vestibulodynia suspected (low-dose OCP + levonorgestrel), trial cessation of OCP and transition to non-hormonal contraception ± topical oestrogen; improvement may take 6 months

**Systemic pharmacological treatment:**
- Amitriptyline 10–75 mg nocte: tricyclic antidepressant; central sensitisation and neuropathic pain; start 10 mg nocte, titrate slowly; effective in many patients; PBS-listed (depression indication; used off-label for pain)
- Duloxetine 30–60 mg daily: SNRI; off-label for vulvodynia; neuropathic pain mechanism; second-line
- Gabapentin 300 mg TDS or pregabalin 75–150 mg BD: neuropathic pain adjunct; sedation and dizziness; specialist initiation for generalised vulvodynia; dependence risk with pregabalin

**Psychosexual therapy:**
- CBT for vulvodynia: addresses catastrophising, avoidance behaviours, fear of pain; evidence-based
- Sex therapy: couple-focused; sexual communication; graduated exposure programme; Relate Australia
- Mindfulness-based therapies: pain acceptance; sensate focus

**Specialist referral (gynaecology or vulval disease clinic):**
- Vestibulectomy: surgical removal of vestibular tissue; considered only after ≥12 months of failed conservative management; effective in carefully selected patients with PVD (60–90% success rates)
- Botulinum toxin injection: pelvic floor hypertonicity refractory to physiotherapy; gynaecology

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Amitriptyline 10 mg nocte** (PBS restricted: depression; off-label for vulvodynia/central sensitisation; start low; counsel about anticholinergic side effects, dizziness, dry mouth; gradual dose increase over weeks; review at 6–8 weeks)
- **Lignocaine 5% ointment** (OTC/PBS: topical anaesthetic; apply 20–30 minutes pre-intercourse; amount: pea-sized; applied externally to vestibule only; do NOT use inside vagina as routine — reduces pleasurable sensation)
- **Vaginal oestradiol cream (Ovestin 0.1%)** (PBS restricted: vulvovaginal atrophy; apply nightly × 2 weeks then twice weekly; safe in COCP-related vestibulodynia; minimal systemic absorption)
- **Note:** Multiple treatment courses over 6–12 months are often required. Reassure patients that improvement is possible but requires patience and multidisciplinary engagement (physiotherapy, psychology, medication). Early multimodal treatment is associated with better outcomes.

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent:**
- Suspected vulval dermatosis (lichen sclerosus, erosive lichen planus): gynaecology for biopsy and specialist management; risk of squamous cell carcinoma in long-term untreated lichen sclerosus
- Vulval malignancy (pigmented lesion, non-healing ulcer, raised lesion): urgent biopsy; gynaecology or dermatology

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Gynaecology / vulval disease clinic: for diagnosis confirmation, biopsy, vestibulectomy consideration
- Pelvic floor physiotherapy: ALL patients with vulvodynia (first-line treatment)
- Psychosexual therapy: when sexual dysfunction, relationship impact, or anxiety features predominate

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Follow-up after in-person diagnosis and examination
- Systemic medication initiation (amitriptyline, duloxetine)
- Pelvic floor physiotherapy referral and education
- Psychoeducation and vulval hygiene advice
- OCP cessation counselling for COCP-associated vestibulodynia

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial presentation: vulval inspection, Q-tip test, vaginal swab; physical diagnosis is essential
- Any suspicious vulval lesion: in-person examination ± biopsy
- Pelvic floor assessment (levator ani tone)
