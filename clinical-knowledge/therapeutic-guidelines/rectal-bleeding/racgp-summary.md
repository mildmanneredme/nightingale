## Rectal Bleeding Assessment (RACGP)

Rectal bleeding is a common presentation in GP practice, occurring in approximately 15–20% of adults each year, with the vast majority attributable to benign anorectal causes (haemorrhoids, anal fissure, perianal skin conditions). However, rectal bleeding is also the cardinal symptom of colorectal cancer (CRC), the second most common cause of cancer death in Australia. The GP's primary role is to stratify risk, identify red flag features requiring urgent investigation, and ensure appropriate referral or surveillance. Key principle: haemorrhoids are the most common cause, but rectal bleeding cannot be attributed to haemorrhoids without first excluding more proximal pathology. National Bowel Cancer Screening Programme (NBCSP): immunochemical faecal occult blood test (iFOBT) offered 2-yearly from age 50; a positive iFOBT mandates colonoscopy regardless of other symptoms. Telehealth has limited utility for initial assessment.

## Clinical Presentation

**Haemorrhoids (most common — internal and external):**
- Bright red blood on toilet paper, on surface of stool, or dripping into bowl
- Blood separate from stool; painless bleeding (internal haemorrhoids); pain with thrombosed external haemorrhoid
- Associated pruritus ani, soiling, prolapse
- Exacerbated by constipation, straining, pregnancy, low-fibre diet

**Anal fissure:**
- Bright red blood on wiping; severe sharp pain during and after defecation; spasm of internal anal sphincter
- Posterior midline tear; sentinel skin tag; associated constipation
- Chronic fissure: fibrotic edges; hypertrophied anal papilla

**Colorectal cancer (RED FLAG):**
- Change in bowel habit: looser or more frequent stools for >4 weeks (especially left-sided colon/rectal cancer)
- Dark red or mixed blood in stool (not just on paper)
- Tenesmus (sensation of incomplete evacuation)
- Weight loss; fatigue; iron deficiency anaemia (right-sided CRC — occult bleeding)
- Age >50; family history; Lynch syndrome; IBD
- Positive iFOBT regardless of symptom pattern

**Diverticular bleeding:**
- Painless, large-volume, bright red or maroon rectal bleeding; older patients; usually stops spontaneously
- No pain (distinguishes from diverticulitis); haemodynamic compromise possible
- History of diverticular disease; colonoscopy required

**Inflammatory bowel disease:**
- Blood mixed with mucus and stool; chronic or episodic; abdominal pain; weight loss; young adults
- Ulcerative colitis: bloody diarrhoea; continuous colitis from rectum
- Crohn's disease: perianal disease; fistulae; skip lesions

**Angiodysplasia / vascular ectasia:**
- Older patients; occult or recurrent bleeding; anticoagulants common; colonoscopy or CT angiography

**Upper GI bleed with rectal passage:**
- Melaena: black, tarry, foul-smelling stool = upper GI bleed (oesophagus, stomach, duodenum)
- Haematemesis ± melaena; haemodynamic compromise possible; 000

## Assessment Approach

**History (risk stratification):**
- Character of bleeding: colour (bright red vs dark red/maroon vs black melaena), amount, relation to stool
- Bowel habit change: onset, duration, character (looser, more frequent, narrow)
- Symptoms suggesting malignancy: weight loss, fatigue, anorexia, tenesmus, change in stool calibre
- Anorectal symptoms: pain (fissure), prolapse, pruritus (haemorrhoids)
- Risk factors: age >50; personal/family history CRC or polyps; IBD; Lynch syndrome; prior CRC
- Medications: anticoagulants, antiplatelet agents, NSAIDs (increase bleeding risk and obscure aetiology)
- NBCSP participation: iFOBT result if done

**Examination:**
- Vital signs: haemodynamic compromise (tachycardia, hypotension) = significant bleed → 000
- Abdominal examination: mass, organomegaly, ascites; left iliac fossa tenderness
- Digital rectal examination (DRE): MANDATORY — palpable rectal mass; anal fissure; blood on glove; sphincter tone; haemorrhoids
- Anoscopy (if trained and equipment available): confirms internal haemorrhoids; anal fissure
- Proctoscopy: limited view of distal rectum and anal canal

**Investigations:**
- FBC: anaemia (iron deficiency = chronic/occult blood loss)
- Iron studies: ferritin, serum iron, TSAT — iron deficiency anaemia mandates colonoscopy even without overt bleeding
- iFOBT: if not recently done; positive result = urgent colonoscopy regardless of other findings
- LFTs: liver disease (portal hypertension and oesophageal varices if melaena)
- Coagulation (INR): if on anticoagulants or suspected coagulopathy
- Colonoscopy: definitive investigation for rectal bleeding with any red flag features; at 6–8 weeks after acute diverticulitis

## Key Differentials

- Haemorrhoids: bright red, surface only, painless (internal) or painful (thrombosed external), no bowel habit change
- Anal fissure: pain on defecation, bright red on paper, posterior midline
- CRC: mixed blood in stool, bowel habit change, age >50, weight loss — colonoscopy urgently
- Diverticular disease: painless large volume bright red, older patient, haemodynamic compromise possible
- IBD: blood mixed with mucus, younger age, abdominal pain, weight loss
- Melaena (upper GI bleed): black tarry stool, haematemesis, haemodynamic compromise — 000
- Proctitis (infectious, radiation, IBD): anorectal pain + bleeding + discharge; proctoscopy

## Management Principles

**Haemorrhoids — conservative management (first-line):**
- High-fibre diet (25–30 g/day); adequate hydration (2 L/day); stool softeners (psyllium husk, lactulose)
- Avoid straining; correct constipation; avoid prolonged sitting on toilet
- Topical preparations: Proctosedyl cream/ointment (hydrocortisone + framycetin + cinchocaine); short-term symptom relief only; no evidence for resolution of haemorrhoids
- Sitz baths (warm water) × 10–15 minutes after defecation: soothes, reduces oedema

**Haemorrhoids — procedural (refer to surgeon or gastroenterologist):**
- Rubber band ligation (RBL): most effective office procedure for grade II–III internal haemorrhoids; banding at base of haemorrhoid; causes sloughing over 7–10 days
- Sclerotherapy: phenol injection; less effective than RBL; older technique
- Haemorrhoidectomy: grade III–IV prolapsed haemorrhoids; thrombosed external haemorrhoids not resolved conservatively; surgical referral

**Anal fissure:**
- Stool softeners and dietary fibre: address underlying constipation
- Topical glyceryl trinitrate 0.2% (GTN) ointment: relaxes internal anal sphincter; BD application; headache common; 60–70% healing rate; PBS-listed
- Diltiazem 2% ointment: fewer headache side effects; comparable efficacy; compounding pharmacy
- Botulinum toxin injection: specialist procedure; 70–80% healing; recurrence after 6 months
- Lateral internal sphincterotomy: surgical; reserved for chronic fissure failing medical management

**Colorectal cancer — urgent referral pathway:**
- Urgent (within 2 weeks): any red flag features → urgent colonoscopy referral; contact GP/hospital colonoscopy service
- Urgent GP referral or public hospital outpatient: positive iFOBT; iron deficiency anaemia without explanation; change in bowel habit >4 weeks + age >50
- CRITICAL: document clearly in referral that CRC is suspected to trigger urgent pathway; 'routine' referral delays diagnosis

**Diverticular bleeding:**
- Haemodynamically stable: colonoscopy within 48 hours after bowel prep
- Haemodynamically unstable: 000; IV resuscitation; urgent colonoscopy or CT angiography + IR embolisation; surgery if uncontrolled

**Anticoagulant management:**
- Do NOT routinely cease anticoagulants for minor rectal bleeding — thromboembolism risk
- Assess: CHADS₂-VASc, prosthetic heart valve — risk of cessation vs. ongoing bleeding
- Discuss with haematologist/cardiologist if therapeutic INR + active significant bleeding

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Glyceryl trinitrate 0.2% ointment (Rectogesic)** (PBS restricted: anal fissure; apply BD to anal margin; headache side effect; not absorbed systemically if correctly applied; first-line for chronic/recurrent fissure)
- **Psyllium husk (Metamucil)** (PBS unrestricted: stool bulking; essential for haemorrhoids and fissure management; take with large glass of water)
- **Lactulose 15 mL BD** (PBS restricted: constipation; stool softener; titrate to soft stool)
- **Proctosedyl cream/suppositories** (OTC: symptom relief for haemorrhoids; hydrocortisone component; short-term only; no evidence for resolution)
- **Note:** iFOBT positive result is a colonoscopy trigger regardless of other symptoms or examination findings. Document clearly in referrals if CRC is suspected to activate urgent colorectal cancer pathways.

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / hospital:**
- Haemodynamic compromise: tachycardia, hypotension, pallor from acute bleeding — IV access; urgent colonoscopy or surgery
- Melaena (black tarry stool) ± haematemesis: upper GI bleed; endoscopy urgently
- Peritonism with rectal bleeding: perforation, strangulated bowel; 000

**Urgent referral (within 2 weeks):**
- ANY red flag features: positive iFOBT, change in bowel habit >4 weeks, iron deficiency anaemia, rectal mass on DRE, blood mixed in stool + age >50, weight loss + rectal bleeding
- Thrombosed external haemorrhoid for surgical review (conservative if >72 hours after thrombosis)

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Symptomatic haemorrhoids failing conservative management: rubber band ligation; surgical outpatient
- Anal fissure not responding to GTN after 8 weeks: surgical review; botulinum toxin

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Review of iFOBT results and explanation of next steps
- Post-colonoscopy result discussion and surveillance planning
- Haemorrhoid dietary advice and follow-up
- Anal fissure follow-up and medication titration
- IBD medication management after initial in-person assessment

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial assessment: DRE and anoscopy mandatory; haemodynamic assessment
- Any patient with red flag features: examination, blood tests, urgent referral
- Thrombosed external haemorrhoid (urgent surgical assessment)
