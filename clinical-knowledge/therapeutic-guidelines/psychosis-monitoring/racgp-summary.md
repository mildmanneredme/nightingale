## Psychosis Monitoring Assessment (RACGP)

Psychotic disorders — including schizophrenia, schizoaffective disorder, schizophreniform disorder, and drug-induced psychosis — are serious mental health conditions affecting approximately 1% of Australians. GPs frequently provide shared care for patients with established psychotic disorders alongside psychiatry and community mental health teams. The GP's role in psychosis monitoring is critical for: early detection of relapse, management of antipsychotic metabolic side effects (weight gain, dyslipidaemia, hyperglycaemia, tardive dyskinesia, QTc prolongation), medication adherence monitoring, and coordination of physical health care — patients with schizophrenia have a life expectancy 15–20 years shorter than the general population, predominantly from cardiovascular disease. This entry covers ongoing GP monitoring of established psychosis; acute first-episode psychosis or acute relapse requires psychiatric assessment and management. Telehealth is partially suitable for ongoing monitoring; acute psychosis and physical examinations require in-person care.

## Clinical Presentation

**Psychotic symptoms:**
- Positive symptoms: hallucinations (auditory most common — hearing voices; may be 2nd or 3rd person; command hallucinations), delusions (fixed false beliefs — paranoid, grandiose, referential), formal thought disorder (disorganised thinking/speech), disorganised behaviour
- Negative symptoms: blunted affect (reduced emotional expression), alogia (poverty of speech), avolition (reduced motivation), anhedonia (reduced pleasure), social withdrawal — often more disabling long-term than positive symptoms; respond poorly to antipsychotics
- Cognitive symptoms: impaired working memory, executive function, attention — present in majority; drives functional disability

**Relapse warning signs (patient and carer education):**
- Sleep disturbance (early and reliable sign)
- Increased suspiciousness, paranoia
- Social withdrawal
- Unusual beliefs or thoughts
- Neglect of personal hygiene and self-care
- Increasing agitation or irritability
- Stopping medication
- Substance use (especially cannabis, amphetamines — common triggers)

**Antipsychotic metabolic side effects — GP monitoring priority:**
- Weight gain: most common; particularly clozapine and olanzapine; baseline and regular weighing
- Metabolic syndrome: type 2 diabetes (2–4× increased risk vs general population); dyslipidaemia; hypertension
- Tardive dyskinesia (TD): involuntary repetitive movements (orofacial, limbs, trunk); from prolonged antipsychotic use; AIMS (Abnormal Involuntary Movement Scale) assessment 6-monthly
- QTc prolongation: cardiac arrhythmia risk; some antipsychotics (quetiapine, haloperidol, ziprasidone) — annual ECG
- Extrapyramidal side effects (EPS): parkinsonism, akathisia (inner restlessness), dystonia — particularly first-generation antipsychotics
- Hyperprolactinaemia: amisulpride, risperidone, haloperidol; causes sexual dysfunction, menstrual irregularity, gynaecomastia, osteoporosis; check prolactin if symptomatic
- Clozapine-specific: agranulocytosis (mandatory neutrophil monitoring); myocarditis (first 4 weeks); constipation (severe, life-threatening); hypersalivation; seizures

## Assessment Approach

**Regular GP monitoring visits (recommended 3-monthly minimum in stable patients):**

Mental state:
- Symptom status: positive, negative, cognitive symptoms; rating scale (BPRS — Brief Psychiatric Rating Scale or PANSS clinician versions require training; GPS can use simpler clinical assessment)
- Relapse warning signs (WRAP — Wellness Recovery Action Plan)
- Insight and medication adherence
- Substance use: cannabis, amphetamines, alcohol — major relapse triggers; assess and address
- Social functioning: accommodation, employment/activity, relationships, carer situation
- Suicide and self-harm risk: chronic and acute risk; major cause of premature death in schizophrenia
- Carer wellbeing: carer fatigue and burnout — ask about carers

Physical health monitoring:
- Weight and BMI: at EVERY visit; waist circumference annually
- Blood pressure: annually
- Fasting glucose and HbA1c: annually (baseline before starting antipsychotics; then annually)
- Fasting lipids: annually
- ECG: annually or if symptomatic; QTc >500 ms — urgent cardiology review
- AIMS examination (tardive dyskinesia): 6-monthly; observe movements; document

**Investigations (annually or as indicated):**
- FBC: clozapine — mandatory regular neutrophil monitoring (frequency determined by clozapine monitoring requirements and local protocol); other antipsychotics annually
- Fasting glucose/HbA1c: metabolic monitoring
- Fasting lipid profile: CVD risk; statin if indicated
- eGFR: renal function; lithium monitoring if co-prescribed
- LFTs: hepatic baseline; interaction with metabolised drugs
- Prolactin: if sexual dysfunction, menstrual irregularity, gynaecomastia, or bone density concern
- Thyroid function: lithium augmentation requires 6-monthly TSH
- ECG: QTc — clozapine, quetiapine, haloperidol, ziprasidone

**Clozapine-specific monitoring (GP must be familiar):**
- Mandatory blood monitoring: neutrophil count required before each dispensing (registered monitoring system — CPMS in Australia)
- Neutrophil thresholds: if ANC <1.5 × 10⁹/L — STOP clozapine; contact psychiatrist immediately
- Constipation: enquire at every visit; aggressive bowel management (macrogol, laxatives); clozapine-induced ileus is life-threatening
- Salivation: nocturnal drooling common; atropine sublingual 1% drops at bedtime (off-label)
- Smoking cessation: smoking INCREASES clozapine metabolism — if patient stops smoking, clozapine levels rise significantly; alert psychiatrist; dose adjustment needed

## Key Differentials

- Acute psychosis vs relapse vs first episode: distinguish for management pathway; first episode always requires psychiatry
- Drug-induced psychosis: cannabis, amphetamines, LSD, methamphetamine; may closely resemble schizophrenia; substance screen
- Bipolar disorder with psychotic features: mood symptoms prominent; manic or depressive episode with psychosis; lithium/mood stabiliser
- Delirium: acute onset; fluctuating consciousness; medical cause; must exclude in any acute mental state change — especially in elderly
- Dementia with psychotic features: older age; cognitive decline + hallucinations; donepezil + quetiapine (low dose for agitation)
- Medical causes of psychosis: temporal lobe epilepsy, brain tumour, autoimmune encephalitis (anti-NMDA receptor), hypothyroidism, SLE, syphilis — exclude with investigations in first-episode psychosis

## Management Principles

**Antipsychotic medications (psychiatrist-initiated; GP continues and monitors):**

Second-generation antipsychotics (SGA — preferred first-line):
- Olanzapine (Zyprexa): effective; high metabolic side effects (most weight gain); sleepiness; well-tolerated otherwise
- Quetiapine (Seroquel): effective; sedating; QTc; less metabolic than olanzapine; widely used; multiple formulations
- Risperidone (Risperdal): effective; moderate metabolic effects; EPS at higher doses; hyperprolactinaemia
- Paliperidone (Invega): active metabolite of risperidone; monthly depot (Invega Sustenna); well-tolerated
- Aripiprazole (Abilify): partial dopamine agonist; minimal metabolic effects; activating (can worsen agitation initially); monthly depot available
- Amisulpride: effective for negative symptoms; hyperprolactinaemia

First-generation antipsychotics (FGA — still used):
- Haloperidol: effective; high EPS; QTc; depot formulation
- Zuclopenthixol, flupenthixol: depot injections; used in non-adherent patients

Clozapine:
- Treatment-refractory schizophrenia (failed 2 antipsychotics): most effective antipsychotic; psychiatrist-initiated; mandatory monitoring; significant side effect profile; reduces suicide and mortality
- GP role with clozapine: blood monitoring compliance, constipation management, smoking cessation caution

Long-acting injectable antipsychotics (LAI/depots):
- For adherence issues: patient receives injection every 2–4 weeks from GP or nurse
- Common in Australia: paliperidone (Invega Sustenna monthly; Invega Trinza 3-monthly), aripiprazole (Abilify Maintena monthly), zuclopenthixol (Clopixol), flupentixol (Fluanxol)
- GP role: administer depot, observe for post-injection syndrome, document, monitor for side effects

**Physical health interventions:**
- Cardiovascular risk management: statin if Framingham risk ≥10%; antihypertensive; glycaemic management; smoking cessation
- Exercise: evidence for reduced metabolic side effects and symptom improvement; exercise physiologist referral (CDM plan)
- Dietitian referral: weight management (CDM plan)
- Smoking cessation: extremely common (40–60%); varenicline most effective; see `smoking-cessation` entry; clozapine dose adjustment if cessation successful

**Psychosocial rehabilitation:**
- Mental health care plan (MBS 2713): 10 psychology sessions; CBT for psychosis (CBTp); coping skills; relapse prevention
- Community mental health teams: case manager; assertive community treatment (ACT) teams for complex presentations
- NDIS: disability support for moderate-severe functional impairment
- Carer support: SANE Australia carer helpline; carers support groups

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Olanzapine (Zyprexa) 5/10 mg** (PBS restricted: schizophrenia and related — Authority required; psychiatrist initiates; GP continues; 5–20 mg daily; monitor weight and metabolic profile at every visit)
- **Quetiapine (Seroquel) 25/100/200 mg** (PBS restricted: schizophrenia; Authority; 300–800 mg daily; divided doses; QTc monitoring; sedation)
- **Risperidone (Risperdal) 1/2/3 mg** (PBS restricted: schizophrenia; Authority; 4–8 mg daily; hyperprolactinaemia monitoring)
- **Aripiprazole (Abilify) 10/15/30 mg** (PBS restricted: schizophrenia; Authority; 10–30 mg daily; least metabolic burden; activating side effects)
- **Long-acting injectable antipsychotics (Invega Sustenna, Abilify Maintena)** (PBS restricted: Authority; administered by GP/nurse every 1–3 months; reduces relapse and hospitalisation; document administration on AIR or patient record)
- **Clozapine (Clorazil, Denzapine)** (PBS restricted S8: treatment-resistant schizophrenia; psychiatrist-initiates; GP provides ongoing monitoring; clozapine register monitoring MANDATORY; consult psychiatrist before any dose change)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / psychiatric emergency:**
- Acute psychotic relapse with risk to self or others: command hallucinations with plan to act; acute agitation; suicidal crisis — psychiatric emergency team (CATT) or 000
- Clozapine agranulocytosis: ANC <1.5 × 10⁹/L — STOP clozapine; hospitalise; urgent haematology
- Clozapine myocarditis (first 4 weeks): chest pain, fever, tachycardia, elevated troponin — stop clozapine; ED immediately
- Clozapine ileus: severe constipation, abdominal pain, absent bowel sounds — abdominal X-ray; surgical review

**Urgent psychiatric review:**
- Early relapse warning signs with mental state deterioration
- Non-adherence to depot or oral medication
- Significant substance use relapse (cannabis, amphetamines)
- New suicidal ideation
- Major metabolic problem requiring antipsychotic change discussion

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Exercise physiologist: metabolic side effects; CDM plan
- Dietitian: weight management; CDM plan
- Cardiologist: QTc >450 ms, arrhythmia, significant cardiovascular disease
- Endocrinologist: diabetes, dyslipidaemia management; when primary care management insufficient

## Telehealth Suitability

Psychosis monitoring is partially suitable for telehealth in stable patients.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Mental state monitoring (symptoms, adherence, relapse warning signs)
- Blood result review (metabolic monitoring, clozapine ANC discussion)
- Medication review and side effect management
- Carers check-in and support
- Social and functional review

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- AIMS examination (tardive dyskinesia — requires direct observation)
- ECG (QTc monitoring)
- Weight, BP, waist circumference
- Long-acting injectable antipsychotic administration
- Physical examination for acute concerns
