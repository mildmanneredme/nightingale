## Headache and Migraine Assessment (RACGP)

Headache disorders are among the most common neurological conditions encountered in general practice. The majority of headaches seen in GP practice are primary headache disorders — migraine, tension-type headache (TTH), and cluster headache. Secondary headaches (caused by an underlying structural, vascular, or infectious cause) are less common but must be excluded when red flags are present. Migraine is highly prevalent, affecting approximately 1 in 7 Australians, and is a leading cause of disability. Effective telehealth management encompasses diagnosis confirmation, acute treatment optimisation, preventive therapy, and trigger identification.

## Clinical Presentation

**Migraine without aura:** Moderate-to-severe unilateral (or bilateral) throbbing headache lasting 4–72 hours, with associated nausea/vomiting, photophobia, or phonophobia; worsened by routine physical activity. Diagnosis requires ≥5 attacks meeting criteria (IHS ICHD-3).

**Migraine with aura:** As above, preceded by reversible neurological aura symptoms (visual disturbance — scintillating scotoma, zigzag lines, flickering; sensory disturbance; speech; or motor — hemiplegic migraine). Aura typically lasts 20–60 minutes and precedes or accompanies headache.

**Tension-type headache:** Bilateral, pressing/tightening (non-pulsating) mild-to-moderate headache; no nausea/vomiting; no worsening with activity. Episodic (≤15 days/month) or chronic (>15 days/month).

**Chronic migraine:** Headache on ≥15 days/month for >3 months with migraine features on ≥8 days/month. Often associated with medication overuse.

**Medication overuse headache (MOH):** Headache on ≥15 days/month in a patient using acute headache medications on ≥10 days/month (triptans, opioids, ergotamines) or ≥15 days/month (simple analgesics). The headaches become frequent and poorly responsive to treatment.

Menstrual migraine: migraine attacks closely linked to menstrual cycle (onset day -2 to +3 of menstruation); often more severe and longer-lasting.

## Assessment Approach

Diagnose using headache history — no investigations required for typical primary headache in the absence of red flags.

Headache diary: ask patient to record frequency, duration, severity (NRS 0–10), associated symptoms, triggers, and analgesic use for 4–6 weeks. Vital for diagnosing MOH and assessing preventive therapy eligibility.

Assess disability: MIDAS (Migraine Disability Assessment Score) — quantifies days lost to migraine over 3 months; guides treatment intensity.

Identify triggers: menstrual cycle, sleep disruption, stress, dehydration, alcohol (particularly red wine), skipped meals, bright light, strong odours, weather changes, hormonal contraceptives (may worsen or improve migraine).

Exclude MOH: ask specifically about frequency of analgesic use — patients often deny or minimise overuse.

Red flag assessment — SNNOOP10 criteria (see Escalation Triggers): any red flag warrants urgent imaging and/or specialist review.

Examination: neurological examination should be normal in primary headache; if any focal neurology, altered consciousness, or papilloedema — urgent referral.

## Key Differentials

- Tension-type headache — bilateral, non-pulsating, mild-moderate, no nausea; distinguish from migraine by associated features
- Cluster headache — strictly unilateral, periorbital, excruciating pain 15–180 min, autonomic features (lacrimation, ptosis, rhinorrhoea); patient is agitated; occurs in clusters with remission periods
- Medication overuse headache — frequent headaches (≥15 days/month) in heavy analgesic user
- Cervicogenic headache — originates from cervical spine; unilateral, reduced neck ROM, precipitated by neck movement
- Secondary headache causes requiring exclusion: SAH (thunderclap), meningitis (fever, neck stiffness), space-occupying lesion (progressive, positional, early morning, papilloedema), giant cell arteritis (>50 years, tender temporal artery, jaw claudication, elevated ESR/CRP), hypertensive crisis

## Management Principles

**Acute migraine treatment (stratified approach):**

Mild-moderate: paracetamol 1 g or ibuprofen 400–600 mg + metoclopramide 10 mg (as antiemetic and to improve absorption) — take early in attack.

Moderate-severe or inadequate response to simple analgesics: triptans are first-line specific migraine therapy. Take at onset of headache (not aura); may repeat after 2 hours if initial dose partially effective. Up to 2 doses in 24 hours.

Anti-emetics adjunct: metoclopramide or prochlorperazine — essential adjuncts that improve triptan absorption and reduce nausea/vomiting.

Avoid opioids for acute migraine — worsen headache frequency and cause MOH; not recommended by RACGP or IHS guidelines.

**Preventive therapy — indications:** ≥3–4 migraine days/month with significant disability; acute treatments ineffective or contraindicated; MOH; hemiplegic or brainstem migraine.

First-line preventive agents (PBS-listed):
- Propranolol 40–120 mg twice daily — avoid in asthma, depression, bradycardia
- Amitriptyline 10–75 mg nocte — useful if comorbid depression or sleep disturbance; start low
- Valproate (sodium valproate / valproic acid) — effective; AVOID in women of childbearing age (teratogenic; pregnancy prevention program required)
- Topiramate — weight loss side effect advantageous in obese patients; cognitive side effects common; teratogenic

CGRP monoclonal antibodies (erenumab, fremanezumab, galcanezumab): highly effective preventives; PBS-listed under specialist criteria for chronic migraine or episodic migraine failing 2+ preventive agents — refer to neurologist for initiation.

**Medication overuse headache management:** Withdrawal of overused medication is the cornerstone of treatment — requires patient education; gradual withdrawal for opioids/benzodiazepines; abrupt withdrawal may be appropriate for triptans and simple analgesics. Bridge with naproxen or amitriptyline. Headaches typically worsen for 2–4 weeks before improving.

**Non-pharmacological:** Regular sleep schedule, hydration, regular meals, aerobic exercise, stress management, trigger avoidance. Biofeedback and CBT-based approaches have Level A evidence.

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Sumatriptan** (PBS restricted: migraine with moderate-to-severe disability — 50 mg or 100 mg oral, also nasal spray and subcutaneous injection) — first-choice triptan given familiarity and cost
- **Rizatriptan** (PBS restricted: as above) — faster onset; available as wafer (useful with nausea)
- **Zolmitriptan** (PBS restricted) — available as nasal spray
- **Naratriptan, eletriptan, frovatriptan** (PBS restricted) — longer half-life; useful for prolonged attacks
- **Propranolol** (PBS unrestricted) — preventive; 40–120 mg twice daily
- **Amitriptyline** (PBS unrestricted — as antidepressant; used off-label for migraine prevention at sub-antidepressant doses)
- **Sodium valproate / valproic acid** (PBS restricted for epilepsy; used off-label for migraine prevention — authority prescription for migraine indication)
- **Topiramate** (PBS restricted for migraine prevention in adults ≥18 years with ≥4 migraine days/month)
- **Erenumab** (Aimovig — PBS restricted: specialist initiation for chronic migraine or episodic migraine after 2+ preventive failures)
- **Metoclopramide** (PBS restricted — antiemetic adjunct; 10 mg oral with acute migraine treatment)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 — thunderclap or first/worst headache:**
- Sudden onset severe headache reaching maximum intensity within seconds to minutes ("thunderclap headache") — subarachnoid haemorrhage until proven otherwise
- Headache with fever and neck stiffness / photophobia / non-blanching rash — bacterial meningitis / meningococcal disease
- Headache with focal neurological deficits, altered consciousness, or seizure — stroke or intracranial mass
- Headache with papilloedema on fundoscopy — raised intracranial pressure

**Urgent in-person review or neurology referral:**
- Progressive headache worsening over days to weeks — space-occupying lesion until excluded
- Headache consistently worse in the morning or on lying down
- New headache in patient >50 years — exclude GCA (check ESR, CRP; temporal artery tenderness); exclude space-occupying lesion
- Post-traumatic headache with neurological symptoms — head CT required
- Suspected giant cell arteritis (GCA) — commence prednisolone urgently and refer to rheumatology/ophthalmology; visual loss is a feared complication

**Non-urgent neurology referral:**
- Diagnostic uncertainty (atypical features, haemiplegic or basilar migraine)
- Chronic migraine refractory to preventive therapy — consider CGRP monoclonal antibody
- Consideration of botulinum toxin for chronic migraine (specialist-administered)
- Complex MOH not responding to GP-managed withdrawal

## Telehealth Suitability

Headache and migraine management is highly suitable for telehealth in patients with established diagnosis.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Diagnosis in patients with typical history and no red flags
- Acute treatment optimisation (triptan selection, anti-emetic combination)
- Preventive therapy initiation and titration
- Headache diary review and trigger identification
- MOH identification and education; withdrawal planning
- Mental health care plan for psychology referral (CBT for migraine)

**Requires in-person or urgent assessment:**
- Any red flag headache feature (see Escalation Triggers) — urgent in-person ± neuroimaging
- New neurological symptoms or examination findings
- Fundoscopy for papilloedema (suspected raised ICP)
- Botulinum toxin administration (specialist clinic)
