## Tension-Type Headache Assessment (RACGP)

Tension-type headache (TTH) is the most common headache disorder worldwide, affecting approximately 38% of Australians at some point. It is characterised by bilateral, pressing/tightening (non-pulsating) head pain of mild to moderate intensity that does not worsen with routine physical activity. Unlike migraine, TTH lacks nausea/vomiting and photophobia/phonophobia (or at most one of these). TTH is subclassified as episodic (infrequent: <12 episodes/year; frequent: 1–14 days/month) or chronic (≥15 headache days/month for ≥3 months). The GP's role is to: apply ICHD-3 diagnostic criteria to confirm TTH, exclude secondary causes (red flags — SNOOP4 criteria), initiate acute and preventive treatment, identify and address triggers, and manage medication overuse headache (MOH) — a critical complication of overusing analgesics and triptans. Telehealth is suitable for established TTH management.

## Clinical Presentation

**ICHD-3 Diagnostic Criteria for TTH (all of the following):**
- Bilateral location (not unilateral as in most migraines)
- Pressing/tightening quality (not pulsating)
- Mild to moderate intensity (not severe)
- NOT aggravated by routine physical activity (walking, climbing stairs)
- No nausea or vomiting
- May have photophobia OR phonophobia (but NOT both)
- Duration 30 minutes to 7 days

**Clinical patterns:**
- Episodic TTH: infrequent or frequent; discrete headache episodes; headache-free intervals
- Chronic TTH: daily or near-daily headache; often coexistent with medication overuse headache (MOH)
- Pericranial tenderness: manual palpation of temporalis, masseter, trapezius, suboccipital muscles — tenderness correlates with TTH diagnosis; tension and anxiety often contribute

**Triggers:**
- Stress: most common; work or personal stress; anxiety
- Poor posture: prolonged computer use; forward head position; cervicogenic contribution
- Sleep deprivation or oversleeping
- Dehydration; meal skipping
- Eye strain; lighting
- Hormonal fluctuation (menstrual)

**Medication overuse headache (MOH):**
- Occurs when analgesia taken ≥10–15 days/month for ≥3 months
- Triptans: ≥10 days/month; simple analgesics (paracetamol, NSAIDs): ≥15 days/month
- Paradoxically worsens headache frequency; "rebound headache"
- Very common in chronic daily headache — must identify and address before preventive treatment
- Treatment: withdrawal of offending medication; supported by preventive therapy

## Assessment Approach

**SNOOP4 Red Flag Criteria (secondary headache — exclude before diagnosing primary TTH):**
- **S**ystemic symptoms: fever, weight loss, rash, immune compromise (infection, malignancy, giant cell arteritis)
- **N**eurological symptoms: focal weakness, diplopia, dysarthria, vertigo, altered consciousness
- **O**nset sudden (thunderclap): worst headache of life — subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH) until proven otherwise → 000
- **O**lder age new headache: >50 — giant cell arteritis (scalp tenderness, jaw claudication, ESR ↑), malignancy, subdural haematoma
- **P**attern change: frequency increasing; progressive worsening; change in character
- **P**ostural aggravation: worse lying flat (raised ICP) or worse standing (low-pressure headache)
- **P**recipitated by Valsalva: cough, sneeze, strain (Arnold-Chiari, intracranial mass)
- **P**apilloedema or peri-ictal: raised ICP

**Headache diary (diagnostic and therapeutic):**
- Headache Australia headache diary: free download at headacheaustralia.org.au
- Record: date, time, severity (VAS 0–10), location, character, duration, associated features, triggers, analgesic use (drug, dose, frequency), response to treatment
- Essential for: diagnosis, tracking treatment response, identifying MOH, identifying patterns

**Examination:**
- Neurological examination: normal in primary headache; any deficit = RED FLAG → CT/MRI
- Fundoscopy: papilloedema (raised ICP)
- Blood pressure: hypertensive headache (severe hypertension); GCA if temporal artery tenderness
- Pericranial muscle palpation: temporalis, masseter, sternocleidomastoid, trapezius — tenderness supports TTH

**Investigations (only if red flags present):**
- CT head without contrast: acute onset thunderclap headache (SAH); focal neurological signs
- MRI brain with contrast: progressive/new pattern headache; concern for mass or vascular abnormality; negative CT but clinical concern remains
- ESR + CRP: age >50 with new headache — giant cell arteritis
- LP (in hospital): CT negative but SAH still suspected (thunderclap headache within 6–12 hours)

## Key Differentials

- Migraine: unilateral pulsating; moderate-severe; nausea/vomiting; photophobia AND phonophobia; worsens with activity; may have aura; sumatriptan responsive
- Cluster headache: strictly unilateral periorbital/temporal; excruciating; autonomic features (lacrimation, nasal congestion, Horner's); restlessness; male predominance; episodic clusters; oxygen and sumatriptan acute treatment
- Cervicogenic headache: unilateral; cervical trigger point; restricted neck movement; referred from C2/C3; physiotherapy
- Sinusitis headache: facial pressure; purulent nasal discharge; fever; CT sinuses; antibiotic + decongestant
- Giant cell arteritis: age >50; temporal tenderness; jaw claudication; ESR >50; visual loss risk; prednisolone urgently
- SAH (thunderclap): worst headache of life; sudden onset; nausea/vomiting; neck stiffness; CT + LP → 000
- Hypertensive headache: BP >180/120 mmHg; posterior headache; treat hypertension

## Management Principles

**Acute (episodic) treatment:**
- First-line: paracetamol 1 g + ibuprofen 400 mg combined (evidence supports combination > either alone); take early in episode
- Aspirin 900 mg: effective; alternative or adjunct
- Caffeine combinations (paracetamol + caffeine): modest additional benefit; OTC available
- Avoid opioids: not recommended — high risk of MOH and dependence; codeine-containing combination products increase MOH risk significantly
- Limit analgesia: maximum 2 days/week; if using more → MOH developing → require preventive treatment

**Non-pharmacological acute strategies:**
- Rest in quiet, dark room; cold or warm pack to head/neck
- Massage of pericranial muscles (temporalis, trapezius, suboccipital)
- Relaxation techniques: diaphragmatic breathing; guided meditation

**Preventive treatment (indicated for frequent TTH ≥8 headache days/month or significant impairment):**
- Amitriptyline 10–75 mg nocte: most evidence; low-dose tricyclic; start 10–25 mg, titrate slowly; reduce pericranial sensitisation; PBS-listed (depression indication; off-label use for headache prevention)
- Venlafaxine 75–150 mg daily: SNRI; alternative to amitriptyline; fewer anticholinergic side effects; off-label
- Propranolol: less evidence for TTH than for migraine; may be used if comorbid migraine or anxiety
- Mirtazapine 15–30 mg nocte: useful if comorbid sleep disturbance and depression; some evidence for TTH

**Non-pharmacological preventive (equal importance):**
- Physiotherapy: postural correction; cervical manipulation; dry needling of trigger points; evidence comparable to amitriptyline
- Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT): stress management; strong evidence for chronic TTH; headache management CBT programs
- Biofeedback and relaxation training: reduces headache frequency; Headache Australia resources
- Acupuncture: evidence comparable to prophylactic medication for TTH prevention; reasonable referral option
- Sleep hygiene: regular sleep schedule; screen time management
- Exercise: regular aerobic exercise reduces frequency of TTH; 30 minutes most days

**Medication overuse headache (MOH) management:**
- Identify: patient using analgesia >10–15 days/month; headache present on waking; daily or near-daily headache
- Educate: explain that the medication is driving the headache worsening; withdrawal will cause temporary worsening before improvement
- Withdraw: abrupt cessation preferable to gradual (unless opioids — taper opioids slowly)
- Bridge therapy: short course prednisolone 50 mg for 5 days can ease withdrawal headache; limited evidence but widely used
- Preventive medication: start amitriptyline simultaneously with withdrawal
- Paediatric note: codeine and opioid-containing analgesics are particularly harmful; avoid entirely in MOH

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Paracetamol 500–1000 mg** (PBS unrestricted: OTC; first-line; safe; effective; max 4 g/day; caution in hepatic impairment)
- **Ibuprofen 400 mg** (PBS unrestricted: OTC; NSAID; combine with paracetamol for acute TTH; avoid in renal impairment, peptic ulcer, third trimester pregnancy)
- **Amitriptyline 10–25 mg nocte** (PBS restricted: depression indication; used off-label as headache preventive at low doses; anticholinergic side effects; do NOT abruptly cease; ECG if QTc concern)
- **Note:** Codeine-containing analgesics (Panadeine, Nurofen Plus) are available OTC in limited quantities in Australia but carry significant risk of MOH and dependence with regular use. Patients presenting with chronic daily headache should be screened for codeine use.

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / hospital:**
- Thunderclap headache (worst headache of life, sudden onset): SAH until proven otherwise; CT ± LP
- Headache + fever + neck stiffness + photophobia: bacterial meningitis; 000; IV benzylpenicillin
- Headache + focal neurological signs + altered consciousness: intracranial haemorrhage, tumour, abscess
- Acute visual loss + headache + age >50: giant cell arteritis; high-dose prednisolone immediately + urgent ophthalmology

**Urgent referral:**
- Giant cell arteritis suspected (age >50, new headache, jaw claudication, ESR ↑): same-day prednisolone + ESR + CRP; ophthalmology/rheumatology within days
- New or progressive headache not fitting TTH criteria with any red flag feature: CT or MRI urgently

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Neurology: chronic daily headache failing amitriptyline; refractory TTH; diagnostic uncertainty; complex MOH
- Pain psychology/CBT: chronic TTH with significant psychosocial overlay; stress and anxiety
- Physiotherapy: cervicogenic component; postural analysis; trigger point management

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Established episodic TTH review; medication adjustment
- Headache diary review and trigger identification
- MOH counselling and withdrawal planning (if no safety concerns)
- Preventive medication initiation and titration (amitriptyline)
- Psychosocial support and CBT referral
- Chronic TTH follow-up

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- First-presentation headache: neurological examination, fundoscopy, BP
- Any red flag feature: examination + investigations
- Thunderclap or rapidly progressive headache: 000
