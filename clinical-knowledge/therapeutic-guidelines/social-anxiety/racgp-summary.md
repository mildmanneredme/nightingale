## Social Anxiety Disorder Assessment (RACGP)

Social anxiety disorder (SAD; social phobia) is the persistent, excessive fear of one or more social or performance situations where the individual believes they may be scrutinised, negatively evaluated, or embarrassed. It is one of the most common anxiety disorders, affecting approximately 7–13% of Australians at some point in their lives. Despite its high prevalence and significant functional impact (work, education, relationships), it is frequently underdiagnosed and undertreated — patients often rationalise their avoidance as personal preference or shyness. Onset is typically in adolescence (median age 13 years). SAD is distinct from shyness (a personality trait) and from agoraphobia (fear of public spaces). Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) is the most effective treatment; SSRIs are first-line pharmacotherapy. Both combined are superior to either alone. Untreated SAD is associated with significant comorbidity (alcohol use disorder, depression, other anxiety disorders). Telehealth is well-suited to SAD management.

## Clinical Presentation

**Core features:**
- Persistent fear of social or performance situations where scrutiny might occur: public speaking, meeting new people, eating in public, using public toilets, telephone calls, social events
- Fear that anxious behaviour (blushing, trembling, sweating, stumbling over words) will be negatively judged
- Exposure to feared situation produces almost invariably intense anxiety (may meet criteria for situational panic attack)
- Situations are either avoided or endured with intense distress
- Fear is disproportionate to the actual threat from the social situation
- Duration >6 months; significant distress or functional impairment

**Typical presentations in general practice:**
- Performance subtype: limited to performance situations only (public speaking, exams, presentations) — generally better response to beta-blockers
- Generalised SAD: feared situations are numerous; most social interactions; more pervasive impairment; higher comorbidity; requires full treatment approach
- Physical manifestations: blushing (erythrophobia), sweating, trembling, tachycardia, voice quivering — often the feared visible sign; patients may seek dermatology or cardiology rather than GP
- Avoidance behaviours: decline promotions, avoid social gatherings, difficulty asserting at work, digital communication preferred over face-to-face, social isolation

**Comorbidities (common and clinically important):**
- Depression: 50% lifetime comorbidity; develop secondary depression from social isolation and lost opportunities
- Alcohol use disorder: 20% of SAD patients; alcohol used as a social lubricant; misuse risk; screen with AUDIT-C
- Other anxiety disorders: generalised anxiety disorder (GAD), panic disorder, specific phobia
- Autism spectrum disorder: social difficulties from different mechanism; distinguish from SAD

## Assessment Approach

**Screening tools:**
- Social Phobia Inventory (SPIN): 17-item validated questionnaire; score ≥19 suggests social anxiety disorder; good sensitivity and specificity; free to use
- Liebowitz Social Anxiety Scale (LSAS): 24 items; clinician-administered; gold standard for severity; tracks treatment response
- K10 (Kessler Psychological Distress Scale): broader psychological distress; included in GP MBS Mental Health Item assessments
- PHQ-9: screen for comorbid depression
- AUDIT-C: screen for comorbid alcohol use

**Diagnostic criteria (DSM-5):**
- Marked fear/anxiety about social situations with possible scrutiny by others
- Fear of acting in a way or showing anxiety symptoms that will be negatively evaluated
- Social situations almost always provoke fear/anxiety
- Situations avoided or endured with intense anxiety
- Fear/anxiety is out of proportion to actual threat
- Persistent (≥6 months)
- Causes significant distress or functional impairment
- Not better explained by substance, medical condition, or other mental disorder

**Distinguish from:**
- Shyness: below threshold; no significant functional impairment
- GAD: generalised worry across many domains not specifically social evaluation
- Panic disorder: unexpected panic attacks not situationally bound to social contexts
- PTSD: fear of trauma-related situations specifically
- ASD: social difficulties arise from different mechanisms (pragmatic language, difficulty reading social cues) — not fear of negative evaluation

## Key Differentials

- Shyness / introverted personality: below clinical threshold; no functional impairment
- Generalised anxiety disorder (GAD): excessive worry about many life domains; social anxiety present but not primary; see `anxiety`
- Panic disorder: recurrent unexpected panic attacks; may develop social avoidance secondary to agoraphobia
- PTSD: trauma-specific triggers; hypervigilance; see `ptsd`
- Selective mutism: speaking in social situations (primarily children)
- ASD: social communication differences; special interests; sensory sensitivities; AASACT or psychologist assessment
- Avoidant personality disorder: more pervasive and stable; often co-diagnosed with generalised SAD; psychotherapy more complex

## Management Principles

**Psychotherapy (first-line):**
- Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT): gold standard; individual or group format; approximately 12–16 sessions
  - Cognitive restructuring: identify and challenge maladaptive beliefs about social evaluation; probability overestimation; catastrophising
  - Exposure therapy: gradual exposure hierarchy to feared social situations with anxiety habituation; homework essential
  - Video feedback: corrects distorted self-image (patients typically believe they appear worse than they do)
- Group CBT: particularly effective for SAD (multiple feared situations addressed simultaneously; normalising)
- CBT online programs:
  - This Way Up: thiswayup.org.au — RACGP-endorsed; evidence-based; social anxiety course
  - Mindspot Clinic: mindspot.org.au — free; clinician-guided online CBT
  - Beyond Blue resources: beyondblue.org.au
- Mindfulness-based cognitive therapy (MBCT): adjunct or alternative for patients who prefer; addresses rumination

**Pharmacotherapy (first-line with or without CBT):**
- SSRIs (first-line):
  - Escitalopram 10–20 mg daily: best tolerated; preferred; PBS-listed for anxiety
  - Sertraline 50–200 mg daily: well-evidenced; PBS-listed for anxiety
  - Paroxetine 20–60 mg daily: effective but higher side effect profile (discontinuation syndrome, weight gain); PBS-listed
  - Onset: 4–6 weeks for anxiety benefit; maximum effect at 12 weeks; trial minimum 12 weeks before assessing response
- SNRIs (second-line if SSRI ineffective):
  - Venlafaxine XR 75–225 mg daily: PBS-listed for anxiety; effective for SAD
- Duration: minimum 12 months treatment; relapse high if stopped sooner
- Review at 6–12 months: if sustained remission, gradual taper (25% every 4 weeks) while maintaining CBT

**Beta-blockers (performance subtype only):**
- Propranolol 10–40 mg 30–60 minutes before performance situation: reduces sympathetic arousal symptoms (palpitations, trembling, sweating, blushing)
- Effective only for discrete performance anxiety (not generalised SAD); not for chronic daily use
- Not PBS-listed for this indication; private prescription; low cost
- Do NOT use: severe asthma, COPD, bradycardia, peripheral vascular disease

**Benzodiazepines:**
- NOT recommended for SAD: no evidence for long-term benefit; promote avoidance rather than habituation; dependence risk; impair CBT benefit
- Short-term only (isolated acute event if severe and no time for other treatment): use with caution

**GP Mental Health Treatment Plan (MHTP):**
- Enables rebated psychology sessions under Medicare Better Access: 10 sessions/year + additional 10 with GP review
- Develop MHTP in consultation with patient; include CBT referral
- Online programs (This Way Up, Mindspot) accessible without MHTP but MHTP + psychologist is optimal

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Escitalopram 10–20 mg (Lexapro)** (PBS restricted: depression and anxiety; first-line for SAD; start at 5–10 mg; titrate to 20 mg if insufficient response; minimal drug interactions; best tolerated SSRI)
- **Sertraline 50–200 mg (Zoloft)** (PBS restricted: depression, anxiety, OCD, PTSD; start 25–50 mg; titrate slowly; preferred in pregnancy and breast-feeding)
- **Venlafaxine XR 75–225 mg (Efexor XR)** (PBS restricted: depression and anxiety; start 37.5–75 mg; SNRI; increases BP and HR at higher doses; discontinuation syndrome if stopped abruptly)
- **Note:** Pregabalin (Lyrica) has some evidence for SAD but is not PBS-listed for this indication and carries dependence risk in this population. Benzodiazepines are contraindicated as regular therapy for SAD.

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent:**
- Active suicidal ideation: SSRI initiation (especially in young adults <25) requires safety netting and close follow-up at 1–2 weeks; document risk discussion
- Comorbid severe depression or alcohol use disorder: acute psychiatric assessment if severe

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Clinical psychologist (MHTP under Better Access): all patients with SAD; CBT is superior to medication alone
- Psychiatry: severe or refractory SAD; diagnostic uncertainty; significant comorbidities
- Group CBT programs: ACGB; Anxiety Recovery Centre Victoria; Beyond Blue programs

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- All aspects of SAD assessment and management
- Mental Health Treatment Plan development
- SSRI initiation, monitoring, and titration
- Review of CBT progress and homework
- Online program recommendation (This Way Up, Mindspot)
- Long-term follow-up

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial physical examination to exclude physical causes of anxiety symptoms (palpitations, tachycardia — exclude cardiac/thyroid)
- Blood tests if medical differential required (TFTs, ECG)
