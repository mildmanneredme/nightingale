## Smoking Cessation Assessment (RACGP)

Smoking is the leading preventable cause of death and disease in Australia, responsible for approximately 21,000 deaths per year. Despite declining overall prevalence (~12% of Australians smoke), smoking rates remain disproportionately high in Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander communities (~40%), people with mental illness (~37%), and those experiencing socioeconomic disadvantage. Brief intervention by GPs — as little as 3 minutes — significantly increases quit rates. Combining behavioural support with pharmacotherapy achieves the highest success rates. Telehealth is highly suitable for smoking cessation assessment, intervention, and pharmacotherapy prescribing.

## Clinical Presentation

Smoking-related health impacts commonly prompting cessation discussions:
- Respiratory: COPD, recurrent URTI, asthma worsening, lung cancer
- Cardiovascular: hypertension, coronary artery disease, peripheral arterial disease, stroke
- Oncological: lung, laryngeal, oropharyngeal, oesophageal, bladder, cervical, kidney cancer
- Reproductive: pregnancy complications, premature birth, SIDS, subfertility
- Dermatological: premature skin ageing, poor wound healing
- Periodontal disease

**Nicotine dependence assessment:**
- Fagerström Test for Nicotine Dependence (FTND) — most validated; 6 items; highest scores indicate highest dependence; key questions: time to first cigarette after waking (<30 min = high dependence), number of cigarettes per day (>20 = high)
- Heaviness of Smoking Index (HSI) — 2-item brief screen using same key FTND items

**Motivation and stage of change:**
- Precontemplation: not thinking about quitting
- Contemplation: thinking about quitting within 6 months
- Preparation: planning to quit in the next 30 days
- Action: actively quitting
- Maintenance: quit for >6 months

## Assessment Approach

**5As framework (RACGP recommended GP approach):**
1. **Ask:** Ask at every consultation; document smoking status; "Do you currently smoke?"
2. **Assess:** Cigarettes/day, time to first cigarette, previous quit attempts, motivation to quit, barriers, mental health, substance use
3. **Advise:** Clear, personalised advice to quit; brief (30–60 seconds); every consultation
4. **Assist:** Pharmacotherapy + behavioural support; quit plan; Quitline referral (13 QUIT); set a quit date
5. **Arrange:** Follow-up within 1–2 weeks of quit date; ongoing support

**Special populations:**
- Pregnancy: strongest motivator; NRT is safer than continued smoking in pregnancy (evidence supports); avoid varenicline and bupropion in pregnancy
- Mental illness: high dependence; smoking worsens mental health long-term despite perceived benefit; clozapine-level monitoring needed if patient quits (smoking induces clozapine metabolism — levels rise on cessation; dose may need reduction)
- Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples: culturally sensitive approach; Quitline ATSI-specific service; yarning approaches; higher intensity support
- Youth: higher susceptibility to nicotine dependence; motivational interviewing; avoid stigmatising language
- COPD: quitting is the single most important intervention in COPD; slows disease progression

## Key Differentials

- Electronic cigarettes (vaping): increasingly common; not approved by TGA as cessation aid (though some evidence of efficacy); regulated as schedule 4 nicotine products in Australia — nicotine vape products require prescription; advise patients of uncertain long-term safety; preferred over continued smoking if patient is unable to quit otherwise; not recommended as primary cessation tool for never-smokers or youth
- Social smoking and low-level smoking: may still require pharmacotherapy depending on dependence level
- Cigar, pipe, or smokeless tobacco: significant nicotine and cancer risk; cessation advice applies

## Management Principles

**Behavioural support (doubles quit rates when combined with pharmacotherapy):**
- Quitline (13 QUIT): free telephone and online support; proven effective; GP referral recommended
- Quit.com.au: free online behavioural program; text message support
- Brief GP advice: even 3 minutes significantly increases cessation
- CBT-based quit plans: identify triggers, plan coping strategies, set quit date, remove cigarettes

**Pharmacotherapy (combination with behavioural support most effective):**

Nicotine Replacement Therapy (NRT) — PBS-subsidised:
- Available forms: patch, gum, lozenge, inhaler, mouth spray
- Patch: 24-hour (Nicotinell) or 16-hour (Nicabate); high nicotine patch (21 mg/24h or 25 mg/16h) for ≥20 cig/day; step down over 8–12 weeks
- Short-acting NRT (gum, lozenge, spray, inhaler): for breakthrough cravings; use as needed
- Combination NRT (patch + short-acting): significantly more effective than single form; recommended for moderate-high dependence
- Safe in most populations including cardiovascular disease and pregnancy (safer than smoking)

Varenicline (Champix/Chantix):
- Most effective single pharmacotherapy for smoking cessation; 3–4× placebo quit rates
- Mechanism: partial agonist at nicotinic receptor; reduces withdrawal AND blocks cigarette reward
- Dosing: 0.5 mg daily for 3 days; 0.5 mg twice daily for 4 days; then 1 mg twice daily for 12 weeks; start 1–2 weeks before quit date; can extend to 24 weeks
- Side effects: nausea (take with food), vivid dreams, insomnia; previously black-boxed for neuropsychiatric events — FDA/TGA removed warning (evidence did not support serious risk); safe to use in mental illness with appropriate monitoring
- PBS-restricted (S4): requires prescription; significant PBS subsidy for up to 12 weeks per quit attempt; 2 courses per year

Bupropion (Zyban):
- Second-line; antidepressant with nicotinic antagonist properties; reduces cravings
- 150 mg daily for 3 days; then 150 mg twice daily for 7–9 weeks; start 1–2 weeks before quit date
- Side effects: insomnia, dry mouth, seizure risk (contraindicated in seizure disorder, eating disorder, abrupt alcohol cessation); lowers seizure threshold
- PBS-restricted: cessation indication; 12 weeks maximum
- Avoid in pregnancy and with MAOIs

**Combination strategies:**
- Varenicline + NRT patch: some evidence of additional benefit; can combine if patient requests additional support
- All pharmacotherapies are substantially more effective when combined with behavioural support

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **NRT patches** (Nicotinell, Nicabate — PBS restricted: nicotine dependence treatment; 7, 14, 21 mg/24h or equivalent 16h patches; authority required for PBS; 12 weeks per attempt)
- **NRT gum / lozenge / spray** (PBS restricted: authority; used with patch for combination NRT)
- **Varenicline** (Champix — PBS restricted: smoking cessation; 0.5 mg/1 mg tablets; authority required; maximum 12 weeks per PBS course; can prescribe 2 courses per year; take with food for nausea)
- **Bupropion** (Zyban — PBS restricted: smoking cessation; 150 mg SR twice daily; authority required; contraindicated in seizure disorder)
- **Note:** All three PBS-subsidised treatments require an authority prescription; combined behavioural support with pharmacotherapy achieves highest quit rates. Electronic cigarettes (nicotine vapes) require a Schedule 4 prescription in Australia — not currently listed on PBS for cessation.

## Escalation Triggers

**Not applicable in most smoking cessation presentations.** However:

**Urgent assessment:**
- Patient with acute coronary syndrome or stroke — smoking cessation is urgent priority; NRT and varenicline can be started in hospital; GP follow-up for maintenance
- COPD exacerbation — link to cessation; see `copd` entry
- Pregnancy — urgent cessation counselling; NRT appropriate; obstetric referral

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Respiratory physician: severe COPD; lung cancer workup if symptoms warrant
- Oncologist: if smoking-related cancer identified
- Mental health clinician: patients with mental illness should have concurrent mental health support during cessation — psychiatric team liaison if on psychotropics (especially clozapine)

## Telehealth Suitability

Smoking cessation support is highly suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Smoking status assessment and brief advice
- 5As framework delivery
- FTND or HSI assessment
- Quit plan development and goal setting
- Quitline referral and coordination
- NRT, varenicline, or bupropion prescribing
- Follow-up at 1–2 weeks post-quit date (high-risk relapse period)
- Long-term maintenance support

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Physical examination for smoking-related disease (spirometry for COPD, auscultation)
- Blood pressure measurement (CVD risk context)
- Investigation for smoking-related cancer if symptoms warrant
