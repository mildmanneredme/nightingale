## Lymphoedema Assessment (RACGP)

Lymphoedema is a chronic condition caused by impaired lymphatic drainage, resulting in protein-rich interstitial fluid accumulation and progressive tissue fibrosis. Primary lymphoedema arises from congenital lymphatic abnormality; secondary lymphoedema (far more common in Australia) results from damage to lymph nodes or lymphatics from cancer treatment (surgery, radiotherapy), infection, or trauma. Breast cancer-related lymphoedema (BCRL) is the most common form encountered in general practice, affecting 20–30% of women after axillary lymph node dissection (ALND) and 5–7% after sentinel lymph node biopsy (SLNB). Secondary limb lymphoedema after pelvic cancer treatment is also common. Lymphoedema is a chronic, progressive condition without cure — optimal management controls oedema, prevents complications (cellulitis, fibrosis), and maintains function. Complete Decongestive Therapy (CDT), administered by a trained lymphoedema therapist, is the evidence-based treatment standard. Early referral prevents progression. The Australasian Lymphology Association (ALA) provides therapist registry. Telehealth is suitable for management after initial assessment.

## Clinical Presentation

**Stages (International Society of Lymphology — ISL):**
- Stage 0 (latent): lymphatic transport impaired but no oedema visible; may have subjective heaviness
- Stage 1 (mild): pitting oedema; elevates overnight; reversible; early intervention most effective
- Stage 2 (moderate): pitting oedema that does NOT reduce with elevation; fibrosis begins; skin changes
- Stage 3 (severe/lymphostatic elephantiasis): non-pitting fibrotic oedema; severe skin changes; papillomatosis; loss of function

**Symptoms:**
- Swelling: limb (upper — post-breast cancer; lower — post-pelvic cancer, primary); genital; head and neck (post-head/neck cancer treatment)
- Heaviness or fullness: early symptom; patients describe arm or leg feeling "heavy" or "tight"
- Aching or discomfort
- Reduced range of motion; difficulty with activities
- Skin changes: non-pitting; progressive; skin thickens (hyperkeratosis); skin folds deepen; papillomatosis (wart-like skin changes in late stage); lymphorrhoea (lymph fluid leaking through skin)

**Secondary complications:**
- Cellulitis (acute lymphangitis): common; recurrent; fever + painful erythema advancing proximally + systemic illness; can be severe; precipitates and worsens lymphoedema
- Lymphangiosarcoma (Stewart-Treves syndrome): very rare; malignant vascular tumour in long-standing lymphoedema; purple-red skin lesion; urgent biopsy

## Assessment Approach

**Clinical assessment:**
- Measure limb circumference: standardised circumferential measurements at 4 cm intervals; compare bilateral; volume calculation
- Pitting vs non-pitting: press firmly for 30 seconds; pitting = Stage 1–2; non-pitting = Stage 2–3
- Skin changes: consistency, thickening, papillomatosis; Stemmer's sign (unable to pinch a skin fold at base of second toe or finger — positive Stemmer's sign = lymphoedema)
- Functional assessment: range of motion; grip strength (upper limb); gait (lower limb)
- Bioimpedance spectroscopy (BIS): L-Dex device measures extracellular fluid ratio; used for subclinical detection post-cancer treatment; L-Dex >10 above baseline = lymphoedema developing

**Secondary lymphoedema — confirm history:**
- Cancer history: breast cancer surgery/radiotherapy; gynaecological (cervical, endometrial, vulval); prostate; melanoma (lymphadenectomy)
- Trauma or infection: chronic venous insufficiency + lymphatic involvement; filariasis (tropical — Wuchereria bancrofti — in international travellers)
- Medications: can cause peripheral oedema (calcium channel blockers, NSAIDs — distinguish from lymphoedema)

**Distinguish lymphoedema from other oedema:**
- Chronic venous insufficiency: bilateral lower limb; varicosities; skin discolouration; lipodermatosclerosis; pitting; responds to leg elevation more; see `thrombosis-dvt`
- Cardiac oedema: bilateral; pitting; worse at end of day; responds to diuretics; no cancer history
- Lymphoedema: unilateral (most common); non-pitting (late); cancer history; Stemmer's sign positive; does NOT respond to diuretics

## Key Differentials

- Cardiac failure: bilateral pitting oedema; dyspnoea; elevated BNP; responds to diuretics
- CKD / hypoalbuminaemia: bilateral; generalised; low albumin; proteinuria
- DVT: unilateral; sudden onset; pain; calf tenderness; Doppler ultrasound; anticoagulation
- Lipoedema: bilateral symmetric non-inflammatory fat accumulation in lower limbs (spares feet); predominantly women; painful; not improved by elevation; associated with obesity
- Filariasis: tropical travel; bilateral lower limb lymphoedema; Wuchereria bancrofti serology; diethylcarbamazine treatment
- Cellulitis / erysipelas: acute onset; fever; spreading erythema; antibiotics

## Management Principles

**Complete Decongestive Therapy (CDT) — gold standard:**
Administered by certified lymphoedema therapist (CLT); two phases:

Phase 1 (Intensive / Decongestive Phase) — 2–4 weeks:
- Manual Lymphatic Drainage (MLD): specialised lymphatic massage; redirects lymph from affected to functional lymphatics; not standard massage
- Multi-layer bandaging (MLB): compression bandaging applied after MLD; worn 23 hours/day during Phase 1; reduces oedema by promoting lymphatic flow
- Lymphoedema exercise: specific limb exercises while bandaged; pumping action aids lymph transport
- Skin care: meticulous hygiene; moisturising (aqueous cream); prevent infection entry points (cracks, cuts)

Phase 2 (Maintenance Phase) — lifelong:
- Compression garment (class 2 or 3): custom-fitted flat-knit stocking or sleeve; worn daytime; replaced every 3–6 months
- Self-MLD: patient performs simplified MLD at home daily
- Exercise: swimming, walking, cycling — evidence for safety and benefit; previously feared — now evidence shows safe and beneficial
- Skin hygiene: lifelong; infection prevention
- Weight management: obesity worsens lymphoedema

**Compression garments:**
- Essential: must be worn consistently for long-term management
- Classes: Class 2 (18–24 mmHg) for most lymphoedema; Class 3 (25–35 mmHg) for severe
- Custom-fitted by lymphoedema therapist: measurements taken when limb volume stable
- Funding: Lymphoedema Garment Subsidy (state-based schemes — check state health department); NDIS funding for eligible patients; Cancer Council Australia financial assistance

**Cellulitis in lymphoedema:**
- Common, serious complication; any entry point (cut, bite, skin crack) can trigger infection
- Acute cellulitis: commence antibiotics immediately; do NOT delay
  - Mild-moderate: cefalexin 500 mg QID or amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg BD × 14 days
  - Severe (systemic illness): hospital; IV benzylpenicillin or flucloxacillin
- Continue compression garment during cellulitis if tolerated (lymphatic drainage reduces spread)
- Recurrent cellulitis (≥2 episodes): long-term antibiotic prophylaxis: phenoxymethylpenicillin 250 mg BD or cefalexin 500 mg daily × 12 months (reduces recurrence rate 50%)
- Emergency kit: patient keeps antibiotics at home; start at first sign of cellulitis without waiting for GP appointment — patient education essential

**Referral to lymphoedema therapist:**
- ESSENTIAL: all patients with lymphoedema; ALT (Australasian Lymphology Association) therapist registry at lymphoedema.org.au
- Medicare: Limited rebate for lymphoedema management through CDM plan; bulk-billing limited; most private (private health insurance — check)
- Cancer patients: many state cancer centres have embedded lymphoedema services

**Patient education:**
- Skin care: moisturise daily; avoid cuts, scratches, insect bites; wear gloves for gardening; no injections or blood pressure cuffs on affected limb (upper limb BCRL)
- Exercise: safe; do not restrict; aerobic exercise and resistance training do NOT worsen lymphoedema in breast cancer survivors
- Weight: maintain healthy weight (reduces severity)
- Heat: extreme heat (saunas, hot baths) may worsen — cool water swimming preferred
- Air travel: compression garment on affected limb during flight; no evidence cabin pressure worsens lymphoedema in compression

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Cefalexin 500 mg QID** (PBS restricted: cellulitis; first-line for mild-moderate cellulitis in lymphoedema; 14-day course; check MRSA risk)
- **Phenoxymethylpenicillin 250 mg BD** (PBS restricted: streptococcal prophylaxis; long-term for recurrent cellulitis in lymphoedema; 12 months typically; review annually)
- **Note:** Diuretics are NOT indicated for lymphoedema management. They reduce intravascular fluid but do NOT improve lymphatic function and may worsen protein concentration in interstitial fluid over time. Diuretics are appropriate for concurrent cardiac oedema but not for lymphoedema per se.

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent:**
- Severe acute cellulitis with systemic sepsis (high fever, hypotension, spreading erythema): hospital; IV antibiotics
- Suspected lymphangiosarcoma (purple-red skin lesion in longstanding lymphoedema): urgent biopsy; oncology referral

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Lymphoedema therapist (ALT certified): all patients — essential for CDT; check lymphoedema.org.au
- Vascular surgeon: if concurrent venous insufficiency needs surgical evaluation
- Cancer centre lymphoedema clinic: patients with cancer-related lymphoedema; integrated care
- Occupational therapy: upper limb functional rehabilitation; adaptive equipment

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Review of compression garment adherence and technique
- Cellulitis antibiotic prescribing (established patient, known presentation pattern)
- Prophylactic antibiotic prescribing for recurrent cellulitis
- Lymphoedema therapist referral coordination
- Patient education and self-management support

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial assessment: limb circumference measurement, Stemmer's sign, skin assessment
- Lymphoedema garment fitting (performed by lymphoedema therapist)
- Acute cellulitis: extent of erythema, systemic illness assessment
