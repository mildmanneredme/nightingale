## Croup Assessment (RACGP)

Croup (viral laryngotracheobronchitis) is the most common cause of acute upper airway obstruction in children, typically affecting children aged 6 months to 6 years, with peak incidence at 1–2 years. It is caused predominantly by parainfluenza virus (types 1 and 3 most common), with epidemics in autumn and early winter. The characteristic clinical triad is: barking cough, inspiratory stridor, and hoarse voice, resulting from subglottic oedema causing upper airway narrowing. Most croup is mild and managed at home with a single dose of oral dexamethasone. Moderate-severe croup requires emergency management with corticosteroids ± nebulised adrenaline. GPs must be able to: assess severity of croup using the Westley Croup Score, administer dexamethasone, recognise when to refer to hospital, and differentiate from serious conditions (epiglottitis, foreign body, anaphylaxis). Telehealth has limited utility for acute presentations — in-person or emergency assessment required for stridor.

## Clinical Presentation

**Classic croup triad:**
- Barking cough ("seal bark" or "brassy" quality): subglottic narrowing causing characteristic resonant barking sound
- Inspiratory stridor: high-pitched noise on inspiration; may also be biphasic (both inspiratory and expiratory) in severe disease
- Hoarse voice: laryngeal involvement; voice change or dysphonia
- Onset: usually preceded by 1–3 days of URTI (rhinorrhoea, low-grade fever); croup symptoms emerge as URTI peaks
- Fever: usually low-grade (37.5–38.5°C)
- Nocturnal worsening: characteristic — symptoms worse at night; multiple nights in sequence; may improve during day

**Westley Croup Score — severity grading (0–17):**
- Stridor: 0 (none) → 1 (with agitation) → 2 (at rest)
- Air entry: 0 (normal) → 1 (decreased) → 2 (markedly decreased)
- Cyanosis: 0 (none) → 4 (with agitation) → 5 (at rest)
- Retractions: 0 (none) → 1 (mild) → 2 (moderate) → 3 (severe)
- Conscious state: 0 (normal) → 5 (altered)

Severity by score:
- Mild (score 0–2): barking cough; no stridor at rest; no retractions
- Moderate (score 3–7): stridor at rest; mild to moderate retractions; agitated but calm when undisturbed
- Severe (score ≥8): marked stridor at rest; significant retractions; markedly decreased air entry; significant distress or altered consciousness

**Typical course:**
- Most croup is mild and lasts 2–5 days
- Symptoms usually peak at 24–48 hours
- Most children (95%) have mild disease managed at home
- Recurrent croup (≥2 episodes): consider structural causes (subglottic stenosis, tracheomalacia); ENT referral

## Assessment Approach

**Clinical assessment:**
- Westley Croup Score: apply at presentation to guide management
- Observation of the child: agitated vs calm; listen to breathing; observe chest wall retractions, nasal flaring, accessory muscle use
- SpO2: if available; <95% in croup indicates severe disease (hypoxia is a very late sign — indicates near-complete obstruction)
- Do NOT agitate the child unnecessarily: crying and distress worsen stridor; avoid invasive procedures in mild-moderate disease
- Do NOT examine throat in suspected epiglottitis or croup with severe distress — risk of precipitating complete obstruction

**Important NOT to do:**
- Do NOT X-ray routinely: "steeple sign" (subglottic narrowing on AP X-ray) non-specific; radiation burden; rarely changes management
- Do NOT perform blood tests acutely: invasive procedures worsen distress and upper airway obstruction
- X-ray only if: diagnostic uncertainty (rule out foreign body, epiglottitis); arrange safely in hospital if needed

## Key Differentials

- Epiglottitis: bacterial (Hib — now rare with vaccination, but occurs); sudden onset high fever, toxic appearance, drooling, tripod posture, muffled voice, NO barking cough; airway emergency — 000; DO NOT examine throat; paediatric anaesthetics + ENT; "thumbprint sign" on lateral neck X-ray in hospital
- Foreign body aspiration: sudden onset, often no prodromal URTI; unilateral wheeze possible; no hoarse voice; partial obstruction — bronchoscopy; complete obstruction — 000, BLS manoeuvres
- Bacterial tracheitis (bacterial croup): rare; severely ill; high fever; toxic appearance; fails to respond to nebulised adrenaline; Staphylococcus aureus; intubation often required; 000
- Anaphylaxis with laryngeal angioedema: allergen exposure; urticaria; rapid onset; stridor + respiratory distress; IM adrenaline + 000
- Retropharyngeal abscess: fever, drooling, neck stiffness, hot potato voice; lateral neck X-ray shows prevertebral widening; ENT + surgery
- Congenital subglottic stenosis: recurrent or severe croup; stridor from birth; ENT

## Management Principles

**Mild croup (Westley score 0–2 — most presentations):**
- Single dose oral dexamethasone 0.15–0.6 mg/kg (max 10 mg): most evidence at 0.15–0.3 mg/kg; rapidly absorbed; prevents deterioration; reduces return to ED; first-line treatment for ALL croup including mild
- No other acute treatment usually required
- Reassurance to parents: croup is usually self-limiting; barking cough may persist 2–5 days; symptoms worst at night; safe to go home with safety netting
- Safety netting: return if:
  - Stridor at rest
  - Significant work of breathing
  - Child not drinking or very drowsy
  - Rapid deterioration
  - Symptoms not improving after 2–3 days or worsening

**Moderate croup (Westley score 3–7):**
- Oral dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg (max 10 mg): first-line; repeat dose may be considered at 12 hours
- Nebulised adrenaline (epinephrine) 1:1000: 0.5 mL/kg (max 5 mL) via nebuliser; diluted in 2 mL saline; rapid-onset (15 minutes); duration of effect 1–2 hours; must observe child for minimum 2–4 hours after for rebound
- Hospital assessment: recommend if moderate; nebulised adrenaline requires observation

**Severe croup (Westley score ≥8) — 000:**
- Call 000: acute severe airway obstruction; child may fatigue rapidly
- Supplemental oxygen if SpO2 <95%
- Nebulised adrenaline immediately: 0.5 mL/kg up to 5 mL; may repeat every 20 minutes
- IV or IM dexamethasone if oral not tolerated: 0.6 mg/kg IV/IM
- IM adrenaline: if IV access unavailable and deteriorating; 0.01 mg/kg of 1:1000 (max 0.5 mg)
- Hospital (ICU capable): paediatric anaesthetics; intubation if complete obstruction

**Parent education:**
- Cool, humid air: no strong evidence but safe; opening freezer, cold night air may help
- Steam inhalation: NOT recommended (burns risk; no evidence)
- Keep child calm: agitation worsens obstruction; cuddle and comfort; avoid distressing procedures
- Return to GP/ED if: symptoms not improving with dexamethasone, worsening stridor at rest, child not drinking, very drowsy
- School/childcare: can return when symptoms resolved; croup itself is not an exclusion criterion but associated URTI is contagious

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Dexamethasone 0.15–0.6 mg/kg (max 10 mg) oral** (PBS unrestricted: steroid for inflammatory conditions; off-label use for croup; key prescribing point — ensure GP has age-appropriate formulation; dexamethasone 0.5 mg/5 mL elixir or 4 mg/mL injectable given orally; calculate weight-based dose)
- **Weight-based dosing table (dexamethasone)**:
  - 5 kg: 0.75–3 mg
  - 10 kg: 1.5–6 mg
  - 15 kg: 2.25–9 mg
  - 20 kg: 3–10 mg (use max 10 mg)
- **Nebulised adrenaline (epinephrine) 1:1000**: not PBS-funded directly; used from emergency supply; 0.5 mL/kg to maximum 5 mL; dilute in 3 mL normal saline; GP practice should have this available for moderate-severe croup
- **Note:** Dexamethasone is superior to prednisolone for croup (longer duration of action, single dose); budesonide nebuliser is an alternative if child cannot swallow (2 mg nebulised = equivalent to oral dexamethasone)
- **Oral prednisolone**: less preferred than dexamethasone for croup but can be used at 1 mg/kg if dexamethasone not available (less evidence, shorter action)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000:**
- Severe stridor at rest: child distressed, retracting severely, cyanosed, or altered conscious state
- Failure to improve or deterioration after nebulised adrenaline
- Suspected epiglottitis, bacterial tracheitis, or anaphylaxis
- Child in extremis: near-complete upper airway obstruction, silent, exhausted

**Transfer to hospital (moderate severity):**
- Westley score 3–7: dexamethasone given; nebulised adrenaline given; observe 2–4 hours in hospital; admit if persistent moderate symptoms
- Recurrent visit within 24–48 hours despite dexamethasone dose

**Non-urgent ENT referral:**
- Recurrent croup (≥2 episodes, especially <12 months of age): exclude structural causes (subglottic stenosis, haemangioma)
- Atypical features: stridor not clearly associated with URTI; persistent stridor between episodes; very young infant

## Telehealth Suitability

Croup acute assessment requires in-person evaluation; follow-up suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Post-recovery follow-up (parent reassurance, duration of barking cough)
- Parent education on croup signs and when to seek help
- Safety netting discussion for mild resolved croup
- Referral planning for recurrent croup

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- ALL presentations with current stridor or active respiratory distress
- Administration of dexamethasone and clinical observation
- Nebulised adrenaline administration
- SpO2 monitoring

## Paediatric Context and Guardian Involvement

- Guardian role: all decisions, medication administration, observation at home; empower parents with clear safety netting criteria
- Weight-based dosing: must know child's weight for dexamethasone dose calculation; keep up-to-date weight in records; ask guardian at each consultation
- Single-dose dexamethasone: low burden for parents; highly effective; reassure that treatment is safe and evidence-based
- Night-time presentations: most common; advise parents to have plan for after-hours GP or emergency if worsening
