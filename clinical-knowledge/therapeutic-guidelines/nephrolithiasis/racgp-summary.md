## Nephrolithiasis (Kidney Stones) Assessment (RACGP)

Nephrolithiasis affects approximately 10–15% of Australians during their lifetime, with a high recurrence rate of 50% within 5 years after a first stone. Calcium oxalate stones are most common (80%), followed by calcium phosphate, struvite (infection-related), uric acid, and cystine. Renal colic is one of the most severe pains patients experience — typically sudden, severe, cramping flank pain radiating to the groin — and is a common ED and GP presentation. Most stones <5 mm pass spontaneously; stones 5–10 mm may pass with medical expulsion therapy; stones >10 mm generally require urological intervention. The GP plays a central role in: acute pain management, initial imaging and investigation, facilitating stone passage, metabolic risk factor management, and prevention of recurrence. Telehealth is suitable for follow-up and preventive care.

## Clinical Presentation

**Acute renal colic:**
- Severe, cramping, often excruciating flank pain; typically unilateral
- Pain radiates from flank to groin and inner thigh (following ureteric course)
- Colicky: waxes and wanes with ureteric peristalsis; patient cannot find a comfortable position (unlike peritoneal pain where patient keeps still)
- Nausea and vomiting common (sympathetic response)
- Haematuria: micro or macroscopic haematuria in ~90%; however absence does not exclude stone
- Urinary urgency/frequency as stone approaches vesicoureteric junction (VUJ)
- No fever unless concurrent infection (obstructing stone + infection = urological emergency — 000)

**Stone size and likelihood of spontaneous passage:**
- <4 mm: ~80% pass spontaneously
- 4–6 mm: ~60% pass spontaneously
- 6–10 mm: ~25–50% pass spontaneously
- >10 mm: unlikely to pass spontaneously; urological intervention usually required

**Red flags / complications:**
- Fever + renal colic: infected obstructed kidney — urosepsis risk; 000; emergency decompression (ureteric stent or nephrostomy)
- Anuria: bilateral obstruction (rare) or solitary kidney obstruction — emergency
- Intractable pain or vomiting not responding to initial analgesia: hospital referral
- Renal impairment: AKI with obstruction; urgent urology

## Assessment Approach

**Acute investigation:**
- Urinalysis (MSU): haematuria, pyuria (infection), urine pH (alkaline = struvite; acid = uric acid)
- Urine MCS: if fever, pyuria, or diabetic
- FBC, electrolytes, creatinine (eGFR): baseline; AKI?
- Serum calcium: hypercalcaemia as cause of stones; parathyroid adenoma
- Uric acid: elevated in gout and uric acid stones
- CT KUB (non-contrast CT kidneys-ureters-bladder): gold standard imaging; sensitivity and specificity >95%; shows stone, hydronephrosis, and stone size/location; preferred over X-ray or ultrasound for acute diagnosis
- Renal ultrasound: alternative to CT (no radiation); less sensitive for ureteric stones; preferred in pregnancy; shows hydronephrosis
- X-ray KUB (plain film): calcium stones visible (radio-opaque); uric acid stones not visible (radiolucent); limited utility alone; useful to monitor passage of radio-opaque stones

**Metabolic workup for recurrent stones (first recurrence or high-risk features):**
- 24-hour urine collection (×2, at least 6 weeks after acute episode): calcium, oxalate, citrate, uric acid, sodium, creatinine, volume, pH — identifies metabolic abnormalities amenable to treatment
- Serum calcium, phosphate, uric acid, PTH (if hypercalcaemia)
- Stone analysis (if stone caught): identifies stone type precisely; guides prevention strategy
- Stone catchers: patient provided with filter or coffee filter to catch passed stone for laboratory analysis

**Risk factors for stone formation:**
- Low fluid intake: most important modifiable risk factor
- High dietary sodium: increases urinary calcium excretion
- High dietary oxalate: spinach, beets, nuts, chocolate, tea — risk for calcium oxalate stones
- Low dietary calcium (paradoxically increases oxalate absorption — recommend normal calcium intake)
- Dehydration (hot climate, outdoor workers, athletes)
- Metabolic: hyperparathyroidism, gout, cystinuria, RTA
- Medications: indinavir (HIV), topiramate, vitamin D excess, furosemide (chronic use), acetazolamide

## Key Differentials

- Musculoskeletal back pain: no haematuria; exacerbated by movement; no radiation to groin
- Appendicitis: right-sided; peritoneal signs; fever; elevated WBC; CT abdomen
- Ovarian pathology (ovarian cyst, torsion, ectopic pregnancy): female patients; pelvic pain; ultrasound; hCG
- Aortic aneurysm (AAA): pulsatile mass; older patient with vascular risk factors; CT angiogram — do not miss; can mimic renal colic fatally
- Testicular torsion: scrotal pain; absent cremasteric reflex; urgent scrotal ultrasound; surgical emergency
- Pyelonephritis: fever, rigors, CVA tenderness; urinalysis; not typically colicky; may coexist

## Management Principles

**Acute pain management:**
- NSAIDs: first-line analgesia in renal colic (superior to opioids in multiple trials); ibuprofen 400–800 mg oral or diclofenac 75 mg IM; reduce ureteric spasm and reduce stone passage time; avoid if renal impairment (eGFR <30) or allergy
- Paracetamol: adjunct; less effective alone
- Opioids: if NSAIDs contraindicated or inadequate; morphine or oxycodone; do not use as first-line
- Anti-emetics: metoclopramide or ondansetron for nausea/vomiting
- Hospitalise if: pain not controlled with oral analgesia, intractable vomiting, fever, AKI, solitary kidney, bilateral obstruction

**Medical expulsive therapy (MET):**
- Alpha-blockers (tamsulosin 0.4 mg once daily): relax distal ureter smooth muscle; evidence for stones 5–10 mm in distal ureter; reduces time to stone passage and need for intervention; continue until stone passes or intervention performed (max 4 weeks)
- Indapamide: reduces ureteric smooth muscle spasm (less evidence than alpha-blockers)
- Adequate hydration: increased fluid intake to 2–3 L/day during stone passage

**Urological referral and intervention:**
- Shockwave lithotripsy (SWL): non-invasive; acoustic waves break stone into passable fragments; outpatient; for kidney stones and proximal ureteric stones <20 mm
- Ureteroscopy (URS) + laser lithotripsy: endoscopic; preferred for distal ureteric stones; day procedure
- Percutaneous nephrolithotomy (PCNL): for large renal stones (>20 mm) or staghorn calculi; specialist inpatient procedure
- Ureteric stent: temporary decompression when definitive intervention delayed; placed cystoscopically under GA
- Nephrostomy: emergency decompression in infected obstruction; interventional radiology

**Stone prevention (long-term — primary care role):**

Dietary and lifestyle:
- Fluid intake: target urine output >2 L/day; water preferred; 2.5–3 L fluid daily
- Reduce dietary sodium (<2.3 g/day): reduces urinary calcium excretion
- Calcium intake normal (1000 mg/day from food): do NOT restrict dietary calcium — increases oxalate absorption paradoxically
- Reduce dietary oxalate for calcium oxalate stones: avoid spinach, nuts, chocolate, strong tea
- Reduce animal protein (generates uric acid and lowers urinary citrate)

Pharmacological prevention based on 24-hour urine results:
- Hypercalciuria: thiazide diuretic (hydrochlorothiazide or indapamide) — reduces urinary calcium
- Hypocitraturia: potassium citrate (Urocit-K) — alkalinises urine and directly binds calcium; increases citrate
- Hyperuricosuria: allopurinol — reduces uric acid production; also prevents uric acid stones
- Hyperoxaluria: reduce dietary oxalate + calcium citrate with meals (binds oxalate in gut)
- Uric acid stones: potassium citrate — alkalinises urine (uric acid stones dissolve in alkaline urine); may avoid surgery
- Recurrent UTI-related struvite stones: long-term low-dose antibiotic prevention; urological review

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Tamsulosin (Flomaxtra)** (PBS restricted: BPH; used off-label for MET in ureteric stones; 0.4 mg daily; limited PBS indication for stone passage)
- **Ibuprofen** (PBS unrestricted: musculoskeletal pain; use for acute renal colic; 400–800 mg TDS; avoid if eGFR <30)
- **Diclofenac IM (Voltaren)** (PBS unrestricted: pain; IM injection for severe acute colic in clinic setting; 75 mg IM)
- **Potassium citrate (Urocit-K)** (PBS unrestricted: urinary alkalisers; stone prevention; 15–30 mEq BD-TDS; GI side effects common)
- **Hydrochlorothiazide / Indapamide** (PBS restricted: hypertension; used for hypercalciuria in stone prevention; reduces urinary calcium)
- **Allopurinol** (PBS restricted: gout; uric acid stones prevention; 100–300 mg daily)
- **Note:** Vitamin C supplements increase urinary oxalate — advise patients with recurrent calcium oxalate stones to avoid high-dose vitamin C; recommend dietary rather than supplemental vitamin C

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Fever + renal colic: infected obstructed kidney — urosepsis; emergency decompression required
- Severe uncontrolled pain not manageable in community
- Suspected AAA (sudden severe back/flank pain in older patient with vascular risk)
- Anuria: bilateral obstruction or solitary kidney obstruction
- AKI with creatinine rising rapidly

**Urgent urology referral:**
- Stone >10 mm: unlikely to pass spontaneously; urological intervention required
- Stone 6–10 mm not passing after 4 weeks of MET: intervention required
- Persistent obstruction with hydronephrosis not resolving
- Recurrent stones despite preventive treatment

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Urology: first stone with metabolic workup indicating surgical risk factor, or any recurrence
- Nephrology: complex metabolic stone disease, cystinuria, primary hyperoxaluria, RTA
- Endocrinology: primary hyperparathyroidism (hypercalcaemia + recurrent calcium stones)
- Dietitian: dietary oxalate reduction, sodium reduction, fluid intake education

## Telehealth Suitability

Stone follow-up and prevention are well suited to telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Follow-up after acute stone episode: symptom review, stone passage confirmation
- 24-hour urine result discussion and prevention planning
- Dietary and lifestyle counselling
- Medication prescribing for stone prevention (thiazides, potassium citrate, allopurinol)
- MET prescribing for small stones (tamsulosin)
- Referral coordination (urology, nephrology)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Acute renal colic: physical examination, IV access for severe pain/vomiting
- Blood and urine tests — pathology arranged
- CT KUB or ultrasound — radiology referral
- Stone passing procedures — urology
