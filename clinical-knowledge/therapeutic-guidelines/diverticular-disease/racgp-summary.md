## Diverticular Disease Assessment (RACGP)

Diverticular disease refers to the spectrum from diverticulosis (presence of diverticula — outpouchings of colonic mucosa through muscular wall — without symptoms) to symptomatic diverticular disease and diverticulitis (inflammation or infection of diverticula). It is extremely common in Western populations: affects approximately 35% of Australians by age 50 and 65% by age 80. Most diverticulosis is asymptomatic; symptomatic disease occurs in approximately 25% of those with diverticulosis. Diverticulitis — the main complication — results from micro-perforation and infection in a diverticulum. The sigmoid colon is affected in >90% of cases. GPs play a central role in: diagnosing acute diverticulitis, managing uncomplicated cases with outpatient antibiotics and diet, recognising complicated disease requiring hospital admission, and managing diet and lifestyle for recurrence prevention. Telehealth is suitable for follow-up; acute presentations require in-person assessment.

## Clinical Presentation

**Diverticulosis (asymptomatic — most cases):**
- No symptoms; incidental finding on colonoscopy, CT, or contrast enema
- No treatment required; high-fibre diet for prevention

**Symptomatic diverticular disease (without inflammation):**
- Left lower quadrant discomfort or pain; bloating; altered bowel habit
- Differentiating from IBS — diverticular disease tends to affect older patients; may overlap

**Acute diverticulitis:**
- Left lower quadrant pain: persistent, often constant; worse on movement; may radiate to back or suprapubic
- Fever: usually low-grade (37.5–38.5°C); high fever suggests complicated disease
- Nausea and vomiting
- Altered bowel habit: constipation or diarrhoea
- Local peritonism: guarding, rebound tenderness at left iliac fossa — suggests local perforation
- Severe: generalised peritonism, high fever, haemodynamic instability — suggests free perforation/generalised peritonitis

**Classification (Hinchey for complicated disease):**
- Uncomplicated (Hinchey 0/Ia): pericolic inflammation or small abscess; managed outpatient
- Complicated (Hinchey Ib–IV): large abscess, purulent or faecal peritonitis — surgical emergency

**Diverticular bleeding:**
- Painless, sudden, large-volume bright red or maroon rectal bleeding
- Often self-limiting (stops spontaneously in 70–80%); can cause haemodynamic compromise
- Requires colonoscopy and urgent investigation — distinct from diverticulitis

## Assessment Approach

**Clinical assessment:**
- Location and character of pain; onset; duration
- Fever, nausea, vomiting; bowel habit change
- Previous episodes of diverticular disease
- Other risk factors: NSAIDs, steroids, immunosuppression (increase complication risk)
- Vital signs: heart rate, temperature, BP, SpO2

**Physical examination:**
- Abdominal palpation: left iliac fossa tenderness, guarding, rebound tenderness
- Generalised rigidity suggests peritonitis — hospital immediately
- Rectal examination: if rectal bleeding component; exclude palpable mass

**Investigations:**
- FBC: WBC elevated (>15 × 10⁹/L suggests severe/complicated disease)
- CRP: markedly elevated in diverticulitis; useful marker of severity and response to treatment
- eGFR, electrolytes: baseline; dehydration
- Urine MCS: exclude UTI (left-sided urinary symptoms common — proximity to sigmoid colon)
- CT abdomen and pelvis with IV contrast: gold standard diagnostic test for acute diverticulitis; confirms diagnosis; grades severity (Hinchey); identifies abscess, perforation, fistula; indicates need for surgery
- X-ray abdomen: limited use; may show free air under diaphragm (perforation) but CT is superior
- Note: colonoscopy is CONTRAINDICATED in acute diverticulitis (perforation risk); schedule 6–8 weeks after acute episode to exclude colorectal malignancy

## Key Differentials

- Colorectal cancer: new diverticular symptoms in older patient; rectal bleeding; weight loss; colonoscopy after acute phase
- Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS): younger age; pain relieved by defecation; no fever or raised inflammatory markers; may overlap with diverticular disease
- Pelvic inflammatory disease: women; cervical motion tenderness; vaginal discharge; STI testing
- Ovarian pathology: ovarian cyst, torsion, ectopic pregnancy — serum hCG; pelvic ultrasound; consider in women
- Appendicitis: right iliac fossa primarily; Rovsing's sign; young patients; CT differentiates
- Acute mesenteric ischaemia: severe pain out of proportion to examination; cardiovascular risk factors; CT angiography; surgical emergency
- Crohn's disease: younger; recurrent symptoms; diarrhoea; perianal disease; colonoscopy

## Management Principles

**Uncomplicated acute diverticulitis (mild, no systemic illness, tolerating oral intake):**

Outpatient management appropriate if:
- Mild pain; tolerating fluids and oral medications
- No significant fever (<38.5°C)
- No significant comorbidities (immunosuppression, steroids, severe CKD)
- Reliable patient with good access to follow-up or emergency care
- No complicated disease on CT (if performed)

Antibiotics (evidence now questioned for mild uncomplicated disease):
- Recent RCTs (AVOD, DIABOLO) show no benefit of antibiotics over observation in mild uncomplicated diverticulitis in immunocompetent patients
- GESA position (2023): antibiotics may be withheld in mild uncomplicated diverticulitis managed outpatient in low-risk patients; however, most Australian GPs and guidelines still prescribe antibiotics for clinical diverticulitis
- If antibiotics used: amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg BD × 5–7 days (covers polymicrobial colonic flora including anaerobes); metronidazole 400 mg BD + cephalexin 500 mg BD × 7 days (if penicillin allergy)
- Clear liquid diet for 48–72 hours then gradual return to normal diet as symptoms improve

**Review at 48–72 hours:**
- Clinical improvement expected: decreasing pain, resolving fever, tolerating diet, normalising inflammatory markers
- No improvement or worsening: CT abdomen (if not already done); consider hospitalisation
- Resolution: colonoscopy at 6–8 weeks (exclude colorectal cancer — diverticulitis can obstruct/mask colonic mass)

**Hospital admission criteria:**
- Generalised peritonitis (peritoneal signs)
- Complicated disease on CT: large abscess, free perforation, fistula
- Unable to tolerate oral intake (dehydration)
- Failure of outpatient antibiotics at 48–72 hours
- Immunocompromised patients; high-dose corticosteroids
- Significant comorbidities; elderly with poor social supports
- Uncertain diagnosis requiring further investigation

**Complicated diverticulitis (Hinchey Ib–IV):**
- Hospitalise immediately: IV antibiotics (piperacillin-tazobactam or ceftriaxone + metronidazole); surgical consultation
- Abscess: CT-guided drainage (radiology) if >3 cm; IV antibiotics; surgical consultation
- Free perforation: emergency surgery (Hartmann's procedure or primary anastomosis); ICU

**Diverticular bleeding:**
- Haemodynamically stable: colonoscopy within 48 hours (after bowel prep); haemostasis during colonoscopy (clips, adrenaline injection)
- Haemodynamically unstable: 000; IV resuscitation; urgent colonoscopy or CT angiography + IR embolisation; surgery if uncontrolled

**Recurrence prevention (long-term):**
- High-fibre diet: 25–30 g/day; fruits, vegetables, legumes, wholegrain bread; reduces recurrence
- Hydration: 2 L/day; essential with increased fibre
- Red meat limitation: associated with increased diverticular disease risk
- Avoid NSAIDs: associated with increased diverticulitis risk and worse outcomes; switch to paracetamol if possible
- Mesalazine (Asacol): some evidence for recurrence prevention; not routinely used; specialist decision
- Elective sigmoid colectomy: after ≥2–3 episodes of complicated diverticulitis; individual risk-benefit; colorectal surgery discussion

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg BD** (PBS unrestricted: covers anaerobes and Gram-negatives; diarrhoea common side effect; take with food; standard 5–7 day course for uncomplicated diverticulitis)
- **Metronidazole 400 mg BD** (PBS unrestricted: anaerobic coverage; combined with cefalexin for penicillin allergy; avoid alcohol; 7 days)
- **Cefalexin 500 mg QID** (PBS unrestricted: Gram-positive and some Gram-negative coverage; combine with metronidazole for complete bowel flora coverage)
- **Note:** Recent evidence challenges routine antibiotic use for mild uncomplicated diverticulitis; however, in Australian clinical practice, most GPs prescribe antibiotics based on clinical diverticulitis diagnosis; discuss recent evidence with patient for shared decision-making

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / surgical emergency:**
- Free perforation with peritonitis: generalised abdominal rigidity, septic shock — Hartmann's procedure
- Acute haemodynamic compromise from diverticular bleeding: resuscitation and urgent colonoscopy or surgery

**Urgent hospital admission:**
- Complicated diverticulitis on CT: abscess, perforation
- Failure of outpatient management at 48–72 hours
- Unable to tolerate oral intake
- Immunocompromised or high-risk comorbidities

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Colonoscopy (6–8 weeks after acute episode): exclude colorectal cancer
- Colorectal surgeon: elective sigmoid colectomy after multiple episodes or complicated disease

## Telehealth Suitability

Diverticular disease follow-up is suitable for telehealth; acute presentations require in-person.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Follow-up after acute episode (48–72 hour review — can be phone/telehealth if clinically improving)
- Dietary counselling and fibre education
- Colonoscopy scheduling and results discussion
- Medication review (avoid NSAIDs)
- Recurrence prevention counselling

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Acute diverticulitis: abdominal examination, blood tests, CT
- Any peritoneal signs or haemodynamic instability — hospital
