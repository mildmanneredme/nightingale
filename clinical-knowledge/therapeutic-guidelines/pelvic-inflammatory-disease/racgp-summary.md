## Pelvic Inflammatory Disease Assessment (RACGP)

Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) is an ascending infection of the female upper genital tract — uterus (endometritis), fallopian tubes (salpingitis), ovaries, and peritoneum. It is predominantly caused by sexually transmitted organisms: Chlamydia trachomatis and Neisseria gonorrhoeae (individually or together), as well as vaginal flora (anaerobes, Gardnerella vaginalis, streptococci). PID affects approximately 100,000 Australian women annually. It is a major preventable cause of long-term reproductive morbidity — tubal factor infertility (15–20% after 1 episode), ectopic pregnancy (6-fold increased risk after 1 episode), and chronic pelvic pain (20%). GPs play a central role in: early empirical treatment (treatment should not wait for pathology results in patients with typical symptoms), STI testing and contact tracing, and long-term follow-up. The threshold for empirical treatment is low — the morbidity of untreated or delayed PID far exceeds the risk of overtreatment. Telehealth has limited utility for PID diagnosis; in-person assessment and treatment are required.

## Clinical Presentation

**Classic PID triad:**
- Lower abdominal and pelvic pain: bilateral; often dull or cramping; may be severe
- Vaginal discharge: mucopurulent (yellow-green); unusual amount; may have odour
- Uterine/adnexal/cervical motion tenderness: on bimanual examination — hallmark finding

**Variable presentation:**
- Mild: minimal symptoms; often subacute; sexually active woman with pelvic pain and adnexal tenderness
- Severe: high fever (>38.5°C), severe pain, peritoneal signs, nausea/vomiting — suggests Tubo-ovarian abscess (TOA)
- Silent PID: subclinical; no or minimal symptoms; discovered on investigation for infertility — chlamydia-related

**Additional features:**
- Dyspareunia: deep pain during intercourse
- Abnormal uterine bleeding: intermenstrual or post-coital
- Fever: low-grade in mild PID; high in TOA or peritonitis
- Urinary symptoms: UTI-like (frequency, dysuria) may coexist

**Risk factors:**
- Sexually active women aged 15–24 years (highest risk)
- Recent new or multiple sexual partners
- Previous PID (repeat episodes common)
- Recent STI (chlamydia, gonorrhoea)
- Recent intrauterine procedure (IUD insertion, hysteroscopy, endometrial biopsy — brief elevated risk)
- Vaginal douching

## Assessment Approach

**Minimum clinical criteria for empirical PID treatment (ASHM — treat empirically if any one present plus no other identifiable cause):**
- Uterine tenderness, OR
- Adnexal tenderness (fallopian tubes/ovaries), OR
- Cervical motion tenderness (chandelier sign)
- In sexually active woman with pelvic pain

**Physical examination:**
- Bimanual pelvic examination: assess uterine tenderness, adnexal masses/tenderness, cervical motion tenderness
- Speculum examination: cervical appearance, mucopurulent discharge, endocervical swabs
- Abdomen: right upper quadrant tenderness (Fitz-Hugh-Curtis syndrome — perihepatitis from ascending PID)

**Investigations (results must NOT delay treatment):**
- Endocervical swabs: NAAT (PCR) for Chlamydia trachomatis and Neisseria gonorrhoeae; Gram stain (GPCs — gonorrhoea)
- High vaginal swab: for microscopy, culture, and sensitivity (vaginal flora, BV)
- Urine NAAT: chlamydia and gonorrhoea (if NAAT from swab not available)
- FBC: elevated WBC suggests infection severity
- CRP: elevated in PID; useful baseline
- Urine MCS: exclude UTI
- Serum hCG (pregnancy test): CRITICAL — must exclude ectopic pregnancy before treating; ectopic presents similarly
- Pelvic ultrasound: if TOA (tubo-ovarian abscess) suspected; free fluid; adnexal mass; thickness of fallopian tube walls — TVUS preferred; does NOT exclude PID if normal

## Key Differentials

- Ectopic pregnancy: ALWAYS exclude with serum hCG — life-threatening emergency; similar pelvic pain + tenderness; 000 if ruptured
- Appendicitis: right-sided pain; rebound and guarding; nausea/vomiting; surgical emergency — if uncertain, surgical review
- Ovarian cyst accident (rupture, torsion): unilateral; sudden onset; torsion requires urgent surgery — ultrasound to differentiate
- Endometriosis: chronic; cyclical; associated with dysmenorrhoea; laparoscopy for diagnosis; not infective
- UTI / pyelonephritis: urinary symptoms predominant; no adnexal tenderness; urine MCS
- Appendiceal abscess: right-sided; CT abdomen

## Management Principles

**Empirical antibiotic treatment — start before pathology results:**

ASHM recommended outpatient regimen (mild-moderate PID — most cases):
- Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM × 1 dose (gonorrhoea coverage) PLUS
- Doxycycline 100 mg BD × 14 days PLUS
- Metronidazole 400 mg BD × 14 days (anaerobic coverage)

Alternatively:
- Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg BD × 14 days PLUS doxycycline 100 mg BD × 14 days (if ceftriaxone not available; less gonorrhoea coverage)

Important:
- 14-day course: minimum; do not truncate even if symptoms improve quickly
- Review at 72 hours: if no improvement — consider hospitalisation, IV antibiotics, or surgical drainage (TOA)
- Modify if pathology returns resistance or culture result changes empirical coverage

**Hospitalisation criteria (intravenous antibiotics required):**
- Tubo-ovarian abscess (TOA)
- Severe systemic illness (high fever >38.5°C, vomiting, peritoneal signs)
- Surgical emergency cannot be excluded (appendicitis, ectopic pregnancy)
- Failure of outpatient therapy at 72-hour review
- Pregnancy
- Immunocompromised patient
- Poor adherence expected to outpatient oral therapy

**Inpatient IV regimen (hospital):**
- Ceftriaxone 1–2 g IV once daily + doxycycline 100 mg BD IV/oral + metronidazole 500 mg IV BD
- Transition to oral when clinically improving (temperature <37.5°C, improving pain); complete 14 days total

**Partner treatment and contact tracing:**
- Treat all sexual partners from the last 60 days: even if asymptomatic
- Male partners: azithromycin 1 g stat + ceftriaxone 500 mg IM (for chlamydia and gonorrhoea)
- Contact tracing: Sexual health clinic assistance; public health notification for gonorrhoea (notifiable)
- Abstain from sex until patient and partner(s) complete treatment and are symptom-free

**IUD in PID:**
- IUD does not need to be removed for mild-moderate PID managed with antibiotics
- Remove IUD if: severe PID, TOA, no improvement on antibiotics, patient prefers removal
- New IUD insertion: delay until PID fully treated; STI-negative

**Follow-up:**
- 72-hour review: clinical improvement? No improvement = hospitalise or modify regimen
- Test of cure (2–4 weeks): repeat NAAT for gonorrhoea (culture-confirmed gonorrhoea)
- 3-month retest: STI retesting at 3 months; high reinfection risk
- Fertility counselling: inform about long-term sequelae (infertility, ectopic risk, chronic pelvic pain); refer to gynaecologist if planning pregnancy or persistent pain

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM** (PBS restricted: serious infections; IM injection; reconstitute with 1 mL lignocaine 1% to reduce injection pain; single dose; covers gonorrhoea empirically including most cephalosporin-sensitive strains; check local gonorrhoea resistance patterns)
- **Doxycycline 100 mg** (PBS unrestricted: STI treatment; BD dosing; 14-day course; take with food to reduce nausea; photosensitivity; avoid in pregnancy — use azithromycin instead if pregnant)
- **Metronidazole 400 mg** (PBS unrestricted: anaerobic infections; BD dosing; 14 days; avoid alcohol during treatment and 48 hours after; metallic taste common; take with food)
- **Note:** PID is frequently underdiagnosed and undertreated — the threshold for empirical treatment should be low; morbidity of untreated PID (infertility, ectopic, chronic pain) far exceeds side effects of a 14-day antibiotic course
- **Note:** Gonorrhoea resistance is increasing — always include adequate gonorrhoea coverage; ceftriaxone remains first-line; notify public health unit for confirmed gonorrhoea (notifiable disease)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Ruptured ectopic pregnancy: acute haemodynamic collapse, severe unilateral pelvic pain + positive hCG — 000 surgical emergency
- Ruptured TOA: sepsis, peritonitis — 000
- Septic shock from PID

**Urgent hospital (same day):**
- TOA on ultrasound: IV antibiotics; drainage if not responding; often requires surgical drainage
- Inability to exclude surgical emergency (appendicitis, ectopic)
- Systemically unwell: high fever, vomiting, severe pain — intravenous antibiotics in hospital

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Gynaecologist: failed outpatient PID (not improving at 72 hours); complex recurrent PID; TOA management; fertility assessment post-PID
- Sexual health clinic: contact tracing support; STI counselling; complex STI management

## Telehealth Suitability

PID requires in-person assessment and management; post-treatment follow-up suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- 72-hour review (symptom improvement assessment — if clinically improving, no new concerns)
- STI results discussion
- Partner treatment coordination
- Repeat NAAT (test of cure) ordering
- Fertility counselling post-PID

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial assessment: bimanual examination, speculum, endocervical swabs — cannot diagnose PID without pelvic examination
- Serum hCG (blood test to exclude ectopic)
- Pelvic ultrasound (if TOA suspected)
- Ceftriaxone IM injection administration
