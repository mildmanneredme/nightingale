## Peripheral Neuropathy Assessment (RACGP)

Peripheral neuropathy refers to damage or dysfunction of the peripheral nerves — sensory, motor, or autonomic — causing numbness, tingling, pain, or weakness. It is common in general practice, with diabetic peripheral neuropathy (DPN) being the most frequent cause (affects 50% of people with diabetes over their lifetime). Other common causes include alcohol-related neuropathy, vitamin B12 deficiency, medications (chemotherapy, metronidazole), and idiopathic neuropathy (20–30% of cases). The GP plays a central role in: identifying neuropathy, investigating the underlying cause, managing neuropathic pain, preventing complications (foot care in diabetic neuropathy), and recognising red flags requiring urgent neurological assessment. Telehealth is suitable for monitoring and medication review.

## Clinical Presentation

**Sensory neuropathy (most common type — including DPN):**
- Numbness and reduced sensation: stocking-glove distribution; feet > hands; bilateral and symmetric
- Paraesthesiae: tingling, burning, pins and needles; often worse at rest and at night
- Allodynia: pain from normally non-painful stimuli (light touch, bedsheets)
- Loss of proprioception: unsteadiness, falls
- Painless wounds: patient unaware of foot injury (diabetic neuropathy — major cause of foot ulceration)

**Motor neuropathy:**
- Distal weakness: foot drop; difficulty dorsiflexing foot; high-stepping gait
- Hand weakness: difficulty with fine motor tasks (buttons, keys)
- Muscle wasting (amyotrophy)

**Autonomic neuropathy (often in advanced diabetes):**
- Postural hypotension: dizziness on standing
- Gastroparesis: early satiety, nausea, unpredictable glucose levels
- Bladder dysfunction: urinary retention, incontinence
- Erectile dysfunction
- Anhidrosis (reduced sweating) in lower limbs with compensatory hyperhidrosis in upper body

**Mononeuropathy / mononeuritis multiplex:**
- Single nerve involvement: carpal tunnel (median), ulnar, peroneal (foot drop), radial nerve palsy
- Multiple discrete nerve lesions: vasculitis, diabetes
- Cranial neuropathy: Bell's palsy (facial); diabetic third nerve palsy (with pupil sparing)

## Assessment Approach

**Clinical examination:**
- Sensation testing: 10-g monofilament (diabetic foot — annual); vibration (128 Hz tuning fork applied to first MTP joint and medial malleolus); pinprick; light touch
- Reflexes: ankle jerk absent in DPN (early sensitive sign); knee jerk
- Motor: foot dorsiflexion strength; hand grip; intrinsic hand muscle wasting
- Autonomic: lying/standing BP; heart rate response to Valsalva

**Investigations (targeted by clinical picture):**
- Fasting glucose, HbA1c: diabetes most common cause; screen at every presentation
- Vitamin B12: deficiency causes subacute combined degeneration; common cause of neuropathy in elderly; vegetarians/vegans at risk; metformin reduces B12 absorption — check annually in patients on metformin
- TSH: hypothyroidism causes neuropathy
- FBC, ferritin: anaemia from B12/folate deficiency; also iron deficiency associated
- LFTs, GGT: alcohol-related neuropathy
- eGFR: uraemic neuropathy in CKD
- Protein electrophoresis (SPEP) and immunofixation: paraproteinaemia (MGUS, myeloma) — important cause of neuropathy
- Vitamin B1 (thiamine): alcoholic neuropathy, Wernicke's encephalopathy risk
- ANA, ANCA: vasculitic neuropathy
- HIV serology: HIV neuropathy
- Lyme serology (if tick exposure): not endemic in Australia; consider in travel to endemic areas
- Anti-ganglioside antibodies (anti-GM1, GQ1b): Guillain-Barré, CIDP, Miller-Fisher syndrome — specialist
- Nerve conduction studies (NCS) and electromyography (EMG): performed by neurologist; confirms type (axonal vs demyelinating), severity, distribution; required for CIDP, GBS diagnosis

**Diabetic foot assessment (annual — critical):**
- 10-g monofilament: 4 sites per foot; any site not felt = high-risk foot
- Vibration: 128 Hz tuning fork
- Inspection: skin integrity, callous, ulcers, deformity (Charcot foot), nail changes
- Vascular: palpate foot pulses (dorsalis pedis, posterior tibial); ABI if absent pulses
- Refer to multidisciplinary diabetic foot clinic if: ulcer, absent pulses, severe deformity, Charcot foot
- Footwear: well-fitting; padded; avoid going barefoot; prescribed therapeutic footwear for high-risk feet

## Key Differentials

- Lumbar spinal stenosis: neurogenic claudication (leg pain, numbness with walking; relieved by rest and flexion); MRI lumbar spine; surgery/physio
- Radiculopathy: dermatomal distribution; back pain; NCS/EMG; see `low-back-pain-chronic` entry
- Myelopathy: upper motor neuron signs (spasticity, hyperreflexia, upgoing plantars); spinal cord compression
- Restless legs syndrome: uncomfortable leg sensations relieved by movement; nocturnal; no sensory deficit on examination; see `restless-legs` entry
- Guillain-Barré syndrome (GBS): acute ascending weakness; areflexia; CSF protein elevated; 000 — rapidly fatal if respiratory muscles involved
- CIDP (Chronic Inflammatory Demyelinating Polyneuropathy): progressive or relapsing; NCS shows demyelination; responds to IVIG or steroids; neurology

## Management Principles

**Treat the underlying cause:**
- Diabetic neuropathy: optimise glycaemic control; HbA1c <7% prevents and slows progression; SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin) and GLP-1 agonists may have neuroprotective effects
- Vitamin B12 deficiency: B12 replacement — oral high-dose B12 (1000 µg daily) as effective as IM in non-malabsorption; IM cyanocobalamin for malabsorption (pernicious anaemia, post-gastrectomy)
- Alcohol neuropathy: cessation (see `alcohol-use` entry); thiamine supplementation (B1) mandatory
- Hypothyroid neuropathy: thyroxine replacement — partial or full reversal
- Medication-induced: cease offending agent if possible; discuss risk-benefit with treating specialist for chemotherapy-induced neuropathy

**Neuropathic pain management (multiple evidence-based options — try in order):**

First-line:
- Duloxetine (SNRI): 30–60 mg daily; strong evidence for DPN; also treats comorbid depression; PBS-listed for depression/GAD — off-label for DPN but widely used; nausea initially — start 30 mg
- Pregabalin (Lyrica): 75–300 mg/day (in divided doses); PBS-listed for diabetic peripheral neuropathy; effective for pain and sleep; sedation and weight gain common; dependence potential
- Gabapentin: similar mechanism; PBS-listed for neuropathic pain; alternative to pregabalin
- Amitriptyline (TCA): 10–75 mg nocte; improves pain and sleep; caution in elderly (falls, anticholinergic); evidence for DPN; not PBS-listed specifically for neuropathic pain but widely used

Second-line:
- Opioids: tramadol (weak opioid with additional SNRI activity) may be useful; strong opioids generally NOT recommended for DPN — see `chronic-pain` entry; if used, limit duration and dose
- Capsaicin patch (high-concentration — 8% Qutenza): specialist-applied; local application; burns initially; PBS-listed for post-herpetic neuralgia; specialist use
- Topical lidocaine patches: localised allodynia; not PBS-listed; limited evidence

Physical and non-pharmacological:
- TENS (transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation): modest evidence; safe; non-invasive
- Physiotherapy: strengthening, balance, gait training for motor neuropathy and fall prevention
- Pain clinic: for refractory neuropathic pain; multimodal; see `chronic-pain` entry

**Foot care (diabetic neuropathy — crucial to prevent amputation):**
- Patient education: inspect feet daily; no barefoot walking; dry between toes; trim nails carefully; medical-grade insoles
- Podiatrist: regular review; PBS-funded for people with type 1 or 2 diabetes (Enhanced Primary Care)
- Urgent referral: any foot ulcer, acute Charcot foot (warm, red, swollen foot — not infected; acute fracture/dislocation; immobilise and refer urgently to orthopaedics/vascular)

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Pregabalin (Lyrica)** (PBS restricted: diabetic peripheral neuropathy — listed indication; also fibromyalgia, epilepsy; 75 mg BD initially, titrate to 150–300 mg BD; sedation, weight gain, dependency risk)
- **Gabapentin** (PBS restricted: epilepsy and neuropathic pain; 300–1200 mg TDS; alternative to pregabalin)
- **Duloxetine (Cymbalta)** (PBS restricted: major depressive disorder and GAD; off-label for DPN but widely prescribed; 30–60 mg daily; nausea initially)
- **Amitriptyline** (PBS unrestricted: depression; 10–25 mg nocte for neuropathic pain; caution in elderly)
- **Cyanocobalamin injection** (PBS restricted: B12 deficiency — pernicious anaemia; IM 1 mg every 3 months maintenance)
- **Oral B12 1000 µg** (PBS unrestricted: B12 supplementation; effective for dietary deficiency; 1000 µg daily)
- **Note:** Opioids are NOT recommended as first-line for neuropathic pain; if needed, tramadol is preferred weak opioid with additional SNRI mechanism

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Guillain-Barré syndrome: acute ascending weakness + areflexia — can progress to respiratory failure within hours; 000; IVIG or plasmapheresis in hospital
- Diabetic Charcot foot: acute hot, red, swollen foot without ulcer in diabetic patient — orthopaedic/vascular emergency; immobilise immediately and refer

**Urgent neurology:**
- Rapidly progressive neuropathy: diagnose GBS, CIDP, vasculitic neuropathy — all treatable
- Mononeuritis multiplex: multiple nerve lesions — vasculitis workup
- Suspected CIDP: subacute progression over months; relapsing-remitting; NCS essential; IVIG treatment

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Neurologist: diagnostic uncertainty; rapidly progressive; atypical features; CIDP; GBS subacute
- Podiatrist: all diabetic neuropathy patients — regular foot care (MBS-funded)
- Diabetic foot clinic: any diabetic foot ulcer, high-risk foot, Charcot foot
- Pain medicine specialist: refractory neuropathic pain

## Telehealth Suitability

Neuropathic pain monitoring is suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Neuropathic pain medication review and titration
- B12, HbA1c, thyroid result discussion
- Patient education (foot care, fall prevention)
- Referral coordination (podiatrist, neurologist, pain clinic)
- Chronic Disease Management plan review

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Neurological examination (monofilament, vibration, reflexes, motor testing)
- Diabetic foot examination
- Blood tests — pathology arranged
- NCS/EMG — neurology specialist
