## Subconjunctival Haemorrhage Assessment (RACGP)

A subconjunctival haemorrhage (SCH) is bleeding between the conjunctiva and the underlying sclera, typically appearing as a bright red patch on the white of the eye. It is a common, usually benign condition that causes considerable patient alarm due to its dramatic appearance despite the patient being asymptomatic. The conjunctival blood vessels are fragile and may rupture following minor trauma, increased intraocular venous pressure (Valsalva manoeuvres — coughing, sneezing, straining), or spontaneously. Most SCHs are idiopathic. The GP's primary role is to: reassure the patient about the benign and self-limiting nature, identify the rare serious causes (trauma, anticoagulation, hypertension, clotting disorder, conjunctival malignancy), and exclude other serious red eye conditions (hyphaema, acute angle-closure glaucoma). Complete resolution takes 2–3 weeks as the blood reabsorbs. No treatment is required in most cases. Telehealth is suitable after diagnosis established.

## Clinical Presentation

**Typical subconjunctival haemorrhage:**
- Bright red, flat, well-defined patch on the conjunctiva; may be small or involve entire conjunctiva
- Usually involves bulbar conjunctiva only; does NOT extend beyond the limbus (corneal margin)
- Painless: the most reassuring feature; any significant eye pain raises concern for other diagnoses
- No visual change: vision is normal; if visual disturbance present → refer urgently
- No discharge: clear; no mucopurulent discharge (distinguishes from conjunctivitis)
- Colour changes over time: bright red → dark red → yellow/orange (haemoglobin breakdown) over 2–3 weeks
- May extend and enlarge over first 24–48 hours before resolving

**Causes:**
- Idiopathic (most common): no identifiable cause; spontaneous rupture of fragile conjunctival capillaries
- Valsalva manoeuvres: coughing (pertussis, COPD), sneezing, straining at stool (constipation), heavy lifting, vomiting (bulimia, morning sickness), childbirth
- Minor trauma: eye rubbing; foreign body; contact lens insertion
- Anticoagulant or antiplatelet therapy: warfarin, direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs), aspirin — SCH more common; usually not an indication to alter therapy
- Hypertension: recurrent SCH without obvious cause → check BP; rarely the sole cause
- Bleeding diathesis: recurrent or bilateral SCH → coagulation screen (FBC, INR, APTT, platelet count)
- Conjunctival amyloidosis or lymphoma: recurrent bilateral; nodular appearance; biopsy required
- Trauma: penetrating eye injury (globe rupture) can present with haemorrhage — rule out by examination

## Assessment Approach

**History:**
- Onset: sudden; patient often discovers on waking or is told by others; painless
- Trauma: direct eye trauma; nose blow; coughing or straining episode
- Medications: anticoagulants (warfarin, apixaban, rivaroxaban), aspirin, clopidogrel, NSAIDs
- Recurrent episodes: previous SCH; frequency
- Systemic: hypertension, bleeding disorders, liver disease, leukaemia
- Symptoms: PAIN, VISION CHANGE, DISCHARGE — any of these = refer

**Examination:**
- Visual acuity: must be formally assessed; any reduction = refer urgently
- Slit lamp (if available) or hand-held biomicroscope: confirm blood is between conjunctiva and sclera (not intracameral — hyphaema); confirm corneal integrity
- Conjunctiva: extent of haemorrhage; does it extend over or under the eyelid? (perilimbal extension may indicate globe rupture)
- Pupil: round, reactive, equal; irregular pupil = serious pathology
- Anterior chamber: clear; hyphaema = blood in anterior chamber = serious; urgent ophthalmology
- Intraocular pressure assessment (if trained): if glaucoma concern
- Blood pressure measurement: particularly if recurrent or bilateral SCH
- Fluorescein stain: if corneal injury or foreign body suspected

**Investigations:**
- Blood pressure: all patients with recurrent SCH
- FBC + coagulation (INR, APTT, platelets): if recurrent, bilateral, or no identifiable precipitant + no anticoagulant use
- INR check: if on warfarin — confirm therapeutic range

## Key Differentials

- Conjunctivitis: discharge (purulent or watery); diffuse redness throughout conjunctiva; not loculated bright red patch; itchy (allergic)
- Hyphaema: blood in ANTERIOR CHAMBER (not conjunctiva); visible fluid level at inferior cornea; often traumatic; significant pain; urgent ophthalmology
- Acute angle-closure glaucoma: severe eye pain; haloes; fixed mid-dilated pupil; rock-hard eye; nausea/vomiting; 000; ophthalmology emergency
- Episcleritis: localised red sector of sclera; flat; blanches with topical phenylephrine (not SCH); aching pain; may be associated with autoimmune disease
- Scleritis: deep, boring, severe eye pain; bluish hue; does NOT blanch with phenylephrine; associated with rheumatoid arthritis; urgently refer
- Globe rupture: post-trauma; peaked/irregular pupil; prolapsed uveal tissue; periorbital oedema; urgent ophthalmology; 000
- Conjunctival melanoma or lymphoma: flat, pigmented or salmon-pink lesion; may be confused with old SCH; biopsy required if suspicious

## Management Principles

**Reassurance (primary management):**
- Explain: the appearance is dramatic but benign; blood is trapped under the clear conjunctiva
- Natural history: bright red → fades over 2–3 weeks; will spread slightly before resolving (gravity-dependent spread)
- No topical treatment is beneficial; drops do not speed resolution
- The patient may feel mild gritty discomfort as it resolves; preservative-free lubricating drops (artificial tears) for comfort if needed

**Artificial tears:**
- Preservative-free lubricating drops (e.g., HyloFresh, Refresh, Systane): for comfort if gritty sensation; OTC; 2–4 times daily as needed; NOT required for resolution but may improve comfort

**Address underlying cause if identified:**
- Anticoagulant-related: generally do NOT alter anticoagulant dosing for uncomplicated SCH; review next scheduled INR if on warfarin; patient education about minimising eye rubbing
- Hypertension: treat to target (see `hypertension`); regular SCH is rarely caused by hypertension alone but should prompt BP check
- Recurrent SCH: investigate underlying coagulopathy; ophthalmology review if recurrent without clear cause

**Valsalva cause — address:**
- Cough: treat underlying cause (URTI, GORD, ACE inhibitor cough); pertussis in unvaccinated
- Constipation: dietary fibre, adequate hydration, laxatives if needed
- Heavy lifting: avoid for a few days until resolved; advise breathe through lifts

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **No prescription required for isolated uncomplicated SCH**
- **Preservative-free artificial tears (HyloFresh, Refresh Tears)**: OTC; for comfort; not required for resolution; advise patient; minimal cost
- **Note:** Topical vasoconstrictors (Visine, Naphazoline) should NOT be used for SCH — they do not help resolution and may mask other pathology; they constrict superficial vessels but do not affect the blood under the conjunctiva.

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ophthalmology emergency:**
- Globe rupture (post-trauma): peaked pupil, prolapsed tissue, severe pain — do NOT apply pressure; shield the eye; 000
- Acute angle-closure glaucoma: severe eye pain, haloes, nausea, fixed mid-dilated pupil — 000; ophthalmology emergency
- Hyphaema (blood in anterior chamber): urgent ophthalmology same-day

**Urgent ophthalmology referral (same-day or within 24–48 hours):**
- Visual acuity reduced: any visual change with red eye = urgent ophthalmology
- Significant eye pain: SCH is painless; pain suggests another diagnosis
- Trauma with SCH: assess for globe rupture; periorbital haematoma ("black eye") with SCH needs slit-lamp examination
- Irregular or non-reactive pupil

**Non-urgent ophthalmology:**
- Recurrent SCH without identifiable cause after coagulation screen
- Suspicious conjunctival lesion (biopsy)

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Reassurance for typical, asymptomatic SCH once clinical diagnosis established
- Follow-up to confirm resolution at 2–3 weeks
- BP management advice for recurrent SCH
- Anticoagulant management discussion (do not alter based on SCH alone)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- ALL first presentations of red eye: exclude hyphaema, acute glaucoma, globe rupture, and other serious conditions; visual acuity must be formally assessed
- Any eye trauma: even if appears minor
