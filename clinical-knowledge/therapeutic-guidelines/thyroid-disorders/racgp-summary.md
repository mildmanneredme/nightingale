## Thyroid Disorders Assessment (RACGP)

Thyroid disorders — predominantly hypothyroidism and hyperthyroidism — are among the most common endocrine conditions managed in Australian general practice. Hypothyroidism affects approximately 1–2% of Australians (more common in women), while hyperthyroidism affects around 0.5%. Both conditions are highly manageable with appropriate pharmacotherapy and monitoring. Telehealth is highly suitable for results review, dose adjustment, and ongoing monitoring of stable thyroid disease.

## Clinical Presentation

**Hypothyroidism:** Fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation, dry skin and hair, hair loss, bradycardia, depression, cognitive slowing, myalgia, heavy menstrual bleeding, peripheral oedema, hoarse voice. Subclinical hypothyroidism: elevated TSH with normal free T4 — often asymptomatic or mildly symptomatic.

**Hyperthyroidism:** Weight loss (despite increased appetite), heat intolerance, palpitations/tachycardia, tremor, anxiety, irritability, insomnia, frequent loose stools, sweating, exertional dyspnoea. Graves' disease additionally: proptosis (exophthalmos), pretibial myxoedema, thyroid bruit. Subclinical hyperthyroidism: suppressed TSH with normal free T3/T4.

**Thyroid nodules:** Usually asymptomatic; found incidentally on imaging or examination. Most benign; minority malignant — risk factors for malignancy include young age, male sex, history of head/neck radiation, family history of thyroid cancer.

**Thyroiditis:** Hashimoto's (chronic autoimmune — most common cause of hypothyroidism in AU); De Quervain's (subacute — painful, post-viral, self-limiting); postpartum thyroiditis (transient hypo- then hyperthyroidism after delivery in ~5–10% of women).

## Assessment Approach

Initial investigation: TSH is the single best screening test for thyroid dysfunction. If TSH abnormal, add free T4 (and free T3 if TSH suppressed, to exclude T3-thyrotoxicosis).

For hypothyroidism: TSH elevated + free T4 low = overt hypothyroidism; TSH elevated + free T4 normal = subclinical hypothyroidism.

For hyperthyroidism: TSH suppressed + free T4/T3 elevated = overt hyperthyroidism; TSH suppressed + normal free T4/T3 = subclinical hyperthyroidism.

Further investigations:
- Thyroid antibodies: anti-TPO (Hashimoto's); TSH-receptor antibodies (TRAb) — Graves' disease
- Thyroid ultrasound: for nodule assessment, goitre, structural abnormality
- Thyroid uptake scan (nuclear medicine): differentiates Graves' from toxic nodule/multinodular goitre — refer to nuclear medicine or endocrinologist

Monitoring stable hypothyroidism: TSH at 6–12 weeks after each dose change; then every 6–12 months when stable.

Monitoring hyperthyroidism on carbimazole: TFTs (TSH, fT4, fT3) 4–6 weekly during titration; FBC for agranulocytosis (bone marrow suppression — rare but serious; patient education essential).

## Key Differentials

- Depression — overlaps significantly with hypothyroidism; thyroid function should be part of initial depression workup
- Anxiety disorder — overlaps with hyperthyroidism; check TFTs in new anxiety presentations
- Cardiac arrhythmias (especially AF) — hyperthyroidism is a cause of new AF; check TFTs in new AF
- Anaemia — fatigue overlap with hypothyroidism; check FBC concurrently
- Adrenal insufficiency — fatigue, weight loss, electrolyte abnormalities; check in patients with pituitary disease
- Thyroid cancer — usually presents as nodule; fine needle aspiration biopsy (FNA) for suspicious nodules on ultrasound

## Management Principles

**Overt hypothyroidism — levothyroxine replacement:**
- Start dose: 1.6 mcg/kg lean body weight daily (lower starting dose 25–50 mcg in elderly or cardiac disease; titrate up slowly)
- Take on an empty stomach 30–60 minutes before food or other medications (significantly affects absorption)
- Target TSH: 0.5–2.5 mIU/L for most adults (individualise for age, symptoms, comorbidities)
- Recheck TSH 6–8 weeks after dose change; adjust by 12.5–25 mcg increments
- Once stable: TSH every 6–12 months

Subclinical hypothyroidism treatment:
- TSH >10 mIU/L: treat (risk of progression and cardiovascular effects)
- TSH 5–10 mIU/L: treat if symptomatic, pregnant or planning pregnancy, anti-TPO antibody positive, or cardiovascular risk factors. Monitor if asymptomatic.

Pregnancy and hypothyroidism: treat all hypothyroid pregnant women; target TSH <2.5 mIU/L in first trimester; dose requirements increase in pregnancy (usually by 25–50%) — close monitoring by obstetric team.

**Hyperthyroidism — management:**

Carbimazole (antithyroid drug): first-line medical therapy; block thyroid hormone synthesis. Starting dose typically 20–40 mg daily; reduce as thyroid function normalises (titrate to maintain euthyroid state). Two approaches: titration (dose reduced as euthyroid) or block-and-replace (high carbimazole + levothyroxine). Duration typically 12–18 months for Graves'; 30–50% remission rate after first course.

Propranolol: symptomatic relief of tremor, palpitations, and anxiety in hyperthyroidism; use until euthyroid. Does not treat underlying disease.

Definitive therapy for Graves' or toxic nodule (specialist-managed):
- Radioactive iodine (RAI) therapy — effective; results in hypothyroidism requiring lifelong levothyroxine
- Thyroidectomy — rapid cure; risk of hypoparathyroidism and laryngeal nerve injury

**Agranulocytosis warning:** Patients on carbimazole must be counselled to stop the medication and present urgently (same day) if they develop sore throat, fever, or mouth ulcers — early sign of agranulocytosis. FBC urgently.

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Levothyroxine (thyroxine)** (Eutroxsig, Oroxine — PBS unrestricted for hypothyroidism) — 25 mcg, 50 mcg, 75 mcg, 88 mcg, 100 mcg tablets; take 30–60 min before food
- **Carbimazole** (PBS restricted: hyperthyroidism) — 5 mg or 20 mg tablets; initiate at 20–40 mg daily; titrate to clinical response and TFTs
- **Propylthiouracil** (PTU — PBS restricted: hyperthyroidism; second-line to carbimazole; preferred in first trimester pregnancy due to lower teratogenicity)
- **Propranolol** (PBS unrestricted — symptomatic relief of hyperthyroid symptoms)
- Note: carbimazole and PTU require FBC monitoring for agranulocytosis — inform patient of sore throat/fever warning at every prescription

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Thyroid storm (thyrotoxic crisis): high fever (>38.5°C), tachycardia/arrhythmia, vomiting, confusion, altered consciousness in known or undiagnosed hyperthyroid patient — rare but life-threatening emergency
- Myxoedema coma: severe hypothyroidism with altered consciousness, hypothermia, bradycardia, hypoventilation — medical emergency

**Urgent same-day assessment:**
- Agranulocytosis on carbimazole: sore throat + fever in patient taking carbimazole — stop carbimazole, urgent FBC, hospital assessment if neutropaenia confirmed
- New AF with TSH <0.1 mIU/L — haemodynamic instability warrants urgent cardiology assessment

**Non-urgent specialist referral:**
- Endocrinology: Graves' hyperthyroidism (RAI or surgery decision), subclinical hyperthyroidism (especially in elderly/cardiac disease), thyroid nodule requiring FNA, suspected thyroid cancer, complex hypothyroidism (secondary/central), refractory cases
- Ophthalmology: Graves' ophthalmopathy (proptosis, corneal involvement)
- Thyroid/general surgeon: thyroid nodule requiring surgery, goitre with compressive symptoms
- Obstetrics: hypothyroid or hyperthyroid pregnant patient — shared care essential

## Telehealth Suitability

Thyroid disorder monitoring is highly suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- TSH/TFT results review and levothyroxine dose adjustment
- Carbimazole monitoring results review
- Symptom assessment (hypothyroid or hyperthyroid)
- Drug interaction counselling (levothyroxine — calcium, iron, PPIs reduce absorption)
- Pregnancy counselling and dose adjustment review

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Thyroid examination (palpation for goitre, nodule, bruit)
- Clinically suspected agranulocytosis — urgent in-person blood test
- New AF requiring ECG
- Graves' ophthalmopathy assessment
- Ultrasound and FNA for thyroid nodules
