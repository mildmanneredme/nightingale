## Advance Care Planning Assessment (RACGP)

Advance care planning (ACP) is a process that supports individuals to reflect on and communicate their values, goals, and preferences about future health care — particularly for times when they may be unable to speak for themselves. ACP encompasses: identifying a substitute decision-maker (enduring power of attorney for personal/health decisions), completing an advance health directive (or advance care plan), and ensuring preferences are known to health professionals and documented in medical records. Studies show that when ACP has been completed, people are more likely to receive care consistent with their wishes, experience better quality of death, and families experience less distress. The GP is ideally placed to initiate and facilitate ACP — particularly for patients with chronic illness, older patients, and those approaching end of life. MBS item 110 (health assessment for older persons ≥75 years) requires ACP as a component. Telehealth is suitable for ACP conversations.

## Clinical Presentation

**When to initiate ACP conversations:**
- Age ≥65 (or ≥45 for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples — higher mortality burden and complex health needs)
- Diagnosis of serious illness: cancer, heart failure, COPD, CKD stage 4–5, dementia (while capacity exists), motor neurone disease
- After a significant health event: hospitalisation, ICU admission, major surgery, TIA/stroke
- Entering residential aged care: ACP should be standard admission process
- Patient or family-initiated: patient raises concerns about future care, death, or not wanting aggressive treatment
- Patient has cognitive decline: while capacity exists and before capacity is lost

**Key components of an ACP conversation:**
- Values and what makes life meaningful to the patient
- Fears and concerns about future health situations
- Goals of medical care: curative, life-prolonging, comfort-focused, or a combination depending on circumstances
- Preferences about specific treatments: CPR, mechanical ventilation, artificial nutrition, hospitalisation, dialysis
- Preferred place of care and death: home, hospital, hospice, residential aged care
- Who the patient wants involved in decisions

**Capacity:**
- ACP requires decisional capacity: ability to understand information, appreciate consequences, reason between options, and communicate a choice
- Once capacity lost: substitute decision-maker acts according to documented wishes and known values
- If no ACP or substitute decision-maker: decision-makers by hierarchy (spouse/de facto first; then adult children; then parents; then guardian appointed by ACAT/QCAT — state specific)

## Assessment Approach

**Starting the ACP conversation:**
- Normalise: "I have these conversations with all my patients. It helps me and your family know what's most important to you if you were ever too unwell to tell us yourself."
- ACP is NOT about predicting death — it's about ensuring care aligns with the patient's values at any age or illness stage
- Ask open questions: "What would be most important to you if you became very ill?" "What worries you about the future?" "Are there treatments you would or wouldn't want?"
- Use ACP Australia's "Talking about end of life" conversation starters and patient resources (advancecareplanning.org.au)

**Legal framework — state-specific (critical to know):**

Australia has different laws in each state and territory for ACP documents and substitute decision-makers:
- NSW: Enduring Guardianship (Guardianship Act); Advance Care Directive (ACD) — legally recognised
- VIC: Enduring Power of Attorney (Medical Treatment); Advance Care Directive (Health Decisions Act 2017 — binding)
- QLD: Advance Health Directive; Enduring Power of Attorney (for personal matters)
- WA: Enduring Power of Guardianship; Advance Health Directive
- SA: Advance Care Directive (ACD) — covers both future consent and substitute decision-making
- TAS: Enduring Power of Attorney (Health and Welfare Guardianship)
- ACT: Health Direction (Medical Treatment Act 1994)
- NT: Advance Personal Plan

All states: substitute decision-maker (regardless of title) must act on the patient's known wishes (not their own preferences); if unknown — must act in patient's best interests

**Documenting ACP:**
- Record ACP discussion in medical record
- Scan and attach signed ACD to health record
- Share with relevant parties: patient, family/carer, substitute decision-maker, hospital, residential aged care facility, ambulance service
- Upload to My Health Record (ACP can be uploaded as a Shared Health Summary note or document)
- Emergency Medical Plan / Acute Resuscitation Plan: specific hospital forms that convey resuscitation wishes to treating team; important in residential aged care — should accompany patient to hospital

**Resuscitation planning:**
- CPR discussion: realistic discussion of CPR outcomes (for in-hospital cardiac arrest, 15–20% survive to discharge; community arrest lower); CPR is painful, undignified, and often unsuccessful in frail or seriously ill patients — honest framing
- DNRO (Do Not Resuscitate Order) / DNRCPR: documented in medical record AND on state-specific ambulance forms (varies: NSW MTP form, VIC AROD/Ambulance Victoria, QLD ADIR)
- Ambulance forms enable paramedics to honour DNRCPR — without this, paramedics are legally obligated to attempt CPR

## Key Differentials

- Advance Care Plan vs Advance Health Directive: ACP is a process and document (informal); AHD is a legally binding document (state-specific) — both are valuable; AHD has legal force
- Enduring Power of Attorney vs Guardianship: EPA covers financial decisions; enduring guardianship/EPA personal covers medical and personal decisions — both needed
- Substitute decision-maker vs next of kin: next of kin has no legal status in most jurisdictions without formal appointment; family members may disagree with medical decisions without formal authority
- Voluntary Assisted Dying (VAD) vs ACP: VAD (legal in all Australian states as of 2023) is an active process of requesting and receiving lethal medication; ACP governs future medical treatment preferences — both may be relevant and are separate processes

## Management Principles

**Three-step ACP approach for GPs:**

Step 1 — Think and talk:
- Encourage patient to think about and discuss values with family/loved ones
- Provide ACP Australia resources: "Planning Ahead" booklet; state-specific forms and guides
- Allow multiple conversations — ACP is rarely completed in one consultation

Step 2 — Choose a substitute decision-maker:
- Identify a person who knows the patient's values and will advocate for their wishes
- Complete enduring power of attorney or guardianship paperwork (state-specific)
- This person can also be given a copy of the advance care plan

Step 3 — Write it down:
- Complete an advance health directive/advance care plan (state-specific form or My Health Record ACP template)
- Sign and witness per state-specific requirements (usually two witnesses, not family members)
- Distribute copies: patient, substitute decision-maker, GP medical record, My Health Record, hospital, aged care facility

**MBS items supporting ACP:**
- MBS item 110: Health Assessment for older persons (≥75 years) — ACP is a required component; rebatable annually; 45+ minutes
- MBS items 3001–3005 (Palliative Care Planning Items 2022): include goals of care discussions; see `palliative-care-gp` entry

**ACP in residential aged care:**
- All residents should have an ACP on admission (ACQSC — Aged Care Quality and Safety Commission standard)
- Emergency Medical Plan / Urgent Care Plan: brief document conveys acute care preferences to ED staff and ambulance
- Quarterly review of ACP in residential care is good practice
- GP visits to residential aged care: MBS item 90065 (extended residential care consultation)

**ACP in culturally and linguistically diverse populations:**
- Many cultures have different views on death, disclosure of diagnosis, and family decision-making
- Interpreter services: Translating and Interpreting Service (TIS National) 131 450 — free for Medicare card holders via GP
- ACP Australia has translated resources for multiple languages
- Explore who the patient wants involved in decisions — may not be the legal decision-maker hierarchy

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- There are no medications specifically prescribed for ACP
- ACP enables appropriate withholding or withdrawal of medications at end of life
- Comfort medications prescribed proactively at end of life (just-in-case medications): see `palliative-care-gp` entry for prescribing details
- Note: Advance Health Directives can legally refuse specific treatments, including blood transfusions, dialysis, or resuscitation — GPs must respect valid legally executed directives

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent ACP discussions (initiate promptly — before capacity is lost):**
- New diagnosis of dementia: while capacity is intact — most important window for ACP
- New diagnosis of terminal or life-limiting illness
- After ICU or major acute illness where capacity was temporarily lost
- Patient with dementia showing significant cognitive decline — act before capacity lost

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Social worker: assistance with completing ACP documents, family communication
- Advance Care Planning facilitator: specialised ACP nurses and facilitators (some hospitals, palliative care services)
- Guardianship tribunal: if decision-maker appointment is complex or contested (ACAT/QCAT — state specific)
- Specialist palliative care: ACP embedded in palliative care process; joint planning with specialist
- Advance Care Planning Australia: 1300 208 582 — free resources and consultation support for health professionals

## Telehealth Suitability

ACP conversations are highly suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Initial ACP conversation and values exploration
- Review and update of existing ACP documents
- Family meeting facilitation (multiple family members can join telehealth)
- Health assessment (MBS item 110) ACP component
- Palliative care planning (MBS items 3001–3005)
- Counselling about resuscitation preferences
- Coordination with substitute decision-makers

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Physical examination for clinical condition informing ACP discussions
- Capacity assessment if cognitive impairment suspected (in-person preferred)
- Witnessing legal documents (AHD/EPA) — state-specific requirements; GP can be witness in some jurisdictions
