## Warts Assessment (RACGP)

Viral warts (verrucae) are common benign skin lesions caused by human papillomavirus (HPV), affecting approximately 12% of the population at any time. They are most prevalent in school-age children and young adults, with a second peak in immunocompromised patients. Most warts in immunocompetent individuals resolve spontaneously within 2 years (65–78%); however, treatment is often sought for cosmetic, painful, or spreading disease. HPV types causing cutaneous warts (types 1, 2, 4, 27, 57) are distinct from genital HPV types (6, 11 — condylomata acuminata; 16, 18 — cervical cancer). GPs manage most cutaneous warts with topical salicylic acid or liquid nitrogen cryotherapy; genital warts require specific management and sexual health counselling. Telehealth has limited utility for wart diagnosis and treatment; in-person treatment is required.

## Clinical Presentation

**Types of warts:**

Common warts (verruca vulgaris — HPV 2, 4):
- Firm, papular, hyperkeratotic skin-coloured papules; rough surface; black dots (thrombosed capillaries — pathognomonic)
- Dorsum of hands and fingers most common; may coalesce into mosaic plaques
- May spread to face, scalp via shaving

Plantar warts (verruca plantaris — HPV 1):
- On weight-bearing surfaces of sole; often painful (pressure from walking)
- Inward growth from pressure; disrupts dermatoglyphics (fingerprint lines)
- Hyperkeratotic with black dots; may be mistaken for callus (callus has intact skin lines; wart disrupts them)
- Mosaic plantar wart: large cluster of confluent lesions — very difficult to treat

Flat warts (verruca plana — HPV 3, 10):
- Small flat-topped pink/brown papules; face, shins, dorsum hands; minimal keratosis
- Commonly spread by shaving (face, legs); multiple lesions in lines (Koebner phenomenon)

Filiform warts:
- Pedunculated, thread-like; face especially eyelids, lips, nose; rapid growth

Genital warts (condylomata acuminata — HPV 6, 11):
- Soft, fleshy, cauliflower-like papules in anogenital region; perianal, vaginal, cervical, urethral, oral
- NOT the same virus as cutaneous warts; sexually transmitted; see sexual health section below
- Do NOT use salicylic acid on genital warts

**Molluscum contagiosum:**
- Pearly, umbilicated papules with central dimple; usually children; NOT warts (different virus — poxvirus); self-limiting; usually no treatment required

## Assessment Approach

**Clinical diagnosis — no investigations required in typical presentations:**
- Characteristic appearance: hyperkeratosis, black dots (thrombosed capillaries), disrupted skin lines (plantar)
- Paring: pare wart surface; black dots confirm diagnosis (callus has no dots)
- Location and number: guides management complexity

**Investigations if uncertain:**
- Dermoscopy: black dots within papillary loops; highly specific for warts; available in dermatoscopy-equipped practices
- Punch biopsy: if diagnosis uncertain (e.g., amelanotic melanoma, squamous cell carcinoma, porokeratosis); any wart that fails to respond to treatment after 2–3 years, bleeds easily, or has atypical features → biopsy
- HPV genotyping: rarely required; if recurrent plantar or recalcitrant warts with concern for higher-risk HPV

**Genital warts — specific assessment:**
- Sexual history: recent partners; HPV vaccination status
- Pelvic examination and cervical screen (Cervical Screening Test): women with genital warts; HPV cervical screen
- STI testing: concurrent STIs common; chlamydia, gonorrhoea, syphilis serology, HIV
- Partner notification: all current/recent sexual partners advised to seek assessment

## Key Differentials

- Callus: weight-bearing area; no black dots; intact skin lines; responds to keratolytic; no wart virus
- Seborrhoeic keratosis: older patients; stuck-on appearance; horn cysts on dermoscopy; older age distribution; face and trunk; no black dots
- Molluscum contagiosum: pearly, umbilicated; no black dots; children usually; poxvirus
- Amelanotic melanoma: history of changing lesion; no black dots; biopsy if unsure
- Squamous cell carcinoma: immunocompromised; non-healing; atypical morphology; biopsy
- Corn (heloma): similar site to plantar wart; intact skin lines; no black dots; painful on direct pressure
- Condylomata lata (secondary syphilis): flat, moist genital lesions; syphilis serology

## Management Principles

**No treatment required (valid option):**
- Immunocompetent patients: 65–78% of cutaneous warts resolve spontaneously within 2 years
- Children especially: high spontaneous resolution; may opt for watchful waiting if asymptomatic
- Discuss with patient: treatment is not mandatory if wart is asymptomatic and not spreading

**Topical salicylic acid (first-line for most cutaneous warts):**
- Mechanism: keratolytic; also irritation-induced immune activation
- Duofilm, Occlusal (salicylic acid 15–17% in collodion): apply daily after soaking; file dead skin; continue for 12–16 weeks
- Higher concentrations (Compound W 40% or Duoplant 16.7%): stronger; for plantar warts especially
- Technique: soak wart in warm water 5 minutes → file hyperkeratotic surface (nail file/pumice stone — do not share) → apply small amount of solution to wart only (avoid surrounding skin) → allow to dry → cover with occlusive tape
- Evidence: comparable to cryotherapy over 3 months; better for home use; cheaper; no pain
- Continue even if progress is slow — consistency over 12 weeks is key
- OTC availability: Duofilm, Occlusal, Compound W — widely available

**Cryotherapy with liquid nitrogen (LN2) — GP procedure:**
- Application: liquid nitrogen spray or cotton-tipped applicator; freeze-thaw cycle; 5–30 seconds freeze depending on lesion size and site
- Blister forms over 24–48 hours; sloughs after 1–2 weeks
- 2–4 week intervals; repeat as necessary; 3–6 sessions typical
- Evidence: success rate ~50–60% at 3 months; no better than salicylic acid in RCTs; preferred for quick visible treatment and patient confidence
- Pain: significant — warn patients; local anaesthetic cream (EMLA) 1 hour before for children or pain-sensitive patients
- Complications: blistering, post-inflammatory hypopigmentation (darker skin types), nail dystrophy (periungual warts)
- Avoid cryotherapy: face, genital warts (use specialist treatment)

**Duct tape occlusion:**
- Limited evidence; safe; applied continuously for 6 days, filed, left uncovered overnight, repeat
- Meta-analyses show modest benefit; may work via irritation-induced immunity; useful in children who tolerate salicylic acid poorly

**Other GP treatments:**
- Topical immunotherapy (diphenylcyclopropenone — DPCP): specialist dermatology; contact sensitiser; induces immune response; for recalcitrant warts
- Intralesional bleomycin: specialist; for treatment-resistant plantar warts
- Imiquimod 5% cream (Aldara): PBS-listed for genital warts; also used off-label for flat warts; immune modulator

**Genital warts:**
- Podophyllotoxin 0.15% cream (Condyline): patient-applied to visible lesions BD × 3 days/week × 4 weeks; NOT for internal, cervical, or anal warts; NOT in pregnancy
- Imiquimod 5% cream (Aldara): patient-applied at bedtime 3× weekly × up to 16 weeks; immune modulator; PBS-listed for condylomata acuminata; may continue post-visible wart clearance for recurrence prevention
- Trichloroacetic acid (TCA 85%): GP-applied to each individual lesion; burns surrounding skin if not careful; effective for moist external genital warts; requires serial applications
- Surgical/laser: large, extensive, or unresponsive warts; genito-urinary or sexual health specialist
- HPV vaccination: Gardasil 9; effective PREVENTION; does NOT treat existing warts; advise all eligible patients and their sexual partners who are eligible

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Salicylic acid products (Duofilm, Occlusal 17%)** (PBS unrestricted or OTC: wart treatment; patient applies daily; review at 6 weeks for response; continue for 12 weeks)
- **Imiquimod 5% cream (Aldara)** (PBS restricted: condylomata acuminata — genital warts; 3× weekly; up to 16 weeks; wash off after 6–10 hours; can cause local irritation; NOT for cervical/vaginal/anal internal warts)
- **Podophyllotoxin 0.15% cream (Condyline)** (PBS restricted: genital warts — external only; patient-applied; BD × 3 days/week × 4 weeks; not in pregnancy; avoid on non-keratinised mucosa)
- **Note:** LN2 cryotherapy for cutaneous warts is performed as a GP procedure — no PBS item number but charged as standard consultation; freeze-thaw technique, not just surface touch
- **HPV vaccine (Gardasil 9)**: NIP-funded for females and males aged 9–25 years (schedule varies); private for adults >26 years; prevents genital wart-causing HPV 6 and 11 as well as oncogenic types 16, 18 + 5 others

## Escalation Triggers

**Non-urgent dermatology:**
- Recalcitrant warts unresponsive to 3–6 months of salicylic acid and 6 cryotherapy sessions: dermatology for advanced treatments (DPCP, intralesional bleomycin)
- Atypical appearance or failure of extensive treatment: biopsy to exclude SCC

**Non-urgent sexual health clinic:**
- Extensive, internal, or recurrent genital warts: sexual health specialist for colposcopy, anoscopy, peniscopy, or podophyllotoxin-resistant disease management

## Telehealth Suitability

Wart management has limited telehealth utility; initial treatment and cryotherapy require in-person attendance.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Review of treatment response to salicylic acid (patient describes/photos)
- Counselling on treatment expectations and persistence
- Genital wart medication prescribing (imiquimod, podophyllotoxin) after initial in-person diagnosis
- HPV vaccination counselling

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial diagnosis (paring and dermoscopy)
- Cryotherapy treatment
- First genital wart assessment (STI screen, pelvic examination)
- Biopsy of atypical lesions
