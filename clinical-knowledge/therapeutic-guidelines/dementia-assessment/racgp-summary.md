## Dementia Assessment (RACGP)

Dementia describes a syndrome of progressive cognitive decline sufficient to interfere with daily functioning, caused by neurodegenerative or vascular disease. Approximately 400,000 Australians live with dementia, making it the second leading cause of death and the leading cause of disability in older Australians. Alzheimer's disease is the most common subtype (60–70%), followed by vascular dementia, Lewy body dementia (LBD), and frontotemporal dementia (FTD). The GP is frequently the first point of contact for patients and families with cognitive concerns. The GP role encompasses: case finding, structured cognitive assessment, identification of treatable reversible causes, diagnostic workup, post-diagnostic support, medications, driving safety, advance care planning, and carer support. Telehealth has limited utility for cognitive assessment but is suitable for ongoing care coordination.

## Clinical Presentation

**Typical presentations by dementia subtype:**

Alzheimer's disease (most common):
- Insidious onset of episodic memory impairment: forgetting recent events, repetitive questions, misplacing items
- Progressive course; later involves language, visuospatial, executive functions
- Personality changes; mood disturbance; behavioural and psychological symptoms of dementia (BPSD)

Vascular dementia:
- Stepwise progression often associated with vascular events (stroke, TIA)
- Focal neurological deficits; executive dysfunction; slowed processing; gait abnormalities
- Risk factors: hypertension, AF, diabetes, smoking — vascular prevention critical

Lewy body dementia (LBD):
- Fluctuating cognition: marked day-to-day variation in alertness and attention
- Visual hallucinations (recurrent, well-formed): a core diagnostic feature
- Parkinsonism (motor features): bradykinesia, rigidity, gait changes
- REM sleep behaviour disorder: acting out dreams
- Extreme sensitivity to antipsychotics: AVOID antipsychotics (haloperidol, risperidone, olanzapine) — life-threatening extrapyramidal reactions; very low doses of quetiapine ONLY if absolutely necessary; liaise with geriatrician or old age psychiatrist

Frontotemporal dementia (FTD):
- Younger onset (typically 45–65)
- Behavioural variant: personality change, disinhibition, apathy, loss of empathy; memory relatively preserved initially
- Language variants (primary progressive aphasia): word-finding difficulty, agrammatism

**Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI):**
- Cognitive decline beyond age-expected but NOT impairing daily functioning
- Subjective complaint + objective test deficit + intact ADLs = MCI
- 10–15%/year conversion to dementia; close follow-up; lifestyle intervention; no approved pharmacotherapy
- Alzheimer's-type MCI: episodic memory impairment; highest conversion risk

**Behavioural and psychological symptoms of dementia (BPSD):**
- Agitation, aggression, wandering, sundowning, hallucinations, depression, anxiety — very common and distressing for carers
- First-line: non-pharmacological strategies (structured activities, environmental modification, carer education)

## Assessment Approach

**Structured cognitive assessment:**

MoCA (Montreal Cognitive Assessment):
- Preferred tool: 30-point; tests multiple cognitive domains; sensitive to MCI and mild dementia; validated in Australian populations
- Available free at mocatest.org; takes 10–15 minutes in clinic
- Score: ≥26 normal; 18–25 mild CI; 10–17 moderate; <10 severe
- Add 1 point if ≤12 years of education

MMSE (Mini-Mental State Examination):
- Traditional tool; 30-point; less sensitive for MCI and FTD; familiar to specialists
- Score: ≥24 normal; 18–23 mild; 10–17 moderate; <10 severe

GPCOG (GP Cognitive Assessment):
- Designed for GP setting; brief; includes informant interview; Medicare-rebatable for cognitive assessment under MBS item 721 (Health Assessment for Older Persons)

Informant history (essential): collateral history from family/carer; documents functional decline in daily activities; ADL and IADL assessment

**Investigations for reversible causes (mandatory):**
- FBC: anaemia
- ESR/CRP: inflammatory or infective cause
- Electrolytes, glucose, calcium: metabolic disturbance
- Renal function, LFTs: metabolic encephalopathy
- TSH: hypothyroidism (very common cause of reversible cognitive impairment)
- Vitamin B12, folate: deficiency causes reversible cognitive impairment
- Fasting glucose / HbA1c: diabetes
- Syphilis serology (RPR/TPPA): in at-risk populations or atypical presentations
- HIV serology: if risk factors
- Urinalysis: urinary tract infection (precipitates delirium, worsens dementia)

**Neuroimaging:**
- CT brain or MRI brain: exclude tumour, subdural haematoma, hydrocephalus, large stroke; MRI preferred (greater sensitivity for vascular changes, hippocampal atrophy)
- Not always needed if clinical picture is clear — discuss with specialist
- PET amyloid or FDG-PET: specialist-arranged; not routinely available in Australian public system; research or specialist practice

**Driving assessment — critical GP obligation:**
- Dementia impairs driving ability; AUSTROADS Medical Standards for Licensing apply
- Mild dementia: may still be safe to drive; requires formal occupational therapy on-road assessment; GP cannot clear patient based on clinical assessment alone
- Moderate-severe dementia: should not drive; GP must advise and document
- Report to licensing authority if patient refuses to comply and continues to drive (state-specific obligations)
- Family involvement essential; sometimes requires difficult conversations

## Key Differentials

- Delirium: acute onset (hours to days); fluctuating level of consciousness; precipitant (infection, medication, metabolic); delirium superimposed on dementia is very common — treat precipitant
- Depression (pseudodementia): cognitive impairment with depression; mood disturbance often precedes cognitive complaint; PHQ-9; trial of antidepressant; geriatric psychiatry if uncertain
- Medication side effects: anticholinergic burden, benzodiazepines, opioids, antihistamines, antipsychotics — medication review is essential; Beers Criteria
- Normal ageing: mild forgetfulness without functional impairment; MoCA ≥26; reassure and monitor
- Hypothyroidism: TSH; reversible
- Vitamin B12 deficiency: subacute combined degeneration; reversible with treatment
- Normal pressure hydrocephalus: triad of dementia + gait apraxia + urinary incontinence; CT brain; neurosurgery referral
- Chronic subdural haematoma: head injury (may be trivial in elderly); headache, cognitive change; CT brain — urgent surgical review

## Management Principles

**Post-diagnostic support (essential regardless of pharmacotherapy):**
- Disclose diagnosis sensitively; involve patient and family/carer
- Dementia Australia: 1800 100 500; national support organisation; case coordinator; counselling; carer support programs; Dementia Support Australia (DBMAS)
- GP Management Plan and Team Care Arrangement (MBS items 721, 723, 729): allocate allied health under CDP
- Advance care planning: discuss while patient has capacity; enduring power of attorney (legal — state-specific); advance health directive; goals of care
- Financial and legal planning: power of attorney; ACAT (Aged Care Assessment Team) — when home care or residential placement considered

**Pharmacotherapy:**

Alzheimer's disease (mild-moderate):
- Cholinesterase inhibitors: donepezil (Aricept) — first-line; 5–10 mg nocte; rivastigmine (patch or oral); galantamine
- PBS-restricted: Alzheimer's disease with MoCA ≥5; require specialist (neurologist, geriatrician, old age psychiatrist) to initiate; GP can continue prescribing
- Modest but clinically meaningful cognitive and functional benefit; not disease-modifying; continue if tolerated
- NMDA receptor antagonist: memantine (Ebixa) — moderate-severe AD; PBS-restricted; specialist initiation; can combine with donepezil
- New: lecanemab and donanemab (anti-amyloid monoclonal antibodies) — FDA approved 2023; not yet TGA approved or PBS-listed in Australia at time of writing; significant adverse effects (ARIA)

Vascular dementia: no specific PBS-listed pharmacotherapy; aggressive vascular risk factor management (BP, lipids, antiplatelet/anticoagulation for AF, diabetes)

BPSD management:
- Non-pharmacological first: structured activity, consistent routine, music therapy, sensory stimulation, carer education
- Depression in dementia: SSRI (sertraline, escitalopram) — modest benefit; start low
- Agitation/psychosis: AVOID antipsychotics where possible (increased mortality in dementia); if necessary — risperidone (PBS indication for dementia-related psychosis) at lowest effective dose × shortest time; LBD — AVOID antipsychotics entirely

**Carer support:**
- 60–70% of people with dementia are cared for at home; carer burden and burnout are major issues
- Refer carer to Dementia Australia and Carers Australia (1800 422 737)
- Carer health assessment: MBS item 701 (GP health assessment for carers)
- Community aged care packages: refer to ACAT for assessment and My Aged Care (1800 200 422)
- Residential care: ACAT assessment required; My Aged Care portal

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Donepezil (Aricept, generic)** (PBS restricted: mild-moderate Alzheimer's dementia; MoCA ≥5; specialist initiation required; GP continuation; 5–10 mg nocte; nausea, diarrhoea, bradycardia, insomnia)
- **Rivastigmine (Exelon — patch preferred)** (PBS restricted: Alzheimer's dementia and Parkinson's disease dementia; specialist initiation; patch 4.6–9.5 mg/24h; also Lewy body dementia — specialist guidance)
- **Memantine (Ebixa)** (PBS restricted: moderate-severe Alzheimer's dementia; specialist initiation; 5–20 mg daily; combined with donepezil)
- **Risperidone** (PBS restricted: dementia-related psychosis indication; 0.25–0.5 mg BD; short term only; avoid in LBD)
- **Note:** Anticholinergic medications worsen cognitive function — review and cease anticholinergics (oxybutynin, tricyclics, first-generation antihistamines) in all patients with dementia

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Acute delirium with unknown cause, medical emergency, or safety risk
- Falls with head injury (subdural haematoma — may be silent in dementia)
- Suicidal ideation or severe behavioural crisis

**Urgent specialist (geriatrician/old age psychiatrist/neurologist):**
- Diagnostic uncertainty — atypical features (young onset, rapid progression, focal neurology)
- Severe BPSD not responding to non-pharmacological management
- LBD suspected — specialist management essential (antipsychotic avoidance)
- Driving safety decision — geriatric OT on-road assessment

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Geriatrician: complex dementia management, BPSD, medication management
- Old age psychiatrist: BPSD, depression, psychosis in dementia
- Neurologist: young onset, FTD, atypical syndromes
- ACAT (Aged Care Assessment Team): 1800 200 422 (My Aged Care) — home care packages, residential care
- Dementia Australia: 1800 100 500

## Telehealth Suitability

Telehealth has limited utility for dementia assessment but is suitable for ongoing support.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Carer review and support
- Medication review (in established, stable dementia)
- Advance care planning discussion
- Referral coordination (ACAT, Dementia Australia)
- Blood result review

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Cognitive testing (MoCA, MMSE) — in-person preferred for accuracy
- Physical examination
- Blood tests — pathology arranged
- Driving assessment — specialist OT on-road testing
- BPSD assessment with physical examination to exclude medical causes
