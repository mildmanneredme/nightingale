## Asthma Assessment (RACGP)

Asthma is a common chronic respiratory condition affecting approximately 2.7 million Australians. It is characterised by variable airflow obstruction, airway hyperresponsiveness, and underlying airway inflammation. Management aims to achieve and maintain good symptom control and minimise future risk (exacerbations, fixed airflow limitation, medication side effects). Most asthma in adults can be effectively managed in general practice using a stepwise approach guided by the Australian Asthma Handbook. Telehealth is well-suited to preventer therapy review, written asthma action plan updating, and symptom monitoring between in-person reviews.

## Clinical Presentation

Classic symptoms: episodic wheeze, dyspnoea, chest tightness, and cough — particularly nocturnal or early morning. Symptoms are typically variable and triggered by viral URTIs, exercise, allergen exposure (grass pollen, house dust mite, pet dander), cold air, or occupational exposures.

Well-controlled asthma: daytime symptoms ≤2 days/week, no limitation of activities, no nocturnal wakening, reliever use ≤2 days/week, no exacerbations.

Poorly controlled asthma: frequent symptoms, reliever use >2 days/week, nocturnal wakening, activity limitation, or recent exacerbation.

Risk factors for asthma-related death: history of near-fatal asthma, frequent ED presentations, overuse of SABA reliever (>2 canisters/month), poor adherence to ICS therapy, food allergy with asthma, and psychosocial factors including depression.

## Assessment Approach

Confirm diagnosis: variable airflow obstruction on spirometry (FEV1/FVC <0.70 with significant bronchodilator response ≥12% and ≥200 mL increase in FEV1), or peak flow variability >20%.

Current control assessment (at every review):
- Frequency of daytime and nocturnal symptoms
- Reliever (SABA) use — if using >2 puffs/week, control is inadequate
- Activity limitation
- Recent exacerbations requiring oral corticosteroids or ED visit
- Inhaler technique — critical; assess at every review including via video call

Trigger identification: allergens, occupational exposures, exercise, aspirin/NSAIDs (aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease), smoking, GORD.

Peak flow or spirometry: objective assessment of lung function at diagnosis and when control is in question. Not required for every telehealth review.

Comorbidities: allergic rhinitis, GORD, obesity, anxiety/depression — all worsen asthma control.

## Key Differentials

- COPD — older patient, significant smoking history, less reversibility on spirometry; may coexist with asthma (ACOS)
- Vocal cord dysfunction — episodic stridor, inspiratory predominant, normal spirometry
- Cardiac causes of dyspnoea — heart failure, particularly in older patients
- Allergic bronchopulmonary aspergillosis — poorly controlled asthma with eosinophilia
- Bronchiectasis — chronic productive cough, recurrent infections

## Management Principles

The Australian Asthma Handbook recommends a stepwise approach based on current symptom control and future risk. Treatment is adjusted up or down every 1–3 months.

**Low-dose inhaled corticosteroid (ICS) is the cornerstone of treatment** — not PRN SABA alone (increases asthma-related death risk).

**Step 1 (mild intermittent):** Low-dose ICS/formoterol as needed (preferred; replaces PRN SABA as reliever in most adults — the SMART/MART approach per Australian Asthma Handbook).

**Step 2 (mild persistent):** Regular low-dose ICS + PRN reliever, or low-dose ICS/formoterol as needed.

**Step 3 (moderate):** Regular low-dose ICS/LABA combination (e.g., budesonide/formoterol or fluticasone/salmeterol).

**Step 4 (severe):** Medium-to-high dose ICS/LABA; add LAMA (tiotropium); consider referral to respiratory physician.

**Step 5 (refractory):** Biologic therapy (anti-IL-5, anti-IgE) — requires specialist referral.

Written asthma action plan: provide to all patients; updated at each review. Covers green/yellow/red zone management and when to call 000.

Non-pharmacological: allergen avoidance where feasible, influenza vaccination annually, pneumococcal vaccination per guidelines, smoking cessation, weight management if obese.

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Budesonide/formoterol** (Symbicort, Bufomix — PBS restricted: moderate-to-severe asthma not controlled on ICS alone; also PBS-listed as MART reliever in appropriate patients)
- **Fluticasone propionate/salmeterol** (Seretide — PBS restricted: moderate-to-severe asthma)
- **Fluticasone furoate/vilanterol** (Breo Ellipta — PBS restricted: asthma not controlled on ICS/LABA)
- **Beclomethasone dipropionate, budesonide, fluticasone propionate** (ICS alone — PBS unrestricted for asthma)
- **Salbutamol** (Ventolin — PBS unrestricted: reliever; counsel on appropriate use)
- **Montelukast** (PBS restricted: add-on therapy; less effective than ICS/LABA)
- **Tiotropium** (PBS restricted: add-on step 4 therapy in adults ≥18 years)
- **Mepolizumab, benralizumab** (PBS restricted: severe eosinophilic asthma — specialist initiation only)
- Note: As per Australian Asthma Handbook, ICS/formoterol combinations used as reliever (MART) require PBS authority in some formulations

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / emergency department — severe or life-threatening asthma:**
- Unable to complete sentences due to breathlessness
- Cyanosis, use of accessory muscles, tracheal tug
- SpO₂ <92% (if measured)
- Not responding to 4–6 puffs of salbutamol repeated every 20 minutes
- Silent chest on auscultation
- Altered consciousness, bradycardia, or exhaustion

**Urgent in-person GP or ED presentation within hours:**
- Worsening symptoms not responding to increased reliever use
- Waking repeatedly overnight with symptoms despite appropriate preventer
- Needing reliever ≥every 3–4 hours
- Yellow zone on action plan not improving within 24 hours of oral prednisolone initiation

**Non-urgent review:**
- Reliever use >2 days/week (inadequate control — step up therapy)
- Exacerbation requiring OCS (review within 1–2 weeks post-exacerbation)
- Any asthma presentation in a high-risk patient (previous ICU admission, food allergy, psychosocial risk)

## Telehealth Suitability

Asthma review is well-suited to telehealth for established patients with stable or mild-to-moderate disease.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Routine control review with symptom questionnaire
- Preventer therapy adjustment (step up or down) based on symptom history
- Inhaler technique review via video — ask patient to demonstrate device use on camera
- Written asthma action plan review and updating
- Trigger identification and avoidance counselling
- Medication adherence and side effect review

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Acute exacerbation or significant deterioration
- Auscultation for wheeze severity
- Spirometry or peak flow measurement
- New diagnosis confirmation
