## Contraception Assessment (RACGP)

Contraception counselling is a core component of GP practice, with approximately 70% of Australian women aged 18–44 using some form of contraception. Effective contraception requires shared decision-making tailored to the individual's reproductive intentions, health history, relationship status, preference for reversibility, and tolerance of side effects. No single method is universally best. Telehealth is highly suitable for contraception initiation counselling, pill review, and ongoing management; some methods (IUD/IUS insertion, implant) require in-person attendance.

## Clinical Presentation

Consultations for contraception typically include: initiating contraception (new user), switching method (side effects, preference change, or change in reproductive intentions), emergency contraception, ongoing pill/patch/ring review, or contraception counselling in the context of new medical conditions.

Key assessment questions: current or desired contraceptive method, menstrual cycle history (regularity, dysmenorrhoea, HMB), sexual health history (STI risk — LARC methods do not protect against STIs), current medications (especially enzyme inducers affecting hormonal contraception efficacy), comorbidities, reproductive intentions (current and future pregnancy plans), breastfeeding status, and smoking history (risk with combined OCP in women ≥35 years who smoke).

## Assessment Approach

Medical eligibility: use the WHO Medical Eligibility Criteria (MEC) categories adapted for Australia:
- MEC 1 — no restriction (method can be used)
- MEC 2 — advantages generally outweigh risks (can use)
- MEC 3 — risks generally outweigh advantages (use with caution, specialist advice)
- MEC 4 — unacceptable health risk (do not use)

Key contraindications to combined oestrogen-containing methods (pill, patch, vaginal ring):
- Current or history of VTE (MEC 4 for COC)
- Migraine with aura (MEC 4 — increased stroke risk)
- Cardiovascular disease or multiple cardiovascular risk factors
- Smoking ≥35 years (MEC 3–4 depending on cigarette quantity)
- Liver disease, hormone-sensitive tumours, undiagnosed abnormal vaginal bleeding
- Postpartum <6 weeks if breastfeeding

BP measurement: essential before commencing COC; hypertension (BP ≥160/100 mmHg) is MEC 3–4 for COC.

## Key Differentials

Assess underlying reason for contraception consultation — differentiate from:
- Dysmenorrhoea or HMB requiring treatment (hormonal IUD Mirena is highly effective for both)
- Suspected pregnancy — test before initiating hormonal contraception
- Intermenstrual or post-coital bleeding — requires investigation before IUD insertion

## Management Principles

**Long-acting reversible contraception (LARC) — most effective methods:**
Recommended as first-line for most women given superior efficacy (<1% failure rate with typical use) and no user-dependent factors.

- **Hormonal IUS (Mirena, Kyleena):** Levonorgestrel-releasing; highly effective for 5–8 years; reduces menstrual bleeding (often amenorrhoea); also PBS-listed for HMB treatment. Insertion in-person (GP or specialist).
- **Copper IUD (non-hormonal):** Effective for 5–10 years; can be used as emergency contraception within 5 days of unprotected sex; suitable for oestrogen contraindication; may increase menstrual flow. Insertion in-person.
- **Implant (Implanon NXT):** Progestogen-only etonogestrel subdermal rod; highly effective for 3 years; suitable for breastfeeding, oestrogen contraindication. Insertion/removal requires trained GP or nurse practitioner.

**Short-acting hormonal methods:**
- **Combined oral contraceptive pill (COC):** Most commonly used pill in Australia; typical use failure rate ~9%; requires daily adherence; multiple formulations available; beneficial for acne, dysmenorrhoea, HMB, endometriosis.
- **Progestogen-only pill (POP — mini-pill):** Suitable for breastfeeding, oestrogen contraindication; must be taken at same time each day (traditional POP) — desogestrel-containing pill (Cerazette, Slinda) has 12-hour window.
- **Contraceptive patch (Evra):** Weekly change; similar efficacy to COC; useful for patients with pill adherence difficulties.
- **Vaginal ring (NuvaRing):** Monthly insertion; oestrogen + progestogen; good adherence profile.

**Injectable (Depo-Provera/Depo-Ralovera):** DMPA 150 mg IM every 12 weeks; highly effective; may cause amenorrhoea or irregular bleeding; delayed return to fertility (up to 12 months) after stopping.

**Barrier methods:** Condoms — only method providing STI protection; should be recommended alongside other methods in new relationships or multiple partners. Female condom also available.

**Emergency contraception (EC):**
- Ulipristal acetate (EllaOne 30 mg) — PBS-listed; effective up to 120 hours (5 days); most effective option >72 hours. Requires prescription.
- Levonorgestrel (Postinor-1, NorLevo — OTC; effective up to 72 hours; less effective in higher BMI women)
- Copper IUD — most effective EC if inserted within 5 days; also provides ongoing contraception

**Pharmacist-prescribing:** In some Australian states, pharmacists can prescribe the combined OCP under collaborative prescribing arrangements — advise patients this may be available.

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Levonorgestrel-releasing IUS** (Mirena 52 mg — PBS restricted: treatment of HMB; not PBS-listed as contraception alone; Kyleena 19.5 mg — not PBS-listed)
- **Copper IUD** (various brands — not PBS-listed)
- **Etonogestrel implant** (Implanon NXT — PBS restricted: female contraception; also for endometriosis under separate criteria)
- **Combined oral contraceptives** (multiple brands — PBS unrestricted for contraception; also restricted for dysmenorrhoea, HMB, acne under separate listings)
- **Progestogen-only pill** (Microlut, Noriday, Cerazette/Slinda — PBS unrestricted)
- **DMPA injection** (Depo-Provera, Depo-Ralovera — PBS unrestricted: injectable contraception)
- **Ulipristal acetate** (EllaOne — PBS-listed: emergency contraception; prescription required)
- **Levonorgestrel EC** (Postinor-1, NorLevo — OTC; not PBS-subsidised)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000:**
- Suspected VTE in a woman on COC (leg pain, swelling, dyspnoea, chest pain, haemoptysis)
- Ectopic pregnancy in a woman with IUD — severe lower abdominal pain and positive pregnancy test is an emergency

**Urgent in-person review:**
- Intrauterine infection after IUD insertion (pelvic pain, fever, offensive discharge) — requires examination and removal if confirmed PID
- Menorrhagia or unexpected pregnancy with IUD in situ
- Side effects requiring method change (severe mood changes, persistent breakthrough bleeding affecting quality of life)

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Family Planning clinic: complex medical history, failed GP contraception management, IUD insertion (if GP not trained), sterilisation counselling
- Gynaecology: suspected endometriosis, structural uterine abnormality affecting contraceptive choice

## Telehealth Suitability

Contraception counselling is highly suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Initial contraception counselling and method selection
- COC, POP, patch, and ring initiation (with BP checked by patient or at pharmacy prior)
- Emergency contraception prescription
- Ongoing pill/method review
- Side effect management and method switching counselling
- Advising on method interactions with other medications

**Requires in-person attendance:**
- IUD or IUS insertion and removal
- Implanon NXT insertion and removal
- Depo-Provera injection
- BP measurement for COC initiation (can be arranged at pharmacy or with home BP monitor)
