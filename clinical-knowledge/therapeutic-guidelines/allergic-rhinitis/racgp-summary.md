## Allergic Rhinitis Assessment (RACGP)

Allergic rhinitis (AR) is the most common allergic disease in Australia, affecting approximately 1 in 5 Australians. It significantly impairs quality of life and productivity and is often undertreated. AR frequently coexists with asthma — treating AR improves asthma control (the "united airway" concept). Seasonal AR (hay fever) is predominant in Australia due to high grass pollen exposure; perennial AR is driven by house dust mite, pet dander, and mould. Telehealth is well-suited to diagnosis, management initiation, and treatment review.

## Clinical Presentation

Cardinal symptoms: nasal obstruction/congestion, sneezing, anterior rhinorrhoea (clear, watery discharge), and nasal pruritus. Ocular symptoms (allergic conjunctivitis) commonly co-occur: itchy, watery, red eyes.

Seasonal AR: symptoms coincide with pollen season (typically August–January in southern Australia; varies by region and pollen type). Grass pollen season peaks October–December in Victoria and NSW.

Perennial AR: year-round symptoms; house dust mite, cat, dog, or mould allergy.

Local allergic rhinitis (LAR): AR symptoms with negative skin prick testing; local IgE production in nasal mucosa; more common than previously recognised.

Severity classification (ARIA guideline):
- Intermittent: <4 days/week or <4 consecutive weeks
- Persistent: >4 days/week and >4 consecutive weeks
- Mild: no impairment of daily activities or sleep
- Moderate-severe: impaired sleep, daily activities, work, or school

Comorbidities: asthma (assess and treat concurrently), allergic conjunctivitis, chronic sinusitis, nasal polyps, otitis media with effusion (especially in children), anosmia.

## Assessment Approach

Clinical diagnosis is usually straightforward based on symptoms and timing. No routine investigations are required for uncomplicated cases.

Allergy testing (not required for initial management):
- Skin prick testing (SPT) — gold standard; refers to allergist or GP with allergy training
- Specific IgE blood testing (RAST) — useful if SPT not available or patient on antihistamines

Assess for:
- Asthma symptoms (wheeze, exertional dyspnoea, nocturnal cough) — important comorbidity
- Nasal polyps — suggest in-person nasal examination
- Medication use: decongestant overuse (rhinitis medicamentosa), aspirin/NSAID sensitivity

Nasal endoscopy or CT sinuses: not required routinely; for suspected polyps, anatomical abnormality, or recurrent sinusitis.

## Key Differentials

- Non-allergic rhinitis (vasomotor) — symptoms triggered by temperature, odours, or irritants; no pruritus, sneezing is less prominent; negative allergy testing
- Infectious rhinitis — acute onset, purulent discharge, fever, associated URTI
- Rhinitis medicamentosa — rebound congestion from prolonged decongestant nasal spray use (>5–7 days)
- Nasal polyps — bilateral persistent obstruction; reduced smell; associated with asthma and aspirin sensitivity
- Structural abnormality — deviated nasal septum; unilateral symptoms; refer to ENT
- Sinonasal malignancy — unilateral symptoms, epistaxis, facial pain; rare but important

## Management Principles

**Step 1 — Allergen avoidance:**
- House dust mite: mattress and pillow encasings, hot washing of bedding weekly, reduce carpets and soft furnishings
- Pet dander: keep pets out of bedroom; HEPA filters
- Pollen: stay indoors during peak pollen times, shower after outdoor exposure, check daily pollen count (AirRater app)

**Step 2 — Intranasal corticosteroid (INCS) — first-line for persistent or moderate-severe AR:**
- Most effective single treatment for AR; reduces all nasal symptoms including obstruction
- Regular use essential — onset of action takes 1–2 weeks
- Counsel on correct technique: sniff gently after spraying to maximise nasal deposition
- Minimal systemic absorption at standard doses; safe for long-term use
- Available OTC in Australia (mometasone, fluticasone propionate, triamcinolone)

**Step 3 — Non-sedating oral antihistamines:**
- Effective for sneezing, itching, and rhinorrhoea; less effective for nasal obstruction
- First-line for mild intermittent AR or as add-on to INCS
- Preferred: cetirizine, loratadine, fexofenadine (non-sedating, once daily)
- Avoid sedating antihistamines (promethazine, chlorpheniramine) due to impaired cognition and driving risk

**Combined therapy:** INCS + antihistamine more effective than either alone for moderate-severe AR.

**Allergen immunotherapy (desensitisation):** Subcutaneous (SCIT) or sublingual (SLIT) immunotherapy — disease-modifying; refer to allergist. Consider if AR is poorly controlled on pharmacotherapy, complicates asthma, or patient has multiple season impacts.

**Ocular symptoms (allergic conjunctivitis):** Sodium cromoglicate or antihistamine eye drops (e.g., olopatadine); or systemic antihistamine also treats ocular symptoms.

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Fluticasone propionate nasal spray** (Flixonase — OTC available; PBS-listed for severe seasonal AR in children 4–11 years and adults)
- **Mometasone nasal spray** (Nasonex — OTC available)
- **Beclomethasone nasal spray** (Beconase — OTC; PBS-listed for severe seasonal AR)
- **Cetirizine** (PBS unrestricted; also widely available OTC) — 10 mg daily
- **Loratadine** (PBS unrestricted; also OTC) — 10 mg daily
- **Fexofenadine** (OTC; PBS-listed for chronic idiopathic urticaria) — 120–180 mg daily for AR (PBS restrictions may apply for seasonal AR indication)
- **Ipratropium nasal spray** (PBS restricted — rhinorrhoea-predominant AR) — useful for watery rhinorrhoea
- **Montelukast** (PBS restricted — adjunct for AR; less effective than INCS; useful if concurrent asthma)
- Decongestants (pseudoephedrine oral) — short-term use only; not suitable for hypertension, cardiovascular disease, MAOI use

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000:**
- Anaphylaxis — rare from AR itself but may follow exposure to allergen; treat with adrenaline auto-injector and call 000

**Urgent in-person GP or ENT:**
- Unilateral nasal symptoms, epistaxis, or facial pain — possible structural or malignant lesion
- Nasal polyps with significant obstruction or anosmia — ENT referral for assessment and possible surgery
- Asthma exacerbation triggered by AR — manage asthma urgently (see asthma escalation pathway)

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Allergist: allergen testing and consideration of immunotherapy; poorly controlled AR despite optimal pharmacotherapy; multiple allergies; coexistent asthma
- ENT: chronic sinusitis, polyps, anatomical obstruction
- Ophthalmology: severe or refractory allergic conjunctivitis

## Telehealth Suitability

Allergic rhinitis is highly suitable for telehealth management.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Diagnosis based on symptom history and timing
- Initiation and review of INCS and antihistamine therapy
- Allergen avoidance counselling
- Seasonal action plan for hay fever patients
- Referral for allergy testing or immunotherapy

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Nasal examination for suspected polyps, structural abnormality, or unilateral symptoms
- Skin prick testing (requires allergy clinic attendance)
- Auscultation for comorbid asthma
