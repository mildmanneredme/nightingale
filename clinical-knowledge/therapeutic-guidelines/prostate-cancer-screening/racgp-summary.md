## Prostate Cancer Screening Assessment (RACGP)

Prostate cancer is the most commonly diagnosed cancer in Australian men, with approximately 24,000 new cases diagnosed annually. It is the second most common cause of cancer death in men after lung cancer. Unlike bowel or cervical cancer, there is no national government-funded prostate cancer screening program in Australia. PSA (prostate-specific antigen) testing is widely used but remains controversial — it detects cancer effectively but cannot distinguish aggressive from indolent disease, leading to overdiagnosis and overtreatment (with harms including sexual and urinary dysfunction). The RACGP and Cancer Council Australia recommend an informed decision-making approach: men aged 50–69 (or ≥40–45 with high-risk factors) should be offered information about the benefits and harms of PSA testing and make an informed personal choice. The GP's role is to facilitate this shared decision and, for those who proceed to testing, manage results appropriately.

## Clinical Presentation

**Who to consider for PSA discussion:**

Average risk:
- Men aged 50–69: discuss benefits and harms of PSA testing; offer informed decision
- Medicare does not fund PSA testing without clinical indication in asymptomatic men (patient pays)

High risk (offer earlier discussion and testing):
- Family history of prostate cancer: father or brother diagnosed with prostate cancer, especially if <65 — consider testing from age 40–45
- BRCA2 gene mutation carriers: substantially increased risk; consider testing from age 40–45
- Men of African or African-Caribbean ancestry: higher risk; consider testing from age 40–45
- Lynch syndrome: slightly elevated prostate cancer risk; surveillance per genetics specialist recommendation

**Symptomatic prostate assessment (not screening — diagnostic):**
- Lower urinary tract symptoms (LUTS): urinary frequency, urgency, nocturia, hesitancy, poor stream, incomplete emptying (often BPH — see `bph` entry, but exclude cancer)
- Haematuria: investigation required; see `rectal-bleeding` / urological referral
- Pelvic, back, or hip pain in older men: consider metastatic prostate cancer; PSA + X-ray/CT/MRI if suspected
- DRE (digital rectal examination): hard, irregular, asymmetric prostate = suspicious for cancer; PSA and urgent urology referral

## Assessment Approach

**Informed consent and shared decision for PSA screening:**

Benefits of PSA testing:
- Detects prostate cancer before symptoms develop
- Early detection enables curative treatment for localised disease
- PSA testing reduces prostate cancer mortality by approximately 20% (ERSPC trial) at population level

Harms of PSA testing:
- Overdetection: many prostate cancers detected are low-grade, slow-growing (Gleason 3+3 = ISUP grade group 1) and would never cause harm
- Overtreatment: radical prostatectomy and radiotherapy cause incontinence (30% at 12 months), erectile dysfunction (60–80%), and bowel symptoms — even for cancers that did not require treatment
- Biopsy harms: TRUS-guided biopsy risks include infection, sepsis, rectal bleeding; MRI-targeted biopsy has improved accuracy
- False positives: elevated PSA not due to cancer causes patient anxiety and further investigation

**PSA interpretation:**
- PSA normal ranges (traditional): <2.5 µg/L age 40–49; <3.5 µg/L age 50–59; <4.5 µg/L age 60–69; <6.5 µg/L age 70–79
- PSA velocity (rate of change): rising PSA >0.75 µg/L/year warrants further investigation
- Free:total PSA ratio: lower ratio (<15–20%) more suspicious for cancer; useful when total PSA 4–10 µg/L
- PSA density (PSA/prostate volume): higher density = higher cancer risk
- Causes of elevated PSA other than cancer: BPH (most common), prostatitis (PSA markedly elevated in acute prostatitis), urinary tract infection, urethral instrumentation, vigorous exercise (bicycle riding), recent ejaculation (PSA rises 24–48 hours post-ejaculation — avoid PSA within 48 hours of sexual activity)

**Post-abnormal PSA workup:**
- PSA ≥4 µg/L (or lower with rising PSA or high-risk features): urology referral
- Multiparametric MRI prostate (mpMRI): PI-RADS scoring system (1–5); performed before biopsy in most centres; PRECISE guidelines followed; PI-RADS ≥3 warrants biopsy
- Prostate biopsy: MRI-targeted transperineal (increasingly replacing TRUS) or TRUS-guided; Gleason score/ISUP grade groups; urology specialist

**Active surveillance (for low-risk localised prostate cancer):**
- Low-risk (Gleason 3+3/ISUP grade 1, PSA <10, clinical stage ≤T2a): active surveillance preferred over immediate treatment
- Surveillance: PSA 3-6 monthly, mpMRI annually, repeat biopsy at 12–18 months then as per protocol; urology managed
- GP role: encourage compliance with surveillance, support decision-making, monitor for disease progression signals

## Key Differentials

- Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH): elevated PSA, LUTS, symmetrically enlarged smooth prostate on DRE; see `bph` entry
- Prostatitis (acute bacterial): PSA markedly elevated (may be >100 µg/L); fever, perineal pain, dysuria; treat infection; retest PSA 6–8 weeks after recovery
- Prostatitis (chronic/CPPS): LUTS, pelvic pain; PSA mildly elevated; urology referral if uncertain
- Bladder cancer: haematuria; cystoscopy; differentiated by urology
- Rectal pathology: mistaken for prostate on DRE; imaging and urology/gastroenterology

## Management Principles

**After diagnosis of prostate cancer (specialist-led — GP supportive role):**

Localised disease management options:
- Active surveillance: for low-risk disease; avoids treatment harms; GP supports adherence
- Radical prostatectomy: surgical removal; curative intent; continence and erectile dysfunction side effects
- Radiotherapy (external beam or brachytherapy): equivalent cure rates to surgery for localised disease; bowel and urinary side effects
- Focal therapy (HIFU, cryotherapy): select centres; emerging; specialist only

Locally advanced / metastatic disease:
- Androgen deprivation therapy (ADT — leuprorelin, goserelin, degarelix): hormonal therapy; hot flushes, osteoporosis, metabolic syndrome, cognitive effects; GP monitors and manages side effects
- Abiraterone, enzalutamide: second-generation anti-androgen; castration-resistant prostate cancer
- Chemotherapy (docetaxel, cabazitaxel): advanced disease; oncologist managed
- PSMA-targeted therapy: lutetium PSMA — available in Australia; PBS-listed for specific indications; nuclear medicine specialist

**GP ongoing role in prostate cancer survivors:**
- ADT monitoring: bone density (DEXA annually), cardiovascular risk, metabolic syndrome, hot flushes (treat with SSRIs if severe), depression screening
- PSA monitoring: indicator of recurrence; regular follow-up with urology/oncology; GP may report results
- Sexual health: erectile dysfunction post-treatment; PDE5 inhibitors (if not on ADT); sexual health referral
- Urinary incontinence: post-prostatectomy; pelvic floor exercises; urology
- Psychological support: anxiety about recurrence; PHQ-9; GP Mental Health Care Plan

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Leuprorelin (Lucrin), goserelin (Zoladex)** (PBS restricted: prostate cancer; ADT; monthly or 3-monthly SC/IM injection; monitor for osteoporosis, cardiovascular risk, metabolic effects)
- **Enzalutamide (Xtandi), abiraterone (Zytiga)** (PBS restricted: castration-resistant prostate cancer; specialist initiation; oncology/urology managed)
- **PDE5 inhibitors** (sildenafil, tadalafil — PBS unrestricted: erectile dysfunction post-prostatectomy; see `erectile-dysfunction` entry)
- **Note:** PSA testing in asymptomatic men is not Medicare-funded; inform patient of out-of-pocket cost; symptomatic men have Medicare-rebated PSA testing
- PCFA (Prostate Cancer Foundation of Australia): 1800 22 00 99; patient support and resources

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Suspected metastatic spinal cord compression: sudden back pain + neurological deficit (weakness, saddle anaesthesia) in man with known prostate cancer — surgical emergency
- Urosepsis from obstructive uropathy: fever, rigors, flank pain in known prostate cancer

**Urgent urology referral:**
- PSA ≥4 µg/L (in younger men or higher in older men per age-specific thresholds)
- Rapidly rising PSA (velocity >0.75 µg/L/year)
- Hard, irregular prostate on DRE — suspicious for cancer; urgent urology
- Any prostate cancer patient with new bone pain, neurological symptoms, or significant PSA rise while on treatment

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Urology: elevated PSA requiring mpMRI and possible biopsy
- Radiation oncology: for treatment planning
- Cancer survivorship support: PCFA, oncology follow-up
- Exercise physiologist: ADT-related metabolic syndrome and bone loss (exercise reduces ADT adverse effects)

## Telehealth Suitability

Prostate cancer screening counselling and post-treatment monitoring are highly suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Shared decision-making discussion about PSA screening benefits and harms
- PSA result interpretation and follow-up planning
- ADT monitoring (bone density results, metabolic monitoring, hot flush management)
- Mental health review for cancer anxiety or depression
- GP Mental Health Care Plan for psychology referral
- Referral coordination (urology, oncology, PCFA)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Digital rectal examination (DRE)
- Blood tests — PSA and monitoring (pathology arranged)
- Prostate MRI and biopsy — specialist
- Bone density (DEXA) — radiology
