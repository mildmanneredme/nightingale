## Atrial Fibrillation Assessment (RACGP)

Atrial fibrillation (AF) is the most common sustained cardiac arrhythmia, affecting approximately 2% of Australians and rising sharply with age (up to 10% in those >80 years). AF significantly increases the risk of stroke (5-fold), heart failure, and death. GP management of stable chronic AF focuses on three pillars: rate control, anticoagulation for stroke prevention, and monitoring for complications. Acute-onset or haemodynamically compromising AF requires urgent hospital management. Telehealth is well-suited to ongoing review of stable, rate-controlled AF with established anticoagulation.

## Clinical Presentation

Symptoms: palpitations (irregular heartbeat), dyspnoea, fatigue, exercise intolerance, dizziness, and chest discomfort. Many patients are asymptomatic and AF is detected incidentally on ECG or pulse check.

AF classification:
- Paroxysmal: self-terminating episodes, typically <48 hours, may last up to 7 days
- Persistent: continuous for >7 days; requires cardioversion to restore sinus rhythm
- Long-standing persistent: continuous >12 months
- Permanent: ongoing AF with accepted as the rhythm (rate control strategy)

Risk factors: hypertension (most common), advancing age, heart failure, structural heart disease (valve disease, cardiomyopathy), diabetes, obesity, OSA, excess alcohol, hyperthyroidism, post-cardiac surgery.

## Assessment Approach

At every stable AF review:
- Symptom control: palpitations, dyspnoea, exercise tolerance, fatigue
- Heart rate control: target resting HR 60–100 bpm (lenient rate control ≤110 bpm acceptable if asymptomatic)
- Anticoagulation review: agent, dose, adherence, bleeding events, recent INR (if on warfarin)
- Stroke risk reassessment: CHA₂DS₂-VASc score — recalculate if new comorbidities (diabetes, HF, stroke, hypertension, vascular disease)
- Bleeding risk: HAS-BLED score — modifiable bleeding risk factors (uncontrolled hypertension, excessive alcohol, NSAIDs, antiplatelet use)
- Comorbidity review: BP control, thyroid function (TSH annually, or if AF worsens), HbA1c, renal function (eGFR — impacts DOAC dosing)
- Medication interaction check: particularly with DOACs (avoid P-gp inhibitors and inducers with some DOACs)

Investigations: 12-lead ECG (confirm AF, assess ventricular rate, exclude WPW or other arrhythmias); echocardiogram (assess structural heart disease, LV function — guides rate control strategy); TFTs; eGFR; FBC.

## Key Differentials

- Other supraventricular tachycardias (SVT) — regular narrow complex tachycardia; require ECG differentiation
- Atrial flutter — regularly irregular; saw-tooth P-waves at 300 bpm; ventricular rate typically 150 bpm (2:1 block)
- Frequent ectopics (PACs, PVCs) — may feel like irregular heartbeat; ECG differentiates
- Wolf-Parkinson-White (WPW) — pre-excitation on ECG (delta wave); AF in WPW is an emergency (avoid AV nodal agents — use procainamide or electrical cardioversion)

## Management Principles

**Stroke prevention — anticoagulation:**
Anticoagulation is the most important intervention in AF management — reduces stroke risk by ~64%.

CHA₂DS₂-VASc score guides anticoagulation:
- Score 0 (male) or 1 (female) — anticoagulation not recommended
- Score 1 (male) — consider anticoagulation (discuss risk/benefit)
- Score ≥2 (male) or ≥3 (female) — anticoagulate (unless high bleeding risk)

Preferred agents (DOACs over warfarin for non-valvular AF):
- Apixaban 5 mg twice daily (2.5 mg twice daily if ≥2 of: age ≥80, weight ≤60 kg, creatinine ≥133 µmol/L)
- Rivaroxaban 20 mg once daily with evening meal (15 mg if eGFR 15–49)
- Dabigatran 150 mg twice daily (110 mg if age ≥80, or with verapamil, or bleeding risk)
- Warfarin (target INR 2.0–3.0): still appropriate for mechanical heart valves or moderate-severe mitral stenosis — these are the only AF indications where warfarin is preferred over DOACs

Contraindications to anticoagulation: active major bleeding, severe thrombocytopaenia. Left atrial appendage occlusion (Watchman device) — specialist-managed alternative in selected patients who cannot tolerate anticoagulation.

**Rate control:**
- Beta-blockers: metoprolol, bisoprolol, carvedilol — first-line; particularly if HFrEF or hypertension
- Non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers: diltiazem, verapamil — alternative; contraindicated in HFrEF
- Digoxin: add-on at rest; limited effect during exercise; useful in HFrEF + AF or as adjunct
- Target: resting HR 60–100 bpm; lenient rate control (≤110 bpm at rest) acceptable for asymptomatic patients

**Rhythm control (specialist-managed):**
Cardioversion (electrical or pharmacological) and antiarrhythmic drugs (amiodarone, flecainide, sotalol) — managed by cardiologist. GP role: ensure anticoagulation for ≥3 weeks before and ≥4 weeks after cardioversion. AF ablation is increasingly effective for paroxysmal and persistent AF.

**Comorbidity management:** BP control is critical (hypertension is the most modifiable stroke risk factor); treat OSA (CPAP reduces AF burden); weight loss in obese patients; alcohol reduction; smoking cessation.

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Apixaban** (Eliquis — PBS restricted: non-valvular AF with ≥1 stroke risk factor; CHA₂DS₂-VASc criteria apply)
- **Rivaroxaban** (Xarelto — PBS restricted: non-valvular AF)
- **Dabigatran** (Pradaxa — PBS restricted: non-valvular AF)
- **Warfarin** (PBS unrestricted — AF; INR monitoring required; narrowing indications as DOACs preferred)
- **Metoprolol succinate** (PBS unrestricted — rate control)
- **Bisoprolol** (PBS unrestricted)
- **Diltiazem** (PBS restricted — rate control in AF; avoid in HFrEF)
- **Digoxin** (PBS restricted — AF rate control adjunct)
- Note: DOAC prescribing under PBS requires documentation of CHA₂DS₂-VASc criteria and contraindications to warfarin — review PBS criteria before initiating

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- New-onset AF with haemodynamic compromise: hypotension, chest pain, syncope, severe dyspnoea — electrical cardioversion required
- AF with rapid ventricular rate (HR >150 bpm) in a symptomatic or haemodynamically unstable patient
- Stroke symptoms in a patient with AF (FAST criteria: Face, Arm, Speech, Time — call 000 immediately; thrombolysis time-critical)
- TIA — urgent same-day hospital review; very high early stroke risk

**Urgent in-person cardiology or ED:**
- Newly detected AF — ECG, echo, thyroid function, INR/DOAC check; initiate anticoagulation urgently if indicated
- Symptomatic AF with poorly controlled rate despite oral therapy
- Chest pain or worsening heart failure in AF patient
- Significant bleeding event in anticoagulated patient (stop DOAC; urgent assessment)
- INR >5 in warfarin patient (supratherapeutic; manage per anticoagulation reversal protocol)

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Cardiology: new AF diagnosis (echocardiogram, rhythm control consideration), poorly controlled rate or symptoms, AF ablation candidacy
- Heart failure clinic: AF with concurrent HFrEF

## Telehealth Suitability

Stable, rate-controlled AF with established anticoagulation is highly suitable for telehealth review.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Routine medication review (DOAC adherence, rate control agent efficacy)
- INR discussion and warfarin dose adjustment (if INR results available)
- Symptom review and quality of life assessment
- CHA₂DS₂-VASc and HAS-BLED reassessment
- Renal function review and DOAC dose adjustment
- Comorbidity management (BP, diabetes, OSA)
- Heart rate check if patient has home BP monitor (reports pulse rate and regularity)

**Requires in-person or specialist assessment:**
- New-onset or recently detected AF — ECG required
- Any haemodynamic instability or new chest pain — emergency referral
- Auscultation for valvular disease
- Echocardiogram (radiology referral)
- Bleeding assessment
