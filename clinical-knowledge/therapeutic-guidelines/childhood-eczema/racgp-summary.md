## Childhood Eczema (Atopic Dermatitis) Assessment (RACGP)

Atopic dermatitis (AD) is the most common inflammatory skin condition in children, affecting approximately 20–30% of Australian children; 60% present before 12 months of age and 90% before age 5. It is characterised by chronic, relapsing pruritic skin inflammation. AD is the skin manifestation of the atopic march (AD → food allergy → allergic rhinitis → asthma); early effective management may reduce progression. Severity ranges from mild (limited dry skin and intermittent itch) to severe (widespread inflammation, sleep disturbance, quality of life impairment). Emollients (moisturisers) are the absolute cornerstone of management for all severity levels — applied generously and frequently. Topical corticosteroids (TCS) are the primary anti-inflammatory treatment; topical calcineurin inhibitors (TCI) are steroid-sparing alternatives. Biologics (dupilumab) are now available for severe refractory paediatric AD. Parents require thorough education on the wet dressing technique, trigger avoidance, and realistic expectations (chronic relapsing condition). Telehealth is suitable after initial in-person assessment; written action plans are essential.

## Clinical Presentation

**Age-related distribution:**
- Infants (0–2 years): cheeks; scalp; extensor surfaces of limbs; spares nappy area (skin folds protected by occlusion)
- Children (2–12 years): flexural surfaces — antecubital fossae, popliteal fossae, wrists, ankles; face less prominent
- Adolescents/adults: flexural predominance; hand and foot eczema; eyelid involvement

**Typical features:**
- Pruritus (itch): the cardinal feature; chronic; worse at night; causes sleep disturbance (affects child and family)
- Dry skin (xerosis): between flares; rough, scaling
- Erythema with papules or vesicles: acute; weeping and crusting; secondary infection common (Staphylococcus aureus colonisation nearly universal in AD)
- Lichenification: chronic rubbing leads to thickened leathery skin; chronic AD
- Excoriations: secondary to scratching; linear marks; increased infection risk

**Severity grading:**
- Mild (majority): <10% BSA; intermittent itch; little sleep disturbance; responds to emollient alone or mild TCS
- Moderate: 10–30% BSA; significant itch; some sleep disturbance; moderate-potency TCS and emollient
- Severe: >30% BSA or facial/periorificial/genital; intense itch; chronic sleep disturbance; significant quality of life impact; specialist dermatology referral

**Triggers:**
- Skin irritants: soap, detergents, wool, synthetic fabrics, overwashing
- Environmental allergens: house dust mite (HDM), pollen, pet dander
- Food allergens: cow's milk, egg, peanut, wheat, soy — more relevant in infants with early-onset severe AD; refer to allergy clinic if suspected
- Heat and sweating; emotional stress; infections (S. aureus skin colonisation — not clinical infection)

## Assessment Approach

**Clinical diagnosis (no routine investigations required):**
- Hanifin and Rajka criteria: pruritus + characteristic morphology/distribution + chronic relapsing course + personal/family history of atopy (asthma, allergic rhinitis, food allergy)
- Severity scoring: SCORAD (Scoring Atopic Dermatitis) or EASI (Eczema Area and Severity Index) for moderate-severe; children's DLQI for quality of life impact
- Sleep assessment: ASK about sleep disturbance — often most impactful feature on child and family wellbeing
- Infection signs: weeping, honey-coloured crusting, increased pain, fever, spreading redness — suggests Staphylococcal superinfection

**Investigations (only if clinical concern):**
- Total IgE: elevated in atopic individuals but not diagnostic; limited clinical utility in isolation
- Specific IgE (RAST) or skin prick testing: only if specific food or environmental allergy suspected; refer to allergy clinic — skin prick testing in primary care without appropriate allergy training is NOT recommended
- Swab for MCS: if clinical infection suspected (localised honey-crusted impetigo, cellulitis)
- Do NOT: routinely screen all AD children for food allergy (most AD is not food-allergy driven after infancy)

**Referral indications:**
- Allergy clinic: infants <2 years with moderate-severe AD and suspected food allergy; immediate-type food reactions; before food challenge or oral immunotherapy
- Dermatology: moderate-severe AD not controlled with appropriate topical therapy; diagnostic uncertainty; consideration for dupilumab

## Key Differentials

- Seborrhoeic dermatitis: scalp and face; non-pruritic; greasy scale; different distribution; infants (cradle cap); see `seborrhoeic-dermatitis`
- Contact dermatitis (allergic): localised; patch-test distribution; occupational or topical allergen exposure
- Psoriasis: plaques with silvery scale; extensor surfaces; nail pitting; scalp; less pruritic
- Scabies: very pruritic; web spaces, wrists, axillae; burrows; nocturnal itch; treat whole household
- Tinea corporis: annular lesion; spreading edge; KOH confirms; treat with antifungal
- Ichthyosis vulgaris: dry, fish-like scale; extensor surfaces; no itch; associated with AD

## Management Principles

**Step-up approach (based on severity):**

Step 1 — All severity (emollient therapy):
- Emollients (moisturisers): ESSENTIAL for all patients regardless of severity; apply generously (at least 250 g/week for child); within 3 minutes of bathing ("soak and seal"); apply 2–4 times daily
- Choice: fragrance-free, preservative-free; cream or ointment (not lotion — too much water); QV cream, Dermeze, Aqueous cream, Aveeno — trial different to find tolerated product
- Bathing: daily lukewarm bath or shower; 5–10 minutes; mild soap-free wash (QV, DermaVeen); pat dry gently
- Avoid: soap, bubble bath, detergent contact; wool and synthetic fabrics; rapid temperature changes

Step 2 — Mild flares:
- Mild-potency topical corticosteroid (TCS): hydrocortisone 1% cream (OTC or PBS): face, nappy area, skin folds; apply once daily during flare; apply emollient 30 minutes before or after TCS
- Moderate-potency TCS: methylprednisolone aceponate 0.1% (Advantan) or mometasone 0.1%: body and limbs; apply once daily × 7–14 days per flare; restart at next flare

Step 3 — Moderate-severe flares:
- Wet dressings: IMPORTANT technique for moderate-severe flares; apply TCS then wet cotton bandages (soaked in warm water) covered by dry layer; 2–4 hours or overnight; very effective; education required
- Potent TCS: betamethasone valerate 0.05% or 0.1% (Betnovate): body; short courses only; avoid face and skin folds; do NOT use under wet dressings
- Topical calcineurin inhibitors (TCI) — steroid-sparing:
  - Tacrolimus 0.03% ointment (Protopic): age ≥2 years; face, eyelids, skin folds where steroids cause atrophy; PBS-listed for moderate-severe AD (facial/flexural); apply BD; stinging on application initially (improves after a few days)
  - Pimecrolimus 1% cream (Elidel): face/skin folds; age ≥2 years; less potent than tacrolimus; PBS-listed for mild-moderate facial AD; steroid-sparing

Step 4 — Severe/refractory (specialist dermatology):
- Dupilumab (Dupixent) — anti-IL-4/IL-13 biologic: PBS-listed for severe AD aged ≥6 years not controlled by standard therapy; subcutaneous injection every 2 weeks; specialist initiation (dermatologist); excellent response rate
- Systemic immunosuppressants: cyclosporine (short-term for acute flares), methotrexate, azathioprine — specialist paediatric dermatology only
- Phototherapy (narrowband UVB): adolescents with widespread chronic AD; specialist

**Itch control:**
- Non-sedating antihistamines: loratadine, cetirizine — limited efficacy for itch in AD (not histamine-mediated); may help if concurrent allergic rhinitis
- Sedating antihistamine (trimeprazine/promethazine) at night: short-term for severe sleep-disrupting itch only; NOT routine; avoid in children under 2 years
- Cool packs; cold-water wet wraps; distraction techniques; cotton mittens at night for infants

**Staphylococcal superinfection management:**
- Topical antiseptics: dilute bleach baths (sodium hypochlorite 0.005% — 1 mL household bleach in 10 L water; 2× weekly); or chlorhexidine wash reduces S. aureus colonisation; reduces flare frequency
- Clinical infection (impetigo, cellulitis): flucloxacillin 25 mg/kg/dose QID × 10 days (maximum 500 mg/dose); or cefalexin if penicillin allergy; topical mupirocin (Bactroban) for small localised areas of impetigo only

**Written Eczema Action Plan:**
- Provide at every consultation: stepwise instructions for emollient, TCS potency, wet dressings; when to seek review; given to parents and ideally the school
- Eczema Association of Australasia: eczema.org.au — resources and action plan templates

## Paediatric Context and Guardian Involvement

- Weight-based dosing: TCS potency and quantity guided by body surface area and age; avoid high-potency TCS on face, skin folds, or under occlusion in children (skin atrophy risk)
- Growth monitoring: systemic absorption of TCS in infants with high BSA involvement; measure height and weight; weigh-up risk vs benefit of prolonged high-potency TCS
- Written action plans: essential for parent/carer management at home; school notification
- Sleep impact: assess sleep quality and quantity at every consultation; sleep disruption = severity indicator; poor sleep affects school performance, behaviour, and parental wellbeing
- Bathing routine education: provide at every consultation — correct bathing technique reduces emollient frequency needed
- Nipple/facial eczema: particularly distressing; TCI (pimecrolimus/tacrolimus) preferred over potent TCS on face
- Immunisation: vaccinations should proceed on schedule; avoid vaccinating into active eczematous skin; use a clear area

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Hydrocortisone 1% cream** (PBS unrestricted/OTC: mild TCS; safe for face and skin folds in all ages; use during flares; ongoing daily use should be avoided — reassess if daily use for >2 weeks)
- **Methylprednisolone aceponate 0.1% (Advantan)** (PBS restricted: moderate TCS; body and limb eczema; once daily; suitable for infants >4 months; more potent than hydrocortisone 1%)
- **Tacrolimus 0.03% ointment (Protopic)** (PBS restricted: moderate-severe AD; facial and flexural in children ≥2 years; authority required; steroid-sparing; apply BD during flares; black box warning for theoretical malignancy risk — not confirmed in practice; reassure parents)
- **Pimecrolimus 1% cream (Elidel)** (PBS restricted: mild-moderate facial AD; steroid-sparing; age ≥2 years)
- **Note:** Parental corticosteroid phobia is very common and significantly contributes to under-treatment of eczema. GPs should proactively address concerns, explain that correctly used TCS are safe, and that under-treated eczema causes more harm (infection, sleep disruption, quality of life) than appropriately used TCS.

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate hospital:**
- Eczema herpeticum: sudden widespread monomorphic punched-out painful vesicles/erosions in eczematous skin; fever; systemically unwell; HSV superinfection → IV aciclovir; hospital admission; urgent dermatology
- Extensive secondary bacterial infection with sepsis: hospital + IV antibiotics

**Urgent dermatology:**
- Suspected eczema herpeticum: even if uncertain; hospitalise and treat; life-threatening in infants
- Severe flare not responding to wet dressings and appropriate TCS within 48–72 hours
- Dupilumab eligibility assessment: severe AD aged ≥6 years

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Dermatology: moderate-severe AD not responding to step 2–3 therapy; diagnostic uncertainty; dupilumab consideration
- Allergy clinic: infant <2 years with moderate-severe AD + suspected food allergy; skin prick testing; oral food challenge

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Established AD: review of flare severity, identify triggers, adjust management step-up/down
- Emollient and TCS prescribing for ongoing management
- Eczema action plan discussion and update
- Infection assessment if patient can share clear photographs
- Parental education and reassurance for corticosteroid concerns

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- New presentation: confirm diagnosis; assess severity and body surface area involvement
- Suspected eczema herpeticum: urgent in-person or hospital
- Suspected secondary infection: examine for extent; swab if needed
- Consideration for specialist referral (dupilumab, wet dressings initiation)
