## Dizziness and Vertigo Assessment (RACGP)

Dizziness is among the most common presenting complaints in general practice, accounting for approximately 7% of GP consultations. The term encompasses a spectrum of sensations including vertigo (illusion of movement — the room spinning), presyncope (lightheadedness, near-fainting), disequilibrium (unsteadiness without head sensation), and non-specific dizziness. The diagnostic approach has shifted from symptom classification (what type of dizziness) to timing and triggers (when it occurs and what provokes it) — this is more accurate and clinically useful. The key diagnostic challenge is distinguishing benign peripheral causes (BPPV, vestibular neuritis, Ménière's disease) from central causes (posterior fossa stroke, cerebellar lesion) that require urgent investigation. Telehealth is moderately suited to dizziness assessment; vestibular examination requires in-person assessment for definitive diagnosis.

## Clinical Presentation

**Types of dizziness:**
- Vertigo: illusion of rotational movement (self or environment); typically peripheral (inner ear/vestibular nerve) or central (brainstem/cerebellum)
- Presyncope: lightheadedness, near-fainting sensation; cardiovascular or autonomic origin
- Disequilibrium: unsteadiness, impaired balance without head symptom; typically central or peripheral neuropathy
- Non-specific dizziness: floating, foggy, or poorly characterised sensation; often anxiety, medication side effects, or anaemia

**Episodic vs constant timing:**
- Episodic seconds-to-minutes triggered by head position change → Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)
- Episodic hours + hearing loss + tinnitus + aural fullness → Ménière's disease
- Episodic seconds with standing → Postural hypotension (autonomic, dehydration, medications)
- Constant/persistent days-to-weeks after viral illness → Vestibular neuritis / labyrinthitis
- Acute onset severe vertigo + neurological features → Posterior fossa stroke — emergency

**Common peripheral vestibular disorders:**
- BPPV: most common cause of vertigo in general practice; brief (<60 seconds) rotational vertigo triggered by head position changes (rolling over in bed, looking up, bending forward); Dix-Hallpike manoeuvre positive; curable with Epley manoeuvre
- Vestibular neuritis: acute onset constant vertigo lasting days; follows viral illness; no hearing loss; horizontal nystagmus away from affected ear; Epley not appropriate; resolves over weeks
- Labyrinthitis: as vestibular neuritis but with hearing loss and tinnitus; cochlear involvement
- Ménière's disease: episodic vertigo lasting 20 minutes to 24 hours; unilateral fluctuating sensorineural hearing loss; tinnitus; aural fullness; recurrent attacks; low-sodium diet + diuretics + specialist referral

## Assessment Approach

**History (highest diagnostic yield):**
- Timing: when does it occur, how long does each episode last, is it continuous or episodic?
- Triggers: specific head position changes (BPPV), standing (postural hypotension), no trigger (Ménière's, vestibular neuritis)
- Associated symptoms: hearing loss, tinnitus, aural fullness (Ménière's), nausea/vomiting, neurological symptoms (diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia, limb weakness, numbness — posterior fossa stroke)
- Medications: many cause dizziness — antihypertensives, benzodiazepines, antidepressants, anticonvulsants, aminoglycosides (ototoxic), loop diuretics
- Cardiovascular risk factors: hypertension, diabetes, atrial fibrillation (embolic stroke risk)
- Falls history: dizziness in elderly — falls risk, injury

**Examination (in-person required for definitive vestibular assessment):**
- Vital signs: blood pressure lying and standing (postural hypotension — >20 mmHg systolic drop with standing)
- Dix-Hallpike test: BPPV diagnosis — posterior canal BPPV produces upbeat-torsional nystagmus, latency of onset, fatigable; < 1 minute
- Head Impulse Test (HIT): tests VOR; abnormal (corrective saccade) in unilateral peripheral vestibular loss (vestibular neuritis); normal in posterior fossa stroke — important in HINTS exam
- HINTS exam (Head impulse, Nystagmus, Test of Skew): in acute prolonged vertigo — differentiates peripheral from central cause
- Romberg test, gait assessment — cerebellar/proprioceptive function
- Cranial nerve examination — if central cause suspected
- Ear examination — otoscopy for otitis media, cholesteatoma; tuning fork (Rinne, Weber) if hearing loss present

**Investigations:**
- Postural BP: in-person measurement
- ECG: arrhythmia assessment in presyncope/syncope presentation
- FBC (anaemia), glucose, TFTs, electrolytes: in non-specific dizziness or relevant comorbidities
- Audiometry: if sensorineural hearing loss suspected (Ménière's, acoustic neuroma)
- MRI brain (posterior fossa): if central cause suspected (neurological features, risk factors for stroke, abnormal HINTS, persisting unexplained vertigo, suspected acoustic neuroma)

## Key Differentials

- BPPV — brief episodic, positional trigger, positive Dix-Hallpike; treat with Epley
- Vestibular neuritis — acute constant vertigo, days, no hearing loss, post-viral; supportive management
- Ménière's disease — episodic, hearing loss + tinnitus + aural fullness; ENT referral
- Posterior fossa stroke / TIA — acute vertigo with ANY neurological feature; HINTS exam abnormal; MRI urgently; do NOT dismiss as peripheral without careful assessment
- Postural hypotension — standing triggers, BP drop on standing; medication or autonomic cause
- Anxiety / panic disorder — dizziness is a common somatic symptom of anxiety; diagnosis of exclusion
- Cardiac arrhythmia — presyncope with palpitations; ECG required
- Acoustic neuroma (vestibular schwannoma) — unilateral sensorineural hearing loss + tinnitus ± unsteadiness; MRI; ENT/neurosurgery
- Medication side effect — check all medications; antihypertensives, benzodiazepines, antidepressants
- Anaemia — lightheadedness; check FBC
- Migraine-associated vertigo (vestibular migraine) — episodic vertigo ± headache, migraine features, photophobia

## Management Principles

**BPPV — Epley canalith repositioning manoeuvre:**
- Highly effective (80–90% resolution after 1–2 treatments) — GP can perform in rooms
- Posterior canal BPPV: Epley manoeuvre — sequence of head positions to move otoconia from posterior semicircular canal to utricle
- Horizontal canal BPPV: Barbecue roll manoeuvre — different repositioning sequence
- Patient instruction: can be self-performed at home using video guidance (Brandt-Daroff exercises); apps available

**Vestibular neuritis:**
- Supportive care: bed rest during acute phase; gradual mobilisation
- Vestibular suppressants: prochlorperazine 5–10 mg three times daily or promethazine 12.5–25 mg twice daily — for symptomatic relief in acute phase only (limit to 48–72 hours); prolonged use delays vestibular compensation
- Corticosteroids: prednisolone 50 mg daily for 3 days then tapering — evidence modest; may be used in severe acute vestibular neuritis
- Vestibular rehabilitation: exercises to promote central compensation; physio referral once acute phase resolved; highly effective

**Ménière's disease:**
- Low-sodium diet (<2 g sodium/day) — reduces endolymphatic pressure
- Diuretic: hydrochlorothiazide/triamterene — reduces fluid retention; specialist supervised
- Betahistine 16–24 mg three times daily — commonly used; evidence modest; trial is reasonable
- ENT referral for ongoing management; intratympanic gentamicin or surgical options for refractory

**Postural hypotension:**
- Review and reduce precipitating medications (antihypertensives, diuretics, alpha-blockers, antidepressants)
- Non-pharmacological: increase fluid and salt intake, compression stockings, rise slowly, avoid prolonged standing, head-of-bed elevation
- Fludrocortisone (9-alpha-fludrocortisone) or midodrine: specialist-supervised for autonomic postural hypotension

**Vestibular migraine:**
- Migrate preventive therapy; lifestyle triggers; see headache-migraine entry

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Prochlorperazine** (Stemetil — PBS unrestricted — vestibular suppressant; 5–10 mg three times daily or 25 mg buccal; limit to acute phase only — delays vestibular compensation)
- **Promethazine** (PBS unrestricted — vestibular suppressant; 12.5–25 mg twice daily; sedating)
- **Betahistine** (Serc — PBS unrestricted — Ménière's disease; 16–24 mg three times daily)
- **Prednisolone** (PBS unrestricted — for severe vestibular neuritis; 50 mg daily tapered over 3 weeks)
- **Diazepam** (PBS restricted — short-term vestibular suppressant in severe acute vertigo; Schedule 8; avoid prolonged use)
- **Note:** Vestibular suppressants (prochlorperazine, promethazine, benzodiazepines) should not be used beyond 48–72 hours as they impair vestibular compensation and slow recovery from vestibular neuritis

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Acute vertigo with ANY neurological features: diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia, facial droop, limb weakness, numbness, ataxia — posterior fossa stroke until proven otherwise; MRI urgently
- HINTS exam suggesting central cause: normal head impulse test + direction-changing nystagmus + vertical skew deviation — posterior circulation stroke; do not discharge without imaging
- Sudden onset severe vertigo with cardiovascular features — risk of vertebrobasilar TIA

**Urgent same-day assessment:**
- First episode of acute vestibular syndrome (days-long vertigo) — in-person assessment needed to perform HINTS exam and exclude central cause
- Vertigo with new onset hearing loss — exclude Ménière's, labyrinthitis, acoustic neuroma
- Falls with injury attributable to vertigo — in-person assessment for safety and investigation

**Non-urgent referral:**
- ENT/otolaryngology: recurrent BPPV, Ménière's disease, unilateral sensorineural hearing loss, suspected acoustic neuroma
- Neurologist: vestibular migraine, central cause, chronic unexplained vestibular dysfunction
- Vestibular physiotherapist: vestibular rehabilitation post-neuritis or for BPPV self-management training
- Audiologist: audiometry for suspected hearing loss

## Telehealth Suitability

Dizziness assessment is moderately suited to telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- History taking — timing, triggers, associated symptoms (highly diagnostic)
- Medication review for vestibulotoxic or hypotensive drugs
- Prescribing vestibular suppressants for confirmed BPPV or established vestibular neuritis
- Patient instruction for home Epley manoeuvre (video demonstration link)
- Betahistine prescribing for established Ménière's disease
- Safety netting and red flag education

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Dix-Hallpike test — definitive BPPV diagnosis requires in-person vestibular examination
- HINTS exam — essential to exclude posterior fossa stroke in acute prolonged vertigo
- Postural blood pressure measurement
- Ear examination (otoscopy)
- Any acute severe vertigo episode — in-person assessment to exclude central cause
