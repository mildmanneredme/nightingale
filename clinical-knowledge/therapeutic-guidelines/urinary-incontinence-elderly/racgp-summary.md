## Urinary Incontinence in Older Adults Assessment (RACGP)

Urinary incontinence (UI) in older adults is common, underreported, and significantly impacts quality of life, self-esteem, and social participation. Prevalence increases with age: affecting approximately 30–40% of community-dwelling older adults and >50% of residential aged care facility (RACF) residents. In older adults, UI has unique features: polypharmacy frequently contributes (anticholinergics cause overflow, diuretics cause urgency, antihypertensives cause orthostatic hypotension); functional incontinence is common (inability to mobilise to toilet due to frailty, arthritis, falls risk); and the usual pharmacological treatments for overactive bladder (anticholinergics) carry DISPROPORTIONATE HARM in the elderly (cognitive impairment, delirium, falls). Mirabegron (beta-3 agonist) is the preferred pharmacological option if medication is needed. Management is multidisciplinary: continence physiotherapy, occupational therapy (environmental modification), medication review (deprescribing contributing drugs), and pelvic floor training. See `urinary-incontinence` for general adult management; this entry focuses on the SPECIFIC considerations in the frail elderly. Telehealth is suitable for management review.

## Clinical Presentation

**Urinary incontinence types in older adults (similar to general population but with geriatric overlays):**

Urgency UI / Overactive Bladder (most common in older adults):
- Sudden compelling urge to void followed by leakage; frequency; nocturia
- Detrusor overactivity: increases with age; neurological disease (stroke, Parkinson's, dementia) amplifies
- Nocturia: waking ≥2× per night to void; causes falls; nocturnal polyuria (excretion of >33% of urine at night) — common in elderly; peripheral oedema mobilises at night

Functional Incontinence:
- Bladder and urethral function normal; patient cannot reach toilet in time
- Causes: frailty (slow gait, poor mobility), dementia (does not recognise need or find toilet), arthritis (difficulty with clothing), depression (loss of motivation)
- Management: environmental modifications; scheduled voiding; caregiver assistance — not pharmacological

Stress UI:
- Present in older women; pelvic floor weakness + reduced urethral closure pressure
- Worsened by obesity; chronic cough (COPD); constipation
- Pelvic floor physiotherapy remains effective even in elderly; surgical options available for fit older women

Overflow Incontinence:
- In older men: BPH; bladder outlet obstruction → urinary retention → overflow
- In both sexes: neurogenic bladder (diabetic autonomic neuropathy, spinal cord pathology)
- Post-void residual >200 mL; urinary catheterisation may be required if acute retention

**Medication-related incontinence in older adults (frequently missed):**
- Diuretics (furosemide, hydrochlorothiazide): urgency; timed voiding required; consider dose timing review
- ACE inhibitors: cough → increased intra-abdominal pressure → stress UI
- Anticholinergics (oxybutynin, solifenacin, tolterodine, TCAs, antihistamines, antipsychotics): can paradoxically cause overflow by reducing detrusor contractions; reduce ACB score (see `polypharmacy-review`)
- Alpha-blockers (prazosin, doxazosin): orthostatic hypotension → urgency to toilet + near-fall → urgency leakage
- Sedatives/hypnotics: reduced awareness of bladder fullness; nocturnal incontinence
- Opioids: urinary retention → overflow

**Delirium and acute-onset UI:**
- New-onset or worsened UI + confusion = delirium until proven otherwise
- UTI is a common precipitant of delirium + UI in frail elderly
- MSU (MCS) mandatory for new-onset confusion + UI

## Assessment Approach

**Focused history (geriatric-specific):**
- Timing and triggers: urgency (urgent desire first), stress (effort), nocturnal, functional (cannot reach toilet)
- Onset: sudden (UTI, delirium, stroke, spinal cord pathology) vs gradual (age-related, BPH, pelvic floor weakness)
- Medications: diuretic timing; anticholinergics; sedatives; alpha-blockers
- Mobility: gait speed; falls history; ability to unbutton clothing quickly
- Cognitive function: abbreviated MMSE or GPCOG; dementia worsens functional incontinence
- Bowel: faecal impaction aggravates UI and urinary retention; always assess

**Physical examination:**
- Abdominal: bladder palpation (overflow — distended bladder); faecal impaction on abdominal palpation
- Rectal: BPH (DRE — men); faecal impaction; rectal prolapse (women)
- Pelvic examination (women): pelvic organ prolapse; vaginal atrophy (GSM)
- Neurological: lower limb power, reflexes; spinal cord compression features
- Gait and mobility: time the 10-metre walk; assess ability to unbutton clothes; falls risk

**Investigations:**
- MSU MCS: exclude UTI (treat and reassess — UTI commonly worsens UI)
- Post-void residual (PVR) bladder scan: all older adults with UI; >200 mL = significant retention; urology referral
- FBS + HbA1c: uncontrolled diabetes causes polyuria
- Serum Ca²⁺: hypercalcaemia causes polyuria + confusion
- Serum Na⁺: hyponatraemia (can exacerbate nocturia); oedema assessment

## Key Differentials

- UTI: treat first; reassess incontinence after treatment
- Delirium: acute-onset confusion + incontinence = delirium; MSU; comprehensive assessment
- Spinal cord pathology (myelopathy): acute onset urinary symptoms + leg weakness → MRI spine; 000
- BPH (men): overflow; hesitancy; poor stream; post-void residual elevated; urology
- Faecal impaction: overflow incontinence + urinary symptoms; rectal examination; urgent bowel treatment (enema, manual removal)
- Pelvic organ prolapse: urinary symptoms + prolapse; urogynaecology

## Management Principles

**Non-pharmacological (always first-line):**

Bladder training:
- Scheduled voiding: every 2–3 hours regardless of urge; with dementia — prompted voiding by caregiver
- Urgency suppression techniques: stop, relax, pelvic floor contraction, wait 3–5 minutes, then walk calmly to toilet
- Bladder diary (3 days): identify patterns; guide voiding schedule

Continence physiotherapy:
- Pelvic floor muscle training (PFMT): effective even in older adults; refer to continence physiotherapist
- Continence Foundation of Australia: helpline 1800 330 066; resource provision; therapist directory

Environmental modifications (critical for functional incontinence):
- Bedside commode: for nocturnal incontinence with limited mobility
- Toilet frame and raised toilet seat: reduces time and effort to transfer to toilet
- Ensure clear path to bathroom: remove trip hazards; adequate lighting; nightlights
- Clothing modification: elastic waistbands; hook and loop fasteners (Velcro); easy-access clothing

Fluid management:
- Adequate fluid intake (1.5–2 L/day): do NOT restrict fluids — concentrates urine → worsens urgency; increases UTI risk
- Reduce evening fluid intake after 6 PM: reduces nocturia
- Avoid caffeine and alcohol: bladder irritants; worsen urgency

**Medication management (specific to older adults):**

DEPRESCRIBE contributing drugs (most impactful intervention):
- Diuretics: review dose and timing; shift diuretic to morning if nocturnal is main problem; reduce dose if possible while maintaining cardiovascular control
- Anticholinergics: cease solifenacin/oxybutynin if contributing to overflow incontinence or cognitive impairment
- Calculate ACB (anticholinergic cognitive burden) score — target reduction; see `polypharmacy-review`
- Sedatives: reduce or cease if contributing to nocturnal incontinence

Topical oestrogen (post-menopausal women):
- Vaginal oestradiol (Vagifem 10 µg twice weekly): reduces urogenital atrophy → reduces urgency and stress UI; minimal systemic absorption; PBS-listed; safe even in breast cancer survivors (discuss with oncologist)

Pharmacological treatment for urgency UI in older adults:
- Mirabegron (Betmiga) 25–50 mg daily: PREFERRED over anticholinergics in older adults; beta-3 agonist; NO anticholinergic effects; PBS-restricted; check BP before initiating (contraindicated if BP >180/110); effective
- Anticholinergics (solifenacin, oxybutynin): AVOID or use cautiously in elderly; risk of cognitive impairment, delirium, falls, constipation, urinary retention; if used — lowest dose; monitor ACB score
- Desmopressin (DDAVP): nocturnal polyuria (voiding >33% urine at night); nasal spray or tablets; effective for nocturia; CAUTION: risk of hyponatraemia (especially in elderly); check Na⁺ before and 3 days after initiating; avoid if heart failure, hyponatraemia, or taking diuretics

**Catheterisation:**
- Intermittent self-catheterisation (ISC): preferred over indwelling catheter for chronic retention
- Indwelling urethral catheter: only when ISC not feasible; skin integrity (intractable perineal skin breakdown); terminal illness comfort
- Long-term catheter complications: UTI, bladder stones, bypassing, urethral trauma; regular review (every 3 months)

**Residential aged care facility (RACF) considerations:**
- Continence aid products: assess type (pads, pants, bed protectors); prescribe appropriate absorbency
- Government Continence Aids Payment Scheme (CAPS): annual payment for eligible Australians with permanent and severe UI; continence.org.au
- Prompted voiding programme: for residents with dementia; evidence-based; requires care staff training

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Mirabegron (Betmiga) 25 mg or 50 mg** (PBS restricted: OAB; preferred agent for elderly — no anticholinergic burden; check BP before initiating; start 25 mg; increase to 50 mg if insufficient response after 4 weeks; monitor BP)
- **Vaginal oestradiol 10 µg (Vagifem)** (PBS restricted: vulvovaginal atrophy; twice weekly maintenance; reduces urgency and stress UI in post-menopausal women; minimal systemic absorption)
- **Desmopressin (DDAVP) 200 µg nasal spray** (PBS restricted: primary nocturnal enuresis; adults; use for nocturnal polyuria contributing to nocturia; CAUTION hyponatraemia in elderly — check Na⁺)
- **Note:** Solifenacin (Vesicare) and oxybutynin are PBS-listed for OAB but should be used with EXTREME CAUTION in older adults. Anticholinergic burden in elderly patients increases risk of dementia, falls, cognitive impairment, constipation, and urinary retention. Mirabegron is the preferred pharmacological option if conservative management is insufficient.

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent:**
- Acute urinary retention with pain: 000 or urgent ED; catheterisation; urology
- New-onset incontinence + confusion (delirium): urgent assessment; MSU; admission if clinically indicated
- Neurological symptoms with new UI (spinal cord myelopathy — weakness, saddle anaesthesia): 000; MRI spine

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Continence physiotherapist: all patients; essential first-line
- Urology (men): BPH with significant post-void residual or obstruction; overflow incontinence
- Urogynaecology (women): prolapse + incontinence; incontinence after conservative management failure
- Geriatric assessment: frail elderly with multiple UI types + functional decline + polypharmacy; comprehensive geriatric assessment

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Bladder diary review; medication adjustment
- Mirabegron or topical oestrogen initiation and titration
- Anticholinergic deprescribing support
- Environmental modification advice; CAPS application support
- Continence physiotherapy referral

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Abdominal and rectal examination (faecal impaction; BPH; overflow detection)
- Post-void residual measurement
- Pelvic floor assessment; vaginal examination
- Falls risk assessment with mobility evaluation
