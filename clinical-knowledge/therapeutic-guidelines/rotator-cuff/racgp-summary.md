## Rotator Cuff Assessment (RACGP)

Rotator cuff disorders are the most common cause of shoulder pain in Australian general practice, accounting for approximately 70% of shoulder presentations. The rotator cuff comprises four muscles (SITS: supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, subscapularis) whose tendons form a cuff around the humeral head, providing shoulder stability and rotation. Rotator cuff pathology exists on a spectrum: tendinopathy (degeneration without tear), partial-thickness tear, and full-thickness tear. Subacromial impingement syndrome describes mechanical compression of rotator cuff tendons under the coracoacromial arch, contributing to tendinopathy. Rotator cuff tears are common (present in 30–50% of adults >60 years, mostly asymptomatic). GPs play a central role in: diagnosing rotator cuff pathology, initiating physiotherapy, managing pain, providing corticosteroid injections, and referring for surgical assessment when indicated. Telehealth is suitable for follow-up; initial shoulder assessment requires in-person examination.

## Clinical Presentation

**Common presentations:**
- Lateral shoulder pain (deltoid region): aching, often worse at night (lying on affected shoulder); worse with overhead activities, reaching behind back
- Painful arc: pain between 60–120° of abduction — subacromial impingement pattern; may be able to get through arc with continued elevation
- Rotator cuff tendinopathy: chronic pain; aggravated by repetitive activity (overhead work, swimming, throwing)
- Rotator cuff tear: sudden onset after acute injury (fall onto outstretched hand, forced abduction); or gradual in older patients with chronic degeneration
- Weakness: difficulty raising arm above shoulder; cannot hold arm elevated against gravity (complete tear — drop arm sign)
- Stiffness: reduced ROM — consider adhesive capsulitis (frozen shoulder) if diffuse loss of movement in all planes

**Distinguishing tear from tendinopathy:**
- Acute severe tear after trauma with marked weakness: likely significant tear
- Gradual pain without clear weakness: more likely tendinopathy or partial tear
- Night pain out of proportion to daytime pain: common in rotator cuff pathology generally
- Supraspinatus most commonly affected muscle/tendon

## Assessment Approach

**Clinical examination:**
- Active and passive ROM: abduction, flexion, external rotation, internal rotation; compare sides
- Painful arc: pain at 60–120° abduction = subacromial impingement
- Special tests:
  - Neer's test: examiner elevates patient's arm in forward flexion with forearm pronated while stabilising scapula; pain = positive subacromial impingement
  - Hawkins-Kennedy test: arm forward flexed to 90°, then internally rotated; pain = positive subacromial impingement
  - Empty can test (Jobe's test): arm at 90° abduction and 30° forward flexion (scapular plane), thumb down; examiner pushes down while patient resists; pain or weakness = supraspinatus tendinopathy or tear
  - External rotation lag sign: if arm drops when held passively in external rotation = infraspinatus/teres minor tear
  - Lift-off test (subscapularis): hand behind back, push away from body — subscapularis integrity
  - Drop arm test: patient slowly lowers from fully elevated position; arm drops = significant supraspinatus tear
- Acromioclavicular joint (ACJ): direct palpation tenderness
- Bicipital tendon: Speed's test (arm forward flexed, supinated, examiner resists); long head biceps pathology

**Investigations:**
- X-ray shoulder (AP, outlet, axillary views): excludes glenohumeral arthritis, ACJ arthritis, calcific tendinopathy (calcium deposits), Hill-Sachs lesion (instability); NOT diagnostic for rotator cuff tear
- Ultrasound shoulder: first-line imaging for rotator cuff pathology; high sensitivity and specificity for full-thickness tear; shows tendon degeneration, partial tears, calcium deposits; dynamic assessment; GP can order
- MRI shoulder: gold standard for tear characterisation (size, retraction, fatty infiltration); required pre-operatively; may need referral to order
- Diagnostic ultrasound-guided injection: if diagnostic uncertainty; if pain improves with subacromial steroid — supports impingement as pain generator

## Key Differentials

- Adhesive capsulitis (frozen shoulder): diffuse painful restriction of ALL shoulder movements (particularly external rotation); distinguishable from rotator cuff (which has limitation mainly in abduction); three stages (freezing, frozen, thawing); treat with physiotherapy and corticosteroid injection
- Glenohumeral osteoarthritis: older age; crepitus; radiographic changes; loss of all movements; see `osteoarthritis` entry
- Acromioclavicular joint pathology: pain localised to AC joint; tender on direct AC palpation; pain with cross-body adduction; Zanca view on X-ray
- Cervical radiculopathy: C5 root radiculopathy mimics shoulder pain; neurological examination; cervical movement reproduction; MRI cervical spine
- Shoulder instability: younger patient; history of dislocations; apprehension and relocation tests
- Calcific tendinopathy: calcium deposits in tendon; acute severe pain; X-ray shows calcification; responds to needling/aspiration ± injection

## Management Principles

**Conservative management (first-line — most patients):**

Physiotherapy (cornerstone):
- Evidence-based: strengthening rotator cuff (especially scapular stabilisers) and shoulder girdle exercises; most effective for tendinopathy and partial tears
- Eccentric exercise loading: particularly for tendinopathy
- Postural correction: thoracic kyphosis correction reduces subacromial space narrowing
- Duration: minimum 6–12 weeks of consistent physiotherapy; most patients improve
- MBS-funded via CDM plan: up to 5 allied health (physiotherapy) sessions per year

Activity modification:
- Avoid provocative activities (overhead reaching, heavy lifting) during acute phase
- Gradual return to activity with physiotherapy guidance

Analgesia:
- Paracetamol: first-line mild-moderate pain
- NSAIDs (ibuprofen 400–600 mg TDS, naproxen 500 mg BD): useful for acute phase or flares; short courses (2–4 weeks); GI protection if prolonged; limited long-term evidence for tendinopathy
- Topical NSAIDs (Voltaren Emulgel): diclofenac topical; reduces local pain; fewer systemic side effects; evidence in tendinopathy

Corticosteroid injection (subacromial):
- Indication: moderate-severe pain not responding to analgesia + physiotherapy ×4–6 weeks; improves short-term pain and function; enables patient to participate in physiotherapy
- Ultrasound-guided subacromial injection: methylprednisolone 40 mg or triamcinolone 40 mg + local anaesthetic (lignocaine 1%); ultrasound guidance improves accuracy
- Benefit: short-term (4–8 weeks); no long-term advantage over physiotherapy alone
- Maximum 2–3 injections per year; space ≥6–8 weeks; avoid >3 injections (tendon weakening risk)
- Calcific tendinopathy: barbotage (needling and aspiration of calcium) ± steroid; specialist/radiologist

**Surgical management (when conservative fails):**
- Arthroscopic rotator cuff repair: indicated for full-thickness tears causing significant symptoms and functional limitation, unresponsive to 3–6 months conservative management
- Subacromial decompression (arthroscopic acromioplasty): for persistent subacromial impingement refractory to conservative treatment; evidence less clear in recent RCTs (UK CSAW study)
- Referral to: orthopaedic shoulder surgeon

**Return to work / sport:**
- Sedentary work: may continue throughout treatment
- Manual work or overhead activities: modified duties; physiotherapy guidance; return when strength ≥ 80% of contralateral side
- Athletes (swimmers, throwers): specific sports physiotherapy; gradual return to sport program

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Methylprednisolone acetate (Depo-Medrol 40 mg/mL)** or **triamcinolone (Kenacort 40 mg/mL)**: subacromial injection; not PBS-listed as supply; practice supply; minimal cost; ultrasound-guided preferred
- **NSAIDs oral (naproxen 500 mg BD, ibuprofen 400–600 mg TDS)** (PBS unrestricted: musculoskeletal pain; short course acute phase; GI protection with PPI if ≥65 years or peptic ulcer risk)
- **Diclofenac topical (Voltaren Emulgel 1%)** (PBS unrestricted: local musculoskeletal pain; apply TDS–QDS to affected shoulder; less systemic absorption than oral NSAIDs; evidence in tendinopathy)
- **Note:** Corticosteroid injections for rotator cuff pathology provide short-term benefit enabling physiotherapy participation; should be combined with structured physiotherapy, not used as definitive monotherapy

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent orthopaedic:**
- Acute complete rotator cuff rupture after acute trauma: significant weakness, cannot hold arm elevated, positive drop arm sign; early surgical repair has better outcomes than delayed; refer within days-weeks
- Massive rotator cuff tear with pseudoparalysis: arm cannot be raised at all despite passive mobility; urgent orthopaedic assessment

**Non-urgent orthopaedic:**
- Full-thickness tear with functional limitation: failed 3–6 months conservative management; surgical repair candidate
- Persistent subacromial impingement refractory to physiotherapy + 2 steroid injections: arthroscopic assessment

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Physiotherapist: first referral for all rotator cuff pathology; CDM plan
- Radiology (ultrasound shoulder): imaging before injection or when clinical diagnosis uncertain

## Telehealth Suitability

Rotator cuff follow-up is partially suitable for telehealth; initial assessment requires in-person.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Review of physiotherapy progress and pain levels
- Medication review (NSAIDs, analgesia)
- MRI result discussion and surgical referral planning
- Corticosteroid injection response assessment
- Work/sport return planning discussion

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Clinical shoulder examination (ROM, provocative tests, strength testing)
- Corticosteroid injection administration
- Ultrasound-guided intervention
- Initial assessment of acute tear presentation
