## Alcohol Use Disorder and Harmful Drinking Assessment (RACGP)

Alcohol use disorder (AUD) and harmful drinking are highly prevalent in Australia — approximately 1 in 6 Australians drinks at levels that put them at risk of alcohol-related harm. AUD is the second most common substance use disorder after tobacco dependence. Alcohol causes over 5,000 deaths and 157,000 hospitalisations annually in Australia. The 2020 NHMRC guidelines revised safe drinking limits downward: no more than 10 standard drinks per week and no more than 4 on any single day to reduce lifetime risk. GP-based brief intervention is one of the most effective and cost-effective treatments for hazardous/harmful drinking. Telehealth is suitable for AUD assessment, brief intervention, and pharmacotherapy management.

## Clinical Presentation

**Spectrum of alcohol use:**
- Low-risk drinking: ≤10 standard drinks/week, ≤4 on any day (2020 NHMRC guidelines)
- Hazardous use: pattern likely to cause harm if continued; not yet causing harm
- Harmful use: drinking causing physical, psychological, or social harm; below AUD threshold
- Alcohol use disorder (AUD): DSM-5 — ≥2 of 11 criteria over 12 months (craving, failed attempts to cut down, continued use despite harm, withdrawal, tolerance, etc.); mild (2–3 criteria), moderate (4–5), severe (≥6)

**Alcohol withdrawal syndrome:** Last drinks typically 6–12 hours ago; peaks at 24–48 hours; resolves by 5–7 days. Features: tremor, sweating, tachycardia, hypertension, nausea/vomiting, anxiety, insomnia. Severe withdrawal: seizures (usually 6–48 hours after last drink), delirium tremens (DTs: confusion, hallucinations, autonomic instability — onset 48–72 hours, risk of death without treatment).

**Common presentations:**
- Routine GP visit with alcohol-related problem (liver disease, hypertension, depression, injuries, falls)
- Patient or family member raising alcohol concern
- Incidental disclosure of heavy drinking during screening (AUDIT)
- Seeking medication to reduce alcohol use or maintain abstinence
- Alcohol withdrawal — patient requesting support for cessation

**Physical complications:** Liver disease (fatty liver → alcoholic hepatitis → cirrhosis), pancreatitis, cardiomyopathy, Wernicke's encephalopathy (thiamine deficiency — confused, ataxic patient), peripheral neuropathy, cancer (mouth, pharynx, oesophagus, liver, colorectal, breast), hypertension, anaemia (macrocytic), immune suppression.

**Psychiatric comorbidities:** Very common — depression, anxiety, PTSD; bidirectional relationship; alcohol commonly used to self-medicate; treatment of both essential.

## Assessment Approach

**Routine screening in general practice:**
- AUDIT (Alcohol Use Disorders Identification Test) — 10-item; AUDIT-C (3-item screener) for rapid use. AUDIT ≥8 (men) or ≥7 (women) = hazardous/harmful drinking; AUDIT ≥15 = likely AUD.
- CAGE questionnaire (4 items) — simpler screen; ≥2 positive = significant concern
- Non-judgemental approach — use motivational interviewing style; note RACGP advocates routine alcohol screening at health assessments (age 45, 75, etc.)

**Detailed assessment (positive screen or clinical concern):**
- Quantity, frequency, pattern of drinking; last drink
- DSM-5 AUD criteria assessment
- Withdrawal risk: Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment for Alcohol (CIWA-Ar) if withdrawal suspected
- Medical complications: LFTs (AST, ALT, GGT — GGT most sensitive for recent heavy drinking), FBC (MCV — macrocytosis), gamma-GT, lipase (pancreatitis), BP
- Mental health: PHQ-9, GAD-7, PTSD screen
- Social: employment, relationships, legal issues, driving; family impact
- Motivation: Stages of Change model — precontemplation, contemplation, preparation, action, maintenance

**Standard drinks reference:** One Australian standard drink = 10 g alcohol (full-strength beer 375 mL = 1.4 std drinks; standard wine 150 mL = 1.4 std drinks; spirits nip 30 mL = 1.0 std drink).

## Key Differentials

- Benzodiazepine or other CNS depressant withdrawal — similar withdrawal syndrome; assess all substance use
- Acute alcohol intoxication vs medical emergency (hypoglycaemia, head injury, metabolic encephalopathy) — never assume altered consciousness is alcohol only
- Wernicke's encephalopathy — confusion + ataxia + ophthalmoplegia in heavy drinker; thiamine deficiency; treat urgently with thiamine IV/IM before glucose
- Alcoholic hepatitis vs other liver disease — clinical and biochemical assessment; may require hepatology
- Delirium from medical cause — infection, metabolic; distinguish from alcohol withdrawal delirium

## Management Principles

**Brief intervention (FRAMES model) — for hazardous/harmful drinking:**
Brief structured advice at GP level reduces drinking by 20–30% at follow-up. Components: Feedback (personalised AUDIT result), Responsibility (patient's own), Advice (recommend low-risk limits), Menu of options, Empathy, Self-efficacy. 5–15 minutes at routine consult; highly effective.

**Motivational interviewing:** Core approach for behaviour change in alcohol use — explore ambivalence, build discrepancy, avoid confrontation, support self-efficacy.

**Alcohol use disorder — management:**

Managed withdrawal (community or inpatient):
- Community withdrawal: suitable for mild-moderate dependence, no severe withdrawal history, supportive home environment; daily GP review or community drug and alcohol service
- Inpatient withdrawal: severe dependence, previous withdrawal seizures, DTs history, poor social support, concurrent medical illness — hospital admission

Medication for withdrawal:
- Diazepam (or other long-acting benzodiazepine): CIWA-Ar guided or fixed-dose reducing regimen; prevents seizures and DTs; titrate to symptom control; monitor closely; avoid in severe liver disease — prefer oxazepam (shorter-acting, no active metabolites)
- Thiamine (Vitamin B1): MANDATORY in any heavy drinker — give before glucose to prevent precipitating/worsening Wernicke's encephalopathy; oral thiamine 300 mg daily during withdrawal; IM/IV if malnutrition, vomiting, or Wernicke's suspected (high-dose parenteral thiamine: Pabrinex — hospital setting)

Pharmacotherapy for relapse prevention (after withdrawal complete):
- Naltrexone: 50 mg daily; blocks opioid reward pathway of alcohol; reduces craving and relapse; first-line for relapse prevention; hepatotoxic at high dose — LFTs before and during; contraindicated in opioid use
- Acamprosate (Campral): 666 mg three times daily; reduces craving via GABA/glutamate modulation; safe in liver disease; renally cleared — avoid in renal impairment; requires commitment to abstinence
- Disulfiram (Antabuse): 200 mg daily; aversive — causes flushing, nausea, palpitations if alcohol consumed; supervised use only; rarely used; risk of severe reaction; hepatotoxic; requires motivated patient with supervision
- Baclofen: off-label; evidence growing for craving reduction; particularly useful in severe liver disease (safer than naltrexone); 10–30 mg 3 times daily

**Psychosocial support (essential, concurrent with pharmacotherapy):**
- Alcohol counselling and psychotherapy (CBT, motivational enhancement therapy)
- Self-help: Alcoholics Anonymous (AA), SMART Recovery Australia; free, accessible
- Community drug and alcohol services — GP referral; free specialist support
- Residential rehabilitation: for severe AUD or failed outpatient attempts
- Address mental health comorbidities concurrently (depression, PTSD, anxiety)

**Driving and alcohol:** Heavy drinkers may drive impaired; GPs have mandatory reporting obligations in some states if patient drives with a condition (alcohol dependence) that impairs driving.

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Diazepam** (PBS restricted — alcohol withdrawal; Schedule 8; short-term use for withdrawal; titrate carefully; PDMS check required in most states)
- **Oxazepam** (PBS restricted — preferred over diazepam in liver disease for withdrawal; Schedule 4; shorter-acting)
- **Thiamine** (PBS unrestricted — oral 100–300 mg daily; IM Pabrinex hospital formulary for severe deficiency)
- **Naltrexone** (Vivitrol, generic — PBS restricted: alcohol dependence; 50 mg daily; check LFTs; not for opioid-dependent patients)
- **Acamprosate** (Campral — PBS restricted: alcohol dependence; 666 mg three times daily; requires abstinence)
- **Baclofen** (PBS unrestricted for other indications; off-label for alcohol craving — requires specialist initiation or experience)
- **Note:** Disulfiram is not PBS-listed; private prescription; rarely used in practice

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Alcohol withdrawal seizure — any new seizure in context of alcohol withdrawal; IV diazepam + thiamine; hospital admission
- Delirium tremens: confusion, hallucinations, autonomic instability 48–72 hours after last drink — medical emergency; high mortality without hospital admission and IV benzodiazepines
- Wernicke's encephalopathy: confusion + ataxia in heavy drinker — high-dose IV/IM thiamine urgently; do not delay for glucose
- Acute alcoholic hepatitis with jaundice and coagulopathy — hepatology urgently
- Alcohol-related self-harm or suicide attempt — mental health emergency

**Urgent same-day review:**
- CIWA-Ar score ≥10 in community withdrawal patient — escalate withdrawal management or consider inpatient admission
- Moderate-severe AUD in pregnancy — high-risk; specialist obstetric and addiction medicine involvement

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Drug and alcohol service / addiction medicine: complex AUD, repeated relapse, comorbid substance use, pregnancy
- Hepatology: suspected cirrhosis, portal hypertension, ongoing ALD
- Psychologist/psychiatrist: comorbid depression, PTSD, anxiety disorder

## Telehealth Suitability

Alcohol use disorder management is suitable for telehealth in stable patients.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- AUDIT screening and brief intervention
- Motivational interviewing and behaviour change support
- Prescribing relapse-prevention pharmacotherapy (naltrexone, acamprosate) for stable patients
- Mental health comorbidity screening and management plan
- Referral to drug and alcohol services

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Moderate-to-severe alcohol withdrawal — in-person CIWA-Ar assessment before community withdrawal
- Physical examination for complications (liver disease, neuropathy)
- Blood tests (LFTs, FBC, GGT monitoring during pharmacotherapy)
- Patients with withdrawal seizure history or DTs — inpatient management preferred
