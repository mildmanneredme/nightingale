## Endometriosis Assessment (RACGP)

Endometriosis is a chronic inflammatory condition in which endometrial-like tissue grows outside the uterus — most commonly on the ovaries, fallopian tubes, peritoneum, and bowel. It affects approximately 1 in 9 Australian women of reproductive age (approximately 830,000 women). It is a leading cause of chronic pelvic pain, dysmenorrhoea, dyspareunia, and infertility. Diagnostic delay is a major problem in Australia — average time from symptom onset to diagnosis is 6.4 years. GPs play a central role in suspecting endometriosis early, initiating empiric hormonal therapy, and making timely gynaecological referral for definitive diagnosis (laparoscopy). Telehealth is suitable for ongoing medication review and pain management; initial assessment and pelvic examination require in-person attendance.

## Clinical Presentation

**Cardinal symptoms (PQRST approach):**
- Dysmenorrhoea: severe cyclical period pain; pain out of proportion to menstrual flow; often starting days before menstruation
- Chronic pelvic pain: persistent or cyclical; worse perimenstrually; not fully relieved by analgesia
- Dyspareunia: deep dyspareunia (pain with deep penetration); posterior fornix or uterosacral tenderness
- Dyschezia: pain with defecation, especially menstrually — suggests posterior cul-de-sac or bowel involvement
- Dysuria: cyclical urinary symptoms — suggests bladder or ureteric involvement
- Subfertility: endometriosis causes 30–50% of infertility in women referred to fertility clinics
- Fatigue: often severe and underrecognised; linked to inflammation and pain

**Patterns of disease:**
- Peritoneal superficial endometriosis: powder burn lesions on peritoneum; may cause little pain or severe pain
- Ovarian endometrioma (chocolate cyst): blood-filled ovarian cyst; visible on transvaginal ultrasound; associated with subfertility
- Deep infiltrating endometriosis (DIE): penetrates >5 mm into tissue; most symptomatic; uterosacral ligaments, rectovaginal septum, bowel — severe dysmenorrhoea, dyschezia, deep dyspareunia

**Endometriosis-associated comorbidities:**
- Depression and anxiety: very common — secondary to chronic pain and diagnostic delay
- IBS: often misdiagnosed as IBS; bowel symptoms that are cyclically worse with periods suggest endometriosis
- Adenomyosis: endometrial glands within myometrium; heavy periods and dysmenorrhoea; often coexists
- Central sensitisation: after years of chronic pain — pain becomes less cyclical and more constant

## Assessment Approach

**Clinical history (key questions):**
- Pain: cyclical vs constant; severity (VAS); impact on daily life, school, work, relationships
- Menstrual history: flow, cycle length, clots, duration — heavy menstrual bleeding common
- Bowel and bladder symptoms: cyclical worsening — key differentiator from other causes
- Sexual function: dyspareunia; impact on relationship
- Fertility: trying to conceive; timing
- Family history: endometriosis has a 7-fold increased risk in first-degree relatives

**Physical examination:**
- Abdomen: tenderness, masses
- Pelvic examination (bimanual): uterosacral nodularity; fixed retroverted uterus; adnexal masses; point tenderness on palpation of posterior fornix
- Speculum: visible lesions on cervix or posterior fornix (rare)
- Note: normal pelvic examination does NOT exclude endometriosis

**Investigations:**
- Transvaginal ultrasound (TVUS): preferred imaging; identifies ovarian endometrioma (chocolate cyst), DIE nodules; poor sensitivity for superficial peritoneal disease; normal TVUS does NOT exclude endometriosis
- CA-125: elevated in endometriosis but non-specific; NOT a diagnostic test; may be used in monitoring known disease
- MRI pelvis: better for mapping DIE; useful pre-operatively; ordered by gynaecologist
- Laparoscopy: gold standard diagnostic and surgical treatment; ideally with histological confirmation

**Symptom severity tools:**
- Endometriosis Symptom Diary: patient-reported; tracks symptom patterns across cycles
- WERF EPHect questionnaire: research tool; GP can use validated VAS and functional impact questions

## Key Differentials

- Primary dysmenorrhoea: no underlying pathology; onset at menarche; improves with NSAIDs; no deep dyspareunia; differentiate from endometriosis with lack of structural features
- Adenomyosis: diffuse uterine tenderness; heavy periods; TVUS shows characteristic features; often coexists with endometriosis
- Ovarian cyst (functional): follicular or corpus luteum cysts; transient on TVUS; non-tender unless ruptured; no cyclical worsening
- Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID): acute pelvic tenderness; vaginal discharge; fever; STI history; treat promptly with antibiotics — see `pelvic-inflammatory-disease` entry
- IBS: bowel symptoms not cyclically linked to periods; no pelvic tenderness; see `irritable-bowel-syndrome` entry
- Uterine fibroid: heavy menstrual bleeding; pressure symptoms; TVUS shows fibroid; rarely causes severe dysmenorrhoea

## Management Principles

**Empirical treatment without laparoscopy (acceptable for mild-moderate symptoms):**

First-line hormonal therapy (suppress ovulation — suppresses endometriosis):
- Combined oral contraceptive pill (COCP): continuous use (no pill-free interval) reduces menstruation and cyclical pain; most evidence; first-line in younger patients; Microgynon, Levlen, Yasmin
- Levonorgestrel IUD (Mirena): reduces dysmenorrhoea and menstrual blood loss; effective for pelvic pain; particularly useful if contraception desired; PBS-listed
- Progestogen-only pill (norethisterone, medroxyprogesterone): continuous use; reduces menstrual bleeding; alternative to COCP
- Depot medroxyprogesterone (Depo-Provera): 3-monthly injection; amenorrhoea in many patients; bone density concern with long-term use

Second-line hormonal:
- Etonogestrel implant (Implanon): effective; 3-year duration
- GnRH agonists (leuprorelin — Lucrin): specialist-initiated; induces medical menopause; maximum 6 months without add-back HRT; used pre- or post-surgery

**Analgesia:**
- NSAIDs: start 1–2 days before menstruation; naproxen 500 mg BD, ibuprofen 400–600 mg TDS; limit to 5 days; first-line for dysmenorrhoea
- Paracetamol: adjunct; limited efficacy alone for endometriosis pain
- Opioids: not recommended for long-term endometriosis pain; short courses only if required for acute flares

**Non-pharmacological:**
- Heat therapy: local heat (heat pack); proven modest benefit for dysmenorrhoea
- Physiotherapy: pelvic floor physiotherapy for dyspareunia; TENS for period pain
- Psychology and pain neuroscience education: central sensitisation management; CBT for chronic pain
- Multidisciplinary endometriosis clinic: for complex DIE or refractory pain — gynaecology, colorectal surgery, physiotherapy, psychology

**Surgical (gynaecologist):**
- Laparoscopic excision: definitive diagnosis + surgical treatment; superior to ablation for deep disease
- Ovarian cystectomy: excision (not drainage) of endometrioma — recurrence lower
- Hysterectomy ± oophorectomy: severe refractory disease; not first-line; not curative (recurrence possible without oophorectomy)

**Fertility:**
- Endometriosis-related infertility: refer to fertility specialist early (CREI — subspecialist in reproductive endocrinology)
- Surgical treatment of endometrioma may improve natural conception rates but reduces ovarian reserve
- IVF: highly effective for endometriosis-related infertility

**Psychological support:**
- Depression screening (PHQ-9) at every review: very common; CBT and antidepressants if indicated
- Acknowledge diagnostic delay, dismissal, and impact on quality of life — validates patient experience
- Endometriosis Australia peer support: endometriosisaustralia.org

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Levonorgestrel IUD (Mirena 52 mg)** (PBS restricted: heavy menstrual bleeding — PBS-listed; also used off-label for endometriosis pain; 5-year duration; requires insertion)
- **Norethisterone 5 mg** (PBS unrestricted: menstrual disorders; continuous use 5–10 mg daily for endometriosis; breakthrough bleeding common initially)
- **Medroxyprogesterone (Depo-Provera 150 mg/mL)** (PBS restricted: contraception; 3-monthly IM injection; bone density monitoring with long-term use)
- **Leuprorelin (Lucrin) GnRH agonist** (PBS restricted: endometriosis — requires specialist initiation; add-back HRT required for courses >6 months)
- **NSAIDs (naproxen, ibuprofen)** (PBS unrestricted: pain; naproxen 500 mg BD preferred; start before onset of period pain)
- **COCP (various)** (PBS unrestricted: contraception; use continuously for endometriosis pain management — skip placebo week)
- **Note:** Endometriosis management requires long-term hormonal suppression; trial of empirical hormonal therapy is appropriate before laparoscopy in patients without urgent surgical indication

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Ruptured ovarian endometrioma: acute abdomen; severe sudden pelvic pain; haemodynamic compromise — surgical emergency
- Bowel or ureteric obstruction from advanced DIE: vomiting, severe pain, hydronephrosis — surgical

**Urgent gynaecology:**
- Suspected endometrioma on TVUS: gynaecology review within 4 weeks for surgical planning
- Active subfertility with suspected endometriosis: early fertility specialist referral (CREI)
- Symptoms refractory to 3–6 months of hormonal therapy: laparoscopic diagnosis and treatment
- Adolescents with severe dysmenorrhoea not responding to COCP + NSAIDs within 3 months: paediatric/adolescent gynaecology

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Gynaecologist: for laparoscopy, surgical management, or complex hormonal management
- Pelvic floor physiotherapist: dyspareunia, pelvic pain
- Psychologist (GP Mental Health Care Plan): depression, anxiety, chronic pain adjustment
- Endometriosis multidisciplinary clinic: complex DIE with bowel/bladder involvement

## Telehealth Suitability

Endometriosis ongoing management is suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Ongoing hormonal therapy review and titration
- Pain management review and analgesia adjustment
- Psychological support referral coordination
- PHQ-9 depression screening and management
- Ultrasound result discussion
- Fertility planning counselling
- Patient education and support resource provision

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial pelvic examination and bimanual assessment
- Speculum examination
- Transvaginal ultrasound — radiology or gynaecology
- IUD insertion (Mirena)
- Laparoscopy — gynaecology
