## Metabolic Syndrome Assessment (RACGP)

Metabolic syndrome is a clustering of cardiovascular and metabolic risk factors — central obesity, hypertension, dyslipidaemia, and impaired glucose regulation — that together markedly amplify the risk of type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and non-alcoholic fatty liver disease. It is highly prevalent in Australia, affecting approximately 30–35% of adults, with rates rising due to increasing obesity rates, sedentary lifestyles, and ageing populations. Metabolic syndrome is not a discrete disease entity but a clinical signal that warrants aggressive lifestyle intervention and systematic management of each component risk factor. GPs are ideally placed to screen for, diagnose, and coordinate management of metabolic syndrome as part of routine chronic disease prevention.

## Clinical Presentation

**Diagnostic criteria (IDF/AHA/NHLBI harmonised 2009 — most widely used in Australia):**

Central obesity (essential component plus any 2 of the following):
- Waist circumference: ≥94 cm men; ≥80 cm women (European/Caucasian thresholds; lower thresholds for Asian: ≥90 cm men, ≥80 cm women)
- Fasting triglycerides ≥1.7 mmol/L OR on lipid-lowering therapy for raised TG
- HDL-cholesterol <1.03 mmol/L men / <1.29 mmol/L women OR on treatment for low HDL
- Blood pressure ≥130/85 mmHg OR on antihypertensive therapy
- Fasting plasma glucose ≥5.6 mmol/L OR on treatment for raised glucose / known T2DM

**Clinical associations:**
- Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD): elevated ALT; steatosis on ultrasound; metabolic syndrome is the most common cause of incidental liver enzyme elevation
- Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS): high prevalence of metabolic syndrome; screen all women with PCOS
- Obstructive sleep apnoea: associated with insulin resistance and metabolic syndrome; screen if obese with daytime sleepiness
- Depression and psychological distress: bidirectional association with metabolic syndrome

**Patient groups at highest risk:**
- Obesity (BMI ≥30 or central obesity)
- Family history of T2DM or CVD
- PCOS
- Prior gestational diabetes
- South Asian, Pacific Islander, Indigenous Australian ancestry (higher cardiometabolic risk at lower BMI)

## Assessment Approach

**Annual screening in high-risk patients:**
- Fasting lipid profile: total cholesterol, HDL, LDL, triglycerides
- Fasting blood glucose: ≥5.6 mmol/L warrants HbA1c or oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT)
- HbA1c: identifies prediabetes (5.7–6.4%) and diabetes (≥6.5%)
- Blood pressure: at every GP visit in at-risk patients
- Waist circumference: annual in all chronic disease patients
- ALT/GGT: elevated in NAFLD; liver screen if suspected
- eGFR and urine ACR: renal risk assessment

**Absolute cardiovascular risk:**
- Calculate 5-year absolute CVD risk using the Australian cardiovascular risk calculator (cvdcheck.org.au) in all patients with metabolic syndrome ≥45 years (or ≥35 years for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples)
- Even low-risk individual risk factors cluster to create substantially higher combined CVD risk

**Prediabetes assessment:**
- HbA1c 5.7–6.4% (39–47 mmol/mol) = prediabetes; risk of progression to T2DM is ~5–10%/year without intervention
- OGTT if HbA1c borderline or other risk factors present

## Key Differentials

- Cushing's syndrome: central obesity, hypertension, glucose intolerance; moon face, purple striae, proximal myopathy; 24-hour urine cortisol or midnight salivary cortisol; endocrinology referral
- Hypothyroidism: dyslipidaemia, weight gain, fatigue; TSH; treat to resolve lipid effects before labelling metabolic syndrome
- Drug-induced: atypical antipsychotics (olanzapine, clozapine, quetiapine) cause significant metabolic syndrome features; corticosteroids; monitor routinely in these patients
- Primary hyperaldosteronism: hypertension with hypokalaemia; renal/endocrine referral; cause of resistant hypertension
- PCOS: hyperandrogenism + oligoovulation + polycystic ovaries; metabolic syndrome common co-occurrence not a differential

## Management Principles

**Lifestyle modification — first-line and foundation of all treatment:**

Weight reduction:
- 5–10% weight loss significantly reduces all metabolic syndrome components
- Caloric deficit 500–1000 kcal/day; Mediterranean diet; reduced refined carbohydrates and saturated fat
- Structured physical activity: 150–300 min/week moderate intensity; resistance training 2 days/week
- Behavioural support: Healthy Weight program (MyHealthyWeightPlan); dietitian referral; psychologist for behavioural change

Dietary advice:
- Mediterranean or DASH dietary pattern — reduce refined carbohydrates, increase vegetables, legumes, wholegrains, olive oil
- Reduce alcohol: alcohol raises triglycerides markedly; aim <10 drinks/week (NHMRC 2020)
- Reduce sodium: <5 g/day for blood pressure

**Component-specific pharmacotherapy:**

Hypertension (≥130/85 or ≥140/90 in metabolic syndrome):
- Treat per hypertension guidelines; ACE inhibitor or ARB preferred (renoprotective and metabolically neutral)
- Thiazides and beta-blockers have slight adverse metabolic effects; second-line in metabolic syndrome without compelling indications

Dyslipidaemia:
- Statin if absolute CVD risk ≥10% or LDL >2.6 mmol/L with moderate CVD risk
- Treat elevated triglycerides: dietary change first; fenofibrate if TG >5.6 mmol/L (pancreatitis risk); omega-3 fatty acids adjunct
- Treat low HDL: lifestyle first; no PBS-listed specific therapy for isolated low HDL

Impaired fasting glucose / prediabetes:
- Lifestyle intervention is the primary treatment — reduces T2DM progression by 58% (DPP study)
- Metformin: consider in high-risk prediabetes (BMI ≥35, HbA1c ≥6.2%, previous GDM); off-label in prediabetes but commonly used; PBS-funded only once T2DM diagnosed
- Refer to Diabetes Australia Living Well program; National Diabetes Services Scheme (NDSS) registration for support

**GLP-1 receptor agonists in metabolic syndrome with obesity:**
- Semaglutide (Ozempic SC — PBS-listed for T2DM; Wegovy — PBS-listed 2024 for obesity with BMI ≥35 or ≥30 with comorbidity)
- Provide weight loss, triglyceride reduction, blood pressure reduction; highly effective in metabolic syndrome
- Refer to obesity medicine or endocrinology if multiple components not responding to lifestyle

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Metformin** (PBS restricted: type 2 diabetes diagnosis required; 500–2000 mg daily; monitor eGFR; not PBS-funded for prediabetes alone)
- **Rosuvastatin / Atorvastatin** (PBS restricted: dyslipidaemia with CVD risk; authority for familial hypercholesterolaemia)
- **Fenofibrate** (PBS restricted: hypertriglyceridaemia or mixed dyslipidaemia; 145 mg daily; avoid if eGFR <30; monitor LFTs)
- **Candesartan / Perindopril** (PBS unrestricted: hypertension; ACE inhibitor or ARB preferred in metabolic syndrome)
- **Semaglutide** (Wegovy — PBS restricted from 2024: BMI ≥35 kg/m², or ≥30 with comorbidity; specialist initiation pathway)
- **Note:** Lifestyle intervention remains primary; pharmacotherapy targets individual components; integrate with absolute CVD risk management

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Hypertensive emergency (BP >180/120 with end-organ damage: chest pain, confusion, visual change, AKI)
- ACS or stroke presenting in patient with known metabolic syndrome — emergency
- Severe hypertriglyceridaemia-associated pancreatitis (TG >10 mmol/L with abdominal pain)

**Urgent specialist review:**
- Suspected Cushing's syndrome — endocrinology
- Suspected primary aldosteronism — endocrinology
- Rapidly progressive NAFLD (rising ALT, suspected cirrhosis) — gastroenterology
- T2DM newly diagnosed — refer to Diabetes Australia NDSS; consider endocrinology if poorly controlled

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Dietitian: weight management, Mediterranean diet coaching
- Exercise physiologist: structured physical activity program for complex patients (MBS-funded in chronic disease management plan)
- Psychologist: behavioural change, depression/anxiety comorbidity (MBS Mental Health Care Plan)
- Endocrinology: prediabetes with multiple metabolic risk factors not responding to lifestyle; GLP-1 agonist initiation

## Telehealth Suitability

Metabolic syndrome monitoring is highly suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Risk factor review and lifestyle counselling
- Pathology result interpretation (lipids, HbA1c, LFTs, eGFR)
- Medication review and adjustment
- Absolute CVD risk calculation and discussion
- Prediabetes management and lifestyle counselling
- Referral coordination (dietitian, exercise physiologist)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Waist circumference measurement
- Blood pressure measurement (home BP monitoring can supplement)
- Blood tests — pathology arranged
- Physical examination if Cushing's, liver disease suspected
