## Obesity and Weight Management Assessment (RACGP)

Obesity is a chronic, complex, relapsing condition defined by excess body fat that impairs health. In Australia, approximately two-thirds of adults are overweight or obese (BMI ≥25 kg/m²), with approximately 28% classified as obese (BMI ≥30 kg/m²). Obesity is a major risk factor for type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, sleep apnoea, osteoarthritis, certain cancers, and mental health conditions. Management requires a non-judgemental, patient-centred, chronic disease approach addressing lifestyle, psychological, pharmacological, and surgical factors. The GP's role includes ongoing monitoring, motivational interviewing, and coordination of multidisciplinary care. Telehealth is well-suited for obesity management reviews, behaviour change support, and medication monitoring.

## Clinical Presentation

**Assessment parameters:**
- BMI (kg/m²): Overweight 25.0–29.9; Class I obesity 30–34.9; Class II 35–39.9; Class III (severe) ≥40
- Note: BMI underestimates adiposity in some Asian-Australian populations (overweight threshold ≥23, obese ≥27.5)
- Waist circumference: elevated risk >94 cm (men), >80 cm (women); high risk >102 cm (men), >88 cm (women)

**Obesity-related comorbidities to assess:**
- Metabolic: type 2 diabetes, prediabetes, dyslipidaemia, hypertension, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD/MASLD)
- Cardiovascular: coronary artery disease, heart failure, stroke risk
- Respiratory: obstructive sleep apnoea (OSA), obesity hypoventilation syndrome, worsening asthma
- Musculoskeletal: osteoarthritis (knee, hip), gout
- Mental health: depression, anxiety, binge eating disorder, low self-esteem, weight stigma
- Reproductive: polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS), subfertility, gestational diabetes, pregnancy complications
- Oncology: endometrial, colorectal, post-menopausal breast, kidney, oesophageal cancers

**Patient history:** Weight trajectory, previous weight loss attempts, dietary patterns, physical activity level, sleep quality, medication history (weight-gain-inducing drugs: antipsychotics, corticosteroids, insulin, sulfonylureas, some antidepressants), mental health history, family history of obesity, binge eating or emotional eating patterns.

## Assessment Approach

**Routine measurements:** Weight, height, BMI, waist circumference at every relevant consultation.

**Investigations:**
- Fasting glucose or HbA1c — screen for type 2 diabetes and prediabetes
- Fasting lipid panel — dyslipidaemia risk
- LFTs — NAFLD/MASLD (elevated ALT; hepatic steatosis on ultrasound if clinically indicated)
- TSH — exclude hypothyroidism
- Fasting insulin and HOMA-IR — insulin resistance assessment (not routine but useful in PCOS, prediabetes)
- Urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio — renal risk in comorbid hypertension/diabetes
- Overnight oximetry or sleep study — OSA screening (STOP-BANG questionnaire as screener)

**Psychosocial assessment:**
- Readiness to change — use motivational interviewing principles; assess motivation, confidence, barriers
- Binge eating disorder screen — Binge Eating Scale or BEDS-7
- PHQ-9 for depression screening
- Weight history and previous treatment attempts — what worked, what didn't

**Obesity medicine frameworks:** 5As approach (Ask, Assess, Advise, Agree, Assist) — non-judgemental structured conversation; acknowledge weight stigma and its harmful health effects.

## Key Differentials

- Secondary causes of obesity — hypothyroidism, Cushing's syndrome (central obesity, easy bruising, striae), polycystic ovarian syndrome, hypothalamic damage (post-trauma, craniopharyngioma)
- Medication-induced weight gain — antipsychotics (olanzapine, quetiapine), insulin, corticosteroids, SSRIs (paroxetine), gabapentinoids; medication review essential
- Oedema masking weight — cardiac failure, lymphoedema, venous insufficiency; check
- Binge eating disorder — psychiatric diagnosis requiring specialist management; not simply "lack of willpower"

## Management Principles

**Behaviour change and lifestyle (core of all treatment):**
- Dietary: energy deficit of 2,500–4,200 kJ/day produces ~0.5–1 kg/week loss; no single best diet — adherence is key; Mediterranean, low-carbohydrate, very-low-energy diets all effective; individualise
- Physical activity: 150–300 min/week moderate-intensity; resistance training preserves muscle mass; exercise alone produces modest weight loss (~2–3 kg) but critical for maintenance
- Behavioural strategies: self-monitoring (food diary, app tracking), goal-setting, problem-solving, cognitive restructuring; structured programs (e.g., CSIRO Total Wellbeing Diet, Jenny Craig, specialist dietitian) increase success
- Dietitian referral: core to all obesity management — refer routinely
- Psychologist: for binge eating disorder, emotional eating, low motivation, weight-related depression

**Pharmacotherapy (adjunct to lifestyle, not replacement):**

Orlistat (Xenical):
- Lipase inhibitor — reduces fat absorption by ~30%; gastrointestinal side effects (oily stools, urgency) common; 2–3 kg additional weight loss vs placebo; PBS-listed under authority for BMI ≥30 or ≥27 with comorbidity

GLP-1 receptor agonists:
- Semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy): originally diabetes drug; Wegovy 2.4 mg weekly SC PBS-listed (restricted: obesity) from 2024 for BMI ≥30 or ≥27 with weight-related comorbidity; 15–17% body weight loss at 68 weeks; also significant cardiometabolic benefits; GI side effects (nausea, vomiting); requires dietary co-management
- Liraglutide (Saxenda): 3 mg daily SC; older GLP-1 RA; ~5–8% weight loss; PBS restricted; less convenient than weekly semaglutide

Naltrexone-bupropion (Contrave):
- 32/360 mg per day; reduces appetite and cravings; ~4–6% additional weight loss; not PBS-listed (private prescription); contraindicated in seizure disorders, opioid use

**Bariatric/metabolic surgery (for eligible patients — specialist referral):**
- Indicated: BMI ≥40 kg/m², or BMI ≥35 kg/m² with significant comorbidity (T2D, OSA, hypertension), failing conservative management
- Most effective long-term intervention: sleeve gastrectomy and Roux-en-Y gastric bypass produce 20–35% weight loss
- Benefits: T2D remission (up to 80%), cardiovascular risk reduction, OSA resolution
- GP role: referral, pre-operative assessment, long-term post-operative monitoring (nutritional deficiencies — iron, B12, calcium, fat-soluble vitamins)

**Weight maintenance:** Most important and most difficult — structured follow-up, ongoing support, pharmacotherapy maintenance all improve long-term outcomes. 5–10% weight loss sustained produces significant health benefits.

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Orlistat** (Xenical — PBS restricted: obesity management; authority prescription required; BMI ≥30 or ≥27 with comorbidity; 120 mg with each main meal)
- **Semaglutide** (Wegovy — PBS restricted: chronic weight management in adults; BMI ≥30 or ≥27 with weight-related comorbidity; 0.25 mg weekly SC escalating to 2.4 mg weekly over 16 weeks)
- **Liraglutide** (Saxenda — PBS restricted: obesity; 3 mg daily SC; escalate over 5 weeks)
- **Naltrexone-bupropion** (Contrave — not PBS-listed; private prescription; 8/90 mg twice daily escalating to 32/360 mg/day; not for patients on opioids)
- **Note:** GLP-1 receptor agonists have significant GI side effects — nausea management essential; contraindicated in personal/family history of medullary thyroid cancer or MEN2; semaglutide (Ozempic) supply constraints have affected access — PBS listing for Wegovy is specific

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent assessment:**
- Obesity hypoventilation syndrome: daytime hypercapnia, severe hypoxia, polycythaemia — urgent respiratory/sleep physician referral
- Severe OSA with cardiovascular complications — cardiology and sleep physician
- Binge eating disorder with severe psychological distress or malnutrition — eating disorder specialist

**Non-urgent specialist referral:**
- Bariatric surgeon: BMI ≥40 (or ≥35 with comorbidity) — surgical assessment
- Endocrinologist: suspected secondary obesity cause (Cushing's, hypothalamic obesity), complex diabetes-obesity management
- Dietitian: all patients — routine referral for structured dietary assessment
- Psychologist: binge eating disorder, significant psychological barriers, emotional eating
- Sleep physician: suspected OSA — overnight sleep study (polysomnography)
- Exercise physiologist: structured supervised exercise prescription

## Telehealth Suitability

Obesity management is well suited to telehealth for ongoing support and monitoring.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Dietary and lifestyle behaviour change counselling and review
- Medication review and prescribing (semaglutide, orlistat) — patient-reported weight and BP
- Motivational interviewing and goal-setting
- Psychoeducation and referral coordination (dietitian, psychologist)
- Comorbidity monitoring (diabetes, hypertension) — blood results reviewed remotely

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial BMI, waist circumference, blood pressure measurement
- Baseline investigations (bloods, ECG if cardiac comorbidity)
- Pre-operative bariatric assessment
- Clinical assessment if OSA, cardiac, or severe comorbidity suspected
