## Chronic Pain Assessment (RACGP)

Chronic pain (persistent pain) is defined as pain lasting >3 months, extending beyond normal tissue healing time. It affects approximately 1 in 5 Australians, is the leading cause of disability, and costs the economy an estimated $73 billion annually. Chronic pain is a complex biopsychosocial condition — it is not simply prolonged acute pain. Understanding the neurobiological mechanisms (central sensitisation, altered pain processing) and psychosocial perpetuating factors is essential for effective management. Treatment aims to improve function and quality of life, not necessarily achieve complete pain elimination. A pain management plan that depends primarily on opioids is rarely optimal and often harmful for chronic non-cancer pain. Telehealth is well-suited for chronic pain reviews, multimodal care coordination, and medication management.

## Clinical Presentation

**Common chronic pain conditions managed in Australian general practice:**
- Chronic low back pain — most prevalent; multifactorial; biopsychosocial model applies
- Chronic widespread pain / fibromyalgia — generalised musculoskeletal pain, fatigue, sleep disturbance, cognitive symptoms; central sensitisation syndrome
- Neuropathic pain — burning, shooting, electric-shock quality; hyperalgesia/allodynia; common causes: diabetic peripheral neuropathy, post-herpetic neuralgia, chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy, complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS)
- Osteoarthritis — chronic joint pain; weight-bearing affected; progressive
- Chronic pelvic pain — endometriosis, IBS, interstitial cystitis, musculoskeletal pelvic floor dysfunction
- Headache disorders — chronic migraine, tension-type headache — see separate `headache-migraine` entry
- Chronic abdominal pain — IBD, functional GI disorders — see `irritable-bowel-syndrome` entry

**Pain characterisation:**
- Duration: when did it start, trajectory
- Quality: nociceptive (aching, throbbing — tissue-related) vs neuropathic (burning, shooting, electric) vs nociplastic (widespread, not mapped to tissue/nerve damage — central sensitisation)
- Functional impact: work, sleep, relationships, activity avoidance
- Psychological: depression (PHQ-9), anxiety (GAD-7), catastrophising (Pain Catastrophizing Scale — PCS), sleep disturbance
- Social: social support, financial stress, litigation/compensation status (impacts outcomes)
- Previous treatments: medications tried, dose and duration, response, adverse effects

**Pain assessment tools:** Numeric Rating Scale 0–10 (NRS); Brief Pain Inventory (BPI) for functional impact; STarT Back Screening Tool for low back pain risk stratification; DN4 questionnaire for neuropathic pain.

## Assessment Approach

Biopsychosocial assessment — comprehensive, structured:
- Thorough pain history (as above)
- Physical examination relevant to pain source
- Psychological screening — PHQ-9, GAD-7, sleep quality, catastrophising
- Functional assessment — what can the patient do vs what are they avoiding?
- Risk stratification for persistent pain: psychosocial yellow flags (fear-avoidance beliefs, catastrophising, depression, occupational conflict, poor social support) predict chronicity
- Opioid risk assessment before initiating or continuing opioid therapy: Opioid Risk Tool (ORT); urine drug screening; review Prescription Drug Monitoring System (PDMS — mandatory in most states before prescribing S8 analgesics)

**Investigations:** Directed by presentation. Avoid over-investigating — unnecessary imaging reinforces structural pathology beliefs and nocebo effects. Only investigate if result will change management. Imaging: plain X-ray for osteoarthritis; MRI for suspected red flags in back pain (neurological compromise, cancer, infection).

**Red flag exclusion (must rule out underlying serious pathology):**
- Spinal: new neurological deficit, saddle anaesthesia, bladder/bowel dysfunction (cauda equina — emergency), fever + back pain, age >50 + new onset, unexplained weight loss, history of cancer
- General: unexplained weight loss, fever, night sweats (malignancy, infection)

## Key Differentials

- Undiagnosed inflammatory arthritis (rheumatoid, ankylosing spondylitis, psoriatic) — early morning stiffness >30 min, elevated CRP/ESR, rheumatology referral
- Malignancy — red flag signs; investigate and refer
- Infection — discitis, osteomyelitis, septic joint; fever, elevated inflammatory markers
- Fibromyalgia — widespread pain, multiple tender points, fatigue, cognitive symptoms, sleep disturbance; no structural pathology
- Central sensitisation syndrome — clinical syndrome; treatment requires pain neuroscience education and psychological approach
- Medication overuse headache — frequent analgesic use paradoxically worsening headache; see headache-migraine entry

## Management Principles

**Non-pharmacological (first-line and ongoing — evidence superior to opioids for function):**

Pain neuroscience education (PNE): explaining modern pain science (central sensitisation, pain as a protective phenomenon not just tissue damage signal) reduces catastrophising, improves outcomes; core component of all chronic pain management.

Exercise and physiotherapy:
- Graded exercise and physical activity is evidence-based for most chronic pain conditions; reduces pain and improves function
- Physiotherapy: targeted exercise, manual therapy, graded activity; essential referral
- Hydrotherapy, yoga, tai chi: evidence for fibromyalgia and chronic low back pain

Psychological therapies:
- CBT for pain (CBT-P): most evidence-based psychological intervention; addresses fear-avoidance, catastrophising, pacing, goal-setting; 8–12 sessions; Medicare-rebatable via GP mental health care plan
- Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT): evidence-based for chronic pain; values-based activity engagement despite pain
- Mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR): evidence for fibromyalgia and chronic low back pain

Multidisciplinary pain programs: coordinated physiotherapy, psychology, occupational therapy, and pain medicine — most effective for complex or disabling chronic pain; refer to hospital-based or private pain management program.

**Pharmacological:**

Neuropathic pain:
- Pregabalin (Lyrica): 75–600 mg/day (in 2–3 divided doses); first-line for diabetic peripheral neuropathy, post-herpetic neuralgia; sedation, dizziness common; PBS-restricted for neuropathic pain
- Amitriptyline (TCA): 10–75 mg nocte; excellent evidence for neuropathic pain at low doses; side effects: dry mouth, sedation, constipation; not PBS-listed specifically for pain (off-label)
- Duloxetine (SNRI): 30–120 mg daily; evidence for diabetic peripheral neuropathy and fibromyalgia; PBS-restricted for pain indications
- Gabapentin: similar to pregabalin; PBS-restricted for epilepsy; off-label for pain; misuse potential

Non-opioid analgesia:
- Paracetamol: modest evidence; recommended as first-line; maximum 4 g/day (2 g/day in liver disease, elderly); safe first-line adjunct
- NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen, celecoxib): effective for musculoskeletal and inflammatory pain; limit to shortest effective duration; GI protection (PPI), renal and cardiovascular monitoring for regular use; avoid in elderly, renal impairment, heart failure

Topical agents:
- Topical diclofenac gel: evidence for localised osteoarthritis; minimal systemic absorption; well-tolerated
- Lidocaine patches (5%): post-herpetic neuralgia; PBS-restricted; local analgesic effect

Opioids for chronic non-cancer pain — important cautions:
- Evidence for opioids in long-term chronic non-cancer pain is poor; function and quality of life not consistently improved beyond short-term
- Harms: dependence, opioid-induced hyperalgesia, immunosuppression, hypogonadism, cognitive effects, overdose risk
- If opioids considered: use lowest effective dose; regular reassessment; check PDMS; opioid treatment agreement; tapering plan from outset
- Opioid tapering: where patient is on long-term opioids without clear benefit — gradual dose reduction (5–10% per 2–4 weeks); pain neuroscience education and psychological support essential for successful taper

Fibromyalgia-specific: Amitriptyline, duloxetine, pregabalin; exercise; CBT; sleep management; avoid opioids — ineffective and harmful in fibromyalgia.

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Pregabalin** (Lyrica — PBS restricted: diabetic peripheral neuropathy, post-herpetic neuralgia, generalised anxiety disorder; 75–600 mg/day; monitor for misuse — Schedule 4 but abuse potential)
- **Duloxetine** (Cymbalta — PBS restricted: diabetic peripheral neuropathy, MDD, generalised anxiety disorder; 30–120 mg daily)
- **Amitriptyline** (PBS unrestricted — off-label for neuropathic pain and sleep; 10–75 mg nocte)
- **Gabapentin** (PBS restricted: epilepsy; off-label for neuropathic pain; use pregabalin for PBS-listed indication where available)
- **Topical diclofenac** (Voltaren Emulgel — PBS restricted for osteoarthritis)
- **Opioids** (oxycodone, morphine, fentanyl patches — PBS restricted; Schedule 8; PDMS check mandatory before prescribing in most states; opioid treatment agreement recommended; regular reassessment required)
- **Paracetamol** (PBS unrestricted — first-line adjunct; 1 g four times daily maximum; 2 g/day if liver disease or elderly)
- **Note:** PDMS (Prescription Drug Monitoring System) mandatory in all Australian states and territories for Schedule 8 prescribing; check before issuing each Schedule 8 prescription

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Suspected cauda equina syndrome — acute onset saddle anaesthesia, bilateral leg weakness, urinary/bowel dysfunction: neurosurgical emergency
- Opioid overdose — sedation, respiratory depression, pinpoint pupils: call 000; administer naloxone if available; see naloxone prescribing for patients on high-dose opioids and their carers
- Suicidal ideation with intent in chronic pain patient — high-risk group; assess at every consultation

**Urgent specialist referral:**
- New neurological signs: foot drop, myelopathy — urgent MRI and neurosurgery/neurology
- Suspected malignancy or infection — urgent investigation and oncology/ID referral
- Severe functional impairment requiring intensive multidisciplinary pain program — pain specialist

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Pain management specialist: complex chronic pain, opioid dependence, opioid tapering support, consideration of interventional procedures (nerve blocks, spinal cord stimulation)
- Psychologist: CBT-P, ACT for pain
- Physiotherapist: graded exercise program
- Occupational therapist: functional capacity assessment, workplace return-to-work planning

## Telehealth Suitability

Chronic pain management is well suited to telehealth for reviews and multimodal care coordination.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Pain assessment using validated tools (NRS, BPI — patient-completed and discussed)
- Medication review and adjustment (non-Schedule 8 agents)
- Psychological screening (PHQ-9, GAD-7) and mental health care plan coordination
- Pain neuroscience education delivery
- Referral coordination (physiotherapy, psychology, pain program)
- Opioid monitoring review — PDMS check, functional assessment, dose review discussion

**Requires in-person assessment or arrangement:**
- Physical examination (neurological, musculoskeletal)
- Red flag assessment requiring imaging
- Schedule 8 opioid prescribing — PDMS check and clinical review; in-person review recommended for initiation and regularly during ongoing therapy
- Urine drug screening for opioid monitoring
