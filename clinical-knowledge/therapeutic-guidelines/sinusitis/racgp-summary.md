## Sinusitis Assessment (RACGP)

Acute rhinosinusitis is inflammation of the nose and paranasal sinuses lasting less than 4 weeks. It is extremely common and predominantly viral in aetiology (>90% of cases). Acute bacterial sinusitis (ABRS) is less common and largely self-limiting even when bacterial. The evidence base strongly supports symptomatic management over routine antibiotic prescription — antibiotics provide only modest benefit in ABRS and are not justified for viral sinusitis. Chronic rhinosinusitis (CRS) involves symptoms persisting >12 weeks. Telehealth is well-suited to diagnosis and management of uncomplicated acute sinusitis.

## Clinical Presentation

Cardinal symptoms: facial pain or pressure (typically over maxillary, frontal, or ethmoid sinuses), nasal obstruction, nasal discharge (initially clear, may become purulent), and reduced or absent sense of smell (hyposmia/anosmia).

Viral rhinosinusitis: symptoms peak at 2–3 days, improve by day 7–10. Purulent nasal discharge alone does not indicate bacterial infection.

Bacterial sinusitis (ABRS) is suggested by:
- Persistent symptoms >10 days without improvement (the primary differentiator)
- Onset with severe symptoms (fever >38.5°C, purulent nasal discharge, facial pain) from the start
- Biphasic illness — initial improvement followed by worsening (double-sickening pattern)

Chronic rhinosinusitis (CRS): symptoms >12 weeks; with or without nasal polyps; requires investigation and specialist involvement if refractory.

Complications (rare): orbital cellulitis, intracranial extension (meningitis, brain abscess) — suspect if periorbital swelling, proptosis, severe headache, neck stiffness, or visual change.

## Assessment Approach

Clinical diagnosis — imaging is not routinely required for acute sinusitis and is not recommended in primary care.

Facial tenderness on palpation over sinus areas can be assessed during in-person examination but is not specific for bacterial sinusitis. Telehealth assessment relies on symptom history.

Assess for:
- Duration and trajectory of symptoms (improving, stable, or worsening?)
- Fever (temperature at home)
- Dental pain (upper tooth pain may indicate maxillary sinusitis; can occasionally be dental in origin)
- Asthma and allergic rhinitis (strong association with CRS and polyps)
- Previous episodes and treatment response

CT sinuses: indicated for CRS, recurrent acute sinusitis (≥4 episodes/year), suspected complication, or pre-operative planning — not for acute uncomplicated sinusitis.

## Key Differentials

- Viral URTI — nasal congestion, discharge, and sinus pressure without predominant facial pain; resolves within 7–10 days
- Allergic rhinitis — perennial or seasonal nasal symptoms; sneezing; pruritus; absent fever
- Dental abscess — localised dental pain; may cause maxillary sinus inflammation
- Migraine / tension headache — facial pain and pressure without nasal symptoms
- Nasal polyps — persistent bilateral obstruction, anosmia; associated with asthma (Samter's triad: asthma + aspirin sensitivity + polyps)

## Management Principles

**Symptomatic management — first-line for all sinusitis:**
- Saline nasal irrigation (hypertonic or isotonic) — reduces symptom severity and duration; can be performed at home; highly recommended (Neti pot, Sinucleanse, Flo Saline Plus)
- Intranasal corticosteroid (INCS) — modest benefit in ABRS; more useful for underlying allergic rhinitis or CRS; safe for self-use
- Paracetamol or ibuprofen — for facial pain and headache
- Steam inhalation — modest symptomatic relief; not routinely recommended in evidence-based guidelines but low risk
- Decongestants (oral pseudoephedrine, topical oxymetazoline): provide symptomatic relief; topical use limited to 3–5 days (risk of rhinitis medicamentosa); avoid in hypertension, cardiovascular disease

**Antibiotics — not routinely required:**
Most patients with acute sinusitis recover without antibiotics. The NPS MedicineWise and RACGP guidelines support a delayed antibiotic strategy for patients who do not improve after 10 days or who meet criteria for ABRS.

When antibiotics ARE appropriate:
- Symptoms >10 days without improvement, or clinical features of ABRS (double-sickening, high fever, severe symptoms)
- Immunocompromised patient
- Suspected complications (orbital cellulitis — immediate referral)

Antibiotic of choice for ABRS:
- Amoxicillin 500 mg three times daily for 5–7 days — first-line; narrow-spectrum; S. pneumoniae and H. influenzae coverage
- Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg twice daily — if no response to amoxicillin after 48–72 hours, or beta-lactamase-producing organisms suspected
- Doxycycline — penicillin allergy alternative

**Chronic rhinosinusitis management:** Daily INCS is cornerstone; saline irrigation; allergen avoidance; specialist referral if refractory (functional endoscopic sinus surgery — FESS).

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Amoxicillin** (PBS unrestricted) — 500 mg three times daily; first-line if antibiotics needed
- **Amoxicillin-clavulanate** (Augmentin, Clavulin — PBS restricted: upper respiratory tract infections including sinusitis; 875/125 mg twice daily)
- **Doxycycline** (PBS unrestricted) — penicillin allergy; 100 mg daily for 5–7 days
- **Fluticasone propionate / mometasone / beclomethasone nasal sprays** (PBS-listed — see allergic rhinitis)
- **Pseudoephedrine** (PBS unrestricted; Schedule 3 Pharmacist Only Medicine in Australia — available behind the counter without prescription; purchase limits apply under Pseudoephedrine Protocol)
- **Oxymetazoline nasal spray** (Drixine — OTC; topical decongestant; limit to 3–5 days)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED — suspected complications:**
- Periorbital or orbital swelling, proptosis, or restricted eye movement — orbital cellulitis (requires IV antibiotics and surgical drainage)
- Severe headache, meningism (neck stiffness, photophobia), altered consciousness — intracranial complication
- High fever with severe systemic illness not responding to antibiotics

**Urgent in-person review:**
- Visual changes with sinus symptoms
- Unilateral facial swelling or dental pain with sinusitis — exclude dental abscess
- Immunocompromised patient not responding within 48 hours

**Non-urgent referral:**
- ENT: recurrent acute sinusitis (≥4 episodes/year), CRS not responding to medical management, nasal polyps, suspected structural abnormality; consideration of FESS
- Allergy: CRS with allergic component; immunotherapy candidacy

## Telehealth Suitability

Acute uncomplicated sinusitis is highly suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Symptom history and management advice (saline, INCS, analgesia)
- Antibiotic prescribing when criteria are met (symptom duration >10 days, ABRS features)
- Delayed antibiotic strategy (prescription provided but patient instructed when to fill it)
- Chronic sinusitis review and INCS management

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Any suspected complication (periorbital swelling, vision change, meningism) — direct to ED
- Suspected dental origin of facial pain
- Nasal endoscopy for chronic sinusitis or polyps (ENT)
