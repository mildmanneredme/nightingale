## Eczema (Atopic Dermatitis) Assessment (RACGP)

Atopic dermatitis (eczema) is the most common chronic inflammatory skin condition in Australia, affecting approximately 10–15% of children and 2–5% of adults. It is part of the "atopic march" — often preceding asthma and allergic rhinitis. Eczema is characterised by episodic flares of intensely pruritic inflamed skin. Most cases are mild and managed in general practice with emollients and topical corticosteroids. Moderate-to-severe eczema requires dermatology referral for topical calcineurin inhibitors, dupilumab (a biologic), or phototherapy. Telehealth with photo assessment is suitable for eczema review and management.

## Clinical Presentation

**Clinical features:**
- Intense itch: the hallmark — "itch that rashes" not "rash that itches"; scratching leads to excoriation and lichenification
- Erythema, vesicles, oozing (acute phase); xerosis, lichenification, scaling (chronic phase)
- Distribution varies with age: infants — face, scalp, extensor surfaces; children/adults — flexural surfaces (antecubital and popliteal fossae, wrists, neck)
- Dry skin (xerosis): year-round; barrier dysfunction is central to pathophysiology
- Koebner phenomenon: eczema exacerbated by skin trauma, friction, contact irritants

**Severity classification:**
- Mild: limited area; minor disruption to sleep/activity; controlled with basic emollients ± short topical corticosteroid courses
- Moderate: frequent flares; widespread or affecting high-impact areas; significant sleep and lifestyle disruption
- Severe: extensive, constant inflammation; major sleep disruption; significant psychological and functional impact

**Trigger identification:**
- Irritants: soaps, detergents, fragrance, wool, synthetic fabrics, sweat
- Allergens: house dust mite, pet dander, pollen (seasonal flares); food allergens (mainly in young children — milk, egg, peanut, wheat)
- Infections: S. aureus colonisation common and drives flares; Herpes simplex (eczema herpeticum — emergency)
- Environmental: heat, humidity changes; swimming in chlorinated pools

## Assessment Approach

**Assessment of severity:**
- SCORAD (Scoring Atopic Dermatitis) or EASI (Eczema Area and Severity Index): formal severity tools; used in clinical trials and dermatology practice
- PO-SCORAD: patient-oriented self-assessment version; patient can complete online
- DLQI (adults) or CDLQI (children): quality of life impact

**History:**
- Age of onset, family history of atopy (asthma, rhinitis, eczema)
- Trigger identification (as above)
- Previous treatment response: topical steroids (which ones, duration, effect); emollient routine
- Sleep impact: sleep disruption from itch is a major quality-of-life burden; assess school/work performance
- Infection history: recurrent impetigo, S. aureus, eczema herpeticum history

**Examination (in-person or photo assessment):**
- Distribution and morphology: acute (erythema, vesicles, oozing) vs chronic (lichenification, scaling)
- Secondary infection: honey-coloured crusting (impetigo/S. aureus); vesicles on red base (eczema herpeticum)
- Scratch marks and excoriation
- Dry skin assessment

**Investigations (not routinely required for clinical diagnosis):**
- Skin swab: if secondary bacterial infection suspected (S. aureus — MSSA or MRSA; guide antibiotic choice)
- Allergy testing (skin prick test or specific IgE): in severe or refractory eczema; refer to allergist/dermatologist; not routine in mild-moderate eczema; food allergy testing most relevant in young children

## Key Differentials

- Contact dermatitis (allergic): well-demarcated rash matching area of exposure; patch testing identifies allergen
- Irritant contact dermatitis: exposure to irritant; hands most common; occupational; xerosis
- Psoriasis: see `psoriasis` entry; well-demarcated silver-scaled plaques; less itch; extensor distribution
- Scabies: intense itch especially at night; burrows between fingers, wrists, genitals; all household contacts affected
- Tinea: ring-shaped with central clearing; scrapings for fungal microscopy; KOH preparation
- Seborrhoeic dermatitis: scalp, nasolabial folds, eyebrows; yellow-oily scale; less pruritic

## Management Principles

**Emollients (cornerstone — all severity levels):**
- Apply liberally and frequently (at least twice daily) to maintain skin barrier; apply within minutes of bathing to lock in moisture
- Ointments (QV Intensive, Vaseline, sorbolene): best for barrier restoration; greasy but most effective; use at night
- Creams (QV Cream, Cetaphil): more convenient; twice daily
- Soap substitutes: replace soap with emollient wash for bathing (QV Wash, Cetaphil Gentle Skin Cleanser); avoid sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS)-containing soaps
- Wet wrap therapy: for moderate-severe flares — dilute topical steroid + wet cotton garments + dry layer; hospital-supervised initially; very effective

**Topical corticosteroids (TCS):**
- Apply once or twice daily to inflamed areas only (not unaffected skin)
- Potency matching to body site: face/flexures/children → mild (hydrocortisone 1%); trunk/limbs → moderate (betamethasone valerate 0.02%); thick plaques on adults → potent (mometasone furoate 0.1%); avoid very potent on face
- Fingertip unit (FTU) guide: one FTU = squeeze from fingertip to first crease; specific guidelines by body area for dosing
- Step-down approach: use TCS to achieve control; maintain with emollient alone; restart TCS for flares
- Proactive therapy: intermittent TCS on previously affected areas 2 days/week prevents flares (evidence-based in moderate-severe eczema)
- Side effects: skin thinning, striae, telangiectasia — risk with prolonged potent TCS; minimised by correct potency selection and intermittent use

**Topical calcineurin inhibitors (TCIs):**
- Tacrolimus ointment 0.03% (children), 0.1% (adults) — Protopic: PBS-restricted; steroid-sparing; for face, eyelids, genitals, skin folds; no atrophy risk; initial burning common; long-term use safe; prescribe if significant corticosteroid use on sensitive areas
- Pimecrolimus cream 1% (Elidel): PBS-restricted; less effective than tacrolimus but better tolerated for mild eczema

**Topical PDE4 inhibitor:**
- Crisaborole (Staquis): PBS-restricted; mild-moderate eczema; non-steroid; less evidence than TCS

**Oral pharmacotherapy (specialist supervised):**
- Dupilumab (Dupixent): SC biologic (IL-4/IL-13 inhibitor); highly effective for moderate-severe eczema; PBS-restricted (DLQI ≥10, failed topical therapy + phototherapy or systemic immunosuppressant); fortnightly injection; minimal side effects (conjunctivitis common); dermatologist initiates; GP can monitor
- Oral immunosuppressants: ciclosporin, azathioprine, methotrexate — short-term for severe flares; significant toxicity monitoring requirements; dermatology supervised
- Oral corticosteroids: only for very severe acute flares; short course (3–7 days); rebound worsening common; avoid long-term use

**Infection management:**
- Bacterial (S. aureus): topical antiseptics (bleach baths — diluted sodium hypochlorite) reduce S. aureus colonisation; antibiotics only for clinical infection (flucloxacillin for MSSA; cefalexin; trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole for MRSA)
- Eczema herpeticum (HSV infection in eczematous skin): multiple punched-out vesicles or erosions; systemically unwell; urgent aciclovir — oral or IV depending on severity; hospital admission if severe

**Antihistamines:**
- Sedating antihistamines (promethazine, doxylamine): short-term for sleep disruption from itch; no evidence for itch reduction in eczema (itch is not histamine-mediated); use sparingly; avoid in young children

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Hydrocortisone 1% cream** (PBS unrestricted — mild eczema; face and flexures; children)
- **Betamethasone valerate** (PBS restricted — moderate eczema; 0.02–0.05% cream/ointment)
- **Mometasone furoate** (Elocon — PBS restricted — moderate-severe eczema; once daily)
- **Tacrolimus** (Protopic — PBS restricted: moderate-to-severe atopic dermatitis; 0.03% for children <16 years; 0.1% for adults; second-line after TCS or for steroid-sensitive areas)
- **Pimecrolimus** (Elidel — PBS restricted: mild-moderate atopic dermatitis; second-line; alternative to TCS)
- **Dupilumab** (Dupixent — PBS restricted: severe atopic dermatitis meeting criteria; DLQI ≥10; failed topical therapy + other treatments; dermatologist initiation and ongoing authority)
- **Flucloxacillin** (PBS unrestricted — secondary bacterial infection with MSSA; 500 mg four times daily)
- **Aciclovir** (PBS restricted — eczema herpeticum; 400 mg five times daily for 7 days)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Eczema herpeticum (HSV): widespread punched-out vesicles or erosions on eczematous skin + systemic toxicity — IV aciclovir; hospitalisation; ophthalmology if ocular involvement

**Urgent assessment:**
- Eczema herpeticum (milder presentation): oral aciclovir; ophthalmology assessment for ocular involvement; in-person assessment required
- Infected eczema with spreading cellulitis or systemic features — antibiotics urgently; consider hospitalisation

**Non-urgent dermatology referral:**
- Moderate-severe eczema not controlled with topical therapy — dupilumab assessment, phototherapy, systemic immunosuppression
- Diagnostic uncertainty
- Allergy assessment — allergist/immunologist for food allergy testing in young children with severe eczema

## Telehealth Suitability

Eczema management is suitable for telehealth with photo assessment.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Photo-based severity assessment and distribution documentation
- Topical corticosteroid and emollient prescribing and patient education
- Trigger identification counselling
- Antibiotic prescribing for secondary infection (if photo confirms clinical infection)
- Monitoring for patients on dupilumab or systemic therapy

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Uncertain diagnosis — clinical examination, possible skin swab or biopsy
- Suspected eczema herpeticum — in-person assessment; can be emergency
- Swab collection for secondary bacterial infection
