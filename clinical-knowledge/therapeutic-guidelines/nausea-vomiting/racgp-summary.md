## Nausea and Vomiting Assessment (RACGP)

Nausea and vomiting are common symptoms with a broad differential spanning benign self-limiting conditions (acute gastroenteritis, motion sickness, medication side effects) to serious pathology (bowel obstruction, raised intracranial pressure, acute abdomen). The clinical approach prioritises identifying serious underlying causes, assessing the degree of dehydration and electrolyte disturbance, and providing targeted antiemetic therapy. Common causes in GP practice: acute gastroenteritis (viral), pregnancy (first trimester — hyperemesis gravidarum at severe end), medications (opioids, NSAIDs, antibiotics, chemotherapy), vestibular disorders (labyrinthitis, BPPV), functional dyspepsia, GORD, and post-operative nausea. Red flags include: headache with vomiting (raised ICP), abdominal pain with vomiting (acute abdomen), blood in vomitus, haemodynamic compromise, and inability to tolerate any fluid over 24 hours. Telehealth is suitable for mild presentations once serious causes are excluded.

## Clinical Presentation

**Acute gastroenteritis (most common):**
- Sudden onset nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea; crampy abdominal pain
- Low-grade fever; myalgia; lasts 24–72 hours
- Contact history or food source; consider food poisoning (rapid onset after shared meal)

**Pregnancy-related:**
- Morning sickness: nausea ± vomiting; first trimester; peaks 6–12 weeks; usually resolves by 20 weeks
- Hyperemesis gravidarum (HG): severe, persistent vomiting; ketonuria; weight loss >5%; dehydration; requires IV rehydration; hospital admission often needed
- Distinguish from appendicitis, cholecystitis — common pregnancy complications

**Vestibular causes:**
- Labyrinthitis / vestibular neuritis: sudden severe vertigo + vomiting; no hearing loss (neuritis) or with hearing loss (labyrinthitis); recent URTI
- BPPV: triggered by head position change; brief; Dix-Hallpike positive; see `dizziness-vertigo`
- Motion sickness: travel-triggered; cinnarizine or promethazine effective

**Medication-induced:**
- Opioids: common; prophylactic antiemetics reduce adherence issues
- Antibiotics (metronidazole, erythromycin): take with food; dose timing adjustment
- Digoxin toxicity: nausea + visual disturbance + bradycardia — check drug level

**Raised intracranial pressure (RED FLAG):**
- Projectile vomiting without nausea; headache worse in morning; papilloedema; altered GCS — 000

**Bowel obstruction (RED FLAG):**
- Faeculent vomiting; constipation; abdominal distension; colicky pain; absent bowel sounds — 000

**Cyclic vomiting syndrome (CVS):**
- Stereotyped episodes of severe vomiting at set intervals; adolescents and adults; cannabis use (cannabinoid hyperemesis syndrome — hot showers relieve)

## Assessment Approach

**History:**
- Timing: acute vs chronic; episodic vs continuous; relationship to meals, position, movement
- Associated symptoms: diarrhoea (gastroenteritis), headache (ICP), vertigo (vestibular), abdominal pain (acute abdomen, pancreatitis), jaundice (hepatitis, cholecystitis)
- Medications: opioids, NSAIDs, antibiotics, chemotherapy, digoxin, alcohol
- Pregnancy: LMP; serum hCG in women of reproductive age with unexplained nausea/vomiting
- Haematemesis: blood in vomitus (peptic ulcer, Mallory-Weiss, oesophageal varices)

**Examination:**
- Vital signs: tachycardia + hypotension = significant dehydration or haemorrhage
- Abdominal: guarding, rebound tenderness, distension, bowel sounds
- Neurological: headache, neck stiffness, focal signs, eye movements, papilloedema
- Skin turgor, mucous membranes (dehydration assessment)

**Investigations (targeted):**
- Serum hCG: all women of reproductive age; exclude pregnancy
- BSL: hypoglycaemia as cause or complication
- FBC, EUC: dehydration, hyponatraemia (prolonged vomiting), hypokalaemia
- LFTs, lipase: hepatitis, pancreatitis
- Urine MCS: UTI (especially in pregnancy); ketonuria in HG
- AXR / CT abdomen: if bowel obstruction suspected

## Key Differentials

- Acute gastroenteritis: contact/food history; diarrhoea; self-limiting; no red flags
- Appendicitis: RIF pain; fever; peritonism; WBC ↑; CT abdomen
- Pancreatitis: epigastric pain radiating to back; lipase ↑; alcohol/gallstones history
- Cholecystitis: RUQ pain; Murphy's sign positive; LFTs ↑; ultrasound
- Hepatitis: jaundice; LFTs ↑; contact/travel/substance history; viral serology
- Meningitis: headache + fever + photophobia + neck stiffness; LP; 000
- Diabetic ketoacidosis: diabetes; fruity breath; ketonuria; BSL ↑; ABG pH ↓
- Addisonian crisis: weakness + confusion + nausea in adrenal insufficiency; electrolytes ↓

## Management Principles

**General measures:**
- Rest; small frequent sips of clear fluid; oral rehydration solution (Gastrolyte, Hydralyte) for dehydration
- Avoid solid food until vomiting settles; then BRAT diet (banana, rice, apple, toast) — return to normal diet quickly
- Avoid anti-diarrhoeal agents in gastroenteritis with fever or blood in stool

**Antiemetic therapy:**

Mild-moderate nausea/vomiting (oral/sublingual):
- Metoclopramide 10 mg TDS PO/SL: prokinetic + antiemetic; short-term use only (max 5 days — tardive dyskinesia risk with prolonged use); most effective; PBS-listed
- Prochlorperazine 5 mg TDS oral or 12.5 mg IM/IV: phenothiazine antiemetic; effective; EPS side effects; avoid in Parkinson's disease
- Ondansetron 4–8 mg oral/wafer: 5-HT₃ antagonist; excellent for chemotherapy/post-op PONV; PBS-restricted to these indications; off-label use common for viral gastroenteritis and HG; safe in pregnancy; QT prolongation (avoid if QTc prolonged)
- Domperidone 10 mg TDS: less CNS penetration than metoclopramide; peripheral dopamine antagonist; less effective; cardiac risk with high doses; not PBS-listed

Vestibular nausea/vertigo:
- Prochlorperazine 12.5 mg IM: acute labyrinthitis; most effective
- Promethazine 25 mg oral: motion sickness and vestibular; sedating; OTC; avoid in children < 2 years
- Cinnarizine (Stugeron): motion sickness prophylaxis; antihistamine/dopamine antagonist; OTC

**Nausea in pregnancy:**
- First-line: dietary measures (small frequent meals, high-carbohydrate low-fat diet, ginger tablets 1 g/day)
- Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine) 25 mg TDS: modest evidence; safe in pregnancy; OTC
- Doxylamine 12.5–25 mg nocte (Restavit, Unisom): antihistamine; safe in pregnancy; evidence for morning sickness
- Metoclopramide or prochlorperazine: second-line if above fail; safe in pregnancy; short-term
- Ondansetron: evidence good for HG; safety data reassuring (no confirmed teratogenicity); use when above measures fail; discuss risks/benefits
- Hyperemesis gravidarum: IV Hartmann's + IV thiamine (Wernicke's prophylaxis) + IV ondansetron + parenteral nutrition; hospital admission; obstetric review

**Opioid-induced nausea:**
- Metoclopramide 10 mg TDS co-prescribed with opioid analgesic; prophylactic
- Review opioid dose; consider opioid rotation if persistent

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Metoclopramide 10 mg** (PBS unrestricted: nausea; maximum 5 days; avoid in Parkinson's, epilepsy, concurrent antipsychotics; tardive dyskinesia risk with prolonged/repeat use; CAUTION in young women — acute dystonia)
- **Prochlorperazine 5 mg (Stemetil)** (PBS restricted: nausea and vomiting, vertigo; IM preparation for severe vomiting; extrapyramidal side effects; avoid in Parkinson's)
- **Ondansetron 4/8 mg wafers (Zofran)** (PBS restricted: chemotherapy-induced nausea and post-operative nausea; NOT PBS-listed for viral gastroenteritis; commonly used off-label for acute nausea; private prescription or PBS AUST R label)
- **Promethazine 25 mg (Phenergan)** (OTC: motion sickness; marked sedation; NOT for children under 2 years — respiratory depression risk)
- **Note:** For hyperemesis gravidarum, hospital-level care with IV thiamine (to prevent Wernicke's encephalopathy from thiamine depletion) is essential before/alongside IV dextrose

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / hospital:**
- Haematemesis (blood in vomitus): peptic ulcer, variceal bleed; IV access; endoscopy
- Vomiting + headache + altered consciousness: raised ICP, meningitis, encephalitis; 000
- Bowel obstruction: faeculent vomiting, distension, peritonism; 000
- Haemodynamic compromise from dehydration: hypotension, tachycardia, altered consciousness

**Urgent hospital:**
- Hyperemesis gravidarum: ketonuria, weight loss >5%, inability to tolerate any fluid; IV rehydration + IV thiamine + obstetric review
- Diabetic ketoacidosis: vomiting + hyperglycaemia + ketonuria

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Cyclic vomiting syndrome / cannabinoid hyperemesis: gastroenterology; cannabis cessation counselling
- Persistent nausea >2 weeks without clear diagnosis: gastroenterology; upper endoscopy
- Chemotherapy-associated nausea: oncology for antiemetic protocol optimisation

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Mild acute gastroenteritis with adequate hydration; dietary advice and ORS
- Medication-induced nausea counselling and antiemetic prescribing
- Pregnancy morning sickness (mild to moderate); pyridoxine, ginger, doxylamine
- Follow-up after hospital discharge for HG
- Vestibular nausea review after initial in-person assessment

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Red flag features (haematemesis, neurological symptoms, peritonism)
- Moderate to severe dehydration assessment
- Hyperemesis gravidarum (blood tests, IV access decision)
- First-presentation vomiting without clear aetiology
