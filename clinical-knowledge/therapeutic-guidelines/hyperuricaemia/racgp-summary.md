## Hyperuricaemia Assessment (RACGP)

Hyperuricaemia is defined as serum uric acid (SUA) >0.42 mmol/L (7 mg/dL) in adults (using newer reference ranges; some labs use >0.36 mmol/L for women). It is the key modifiable risk factor for gout (see also `gout`) but also an independent cardiovascular risk factor and is associated with chronic kidney disease, hypertension, and metabolic syndrome. Most hyperuricaemia is asymptomatic — does not require urate-lowering therapy (ULT) in isolation. ULT is indicated primarily for: recurrent gout (≥2 episodes/year), gout with complications (tophi, renal calculi, chronic gouty arthropathy), or severe CKD with coexisting gout. Secondary causes of hyperuricaemia (medications — particularly thiazide diuretics, low-dose aspirin, ciclosporin; renal impairment; hypothyroidism; myeloproliferative disorders) must be identified and managed where possible. Asymptomatic hyperuricaemia in the absence of gout: treat the underlying cause and cardiovascular risk factors; ULT not routinely indicated. This entry focuses on the biochemical management and long-term ULT strategy for patients with hyperuricaemia and recurrent gout; see `gout` for acute attack management.

## Clinical Presentation

**Asymptomatic hyperuricaemia:**
- SUA elevated on incidental bloods; no history of gout or urate crystal deposition
- Very common: approximately 20% of Australian adults have SUA >0.42 mmol/L
- Most will NEVER develop gout; treat secondary causes and cardiovascular risk
- Monitor for development of gout symptoms

**Gout (urate crystal deposition disease):**
- Acute gout: sudden, severe monoarthritis; first MTP joint most common (podagra); ankle, knee, wrist
- Recurrent gout: ≥2 episodes in 12 months → ULT indicated
- Tophaceous gout: deposits of urate crystals in soft tissue; tophi on helix of ear, finger pads, over Achilles tendon, around joints; indicates chronic elevated SUA; ULT urgently indicated
- Gouty nephropathy / urate nephrolithiasis: uric acid kidney stones; recurrent renal calculi; ULT indicated
- See `gout` for acute attack management

**Secondary causes of hyperuricaemia (identify and address):**
- Medications: thiazide diuretics (hydrochlorothiazide, bendroflumethiazide), loop diuretics (furosemide), low-dose aspirin (≤150 mg/day), ciclosporin, tacrolimus, pyrazinamide (TB treatment), niacin
- Renal impairment: reduced uric acid excretion; CKD raises SUA; dose adjustment required for ULT
- Hypothyroidism: reduced renal urate excretion; TFTs
- Myeloproliferative / haematological malignancy: rapid cell turnover; tumour lysis syndrome (severe)
- Alcohol excess: increased uric acid production and reduced excretion
- High-purine diet: organ meats, shellfish, red meat, alcohol (beer especially), fructose-sweetened drinks

## Assessment Approach

**Investigations for hyperuricaemia:**
- Serum uric acid: confirm elevation on fasting sample (post-acute attack; SUA may be falsely low during acute attack due to redistribution)
- Renal function (eGFR, creatinine): CKD reduces uric acid clearance; affects ULT choice and dosing
- Urine uric acid/creatinine ratio: distinguishes over-producers (uric acid >5 mmol/day in 24-hour urine) from under-excretors (most patients); helps guide choice of ULT
- TFTs: exclude hypothyroidism
- FBC: haematological malignancy if very high SUA without obvious cause
- Lipids, BSL, HbA1c: metabolic syndrome association; CVD risk assessment

**Gout diagnosis confirmation (where uncertain):**
- Joint aspiration: identification of negatively birefringent needle-shaped monosodium urate (MSU) crystals in synovial fluid under polarised light microscopy = gold standard; NOT always required if classic clinical presentation
- Dual-energy CT (DECT): non-invasive imaging of urate deposits; increasingly available; sensitive
- Ultrasound: "double contour sign" on cartilage surface = urate deposition; tophi identification
- X-ray: punch-out lesions (late tophaceous gout); early disease normal

## Key Differentials

- Septic arthritis: acute monoarthritis + fever + systemically unwell; MUST exclude; joint aspiration for microscopy, culture, and crystals; IV antibiotics if suspected
- Pseudogout (CPPD — calcium pyrophosphate crystal deposition): similar acute monoarthritis; knee and wrist more common than first MTP; calcium pyrophosphate crystals (positively birefringent); chondrocalcinosis on X-ray; treat same as gout for acute attack
- Reactive arthritis: post-infectious; asymmetric; urethritis/cervicitis; conjunctivitis; HLA-B27
- Rheumatoid arthritis: symmetrical; small joints; morning stiffness; anti-CCP; differentiate from tophaceous gout
- Cellulitis: skin infection with swelling and redness; no joint swelling; fever

## Management Principles

**Lifestyle modification (all patients with hyperuricaemia ± gout):**
- Dietary reduction of high-purine foods: organ meats (liver, kidney, offal), seafood (anchovies, sardines, shellfish), red meat; moderate consumption
- Reduce fructose: fructose-sweetened drinks (soft drinks, fruit juice, sports drinks) increase SUA significantly; switch to water
- Limit alcohol: particularly beer (highest purine content); moderate wine consumption; no alcohol during acute attacks
- Increase low-fat dairy: protective against gout; reduces SUA
- Weight loss: reduces SUA; reduces gout frequency
- Review medications: substitute thiazide diuretics if possible; low-dose aspirin (clinical indication usually outweighs gout risk; continue with ULT)

**Urate-lowering therapy (ULT) — indications (Australian Rheumatology Association 2020):**
- Recurrent gout: ≥2 attacks in 12 months
- Tophaceous gout: any tophi on examination or imaging
- Chronic gouty arthropathy: joint damage; radiological erosions
- Uric acid nephrolithiasis: recurrent kidney stones
- First episode of gout with eGFR <60 or in transplant patients or with ≥1 tophi
- Note: asymptomatic hyperuricaemia alone is NOT an indication for ULT in most patients

**Allopurinol (first-line ULT):**
- Mechanism: xanthine oxidase inhibitor; reduces uric acid production
- Starting dose: 50–100 mg daily; titrate slowly (25–50 mg every 2–4 weeks)
- Target SUA: <0.36 mmol/L (6 mg/dL) — below saturation threshold; further to <0.30 mmol/L if tophi
- Maximum dose: 900 mg/day; titrate based on SUA; eGFR guides dosing (dose-reduce in renal impairment — eGFR <30: maximum 100 mg/day)
- CRITICAL: Start ULT only when patient is NOT in acute attack; wait 2–4 weeks after acute attack resolution; starting ULT during attack can prolong attack
- Co-prescribe prophylaxis when initiating ULT: colchicine 0.5 mg daily (or NSAID if contraindicated) for minimum 6 months — prevents ULT-precipitated gout flares
- Monitor: SUA and eGFR at 4–6 weeks after dose change; titrate until target achieved
- DRESS/SJS risk: rare but severe hypersensitivity; higher risk in: HLA-B*58:01 genotype (especially in patients of Han Chinese, Korean, Thai ancestry) — consider HLA testing before initiating in these populations; educate all patients about early rash

**Febuxostat (second-line):**
- Xanthine oxidase inhibitor; not a purine analogue; no risk of HLA-B*58:01 DRESS
- Indicated: allopurinol intolerance; inadequate response to maximum allopurinol dose
- 40–80 mg daily; PBS-restricted for gout when allopurinol contraindicated or not tolerated
- Caution: cardiovascular safety concerns (CARES trial); use with caution in established CVD; monitor
- Monitor: LFTs (mild transaminase elevation possible)

**Probenecid (uricosuric — increases renal urate excretion):**
- Alternative for under-excretors of urate who tolerate allopurinol poorly
- 500 mg BD to maximum 2 g/day; take with adequate hydration (2+ L/day) to prevent uric acid calculi
- Avoid if: eGFR <30; urolithiasis history; not PBS-listed for routine gout management
- NOT first-line in Australian practice

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Allopurinol 100–300 mg** (PBS restricted: gout prophylaxis; PBS-listed; start when NOT in acute attack; co-prescribe colchicine 0.5 mg daily prophylaxis for 6 months when initiating; titrate to SUA <0.36 mmol/L; dose-reduce in renal impairment)
- **Colchicine 0.5 mg** (PBS restricted: gout prophylaxis AND acute attack; 0.5 mg daily for 6 months when starting ULT; acute attack 1 mg then 0.5 mg 1 hour later — not the old high-dose regimen; diarrhoea at higher doses; renally cleared — dose reduce if eGFR <30)
- **Febuxostat 40–80 mg (Adenuric)** (PBS restricted: gout when allopurinol contraindicated or not tolerated; Authority required; monitor LFTs; caution in CVD)
- **Note:** The most common reason for treatment failure in gout is inadequate ULT dosing. Many patients receive allopurinol 300 mg without ever titrating to achieve target SUA. Titrate until SUA <0.36 mmol/L regardless of allopurinol dose required (within renal tolerance). Document SUA target at every review.

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent:**
- Septic arthritis must be excluded: acute monoarthritis + fever + systemically unwell → joint aspiration + blood cultures → hospital or urgent rheumatology
- Tumour lysis syndrome (acute haematological malignancy + very high SUA): hospital; IV hydration; IV allopurinol or rasburicase

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Rheumatology: refractory gout not achieving SUA target with allopurinol + febuxostat; tophaceous gout with joint destruction; diagnostic uncertainty; surgical debridement of large tophi
- Nephrology: CKD + gout + hyperuricaemia; ULT dose guidance; proteinuria + urate nephropathy

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- ULT initiation and dose titration review
- SUA target monitoring and allopurinol dose adjustment
- Lifestyle counselling and medication review
- Management of recurrent gout attacks while on ULT (review compliance, SUA level)
- Prophylactic colchicine management

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Acute joint attack (must exclude septic arthritis — joint aspiration if uncertain)
- Tophi assessment and measurement
- Initial cardiovascular and metabolic risk assessment
