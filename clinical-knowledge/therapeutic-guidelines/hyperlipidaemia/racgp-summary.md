## Hyperlipidaemia / Dyslipidaemia Assessment (RACGP)

Dyslipidaemia — abnormalities of lipid levels including elevated LDL-cholesterol, reduced HDL-cholesterol, elevated triglycerides, and elevated total cholesterol — is a major modifiable cardiovascular risk factor. Approximately 35% of Australian adults have dyslipidaemia. Management decisions are based on the patient's absolute cardiovascular disease (CVD) risk rather than lipid levels alone. Australia uses the Australian absolute CVD risk calculator incorporating age, sex, blood pressure, smoking status, lipids, and diabetes status. Telehealth is highly suitable for lipid management including results review, risk discussion, and prescribing.

## Clinical Presentation

Most dyslipidaemia is asymptomatic and detected on routine screening. Clinically apparent signs occur only in severe or familial hyperlipidaemia:
- Xanthelasma (around eyes), xanthomas (tendons, skin)  — familial hypercholesterolaemia
- Corneal arcus before age 45 — suggests familial hypercholesterolaemia
- Lipaemia retinalis — very high triglycerides (>10 mmol/L)
- Eruptive xanthomas, pancreatitis — severe hypertriglyceridaemia (>10 mmol/L)

**Familial hypercholesterolaemia (FH):** Autosomal dominant disorder — LDL-C ≥4.9 mmol/L without secondary cause, family history of premature CVD or FH; Dutch Lipid Clinic Network Score used for diagnosis; requires specialist referral and cascade family screening.

**Lipid panel interpretation:**
- Total cholesterol (TC): ideally <4.5 mmol/L in high CVD risk
- LDL-cholesterol (LDL-C): primary treatment target; <2.5 mmol/L (high risk); <1.8 mmol/L (very high risk — established CVD, diabetes with target organ damage)
- HDL-cholesterol: low HDL (<1.0 mmol/L men, <1.3 mmol/L women) increases CVD risk
- Triglycerides (TG): elevated (>2.0 mmol/L) — insulin resistance marker; >10 mmol/L risks pancreatitis
- Non-HDL cholesterol: total cholesterol minus HDL; increasingly used as treatment target

## Assessment Approach

**Absolute CVD risk assessment (Australian guidelines — 5-year risk):**
- High risk (≥15%): pharmacotherapy recommended regardless of individual lipid levels
- Intermediate risk (10–15%): treat if additional risk factors; lifestyle first; pharmacotherapy often appropriate
- Low risk (<10%): lifestyle modification; pharmacotherapy if LDL-C persistently very elevated

**Automatic high risk (no calculator required):**
- Established atherosclerotic CVD (MI, stroke, PCI/CABG, PAD)
- Diabetes + ≥40 years, or ≥20 years with diabetes, or with microalbuminuria/retinopathy
- Familial hypercholesterolaemia
- Severe CKD (eGFR <45)

**Investigation:**
- Fasting lipid panel (TC, LDL-C, HDL-C, TG, non-HDL-C): for diagnosis and monitoring
- Non-fasting sample: acceptable for initial screening (TG may be elevated)
- Secondary causes to exclude: TSH (hypothyroidism), fasting glucose/HbA1c (diabetes), LFTs (liver disease — obstructive cause), UEC (CKD), urine albumin (nephrotic syndrome — causes severe hyperlipidaemia)

## Key Differentials

- Secondary hyperlipidaemia — hypothyroidism (elevated TC, TG); nephrotic syndrome (severe hyperlipidaemia); obstructive liver disease; type 2 diabetes (elevated TG, low HDL); medications (corticosteroids, thiazides, beta-blockers, retinoids, antipsychotics raise TG or TC)
- Familial hypercholesterolaemia — markedly elevated LDL-C (>4.9 mmol/L) + family history; genetic testing and specialist referral
- Pancreatitis from hypertriglyceridaemia — triglycerides >10 mmol/L; dietary and fibrate therapy; acute pancreatitis requires hospital management

## Management Principles

**Lifestyle (first-line for all):**
- Reduce saturated fats (red meat, full-fat dairy, processed foods); replace with unsaturated fats (olive oil, fish, nuts)
- Mediterranean diet: evidence for CVD risk reduction; reduces LDL-C 5–15%
- Increase dietary fibre (soluble fibre — oats, legumes, psyllium) — reduces LDL-C ~5–10%
- Regular aerobic exercise — improves HDL-C; reduces TG; modest LDL-C lowering
- Weight loss — reduces TG, improves HDL-C, modest LDL-C reduction
- Smoking cessation — improves HDL-C; reduces CVD risk
- Alcohol reduction — lowers TG (alcohol increases TG substantially)
- Plant sterols (2 g/day via fortified foods or supplements) — reduces LDL-C ~10%

**Pharmacotherapy:**

Statins (HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors — first-line):
- Rosuvastatin 5–40 mg daily: most potent statin (LDL-C reduction 38–55%); once daily, any time; preferred for high/very high risk; PBS-restricted for primary and secondary prevention
- Atorvastatin 10–80 mg daily: high-intensity statin; excellent evidence base; LDL-C reduction 30–51%
- Simvastatin 10–40 mg nocte: older; less potent; drug interactions (avoid with amiodarone >20 mg; macrolides — myopathy risk)
- Side effects: myalgia (5–10% — mostly mild); check CK if symptomatic; rhabdomyolysis rare; LFT elevation rare; statin intolerance — switch statin or reduce dose; check CoQ10 deficiency (no strong evidence but widely used by patients)

Ezetimibe:
- 10 mg daily; reduces LDL-C additional 18–20% when added to statin; well tolerated; second-line or combination; PBS-restricted

PCSK9 inhibitors (specialist-initiated in Australia):
- Evolocumab (Repatha), alirocumab (Praluent): subcutaneous injection fortnightly or monthly; LDL-C reduction >50%; for very high risk not at target on maximal statin + ezetimibe; PBS-restricted (requires specialist authority — endocrinologist, cardiologist, lipidologist)

Fibrates (for elevated triglycerides):
- Fenofibrate 145 mg daily: reduces TG 20–50%; modest HDL-C increase; little effect on LDL-C; PBS-restricted for severe hypertriglyceridaemia; renal dose adjustment required

Fish oil (omega-3):
- High-dose prescription omega-3 fatty acids: reduces TG; modest CV benefit data; useful adjunct for severe hypertriglyceridaemia

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Rosuvastatin** (Crestor, generic — PBS restricted: primary hypercholesterolaemia, mixed dyslipidaemia, high CVD risk; 5–40 mg daily)
- **Atorvastatin** (Lipitor, generic — PBS restricted: as per rosuvastatin; 10–80 mg daily)
- **Simvastatin** (PBS restricted: 10–40 mg nocte; significant drug interaction risk)
- **Ezetimibe** (Ezetrol — PBS restricted: combined with statin when LDL-C not at target; 10 mg daily)
- **Fenofibrate** (PBS restricted: severe hypertriglyceridaemia or combined dyslipidaemia; 145 mg daily)
- **Evolocumab/alirocumab** (PBS restricted: very high CVD risk not at LDL-C target on maximal therapy; specialist authority required)
- **Note:** Statin monitoring — baseline LFTs; repeat if symptomatic; CK only if myalgia; annual lipid panel to confirm target achieved

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent assessment:**
- Severe hypertriglyceridaemia (TG >10 mmol/L) with abdominal pain — pancreatitis risk; hospital assessment
- Rhabdomyolysis from statin: severe muscle pain, dark urine, elevated CK >10× ULN — cease statin; IV fluids; hospital

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Lipidologist / cardiologist / endocrinologist: FH, LDL-C persistently >3.5 mmol/L despite maximal therapy, PCSK9 inhibitor consideration, complex dyslipidaemia, statin intolerance requiring alternatives
- Dietitian: all patients with dyslipidaemia — structured dietary assessment and Mediterranean diet advice

## Telehealth Suitability

Dyslipidaemia management is highly suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Absolute CVD risk assessment (patient provides demographic and clinical data)
- Lipid result review and treatment decision
- Statin initiation and dose titration
- Lifestyle counselling
- Statin side effect review and alternative prescribing
- Monitoring review (LFTs, CK, annual lipid panel)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Blood tests — pathology referral for lipid panel, LFTs, CK
- Blood pressure measurement (required for absolute CVD risk calculation)
- Physical examination for xanthelasma, xanthomas, corneal arcus (FH)
