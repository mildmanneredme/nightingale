## Epilepsy Assessment — GP Monitoring (RACGP)

Epilepsy is a chronic brain disorder characterised by recurrent unprovoked seizures. It affects approximately 250,000 Australians. Most epilepsy is managed by neurologists (initiation and complex management) with the GP providing ongoing monitoring, medication review, lifestyle counselling, and coordination of comorbidity management. GPs must be familiar with: first-aid for seizures, recognition of status epilepticus, antiepileptic drug (AED) monitoring, and critically important issues around driving, pregnancy, and contraception. Telehealth is suitable for routine epilepsy monitoring.

## Clinical Presentation

**Seizure classification (ILAE 2017):**

Focal onset: originating in one hemisphere; may be aware (no impaired consciousness) or with impaired awareness; may evolve to bilateral tonic-clonic (formerly called "secondarily generalised")

Generalised onset: simultaneously affecting both hemispheres; subtypes:
- Tonic-clonic (grand mal): loss of consciousness, tonic stiffening, rhythmic clonic jerking; post-ictal confusion (helps distinguish from syncope)
- Absence (petit mal): brief staring episodes; sudden onset/offset; no post-ictal phase; most common in children
- Myoclonic: brief, sudden muscle jerks; often in the morning
- Atonic ("drop attacks"): sudden loss of muscle tone; falls; injury risk

**Post-ictal phase:** Confusion, headache, fatigue after generalised seizures; duration 15 minutes to hours; distinguishes epileptic from syncopal LOC.

**Status epilepticus:** Continuous seizure or repeated seizures without recovery >5 minutes; medical emergency; 000 immediately.

**Triggers in established epilepsy:** Sleep deprivation, alcohol, missed AED doses, fever, specific medications (tramadol, fluoroquinolones, antipsychotics lower seizure threshold), hormonal changes (catamenial epilepsy — seizure increase perimenstrually).

## Assessment Approach

**GP role in established epilepsy:**
- Seizure frequency monitoring: diary; any breakthrough seizures; triggers identified
- Medication adherence and side effect review
- AED blood level monitoring (selected AEDs — see below)
- Drug interactions: AEDs have many significant interactions; check at every prescription change
- Comorbidities: depression (40% of people with epilepsy), anxiety, cognitive effects of AEDs, bone health (enzyme-inducing AEDs reduce vitamin D)
- Vaccination: annual influenza, COVID-19; pneumococcal — fever can lower seizure threshold
- Lifestyle: sleep hygiene, alcohol, swimming safety

**AED monitoring:**
- Phenytoin: narrow therapeutic index; level monitoring required; aim 10–20 mg/L; zero-order kinetics — small dose changes can cause toxicity
- Phenobarbitone: level monitoring useful; aim 10–40 mg/L
- Lamotrigine, levetiracetam, valproate: levels less routinely required but can guide in suspected toxicity or non-adherence; valproate hepatotoxicity monitoring (LFTs periodically)
- Carbamazepine: levels useful; 4–12 mg/L; also FBC (aplastic anaemia rare)
- Oxcarbazepine: hyponatraemia monitoring — Na+ at initiation and periodically

**Important blood tests in epilepsy monitoring:**
- LFTs: valproate hepatotoxicity; carbamazepine
- FBC: carbamazepine (aplastic anaemia, agranulocytosis — rare)
- Electrolytes (Na+): oxcarbazepine
- Bone density (DEXA): long-term enzyme-inducing AEDs (phenytoin, phenobarbitone, carbamazepine) reduce vitamin D and increase fracture risk; vitamin D supplementation; DEXA at 5 years of treatment
- Pregnancy: teratogenicity and dose management — see below

**Driving regulations — critical GP obligation:**
- NSW: seizure-free for 6 months (private vehicle) or 12 months (heavy vehicle) — required before driving
- VIC, QLD, SA, WA, TAS, NT, ACT: varies by state; generally similar minimum periods; commercial licensing stricter
- AUSTROADS Standards — Medical Standards for Licensing, 5th edition: authoritative reference
- GP must document advice given to patient not to drive; MO has duty to notify licensing authority if patient refuses to comply and continues driving with condition that poses risk (state-specific obligations)
- Breakthrough seizure: patient must cease driving and notify licensing authority; re-assess period restarts

**Contraception and epilepsy:**
- Enzyme-inducing AEDs (carbamazepine, phenytoin, phenobarbitone, oxcarbazepine, eslicarbazepine, perampanel) significantly reduce effectiveness of hormonal contraception (OCP, patch, ring, implant — Implanon); progestogen-only pill also affected
- Use higher dose OCP (50 mcg ethinylestradiol) or alternative non-hormonal or depot medroxyprogesterone (Depo-Provera) with enzyme-inducing AEDs
- Non-enzyme-inducing AEDs (lamotrigine, levetiracetam, valproate, topiramate at low dose, benzodiazepines): do not affect hormonal contraception efficacy
- OCP reduces lamotrigine levels by ~50% — may need dose increase during OCP use

**Pregnancy and epilepsy (specialist management — GP awareness):**
- All AEDs carry some teratogenic risk; valproate highest risk (10–15% major malformations, neurodevelopmental delay — AVOID in women of childbearing potential if alternatives exist; REMS program)
- Folate 5 mg daily: all women with epilepsy planning pregnancy; ideally started ≥3 months preconception
- Lamotrigine, levetiracetam: preferred AEDs in pregnancy (lower malformation rates)
- Seizure control in pregnancy is critical — risk of maternal and fetal injury from uncontrolled seizures outweighs most AED risks
- Refer to neurologist and maternal-fetal medicine for preconception counselling and pregnancy management

## Key Differentials

- Syncope vs epilepsy: syncope — brief LOC, no post-ictal phase, triggered by standing/emotional stress; epilepsy — post-ictal confusion, no positional trigger; eyewitness accounts crucial
- Psychogenic non-epileptic seizures (PNES): epilepsy mimic; normal EEG; often psychiatric history; video EEG monitoring in hospital differentiates; psychological treatment
- Transient ischaemic attack: focal neurological symptoms but not convulsive; different age group; imaging
- Metabolic causes of seizure: hypoglycaemia (diabetic), hyponatraemia, hypocalcaemia, uraemia; check bloods; provoked seizure
- Alcohol withdrawal seizure: 6–48 hours after last drink; provoked seizure; does not require AED long-term

## Management Principles

**Long-term AED therapy (neurologist-initiated and monitored; GP co-management):**

Common AEDs in Australian practice:
- Levetiracetam (Keppra): broad-spectrum; well-tolerated; minimal drug interactions; irritability/mood side effects; no blood level monitoring required; preferred in women of reproductive age (lower teratogenicity); PBS-listed
- Lamotrigine: broad-spectrum; well-tolerated; slow titration required (to prevent SJS risk); monitoring in pregnancy (OCP reduces levels); skin rash important side effect; PBS-listed
- Valproate (Epilim): highly effective; avoid in women of childbearing potential; weight gain; tremor; hepatotoxicity monitoring; PBS-listed
- Carbamazepine: focal epilepsy; enzyme inducer; interactions; HLA-B*1502 testing before initiation in patients of Han Chinese, Thai, or South Asian ancestry (Stevens-Johnson syndrome risk)
- Phenytoin: older agent; zero-order kinetics; drug interactions; gingival hyperplasia, hirsutism; narrow therapeutic index
- Topiramate: cognitive side effects ("dopeiramate"); kidney stones; weight loss; contraception interaction at higher doses

**Seizure first aid (patient and family education):**
- Protect from injury (clear surroundings, cushion head)
- Place in recovery position after tonic-clonic phase
- Do NOT restrain; do NOT put anything in mouth
- Administer rescue medication if prescribed (midazolam buccal or intranasal — for patients with known prolonged seizures)
- Call 000 if: seizure >5 minutes (status epilepticus), no recovery between seizures, first seizure ever, injury occurred, pregnant

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Levetiracetam** (Keppra, generic — PBS restricted: epilepsy; authority required; 500 mg–3000 mg/day in divided doses)
- **Lamotrigine** (PBS restricted: epilepsy; authority required; titrate slowly — 25 mg daily increasing by 25 mg every 2 weeks to target dose; may need dose increase during OCP use)
- **Valproate** (Epilim — PBS restricted: epilepsy; avoid in women of childbearing potential; LFT monitoring; 500–2000 mg/day)
- **Carbamazepine** (PBS restricted: epilepsy; enzyme inducer; FBC monitoring; HLA-B*1502 testing in Asian populations)
- **Phenytoin** (PBS restricted: epilepsy; narrow therapeutic index; level monitoring required)
- **Midazolam buccal** (Midazolam Intensol, or intranasal — for rescue treatment of prolonged seizures; PBS-restricted; patient/carer education essential)
- **Folate 5 mg** (PBS restricted: epilepsy in women of childbearing potential; periconceptional and throughout pregnancy)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Status epilepticus: seizure >5 minutes or repeated seizures without recovery — IV benzodiazepines required; fosphenytoin or levetiracetam second-line in hospital
- First-ever seizure: all patients require urgent assessment to exclude structural cause (brain tumour, bleed, encephalitis, metabolic) and initiate investigation; do not drive until assessed

**Urgent neurology assessment:**
- Breakthrough seizure after ≥12 months seizure-free (especially in licensed driver)
- AED toxicity (ataxia, diplopia, confusion)
- SJS/TEN rash — rash + systemic features after AED initiation — medical emergency; cease AED; hospital

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Neurologist: all new epilepsy diagnoses; AED change or treatment failure; pre-pregnancy planning in women with epilepsy; unexpected seizure recurrence
- Psychologist: depression, anxiety, psychological adjustment to epilepsy
- Epilepsy nurse specialist: education, self-management support, Epilepsy Australia resources

## Telehealth Suitability

Epilepsy monitoring is well suited to telehealth for stable patients.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Seizure frequency review and diary assessment
- AED dose review and renewal
- Side effect assessment
- Blood result interpretation (AED levels, FBC, LFTs, electrolytes)
- Contraception review and counselling
- Driving restriction counselling documentation
- Lifestyle advice (sleep, alcohol, triggers)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- New seizure presentation — clinical examination and investigations
- AED blood level testing — pathology arranged
- ECG (phenytoin, carbamazepine may affect cardiac conduction)
- Bone density (DEXA) — radiology referral
