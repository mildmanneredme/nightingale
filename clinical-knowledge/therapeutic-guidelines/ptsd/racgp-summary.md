## PTSD Assessment (RACGP)

Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) is a trauma- and stressor-related disorder that may develop after exposure to actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence. Approximately 12% of Australians will develop PTSD in their lifetime, with higher rates in specific populations: emergency responders, defence force veterans, survivors of interpersonal violence, refugees, and Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples (where transgenerational trauma is significant). PTSD commonly presents to general practice — often years after the traumatic event, with somatic complaints, chronic pain, sleep difficulties, depression, or substance use masking the underlying diagnosis. The GP plays a critical role in: trauma-informed assessment, early recognition, psychoeducation, first-line treatment initiation, and coordination with specialist mental health services. The Phoenix Australia Australian PTSD Clinical Practice Guideline 2020 is the authoritative Australian evidence-based resource. Telehealth is suitable for PTSD assessment and monitoring.

## Clinical Presentation

**DSM-5 PTSD diagnostic criteria (simplified):**

Following exposure to a traumatic event (actual or threatened death, serious injury, sexual violence — as direct victim, witness, or by learning it happened to a close other):

- Intrusion symptoms (≥1): flashbacks (reliving the trauma), nightmares, intrusive memories, intense distress at trauma reminders, physiological reactivity to cues
- Avoidance (≥1): avoidance of trauma-related thoughts, feelings, memories; avoidance of external reminders (people, places, situations)
- Negative cognitions/mood (≥2): inability to remember important aspects of trauma; persistent negative beliefs about self/world; distorted blame of self or others; persistent negative emotions (fear, horror, anger, guilt, shame); markedly diminished interest in activities; feeling detached from others; inability to experience positive emotions
- Arousal/reactivity (≥2): irritable or aggressive behaviour; reckless behaviour; hypervigilance; exaggerated startle; concentration difficulties; sleep disturbance

**Duration:** >1 month; significant functional impairment

**Commonly missed presentations:**
- Somatic: chronic pain, headaches, GI symptoms, pelvic pain (especially in sexual trauma survivors)
- Sleep: insomnia, nightmares, REM behaviour disorder
- Anger and irritability (particularly in men and veterans)
- Substance use: self-medication with alcohol, cannabis — screen for PTSD in substance use presentations
- Chronic back pain — high PTSD comorbidity

**Validated screening tools:**
- PCL-5 (PTSD Checklist for DSM-5): 20-item; score ≥31–33 provisional PTSD; free to use; patient-completed in waiting room
- Primary Care PTSD Screen (PC-PTSD-5): 5-item; brief; positive if ≥3/5 items; ideal for opportunistic screening

**Special populations:**
- Veterans and first responders: Open Arms (1800 011 046) — Veterans and Families Counselling (free); DVA-funded care
- Refugees and asylum seekers: high PTSD prevalence; cultural safety; interpreter services; STARTTS (NSW Service for the Treatment and Rehabilitation of Torture and Trauma Survivors) — state-specific services
- Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples: cultural trauma, intergenerational trauma; culturally safe care; Aboriginal Community Controlled Health Organisations
- Children and adolescents: specialist CAMHS referral

## Assessment Approach

**Trauma-informed approach:**
- Create a safe, non-judgmental environment; avoid detailed trauma retelling (retraumatising)
- Ask about trauma exposure with a normalising framing: "Many people I see have been through difficult experiences. Has anything happened to you in the past that still troubles you?"
- Screen with PCL-5 or PC-PTSD-5 opportunistically in high-risk presentations (chronic pain, sleep disorders, substance use, depression)
- Assess safety: suicide risk (high in PTSD), domestic violence, self-harm — mandatory at first presentation

**Comprehensive assessment:**
- Full psychiatric history: trauma history, onset, duration, functional impairment
- Comorbidities: depression (70% comorbidity), anxiety, substance use disorder, chronic pain — almost always present
- Previous trauma: complex PTSD from repeated or childhood trauma (different treatment approach — specialist)
- Suicide risk assessment: PTSD has 3-fold increased suicide risk; explicit inquiry essential at every contact
- Medication history: prior treatment attempts; what helped/failed

**Complex PTSD (ICD-11):**
- Results from prolonged, repeated trauma (childhood abuse, domestic violence, captivity)
- Core PTSD features + disturbances in: emotion regulation, identity/self-concept, interpersonal relationships
- More complex treatment course; specialist psychological care recommended; trauma-specific therapies

## Key Differentials

- Major depressive disorder: low mood, anhedonia — PTSD and MDD commonly coexist; screen for trauma in depression
- Generalised anxiety disorder: pervasive worry — distinguish from hypervigilance and PTSD-specific anxiety
- Borderline personality disorder: emotional dysregulation, impulsivity, interpersonal difficulties — significant overlap with complex PTSD; psychiatric assessment
- Acute stress disorder: PTSD-like symptoms within first 4 weeks of trauma; if resolves = acute stress disorder; if persists >1 month = PTSD
- Adjustment disorder: distress in response to identifiable stressor without full PTSD criteria; shorter duration
- Substance-induced mood/anxiety disorder: differentiate PTSD from substance-induced symptoms (both may coexist)
- TBI (traumatic brain injury): cognitive and emotional symptoms after head injury; neuropsychological testing; neurology

## Management Principles

**Psychological therapies — first-line (Phoenix Australia 2020):**

Trauma-focused CBT (TF-CBT):
- Most evidence-based; includes prolonged exposure (PE) and cognitive processing therapy (CPT)
- 8–15 sessions; structured; trauma narrative and cognitive restructuring
- GP Mental Health Care Plan (MBS 2713) for psychology referral

EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitisation and Reprocessing):
- Strong evidence; equivalent to trauma-focused CBT; recommended first-line
- Requires EMDR-trained clinician; many private psychologists trained in EMDR
- GP Mental Health Care Plan for referral

Written exposure therapy (WET): briefer (5 sessions); evidence-based; emerging option

Narrative exposure therapy (NET): developed for refugee populations; evidence-based

NOT recommended as primary PTSD treatment: generic supportive counselling, non-specific CBT, debriefing within days of trauma (psychological debriefing does NOT prevent PTSD)

**Pharmacotherapy (second-line — when psychological therapy not available, declined, or insufficient):**

Sertraline (first-line SSRI):
- 50–200 mg daily; FDA/TGA indication for PTSD; 8–12 week trial before assessing response
- Improves intrusion, avoidance, hyperarousal; less effective for emotional numbing
- Start low (25–50 mg); titrate over 4 weeks

Paroxetine (alternative SSRI — also TGA-indicated for PTSD):
- Discontinuation syndrome risk — gradual cessation; avoid abrupt cessation

Venlafaxine (SNRI — evidence-based, off-label for PTSD in Australia):
- 75–225 mg daily; strong evidence; useful if comorbid depression or anxiety

Prazosin (alpha-1 antagonist — off-label):
- Specifically reduces nightmares in PTSD; 1–10 mg nocte; monitor for orthostatic hypotension
- Used as adjunct; not first-line monotherapy

What NOT to prescribe:
- Benzodiazepines: NOT recommended for PTSD (impair trauma processing; cause dependency; worsen long-term outcomes); avoid if possible; cease if already prescribed
- Antipsychotics: not first-line; specialist use only for severe agitation or psychotic features
- Mood stabilisers: limited evidence as monotherapy; some use as adjunct for affect dysregulation

**Psychoeducation and supportive care:**
- Explain PTSD as a normal response to an abnormal event; reduce shame and self-blame
- Validate symptoms; trauma is not the patient's fault
- Provide information about treatment options; emphasise that PTSD is treatable
- Establish safety plan if suicide risk; provide crisis contacts (Lifeline 13 11 14; Beyond Blue 1300 22 4636)
- Assess substance use and address concurrently; substance use is commonly self-medication for PTSD

**Veteran-specific resources:**
- Open Arms Veterans & Families Counselling: 1800 011 046; free counselling for veterans and families
- DVA (Department of Veterans' Affairs): White Card (service-related conditions), Gold Card (all conditions), DVA Mental Health Clinician support
- For veterans — use Open Arms as first referral point before GP Mental Health Care Plan

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Sertraline** (PBS restricted: major depressive disorder, PTSD; authority; 50–200 mg daily; most widely used SSRI for PTSD)
- **Paroxetine** (PBS restricted: MDD, PTSD, GAD; authority; discontinuation syndrome — taper slowly; 20–60 mg daily)
- **Venlafaxine (Effexor XR)** (PBS restricted: MDD and GAD — PTSD off-label; 75–225 mg daily; SNRI; avoid abrupt cessation)
- **Prazosin** (PBS unrestricted: hypertension; off-label for PTSD nightmares; 1–10 mg nocte; orthostatic hypotension monitoring)
- **Note:** Benzodiazepines are NOT recommended for PTSD; avoid prescribing; if patient already on benzodiazepines — gradual taper plan in collaboration with psychiatrist; psychological therapy is always superior long-term

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / Crisis line:**
- Active suicidal ideation with plan or intent — 000 or mental health crisis team
- Domestic violence safety risk — 000; safety planning; DV Connect (1800 811 811)
- Acute psychiatric emergency — severe agitation, psychosis, inability to care for self

**Urgent psychiatric review:**
- Severe PTSD with inability to function or care for self
- Psychotic features (rare but serious)
- Comorbid severe depression with suicide risk
- Complex PTSD — specialist trauma-focused psychological care
- Failure of first-line treatment after adequate trial

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Psychologist: TF-CBT or EMDR via GP Mental Health Care Plan (MBS 2713)
- Open Arms (veterans): 1800 011 046
- STARTTS (refugees — NSW) or state-equivalent services: cultural trauma support
- Psychiatrist: diagnostic complexity, severe PTSD, medication management
- Drug and alcohol service: comorbid substance use disorder

## Telehealth Suitability

PTSD assessment and monitoring is suitable for telehealth with appropriate trauma-informed care.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Initial screening and psychoeducation
- PCL-5 administration and result review
- Safety assessment (suicidal ideation, domestic violence) — document carefully; have in-person option available
- Medication prescribing and monitoring (SSRI, prazosin)
- Referral coordination (psychology, Open Arms)
- GP Mental Health Care Plan (MBS 2713)
- Ongoing support and monitoring for established PTSD

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- First presentation if safety concerns require in-person risk assessment
- Trauma-focused psychological therapy (TF-CBT, EMDR) — delivered in person or telehealth by trained psychologist
- Physical examination for somatic presentations
