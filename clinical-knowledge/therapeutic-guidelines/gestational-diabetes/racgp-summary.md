## Gestational Diabetes Assessment (RACGP)

Gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM) is glucose intolerance first identified during pregnancy, affecting approximately 12–15% of Australian pregnancies — one of the most common medical complications of pregnancy. Prevalence is increasing with rising obesity rates and maternal age. GDM is associated with: macrosomia (large baby), birth injury, neonatal hypoglycaemia, pre-eclampsia, preterm birth, and increased caesarean rate. Women with GDM have a 50–60% lifetime risk of developing type 2 diabetes. GPs play a central role in: universal GDM screening at 24–28 weeks, providing ongoing metabolic monitoring during pregnancy, and managing GDM long-term postpartum risk. Obstetric team (obstetrician, diabetes educator, dietitian) manages the pregnancy itself; GP provides primary care context, manages risk factors, and coordinates postpartum diabetes prevention. Telehealth is suitable for ongoing monitoring and support.

## Clinical Presentation

**Gestational diabetes — typically asymptomatic:**
- Usually identified on routine screening at 24–28 weeks gestation
- Rarely: polydipsia, polyuria, glycosuria (but these are non-specific in pregnancy)
- Risk factors for GDM (higher pre-test probability — some guidelines screen earlier):
  - Previous GDM
  - Obesity (BMI >30)
  - Family history of type 2 diabetes
  - Previous macrosomic infant (birthweight >4.5 kg)
  - South Asian, East Asian, Middle Eastern, Pacific Islander, or Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander background
  - PCOS
  - Glucocorticoid use
  - Maternal age >40

**Maternal and fetal complications of untreated GDM:**
- Fetal macrosomia: large for gestational age; shoulder dystocia; birth injury (brachial plexus)
- Neonatal hypoglycaemia: first hours of life — neonatal unit monitoring
- Pre-eclampsia: GDM increases risk
- Preterm labour: associated
- Stillbirth: increased risk in poorly controlled or undiagnosed GDM
- Neonatal respiratory distress syndrome
- Long-term: child at increased risk of obesity and type 2 diabetes in adolescence/adulthood

## Assessment Approach

**Screening (ADIPS guidelines — universal at 24–28 weeks):**

Step 1 — Non-fasting 50 g Glucose Challenge Test (GCT) (ADIPS 2014 — still used in some centres):
- 50 g oral glucose load; venous blood glucose at 1 hour
- Positive (≥7.8 mmol/L): proceed to full 75 g OGTT
- Some centres (HAPO study-based) now use direct 75 g OGTT without GCT step

Step 2 — Fasting 75 g Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT):
- Gold standard diagnostic test in Australia (ADIPS)
- Fasting overnight; venous blood glucose at: 0 hours (fasting), 1 hour, 2 hours after 75 g glucose
- Diagnostic thresholds (ADIPS 2014): GDM diagnosed if ANY one value met or exceeded:
  - Fasting ≥5.1 mmol/L
  - 1-hour ≥10.0 mmol/L
  - 2-hour ≥8.5 mmol/L
- Women who meet WHO type 2 diabetes criteria on OGTT: overt diabetes in pregnancy (different management)

**Early GDM screening (<24 weeks):**
- At first antenatal visit if: multiple risk factors (obesity + family history + previous GDM etc.)
- Fasting glucose ≥5.1 mmol/L at first visit (before 20 weeks) = GDM if no pre-existing diabetes diagnosis
- HbA1c not used for GDM diagnosis (poor sensitivity; affected by haemodilution of pregnancy)

**Monitoring during pregnancy:**
- Home blood glucose monitoring (BGM): fasting and 1–2 hours post-meals; targets:
  - Fasting: <5.0–5.3 mmol/L
  - 1-hour post-meal: <7.4 mmol/L (ADIPS)
  - 2-hour post-meal: <6.7 mmol/L
- Weight gain monitoring: appropriate gestational weight gain targets (BMI-dependent)
- Ultrasound fetal growth: 28–32 weeks if glycaemia not well controlled (macrosomia surveillance)

**Postpartum assessment (critical — often missed):**
- 75 g OGTT at 6–12 weeks postpartum: diagnose persistent glucose intolerance; WHO criteria apply (diabetes, IGT, IFG, or normal)
- If normal at 6–12 weeks: annual fasting glucose or HbA1c for life (50–60% develop type 2 diabetes within 10 years)
- Breastfeeding: encouraged; reduces maternal T2DM risk

## Key Differentials

- Pre-existing type 2 diabetes: undiagnosed before pregnancy; HbA1c may be elevated; fasting glucose >7.0 at first antenatal visit
- Pre-existing type 1 diabetes: autoimmune; insulin-dependent; present before pregnancy; anti-GAD antibodies
- Impaired fasting glucose (IFG) or impaired glucose tolerance (IGT): OGTT values below GDM threshold but abnormal; monitor closely; dietary management
- Glycosuria without GDM: reduced renal threshold in pregnancy is common; does NOT confirm GDM without OGTT

## Management Principles

**Medical nutrition therapy (first-line — all GDM):**
- Dietitian referral: essential; MBS-funded via CDM plan; individualised carbohydrate-controlled diet
- Target: adequate nutrition for fetal growth + glucose targets; NOT caloric restriction (harm to fetus)
- Carbohydrate distribution: 3 meals + 2–3 snacks; low GI carbohydrates; avoid sugar-sweetened beverages
- Gestational weight gain: target appropriate gain per pre-pregnancy BMI (Institute of Medicine guidelines)

**Physical activity:**
- 150 minutes/week moderate intensity activity: walking, swimming; safe in uncomplicated pregnancy
- Post-meal walking: reduces postprandial glucose spikes particularly effectively

**Insulin therapy (when dietary measures insufficient):**
- Commence insulin when: BGM targets not met on 2+ occasions per week on dietary management alone
- First-line insulin regimen: NPH insulin at bedtime (for fasting hyperglycaemia) and/or rapid-acting insulin (NovoRapid, Humalog) before meals for postprandial peaks
- Insulin is safe in pregnancy: does not cross placenta; gold standard for glucose control in GDM when diet insufficient
- Monitoring: BGM daily; dose titration by diabetes team or experienced GP/obstetrician
- Delivery: basal-bolus regimen typical for multiple excursions; initiated by obstetric team or endocrinologist

**Metformin in GDM:**
- Metformin crosses the placenta: fetal safety data reassuring for short-term outcomes; long-term data uncertain
- ADIPS position: metformin may be used as adjunct or alternative to insulin for some patients (lower adherence barrier); patient preference-based decision; discuss risk-benefit
- Preferred in specific situations: insulin refusal; marked insulin resistance; non-insulin hospital protocols

**Intrapartum management:**
- Aim for normoglycaemia in labour: 4–8 mmol/L
- Diet-controlled GDM: often no special management needed in labour; BGM monitoring
- Insulin-requiring GDM: IV glucose + insulin infusion or variable-rate insulin infusion in labour — obstetric team
- Birth timing: 39–40 weeks for well-controlled GDM; earlier if macrosomia or poor control

**Postpartum care:**
- Cease insulin/metformin immediately after delivery: glucose usually normalises
- Breastfeeding support: reduces maternal T2DM risk by 40–50%; reduces neonatal obesity risk
- OGTT at 6–12 weeks postpartum: GP-ordered; critical follow-up (often missed)
- Lifestyle counselling: Mediterranean diet, exercise; Australian Diabetes Prevention Program resources
- Weight management: return to healthy weight post-pregnancy
- Annual FPG or HbA1c: lifelong monitoring for T2DM development

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **NPH insulin (Humulin NPH, Protaphane)** (PBS restricted: diabetes mellitus; bedtime dose for fasting hyperglycaemia; titrated by diabetes/obstetric team; does not cross placenta)
- **Rapid-acting insulin (NovoRapid, Humalog)** (PBS restricted: diabetes mellitus; before meals for postprandial hyperglycaemia; titrated by team)
- **Metformin** (PBS restricted: type 2 diabetes; off-label in GDM; crosses placenta; patient preference decision; discuss with obstetric team; continue after delivery only if progressing to T2DM treatment)
- **Blood glucose monitoring strips**: MBS/PBS subsidised for people with diabetes on insulin; subsidised under NDSS (National Diabetes Services Scheme) for GDM patients
- **NDSS (National Diabetes Services Scheme)**: free or subsidised access to BGMS lancets, strips, insulin syringes for GDM patients; GP registers patient with NDSS

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate obstetric team / hospital:**
- Fasting glucose ≥7.0 mmol/L or 2-hour OGTT ≥11.1 mmol/L: overt diabetes in pregnancy — obstetric endocrinology urgently
- Severe hypoglycaemia on insulin: <3.0 mmol/L + symptoms — glucagon if impaired consciousness; hospital if unable to maintain glucose
- Suspected macrosomia + poorly controlled GDM: urgent obstetric review; timing of delivery

**Non-urgent specialist:**
- Endocrinologist or physician with diabetes expertise: insulin initiation; type 1/overt DM in pregnancy; complex management
- Diabetic educator: insulin training, BGM technique, dietary management

**Postpartum:**
- OGTT at 6–12 weeks: if positive (diabetes or IGT) — GP primary care management + diabetes educator referral
- Annual screening: fasting glucose or HbA1c for life — GP responsibility

## Telehealth Suitability

GDM monitoring is suitable for telehealth in stable pregnancy.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- BGM log review and dietary counselling
- Insulin dose discussion and titration (with concurrent obstetric team)
- Lifestyle counselling (diet, physical activity)
- Postpartum GDM follow-up planning
- OGTT result discussion
- NDSS registration and resource provision

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Antenatal OGTT (blood test at pathology)
- Obstetric assessments (fetal growth, blood pressure)
- Insulin initiation education (subcutaneous injection technique)
- Postpartum OGTT blood test
