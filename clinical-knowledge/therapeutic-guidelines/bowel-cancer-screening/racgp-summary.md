## Bowel Cancer Screening Assessment (RACGP)

Colorectal cancer (CRC) is the second most common cause of cancer death in Australia, affecting approximately 15,500 Australians annually. The National Bowel Cancer Screening Program (NBCSP) offers a free immunochemical faecal occult blood test (iFOBT/FIT) to all Australians aged 45–74 every 2 years, mailed directly to participants' homes. Early-stage CRC detected through screening has a 5-year survival rate of >90%, compared with <14% for stage IV disease. The GP plays a pivotal role in: promoting participation in the NBCSP, explaining positive FIT results and the colonoscopy pathway, assessing individual risk (family history, personal history of polyps or IBD), recommending risk-stratified surveillance intervals, and managing symptomatic presentations that require urgent colonoscopy irrespective of FIT result. Telehealth is suitable for result discussion and screening counselling.

## Clinical Presentation

**Who requires CRC screening:**

Average risk (NBCSP — no personal or family history of CRC or adenomas):
- Age 45–74: free 2-yearly iFOBT via NBCSP mailed kit
- iFOBT positive: colonoscopy required regardless of other symptoms (positive FIT means colonoscopy, not repeat FIT)

Above-average risk (requires tailored surveillance — not covered by NBCSP alone):
- First-degree relative with CRC at age <55: colonoscopy from age 40 (or 10 years before the youngest affected relative)
- Two or more first-degree relatives with CRC: colonoscopy from age 40; 5-yearly if normal
- Personal history of adenomatous polyps: surveillance colonoscopy interval depends on number, size, histology (gastroenterologist arranges)
- Personal history of CRC: post-resection surveillance colonoscopy (gastroenterologist managed)
- Inflammatory bowel disease (extensive UC or Crohn's colitis ≥8 years): annual/biennial colonoscopy surveillance (see `inflammatory-bowel-disease` entry)

High risk — hereditary syndromes (gastroenterology and genetics referral):
- Lynch syndrome (HNPCC): 80% lifetime CRC risk; annual colonoscopy from age 25–35; MSH2, MLH1, MSH6, PMS2 gene mutations
- Familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP): APC gene mutation; hundreds of polyps; near-100% CRC risk without treatment; prophylactic colectomy; annual flexible sigmoidoscopy from puberty
- MUTYH-associated polyposis: biallelic MUTYH mutations; similar to attenuated FAP

**Symptoms requiring urgent (not screening) colonoscopy:**
- Rectal bleeding (fresh blood or melaena not explained by haemorrhoids/fissure)
- Change in bowel habit persisting >6 weeks in patients ≥50
- Unexplained weight loss + bowel symptoms
- Iron deficiency anaemia (in men or postmenopausal women — exclude CRC)
- Palpable abdominal or rectal mass
- In these cases: colonoscopy as a diagnostic (not screening) procedure regardless of FIT result

## Assessment Approach

**FIT/iFOBT and NBCSP participation:**
- Eligible Australians receive free kits every 2 years by mail; test at home; return via prepaid envelope
- FIT detects haemoglobin from low-volume blood in stool; sensitivity for CRC ~79%; specificity ~95%
- GP role: proactively encourage patients to participate; check NBCSP status in patients aged 45–74 (My Health Record may have results)
- Positive FIT: colonoscopy required within 120 days (NBCSP guideline); GP referral to gastroenterologist; communicate urgency and reassurance to patient

**Family history risk assessment at every 45+ consultation:**
- Ask: any family members (parents, siblings, children) with bowel cancer? At what age?
- Document family history in medical records; update risk stratification if history changes
- Refer to familial cancer clinics if Lynch syndrome or FAP suspected (Amsterdam/Bethesda criteria)

**Investigations for symptomatic patients:**
- FBC: iron deficiency anaemia (CRC indicator in men and postmenopausal women)
- CEA (carcinoembryonic antigen): not a screening test; elevated in established CRC; used for monitoring recurrence post-treatment
- CT colonography (virtual colonoscopy): alternative to optical colonoscopy for patients unfit for procedure (elderly, comorbidities); sensitivity ~96% for polyps ≥6 mm
- Flexible sigmoidoscopy: left colon only; limited sensitivity; not routinely used in Australia for CRC screening

## Key Differentials

- Haemorrhoids or anal fissure: bright red rectal bleeding; visible on examination; but do not assume — FIT positive or change in bowel habit still warrants colonoscopy
- Diverticular disease: painless rectal bleeding; older patient; colonoscopy still needed if first episode or uncertain
- IBD: bloody diarrhoea; younger onset; colonoscopy with biopsies diagnoses and stages
- IBS: altered bowel habit; no bleeding; FIT negative; weight maintained
- CRC: any persistent change in bowel habit, rectal bleeding, iron deficiency in appropriate age group — colonoscopy is the only way to exclude

## Management Principles

**Promoting NBCSP participation:**
- NBCSP participation rate is ~40% — substantial under-participation; GP recommendation significantly increases uptake
- Simple, direct encouragement: "I recommend you complete the bowel cancer screening test — it's free and important"
- Explain: testing at home, simple procedure (stool sample), free kit mailed by government
- Follow up if eligible patients have not returned kit within expected window
- NBCSP helpline: 1800 118 868

**Managing positive FIT — GP pathway:**
- Explain positive result: "The test found a small amount of blood in your stool — this may have many causes, but we need to check your bowel with a colonoscopy to make sure everything is okay"
- Do not minimise or delay: organise colonoscopy referral urgently (NBCSP target within 120 days)
- Refer to gastroenterologist or colorectal surgeon; if public system — refer with urgency code "FIT positive"
- Reassure: 50% of positive FITs have no CRC or significant polyps found; many causes of a positive result; colonoscopy is the next step

**Post-colonoscopy surveillance:**

Normal colonoscopy:
- Return to 2-yearly NBCSP FIT (average risk)
- High-risk family history patients: 5-yearly colonoscopy

Low-risk adenoma (1–2 small tubular adenomas <10 mm):
- 5-year colonoscopy surveillance

High-risk adenoma (≥3 adenomas, any adenoma ≥10 mm, villous histology, high-grade dysplasia):
- 3-year colonoscopy surveillance (gastroenterologist manages)

Serrated polyps:
- Sessile serrated lesions ≥10 mm or with dysplasia: 1–3 year surveillance (gastroenterologist)

**CRC diagnosis and post-treatment management (specialist-led — GP role):**
- Multidisciplinary team (MDT) management: colorectal surgeon, oncologist, radiation oncologist, stomal therapy nurse
- GP post-treatment care: surveillance colonoscopy, CEA monitoring, fatigue, bowel function, stoma management, mental health support, survivorship (see `cancer-survivorship` entry)
- Lynch syndrome family screening: refer unaffected first-degree relatives for genetic counselling and testing

**Lifestyle modification (CRC risk reduction):**
- Red and processed meat: limit; associated with increased CRC risk
- Dietary fibre: protective; wholegrains, vegetables, legumes
- Physical activity: regular exercise protective; obesity increases CRC risk
- Alcohol: limit; >1–2 standard drinks/day associated with increased CRC risk
- Smoking: associated with increased risk; cessation (see `smoking-cessation` entry)
- Aspirin: regular low-dose aspirin reduces CRC risk by ~20%; not routinely recommended for CRC prevention alone; benefit may outweigh risk in Lynch syndrome (CAPP2 trial) — specialist decision

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Note:** CRC screening is a nationally funded government program (NBCSP); colonoscopy under Medicare (MBS item for colonoscopy after positive FIT); no specific PBS medications for CRC screening
- **Ferrous sulfate** (PBS unrestricted: iron deficiency anaemia post-CRC diagnosis or surgery; oral iron)
- **Folic acid / B12** (after ileal resection affecting vitamin B12 absorption — IM replacement)
- **Note:** Aspirin for CRC prevention is NOT a standard PBS-supported indication; Lynch syndrome patients may be recommended aspirin by specialist — individualised

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Large volume rectal bleeding with haemodynamic instability — 000
- Suspected acute bowel obstruction (colicky pain, distension, absolute constipation) — 000

**Urgent gastroenterology / colorectal surgery:**
- Positive FIT result — colonoscopy referral within 120 days (NBCSP standard); GP should make this referral promptly
- Symptomatic patient with rectal bleeding, change in bowel habit ≥6 weeks age ≥50, iron deficiency anaemia — urgent colonoscopy referral
- Suspected CRC on imaging or examination — urgent MDT referral; notify patient

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Gastroenterologist: post-polypectomy surveillance; above-average risk surveillance planning
- Familial cancer clinic / genetic counselling: Lynch syndrome or FAP suspicion (two or more first-degree relatives, young age of onset)
- Dietitian: post-bowel surgery dietary adjustment; stoma nutrition
- Stoma therapy nurse: stoma care and management

## Telehealth Suitability

Bowel cancer screening counselling is highly suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- NBCSP result discussion (positive or negative FIT)
- Colonoscopy referral and explanation
- Family history assessment and risk stratification counselling
- Surveillance interval discussion post-colonoscopy
- Lifestyle counselling for CRC risk reduction
- Post-CRC treatment survivorship monitoring review

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Rectal examination (if bleeding or mass suspected)
- Colonoscopy referral (gastroenterologist performs in-person)
- Blood tests — pathology arranged
- Iron deficiency assessment if symptomatic
