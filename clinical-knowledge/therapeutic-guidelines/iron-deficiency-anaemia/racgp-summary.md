## Iron Deficiency Anaemia Assessment (RACGP)

Iron deficiency (ID) is the most common nutritional deficiency worldwide and the leading cause of anaemia in general practice. Iron deficiency without anaemia (IDWA) also causes significant symptoms. In Australia, the most common causes are inadequate dietary intake, increased demands (pregnancy, growth), and blood loss (menstrual, GI). Identification of the underlying cause is essential — iron deficiency in a postmenopausal woman or adult male without a clear dietary cause requires exclusion of GI malignancy. Telehealth is highly suitable for results review, treatment initiation, and monitoring.

## Clinical Presentation

Symptoms of iron deficiency and iron deficiency anaemia: fatigue, reduced exercise tolerance, pallor, dyspnoea on exertion, palpitations, headache, poor concentration, restless legs, hair loss, brittle nails, koilonychia (spoon-shaped nails), glossitis, angular cheilitis, pica (craving non-food items such as ice).

Many patients are asymptomatic and diagnosed on routine blood tests or screening.

Population groups at higher risk: women of reproductive age (menstrual loss), pregnant women (increased demand), infants and toddlers (rapid growth, delayed introduction of iron-rich foods), vegetarians and vegans (non-haem iron less bioavailable), athletes (haemolysis, sweat losses, high turnover), older adults (reduced intake, chronic disease, medications).

## Assessment Approach

Confirm diagnosis with full blood count and iron studies:
- Ferritin <30 µg/L: iron deficiency (note ferritin is an acute phase reactant and can be falsely normal in inflammation — in the context of chronic inflammation, ferritin up to 100 µg/L may still indicate iron deficiency)
- Haemoglobin below reference range with microcytic, hypochromic indices (low MCV, low MCH) confirms iron deficiency anaemia
- Transferrin saturation <20% and elevated TIBC support iron deficiency

Determine the cause — essential before or concurrently with treatment:
- Menstrual history: heavy menstrual bleeding most common cause in premenopausal women
- Dietary history: vegetarian/vegan diet, restricted eating patterns
- GI symptoms: rectal bleeding, change in bowel habit, weight loss, dysphagia (requires urgent investigation regardless of age)
- Blood donation history, pregnancy, breastfeeding
- Medications: NSAIDs, aspirin, PPIs (reduce iron absorption), anticoagulants

GI investigation indications:
- All adult males with iron deficiency — colonoscopy +/- gastroscopy to exclude GI malignancy
- Postmenopausal women without an identified cause — as above
- Pre-menopausal women with iron deficiency not explained by menstrual loss, or not responding to treatment

Check coeliac serology (tTG-IgA) — coeliac disease causes malabsorption of iron and is a common under-recognised cause of iron deficiency in adults.

## Key Differentials

- Anaemia of chronic disease — normochromic normocytic or mildly microcytic; ferritin normal or elevated; TIBC low or normal; associated with chronic inflammation or malignancy
- Thalassaemia trait — microcytic anaemia with normal or elevated ferritin; low MCV and MCH; target cells on film; diagnose with Hb electrophoresis or HPLC
- B12 / folate deficiency anaemia — macrocytic anaemia; check B12 and folate; neurological symptoms may be present
- Haemolytic anaemia — elevated reticulocytes, bilirubin, LDH; low haptoglobin
- Sideroblastic anaemia — microcytic or dimorphic; requires haematology review

## Management Principles

**Identify and treat the underlying cause** — iron replacement treats the deficiency but the cause must be addressed to prevent recurrence.

**Oral iron — first-line:**
- Ferrous sulfate 325 mg (approximately 65 mg elemental iron) once or twice daily
- Alternate-day dosing improves absorption and tolerability compared to daily dosing (2020 evidence)
- Take on an empty stomach or with vitamin C (ascorbic acid) to enhance absorption
- Avoid taking with calcium-rich foods, tea, coffee, or antacids (impair absorption)
- Side effects: constipation, nausea, dark stools; reduce dose or switch to ferrous gluconate if intolerant
- Duration: continue for 3–6 months after Hb normalises to replenish stores; confirm ferritin >50 µg/L before stopping
- Monitor: recheck FBC and ferritin at 4–6 weeks; Hb should rise ≥10 g/L in 4 weeks if responding

**Intravenous iron — indications:**
- Intolerance or non-response to oral iron
- Ongoing blood loss exceeding oral replacement capacity
- Malabsorption (coeliac, IBD, post-bariatric surgery)
- Severe symptomatic anaemia requiring rapid replenishment
- Pregnancy with severe or late-presenting iron deficiency anaemia
- Options (AU PBS-listed): ferric carboxymaltose (Ferinject), iron polymaltose (Ferrum H — not PBS-listed for adult IDA), ferric derisomaltose (Monofer)
- Requires in-person administration at infusion centre or clinic

Blood transfusion: reserved for severe symptomatic anaemia (Hb <70 g/L or haemodynamic compromise) — requires hospital-level care.

**Dietary advice:** Red meat (haem iron — highest bioavailability), legumes, dark leafy vegetables, iron-fortified cereals (non-haem iron). Combine with vitamin C-rich foods.

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Ferrous sulfate** (Ferro-Gradumet, Ferro-tab — PBS unrestricted) — most cost-effective oral option
- **Ferrous gluconate** (Fergon — PBS unrestricted) — better tolerated than ferrous sulfate; lower elemental iron per tablet
- **Ferric carboxymaltose** (Ferinject — PBS restricted: iron deficiency anaemia where oral iron contraindicated or ineffective; must meet specific criteria including confirmed ID on bloods)
- **Ferric derisomaltose** (Monofer — PBS restricted: as for ferric carboxymaltose; allows larger single-dose administration)
- **Iron polymaltose complex** (Maltofer — oral; PBS unrestricted for IDA)
- Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) is not PBS-listed but widely available OTC; advise 250 mg with iron dose

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Severe symptomatic anaemia with haemodynamic instability (syncope, hypotension, tachycardia)
- Acute significant haemorrhage — rectal bleeding with haemodynamic compromise, haematemesis, melaena

**Urgent in-person GP or gastroenterology review:**
- Any GI alarm symptoms accompanying iron deficiency — rectal bleeding, weight loss, change in bowel habit, dysphagia — require urgent investigation regardless of severity of anaemia
- Iron deficiency in adult male or postmenopausal female without identified cause — colonoscopy referral should not be delayed
- Non-response to oral iron at 4–6 weeks (re-evaluate cause, consider coeliac serology, IV iron referral)

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Gastroenterology: for colonoscopy/gastroscopy as above
- Haematology: unexplained microcytic anaemia, suspected thalassaemia, refractory iron deficiency
- Gynaecology: heavy menstrual bleeding as cause of iron deficiency

## Telehealth Suitability

Iron deficiency anaemia is highly suitable for telehealth management once results are available.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Results review and diagnosis discussion
- Oral iron initiation, dosing advice, and side effect counselling
- Dietary counselling
- Monitoring response to treatment (pathology review at 4–6 weeks)
- Referral coordination (GI, gynaecology, IV iron infusion service)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Physical examination for pallor, hepatosplenomegaly, or mass suggesting underlying cause
- IV iron infusion (requires clinical setting)
- Colonoscopy / gastroscopy (specialist referral)
