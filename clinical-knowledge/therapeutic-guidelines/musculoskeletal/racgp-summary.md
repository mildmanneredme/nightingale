## Musculoskeletal Assessment (RACGP)

Musculoskeletal (MSK) conditions are among the most common presentations in Australian primary care. Most acute non-traumatic musculoskeletal pain is benign and self-limiting, but it is important to identify serious pathology (fractures, inflammatory arthritis, red-flag back pain) requiring urgent investigation or referral.

## Common MSK presentations

**Acute low back pain**: The most common MSK presentation. Over 90% of acute low back pain is non-specific and resolves within 6–12 weeks with appropriate self-care. Activity modification and analgesia are first-line. Imaging is not routinely indicated for acute non-specific low back pain.

**Neck pain**: Often associated with poor posture, muscle strain, or cervical spondylosis. Most cases are self-limiting. Assess for radiculopathy (arm pain, weakness, numbness) and myelopathy.

**Shoulder pain**: Common causes include rotator cuff pathology, impingement syndrome, and acromioclavicular joint injury. Assessment of range of motion and strength is important.

**Knee pain**: Common causes include osteoarthritis, patellofemoral syndrome, meniscal injury, and ligamentous injury. History of trauma, locking, or significant swelling warrants further assessment.

## Red flags requiring urgent assessment

The following features require urgent investigation (possible serious spinal or systemic pathology):
- Back pain with bladder or bowel dysfunction (cauda equina syndrome — medical emergency)
- Back pain with lower limb weakness or numbness below the knee
- Night pain, pain at rest, or progressive pain not responding to analgesia
- Unexplained weight loss with back pain (possible malignancy)
- History of malignancy, significant trauma, or osteoporosis
- Fever with back pain (possible spinal infection)
- Back pain in patients under 20 or over 50 years presenting for the first time

**Call 000 or go to the nearest emergency department immediately for**: saddle anaesthesia, sudden onset of bilateral leg weakness, or acute loss of bladder/bowel control.

## Management principles

Self-care for acute non-specific MSK pain:
- Continue normal activities as tolerated (avoid bed rest)
- Analgesia as directed by GP (paracetamol first-line; NSAIDs with caution)
- Heat or ice packs for symptomatic relief
- Physiotherapy referral for persistent or recurrent symptoms
- Address workplace ergonomics and posture

Opioid analgesics are not recommended for non-specific acute low back pain.
