## Frailty Assessment (RACGP)

Frailty is a clinical syndrome of increased vulnerability to stressors resulting from cumulative decline across multiple physiological systems, beyond what would be expected from chronological age alone. Frailty is distinct from but related to disability and comorbidity. It affects approximately 10–15% of community-dwelling adults ≥65 and up to 50% in residential aged care. Frailty is a strong predictor of: falls, hospitalisation, functional decline, surgical complications, adverse drug reactions, and mortality. The GP plays a central role in: identifying frailty in older patients, conducting comprehensive geriatric assessment (CGA), targeting reversible contributors, managing polypharmacy (a major contributor), and coordinating aged care services. Early identification allows intervention before decompensation occurs. Telehealth is suitable for frailty monitoring; in-person assessment is required for physical testing.

## Clinical Presentation

**Fried Phenotype (most validated frailty model — 5 criteria):**

Frail = 3–5 criteria; Pre-frail = 1–2 criteria; Robust = 0 criteria:
1. Unintentional weight loss (>4.5 kg or >5% body weight in past year)
2. Exhaustion (self-reported fatigue most days)
3. Weak grip strength (dynamometer measurement; below sex- and BMI-specific thresholds)
4. Slow gait speed (<0.8 m/s on 4-metre walk test)
5. Low physical activity (below threshold for age and sex)

**Rockwood Clinical Frailty Scale (CFS — most practical for GP):**
- 9-point scale from Very Fit (1) to Terminally Ill (9)
- CFS ≥5 = Mildly Frail (some slowness, need help with instrumental ADLs)
- CFS ≥7 = Severely Frail (dependent for all personal care)
- Can be scored from clinical history without physical testing; validated for predicting outcomes

**Clinical presentations suggesting frailty:**
- Unexplained weight loss
- Recurrent falls (see `falls-prevention` entry)
- Delirium in hospital from minor stressor (infection, medication change, dehydration)
- Prolonged recovery from illness or surgery
- Functional decline (unable to manage previously performed tasks)
- Polypharmacy (≥5 medications) — often driver and consequence of frailty
- Multiple comorbidities
- Social isolation, poor nutrition, low activity levels

**Frailty vs normal ageing:**
- Normal ageing: gradual slowing; maintained function; not frail
- Frailty: vulnerability to adverse outcomes from small stressors; identifiable and partially reversible

## Assessment Approach

**Frailty assessment tools in GP:**

PRISMA-7 (screening tool):
- 7 yes/no questions; score ≥3 = high risk of disability/frailty; triggers comprehensive assessment

Edmonton Frail Scale (EFS):
- Multidomain; 11 items; cognition, general health, functional independence, social support, medication use, nutrition, mood, continence, functional performance
- ≥8 = moderate-severe frailty; practical and quick

Clinical Frailty Scale (CFS):
- Single rater assessment; 1–9 scale; most practical for routine GP use

**Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment (CGA) — trigger when frailty confirmed:**

Medical:
- Comorbidity review: list and prioritise active conditions
- Medication review (STOPP/START criteria): identify drugs increasing frailty risk (sedatives, anticholinergics, antihypertensives causing orthostasis)
- Nutritional status: BMI, weight trend, MNA (Mini Nutritional Assessment); malnutrition and frailty bidirectionally linked
- Sensory: vision (optometrist), hearing (audiologist)

Functional:
- ADLs (Activities of Daily Living): bathing, dressing, toileting, continence, transfer, feeding
- IADLs (Instrumental ADLs): cooking, shopping, medications, finances, phone, transport
- Mobility: TUG test; gait speed

Cognitive:
- MMSE or MoCA: cognitive impairment is common in frailty; drives functional decline

Psychological:
- Depression (PHQ-9): extremely common; undertreated in older patients; treat aggressively
- Anxiety: GAD-7

Social and environmental:
- Support network: family, carer involvement, social isolation
- Housing safety: OT home assessment for hazards
- Financial: Centrelink aged care benefits; My Aged Care

**Aged care assessment pathway:**
- ACAT (Aged Care Assessment Team): 1800 200 422 (My Aged Care); required for:
  - Home Care Packages (Level 1–4) — government-funded in-home support
  - Residential aged care (nursing home) entry — ACAT assessment mandatory
  - Short-term restorative care programs
- RAS (Regional Assessment Service): for lower-level Commonwealth Home Support Program (CHSP) — basic help at home

## Key Differentials

- Frailty vs sarcopenia: sarcopenia is muscle mass/strength/function loss; component of frailty; DEXA or BIA measures muscle mass; treated with resistance exercise + protein; they overlap significantly
- Frailty vs chronic disease: e.g., severe COPD, heart failure — can cause frailty-like functional decline; treat underlying condition; frailty may be additional concurrent diagnosis
- Frailty vs deconditioning: deconditioning from hospital bed rest, prolonged illness — potentially more reversible than chronic frailty; same interventions (exercise, nutrition)
- Frailty vs depression: depression causes fatigue, weight loss, low activity — overlaps significantly; treat depression; reassess frailty
- Frailty vs cognitive impairment: dementia causes functional decline; both may coexist; screen for both

## Management Principles

**Exercise (most evidence-based intervention for frailty):**
- Resistance training: most effective; prevents sarcopenia; improves strength; can be started safely at any age including ≥90 years
- Balance and gait training: Otago Exercise Programme; Tai chi; reduces falls
- Aerobic exercise: walking, hydrotherapy
- Exercise physiologist referral: MBS-funded under Chronic Disease Management plan
- Home-based exercise: prescribe specific exercises with supervision initially

**Nutrition:**
- Protein: minimum 1.2 g/kg/day protein (vs 0.8 g/kg standard adult RDI); recent evidence for 1.2–1.5 g/kg/day in frail older adults; leucine-rich foods (meat, fish, dairy, eggs, legumes)
- Nutritional supplements: Ensure, Sustagen for weight loss or poor intake
- Vitamin D: supplement to >50 nmol/L; reduces falls and muscle weakness
- Calcium: 1200 mg/day through diet and supplements
- Dietitian referral: MBS-funded under Chronic Disease Management plan

**Medication review (polypharmacy management):**
- STOPP/START criteria: systematic evidence-based tool for inappropriate prescribing in older adults
- Target: cease or reduce anticholinergic medications (most harmful; anticholinergic burden score), benzodiazepines, sedating antihistamines, opioids (falls risk), excessive antihypertensives (orthostatic hypotension)
- Deprescribing: collaborative; discuss with patient and carer; set realistic goals; monitor after each change
- Clinical pharmacist: medication review; MBS-funded for residential aged care; increasingly available for community

**Social connection and cognitive engagement:**
- Social isolation worsens frailty; connect to community programs, social supports, seniors groups
- Cognitive engagement: reading, crosswords, social activities — may slow cognitive decline in frailty
- Carer support: carer fatigue assessment; refer to Carers Australia 1800 422 737

**Aged care services (via My Aged Care 1800 200 422):**
- Commonwealth Home Support Programme (CHSP): basic services (meal delivery, domestic assistance, transport); entry-level; assessed by RAS
- Home Care Packages (Level 1–4): more complex needs; ACAT assessment; can take months to access — refer early
- Short-term restorative care (STRC): 8-week intensive allied health to improve function after acute illness or functional decline
- Residential aged care: when home care no longer safe or sufficient; ACAT assessment mandatory

**Advance care planning:**
- All frail older patients: initiate ACP early; see `advance-care-planning` entry
- Goals of care: particularly relevant in hospitalisation planning; escalation planning in residential care

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Vitamin D (colecalciferol)** (PBS unrestricted: 1000–2000 IU daily; monitor 25-OH vitamin D level target >50 nmol/L)
- **Protein supplements (nutritional formulas)** (not PBS; available OTC as Ensure, Sustagen, Boost; dietitian recommends specific product)
- **Melatonin** (PBS restricted: insomnia in ≥55 years; safer than benzodiazepines or Z-drugs for sleep in frail elderly; 2 mg MR)
- **Note:** Frailty management is primarily non-pharmacological; polypharmacy reduction is the key pharmacological intervention; target medications with lowest benefit-to-harm ratio using STOPP/START criteria
- Bisphosphonates/denosumab: if osteoporosis confirmed on DEXA; see `osteoporosis` entry

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent hospital:**
- Acute delirium in frail patient: always investigate treatable cause (UTI, pneumonia, medication toxicity, pain) — frail patients decompensate rapidly with minor stressors; hospital if cannot manage in community
- Rapid functional decline (days to weeks) — exclude acute medical cause

**Urgent geriatric assessment:**
- Moderate-severe frailty (CFS ≥6) with complex medical and functional issues: geriatrician review for comprehensive assessment
- Falls with injury: fracture; functional decline; review full frailty management plan

**Non-urgent referral:**
- ACAT (My Aged Care 1800 200 422): Home Care Package or Residential Care assessment — refer early (waitlists can be months)
- Exercise physiologist: resistance and balance training program
- Dietitian: nutritional assessment and supplementation
- Clinical pharmacist: medication review (available in some Chronic Disease Management plans)
- Social worker: social isolation, family support, financial concerns
- Occupational therapist: home assessment and modifications; aids and equipment

## Telehealth Suitability

Frailty monitoring is partially suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- CFS scoring from clinical history
- Medication review and deprescribing planning
- Social support assessment
- Nutritional counselling and supplement prescribing
- Advance care planning discussion
- My Aged Care referral coordination
- Exercise counselling and monitoring

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Physical performance tests (TUG, grip strength, gait speed)
- Physical examination
- Cognitive assessment (MoCA/MMSE)
- Home assessment — OT in-person visit
- Blood tests — pathology arranged
