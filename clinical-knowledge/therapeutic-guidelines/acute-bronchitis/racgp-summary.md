## Acute Bronchitis Assessment (RACGP)

Acute bronchitis is an acute inflammation of the tracheobronchial tree, characterised by cough lasting up to 3 weeks, typically following a viral upper respiratory tract infection. It is one of the most common presentations in Australian general practice and one of the most common reasons for inappropriate antibiotic prescribing. In otherwise healthy adults, acute bronchitis is caused by viruses in >90% of cases — antibiotics are not indicated and provide no clinically meaningful benefit while contributing to antimicrobial resistance. Key clinical tasks are differentiating acute bronchitis from pneumonia (which requires antibiotics) and reassuring patients with evidence-based information. Telehealth is highly suitable for acute bronchitis assessment and management.

## Clinical Presentation

- Cough: central feature; initially dry, may become productive (mucopurulent sputum does NOT indicate bacterial infection — sputum colour correlates poorly with bacterial cause in acute bronchitis)
- Duration: typically 10–14 days; range 3–4 weeks; cough lasting >3 weeks → consider other diagnoses (post-infectious cough, pertussis, asthma, GORD, ACE inhibitor cough)
- Preceded by URTI: sore throat, nasal congestion, rhinorrhoea — often still present
- Low-grade fever: present in minority; high fever or persistent fever after 3–4 days raises concern for pneumonia
- Chest tightness, wheezing: bronchospasm common — increased airway reactivity; does not necessarily indicate asthma
- Haemoptysis: uncommon; if present — consider pneumonia, lung cancer, PE

**Signs suggesting pneumonia rather than bronchitis:**
- High fever (>38.5°C persisting >3–4 days)
- Respiratory rate ≥30/min
- SpO2 <93%
- Signs of consolidation on examination (dullness, bronchial breathing)
- Systemic toxicity (elderly, immunocompromised)

## Assessment Approach

**History:**
- Duration of cough, character (dry vs productive), sputum colour (note: colour is not reliable indicator of bacterial vs viral)
- Presence of fever and its degree
- Associated URTI symptoms (nasal, throat — viral aetiology more likely)
- Dyspnoea: if present, assess severity; SpO2 if available
- Smoking history: chronic bronchitis may be underlying
- Asthma history: bronchospasm component may be significant
- High-risk features: elderly, immunocompromised, underlying chronic lung disease (COPD, bronchiectasis), cardiac disease, pregnancy

**Examination (where in-person):**
- Vital signs: temperature, respiratory rate, SpO2
- Auscultation: bilateral wheeze common in acute bronchitis; localised crackles or bronchial breathing suggest pneumonia
- Throat: pharyngeal erythema from coexisting URTI

**Investigations:**
- Not routinely required for typical acute bronchitis in otherwise healthy adults
- SpO2: if dyspnoea or severity concern; <93% = hospital review
- CXR: only if pneumonia suspected clinically (fever, consolidation signs, severe systemic illness, SpO2 low)
- CRP point-of-care: NPS evidence: CRP <20 mg/L = unlikely bacterial pneumonia; CRP 20–100 = borderline; CRP >100 = likely bacterial; aids antibiotic prescribing decision in uncertain cases
- Pertussis PCR: if cough >2–3 weeks, severe paroxysmal cough, adolescent/adult in household with infants — notifiable disease

## Key Differentials

- Community-acquired pneumonia: fever, consolidation signs, SpO2 low, severe systemic illness; requires antibiotics; see `community-acquired-pneumonia` entry
- Pertussis (whooping cough): increasingly common in adults; paroxysmal cough, post-tussive vomiting, inspiratory whoop (may be absent in adults); PCR confirms; treat with azithromycin; notify; see `whooping-cough` entry
- Asthma: cough ± wheeze; post-viral exacerbation common; assess for underlying asthma diagnosis; spirometry; see `asthma` entry
- GORD-related cough: chronic persistent cough, worse at night or post-prandial; improve with PPI; see `gord` entry
- ACE inhibitor cough: dry persistent cough in patient on ACE inhibitor (lisinopril, enalapril, perindopril, ramipril); switch to ARB resolves cough
- Post-infectious cough: benign cough persisting >3 weeks after resolution of acute illness; reassurance; may use inhaled corticosteroid short course
- Lung cancer: particularly smokers >50 years with new persistent cough, haemoptysis, weight loss; CXR

## Management Principles

**Antibiotics — not indicated in otherwise healthy adults with uncomplicated acute bronchitis:**
- Evidence: antibiotics reduce symptom duration by only ~0.5 days while causing adverse effects (GI side effects, allergic reactions, C. difficile risk) and contributing to antimicrobial resistance
- RACGP and NPS guidelines: defer antibiotics in otherwise healthy adults with acute bronchitis
- If patient expects antibiotics: evidence-based communication; explain viral cause; delayed prescribing approach (prescribe but advise not to fill unless worse after 5 days) reduces actual antibiotic use

**High-risk populations where antibiotic threshold is lower:**
- Elderly (≥75) and frail: lower threshold; clinical judgement; amoxicillin or doxycycline
- Severe COPD with purulent exacerbation: may have bacterial superinfection; see `copd` entry
- Immunocompromised patients: lower threshold for antibiotics
- If pertussis confirmed or suspected in contact with vulnerable individuals: azithromycin (reduces transmission even if patient past peak infectivity)

**Symptomatic management:**
- Paracetamol or ibuprofen: for fever and discomfort
- Adequate hydration: thins secretions
- Honey: evidence for symptom relief (especially in children); 1–2 teaspoons can be recommended (not for children <1 year — botulism risk)
- Steam inhalation, menthol: symptom relief only; no evidence for cure
- Cough suppressants (codeine, pholcodine): minimal evidence; not routinely recommended; codeine abuse potential; avoid in children
- Salbutamol inhaler: for significant bronchospasm/wheeze component; short course MDI; useful in post-viral reactive airways

**Patient education and safety netting:**
- Explain self-limiting viral illness; typical duration (10–21 days)
- Return if: fever >38.5°C persisting >5 days, SpO2 drops, breathlessness at rest worsens, haemoptysis, no improvement at 3 weeks

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Salbutamol** (Ventolin MDI — PBS unrestricted — for acute wheeze/bronchospasm component of acute bronchitis; short course 2–4 puffs 3–4 times daily via spacer)
- **Paracetamol / ibuprofen** (PBS unrestricted — symptomatic)
- **Amoxicillin** (PBS restricted — lower respiratory infections; only if bacterial pneumonia or secondary bacterial infection — not routine bronchitis)
- **Doxycycline** (PBS unrestricted — respiratory infections; for pertussis or in penicillin allergy with bacterial superinfection concern)
- **Azithromycin** (PBS restricted — pertussis treatment; 500 mg day 1, then 250 mg days 2–5)
- **Note:** Antibiotic prescribing for uncomplicated acute bronchitis in otherwise healthy adults is not supported by evidence and should be actively avoided as a contribution to antibiotic stewardship

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- SpO2 <93%, severe dyspnoea at rest, cyanosis
- CURB-65 ≥2 features suggesting pneumonia: altered consciousness, urea >7, RR ≥30, BP <90/60, age ≥65 — hospital assessment
- Haemoptysis with systemic features

**Urgent in-person assessment (same day):**
- Clinical features suggesting pneumonia: high fever persisting, crackles/bronchial breathing on auscultation, significant dyspnoea
- High-risk patients (elderly, immunocompromised, severe COPD) with worsening symptoms
- Suspicion of pertussis with infant household contact — notification and treatment

**Non-urgent follow-up:**
- Persistent cough >3–4 weeks: reassessment for post-infectious cough, underlying asthma, GORD, pertussis, ACE inhibitor cough
- Recurrent "bronchitis": underlying COPD or asthma may need spirometry diagnosis

## Telehealth Suitability

Acute bronchitis assessment and management is highly suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- History-based clinical diagnosis of uncomplicated acute bronchitis
- Antipyretic and symptomatic management advice
- Antibiotic stewardship counselling — reassurance of viral aetiology
- Delayed prescribing approach if patient insistent
- Safety netting instructions for worsening features
- Salbutamol prescribing for significant wheeze

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- SpO2 measurement if dyspnoea present (patient-owned oximeter is useful alternative)
- Auscultation if pneumonia suspected
- CXR if clinically indicated — radiology referral
