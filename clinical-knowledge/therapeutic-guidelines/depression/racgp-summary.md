## Depression Assessment (RACGP)

Depression (major depressive disorder and related depressive conditions) is the most prevalent mental health condition in Australia, affecting approximately 1 in 7 Australians in their lifetime. Depression is a leading cause of disability and is strongly associated with reduced quality of life, impaired relationships, loss of employment, physical health comorbidities, and suicide. GPs diagnose and manage the large majority of depression in Australia, including initiating and monitoring pharmacotherapy, creating GP Mental Health Care Plans (Medicare Item 2713), and coordinating psychology referral. Telehealth is highly suitable for depression assessment, treatment initiation, and ongoing management.

## Clinical Presentation

**DSM-5 Major Depressive Disorder criteria:** Depressed mood or anhedonia (loss of interest/pleasure) for ≥2 weeks, plus ≥4 additional symptoms:
- Significant weight change or appetite disturbance
- Insomnia or hypersomnia
- Psychomotor agitation or retardation
- Fatigue or loss of energy (almost every day)
- Feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt
- Diminished concentration or indecisiveness
- Recurrent thoughts of death or suicidal ideation

**Subtypes:**
- Melancholic depression: severe; anhedonia; diurnal variation (worse in morning); weight loss; psychomotor changes; responds well to antidepressants
- Atypical depression: mood reactivity (lifts in response to positive events); hypersomnia; leaden paralysis; interpersonal rejection sensitivity; monoamine oxidase inhibitors (MAOIs) historically preferred; SSRIs effective
- Persistent depressive disorder (dysthymia): chronic, milder symptoms for ≥2 years; often more resistant to treatment
- Postnatal depression: onset within 12 months of birth; see `postnatal-depression` entry
- Seasonal affective disorder (SAD): depressive episodes in winter; light therapy ± SSRI
- Psychotic depression: delusions or hallucinations with severe depression; requires antipsychotic + antidepressant; specialist input

**Common presentations in GP:**
- Somatic complaints: headache, fatigue, chronic pain, insomnia without explanation
- Unexplained medical symptom burden
- Relationship or work difficulties
- Alcohol or substance use increase
- Withdrawal from social activities

## Assessment Approach

**Validated screening tools:**
- PHQ-9 (Patient Health Questionnaire — 9 items): score 0–27; 0–4 minimal; 5–9 mild; 10–14 moderate; 15–19 moderately severe; 20–27 severe
- PHQ-2 (first 2 items of PHQ-9): rapid 2-item screen; ≥3 = positive screen, proceed to full PHQ-9
- DASS-21: depression, anxiety, and stress; widely used in Australian practice

**Comprehensive mental health assessment:**
- Onset, duration, precipitants, previous episodes and treatment response
- Functional impact: work, relationships, self-care, daily activities
- Suicidal ideation assessment (mandatory at every assessment):
  - Passive: "I wish I were dead" vs Active: plan, intent, means, timeframe
  - Protective factors: reasons for living, children, social support
  - Risk factors: previous attempts, substance use, hopelessness, isolation, access to means
- Medications: numerous drugs cause depressive symptoms (corticosteroids, beta-blockers, isotretinoin, certain antihypertensives)
- Substance use: alcohol and drug use screening (AUDIT, DAST)
- Medical causes to exclude: hypothyroidism (TSH), anaemia (FBC), B12/folate deficiency, hypercalcaemia, Cushing's syndrome, Parkinson's disease

**Bipolar disorder screening (critical before prescribing antidepressants):**
- Family history of bipolar disorder
- Prior manic or hypomanic episodes (elevated mood, decreased need for sleep, pressured speech, grandiosity, risky behaviour)
- Antidepressant-induced hypomania or rapid cycling
- MDQ (Mood Disorder Questionnaire) — screening tool; positive screen → psychiatry before antidepressant initiation

## Key Differentials

- Bipolar disorder — exclude before SSRI initiation; antidepressants alone can trigger mania; PHQ-9 + MDQ screening; psychiatry referral if suspected
- Hypothyroidism — fatigue, cognitive slowing, low mood; check TSH
- Grief / bereavement — normal grief response; does not require antidepressants unless persistent and impairs function (see `grief-bereavement` entry)
- Adjustment disorder — depressive symptoms in response to identifiable stressor; resolve within 6 months of stressor ending; psychological support; antidepressants generally not indicated
- Substance-induced depression — alcohol, benzodiazepines, stimulant withdrawal; substance use assessment; treat underlying substance use
- Medical illness: chronic pain, cancer, cardiac disease — depression is a common comorbidity; treat concurrently
- Anxiety disorder — frequently coexists with depression; PHQ-9 + GAD-7

## Management Principles

**Psychoeducation and supportive care:**
- Explain depression as a medical illness: biological, psychological, and social factors; reduces stigma; helps patients engage with treatment
- Regular GP contact: therapeutic alliance is itself helpful; schedule regular check-ins

**Psychological therapies (first-line for mild-moderate depression, concurrent with pharmacotherapy for severe):**
- Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT): most evidence-based; addresses negative thinking patterns and behaviours; 8–12 sessions; Medicare-rebatable via GP Mental Health Care Plan (up to 10 sessions/year; extended to 20 for complex cases under mental health review criteria)
- Behavioural Activation: schedules rewarding activities to counter withdrawal; simpler than full CBT; GP can facilitate basic components
- Interpersonal Therapy (IPT): addresses relationships and role transitions; comparable to CBT
- Digital programs: Mindspot, This Way Up, MoodGym — effective for mild-moderate depression; accessible; reduce wait times for face-to-face therapy

**GP Mental Health Care Plan (MBS Item 2713):**
- Allows referral to psychologist with Medicare rebate (up to 10 sessions/year)
- Requires documented assessment, mental health history, treatment goals, and GP management component
- Annual review required for additional sessions

**Pharmacotherapy (moderate-severe depression, or mild-moderate unresponsive to psychological therapy):**

First-line — SSRIs:
- Escitalopram 5–20 mg daily: preferred (fewer drug interactions, well tolerated); start 5 mg for 1–2 weeks to reduce activation
- Sertraline 25–200 mg daily: excellent evidence base; safe in most comorbidities; preferred in cardiac disease
- Fluoxetine 20–60 mg daily: long half-life (less discontinuation syndrome); more activating; useful in atypical/psychomotor-retarded depression
- Time to effect: 4–6 weeks for full effect; do not switch or add unless adequate dose trial (6–8 weeks)

SNRIs:
- Venlafaxine XR 37.5–225 mg daily: SNRI; effective; BP monitoring at higher doses; discontinuation syndrome common
- Duloxetine 30–120 mg daily: SNRI; also for chronic pain comorbidity

Other agents:
- Mirtazapine 15–45 mg nocte: NaSSA; sedating (useful if insomnia prominent); weight gain common; useful in elderly, post-surgical, with appetite/nausea issues
- Bupropion 150 mg SR twice daily: NDRI; activating; useful for anhedonia and fatigue; safe in cardiac disease; helpful in nicotine dependence; avoid in seizure disorder, eating disorders
- Agomelatine: melatonin agonist; minimal sexual side effects; limited PBS listing

**Duration of antidepressant treatment:**
- First episode: continue 6–12 months after remission (reduces relapse risk)
- Second episode: continue 2 years after remission
- Three or more episodes: consider indefinite maintenance
- Taper slowly over months when ceasing (discontinuation syndrome risk — especially venlafaxine, paroxetine)

**Monitoring after initiation:**
- Review at 1–2 weeks (especially first episode, younger patient — risk of increased suicidality in early treatment with SSRIs in <25 years)
- PHQ-9 at each review — track response
- Assess side effects: sexual dysfunction (common — 30–40%), GI, insomnia, activation
- Blood tests: no routine monitoring for SSRIs; check Na+ in elderly (hyponatraemia risk), LFTs if hepatic comorbidity

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Escitalopram** (Lexapro, generic — PBS restricted: major depressive disorder; 5–20 mg daily; preferred first-line SSRI)
- **Sertraline** (PBS restricted: MDD, panic disorder, social anxiety disorder; 25–200 mg daily)
- **Fluoxetine** (Prozac, generic — PBS restricted: MDD, obsessive-compulsive disorder; 20–60 mg; long half-life)
- **Venlafaxine XR** (PBS restricted: MDD, GAD, social anxiety disorder; 37.5–225 mg daily; taper slowly on cessation)
- **Mirtazapine** (PBS restricted: MDD; 15–45 mg nocte; sedating; weight gain)
- **Bupropion SR** (PBS restricted: smoking cessation [Zyban]; off-label for depression — private script if used for MDD only)
- **Note:** Antidepressants are not indicated for mild depression responding to psychological therapy; SSRIs carry a black box warning for increased suicidality in patients under 25 — monitor closely in first weeks; never prescribe MAOIs without specialist guidance

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Active suicidal ideation with plan, intent, or access to means — mental health emergency
- Severe depression with psychotic features (delusions, hallucinations) — psychiatric emergency
- Severe self-harm, refusal to eat/drink

**Urgent same-day mental health assessment:**
- Passive suicidal ideation with increasing severity or hopelessness
- Significant functional incapacity — unable to care for self or dependants
- Suspected bipolar disorder with depressive episode — psychiatric assessment before pharmacotherapy

**Non-urgent specialist referral:**
- Psychiatrist: failure of 2 adequate SSRI/SNRI trials, bipolar disorder, psychotic depression, complex comorbidities, ECT consideration
- Psychologist: CBT via GP Mental Health Care Plan
- Headspace (youth 12–25 years): free mental health service

## Telehealth Suitability

Depression management is highly suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- PHQ-9 screening and scoring (patient can complete before consultation)
- Comprehensive mental health assessment including suicidal ideation (document suicide risk assessment)
- SSRI/SNRI initiation, dose titration, and monitoring
- GP Mental Health Care Plan creation and psychology referral (MBS Item 2713)
- Psychoeducation and digital therapy referral (Mindspot, This Way Up)
- Safety planning with patient (if suicidal ideation present but low immediate risk)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Investigations to exclude medical cause (TSH, FBC, B12 — pathology arranged)
- High-risk presentations where safety assessment requires in-person engagement
- Mental health emergency — in-person or 000
