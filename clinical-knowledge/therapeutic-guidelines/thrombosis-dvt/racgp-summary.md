## DVT/VTE Assessment (RACGP)

Venous thromboembolism (VTE) encompasses deep vein thrombosis (DVT) and pulmonary embolism (PE). DVT most commonly affects the deep veins of the lower limb (calf, popliteal, femoral, iliac), and proximal DVT (popliteal or above) carries substantial risk of PE. Australia has an annual VTE incidence of approximately 1–2 per 1000 persons, with PE accounting for up to 15,000 hospital admissions per year. VTE risk is highest in the postoperative period, during immobility, with cancer, and in pregnancy/postpartum. Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) have transformed VTE management — apixaban and rivaroxaban require no injectable lead-in for DVT/PE treatment, simplifying outpatient management. The GP plays a central role in: initial diagnosis and risk stratification, initiating or supervising anticoagulation, monitoring for treatment complications, and managing extended secondary prevention. Telehealth is suitable for stable VTE monitoring.

## Clinical Presentation

**DVT symptoms:**
- Unilateral leg swelling (most specific feature)
- Calf or thigh pain (may be minimal)
- Erythema, warmth along the course of thrombosed vein
- Superficial venous distension
- Symptoms may be absent — up to 50% of proximal DVTs are asymptomatic

**Pulmonary embolism symptoms (range from minimal to life-threatening):**
- Sudden dyspnoea (most common presenting symptom)
- Pleuritic chest pain (sharp, worse with breathing)
- Haemoptysis
- Tachycardia, tachypnoea
- Syncope or pre-syncope (massive PE — haemodynamically significant)
- Massive PE: hypotension, RV failure, cardiac arrest — 000 immediately

**DVT risk factors (Virchow's triad):**
- Venous stasis: immobility, long-haul travel, hospitalisation, CCF, obesity
- Hypercoagulability: cancer, thrombophilia, pregnancy/postpartum, combined OCP, HRT, antiphospholipid syndrome
- Endothelial injury: surgery, trauma, IV lines

**Provoked vs unprovoked VTE:**
- Provoked: associated with identifiable transient risk factor (major surgery, trauma, hospitalisation, pregnancy, combined OCP) — lower recurrence risk after 3 months anticoagulation
- Unprovoked: no identifiable risk factor — higher recurrence risk; longer or indefinite anticoagulation considered

## Assessment Approach

**Clinical pre-test probability (Wells score for DVT):**
- Active cancer (1), paralysis/paresis/immobilisation (1), bedridden >3 days or surgery within 12 weeks (1), localised tenderness along deep vein (1), entire leg swollen (1), calf swelling ≥3 cm compared to asymptomatic leg (1), pitting oedema (1), dilated superficial veins (1), previously documented DVT (1), alternative diagnosis as or more likely than DVT (-2)
- Score ≥2: high probability; score 0–1: low probability

**D-dimer:**
- High sensitivity, low specificity; use with Wells score
- Low pre-test probability + negative D-dimer: DVT effectively excluded (NPV >99%)
- Elevated D-dimer: requires imaging regardless of pre-test probability
- D-dimer loses utility in pregnancy, post-surgery, cancer, inflammatory states — near-universally elevated

**Compression ultrasound (CUS):**
- First-line imaging for suspected DVT; sensitive and specific for proximal DVT; less sensitive for isolated calf DVT
- If negative CUS and high pre-test probability: repeat CUS in 5–7 days OR proceed to venography
- Whole-leg CUS: detects distal (calf) DVT; preferred if available

**PE investigation:**
- Wells PE score: clinical probability assessment
- CT pulmonary angiography (CTPA): gold standard for PE diagnosis; sensitivity >95%
- V/Q scan: alternative to CTPA (preferred in renal impairment, contrast allergy, pregnancy — radiation dose considerations)
- ECG: sinus tachycardia most common; S1Q3T3 (rare, classic but non-specific); right heart strain
- ABG: hypoxaemia, hypocapnia, respiratory alkalosis
- BNP/NT-proBNP: RV strain marker; prognostic in massive PE

**Thrombophilia testing:**
- NOT recommended acutely (anticoagulation affects most assay results)
- Consider after completing anticoagulation in: unprovoked VTE, recurrent VTE, VTE at unusual site, strong family history of VTE, young patient (<50)
- Tests: protein C, protein S, antithrombin III, factor V Leiden, prothrombin G20210A mutation, antiphospholipid antibodies (lupus anticoagulant, anticardiolipin, anti-β2 glycoprotein)

**Cancer screening post-unprovoked VTE:**
- Unprovoked VTE in patients >40 years: consider limited cancer screening — FBC, LFTs, urinalysis, CXR, age-appropriate cancer screening (PSA, faecal immunochemical test); avoid extensive CT-of-everything searches (low yield, harm from false positives)

## Key Differentials

- Musculoskeletal calf strain: localised muscle tenderness; trauma history; Wells score low; normal D-dimer and CUS
- Cellulitis: diffuse erythema, warmth, fever, leukocytosis; can coexist with DVT
- Ruptured Baker's cyst: fluid from behind knee tracking down calf; ultrasound distinguishes
- Chronic venous insufficiency: bilateral swelling; chronic, not sudden onset; varicosities
- Lymphoedema: pitting oedema; often bilateral; post-surgery or malignancy history
- PE vs pleuritis (viral or bacterial): pleuritic chest pain; CXR, CTPA to distinguish
- Anxiety/hyperventilation: dyspnoea but no hypoxaemia; normal CTPA
- ACS: chest pain, ECG changes, troponin elevation; CTPA vs ACS workup based on predominant features

## Management Principles

**DVT/PE treatment — DOAC first-line (outpatient for most DVT and low-risk PE):**

Apixaban (most widely used for VTE in Australia — no lead-in heparin required):
- DVT/PE treatment: 10 mg twice daily for 7 days, then 5 mg twice daily
- Lowest bleeding risk of DOACs (AMPLIFY trial); preferred in elderly, high bleeding risk
- Extended prevention: 2.5 mg twice daily after initial 6 months (AMPLIFY-EXT)

Rivaroxaban (no lead-in heparin required):
- DVT: 15 mg twice daily for 3 weeks with food, then 20 mg once daily
- PE: same regimen as DVT; once-daily maintenance advantageous for adherence

Dabigatran:
- Requires parenteral lead-in (LMWH or UFH for ≥5 days) before oral therapy
- 150 mg twice daily after lead-in; more renally cleared; idarucizumab reversal

Warfarin:
- Requires LMWH bridge until INR therapeutic (2.0–3.0) for ≥2 days; INR monitoring ongoing
- Now second-line for VTE; reserved for: mechanical heart valves, severe renal impairment, pregnancy (after first trimester — heparin only in first trimester), antiphospholipid syndrome (warfarin superior to DOACs in APS for VTE)

LMWH (enoxaparin — Clexane):
- Preferred for: cancer-associated VTE (LMWH historically gold standard — LMWHs may be inferior to DOACs in GI and GU cancer), pregnancy (DOACs contraindicated), severe renal impairment
- Cancer VTE: apixaban and rivaroxaban increasingly used in cancer VTE (SELECT-D, HOKUSAI-Cancer trials) — discuss with haematologist or oncologist; avoid in high GI bleeding risk cancers
- 1 mg/kg SC twice daily (therapeutic dose for VTE); dose-adjust at eGFR <30

**Duration of anticoagulation:**
- Provoked VTE (major transient risk factor): 3 months — then assess recurrence risk
- Unprovoked DVT/PE (first episode): minimum 3 months; consider extended treatment (indefinite at reduced dose or full dose) based on bleeding risk, patient preference, recurrence risk, sex (men have higher unprovoked recurrence than women)
- Cancer-associated VTE: treat for duration of active cancer
- Recurrent unprovoked VTE: indefinite anticoagulation

**Outpatient DVT treatment (most proximal and calf DVTs):**
- Most patients with DVT and low-risk PE can be managed outpatient with DOAC
- Hestia criteria (for PE): if any positive — hospital admission (BP <100, need supplemental O2, high bleeding risk, thrombolysis needed, renal/liver impairment, pregnant, PE on anticoagulation, severe pain, social reason)
- Provide patient education: symptoms of PE, bleeding precautions, drug interactions

**Compression stockings:**
- Previously recommended to prevent post-thrombotic syndrome (PTS) — SOX trial showed NO benefit; NOT routinely recommended post-DVT; use only if leg swelling/pain causing symptoms

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Apixaban (Eliquis)** (PBS restricted: DVT/PE treatment and secondary prevention; 10 mg BD × 7 days then 5 mg BD; no lead-in; preferred for most patients)
- **Rivaroxaban (Xarelto)** (PBS restricted: DVT/PE; 15 mg BD × 3 weeks then 20 mg daily with food; no lead-in)
- **Dabigatran (Pradaxa)** (PBS restricted: DVT/PE after parenteral lead-in; 150 mg BD; most renally eliminated)
- **Enoxaparin (Clexane)** (PBS restricted: DVT/PE treatment; 1 mg/kg SC BD; adjust at eGFR <30; preferred in pregnancy and cancer VTE)
- **Warfarin** (PBS unrestricted: DVT/PE; requires LMWH bridge and INR monitoring; second-line for most indications)
- **Note:** DOACs are contraindicated in pregnancy (use LMWH throughout); DOACs require adequate renal function — check eGFR before prescribing

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Massive PE: haemodynamic instability (hypotension, shock, syncope, cardiac arrest) — IV thrombolysis or surgical embolectomy; 000
- DVT with PE and haemodynamic compromise
- Phlegmasia cerulea dolens: massive DVT with limb ischaemia — surgical emergency

**Urgent same-day hospital assessment:**
- Suspected PE (any severity) — CTPA required; hospital assessment
- DVT in pregnancy — inpatient LMWH initiation preferred
- DVT in cancer patient — haematology/oncology input on anticoagulant choice
- Proximal DVT with bilateral swelling or suspected IVC/iliac thrombosis
- DVT with therapeutic anticoagulation failure — haematology

**Non-urgent follow-up:**
- 3-month review: duration of anticoagulation decision; recurrence risk assessment; thrombophilia testing if appropriate
- Haematology: thrombophilia workup, antiphospholipid syndrome, recurrent VTE, cancer-associated VTE management
- Vascular surgery: post-thrombotic syndrome causing severe chronic venous insufficiency

## Telehealth Suitability

DVT/VTE follow-up monitoring is highly suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Anticoagulation duration review and extension prescribing
- Blood result interpretation (eGFR, FBC, INR if on warfarin)
- Medication adherence counselling
- Recurrence risk assessment and education
- Thrombophilia result discussion (after appropriate testing)
- Driving and work clearance advice

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Suspected acute DVT — ultrasound referral and clinical assessment
- Suspected acute PE — 000 or urgent ED
- Bleeding complication assessment
- INR testing (pathology collection)
- Compression ultrasound (radiology referral)
