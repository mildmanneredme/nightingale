## Osteoarthritis Assessment (RACGP)

Osteoarthritis (OA) is the most common joint disease, affecting approximately 2.2 million Australians. It is a chronic, progressive condition involving cartilage degradation, subchondral bone changes, osteophyte formation, and synovial inflammation. The knees and hips are most commonly affected. OA causes significant pain, functional impairment, and disability. Management is primarily non-pharmacological; pharmacotherapy provides symptomatic relief only. The GP plays a central role in OA diagnosis, pain management, weight management, and surgical referral when conservative measures fail. Telehealth is suitable for OA management reviews and medication prescribing.

## Clinical Presentation

**Characteristic features:**
- Joint pain: mechanical — worsened by activity, relieved by rest (early disease); later becomes persistent at rest and nocturnal
- Stiffness: brief morning stiffness (<30 minutes — contrast with inflammatory arthritis where stiffness is >1 hour); "gelling" after inactivity
- Joint tenderness, crepitus, bony swelling (osteophytes)
- Reduced range of motion: hip (internal rotation often first); knee (extension)
- No systemic features: no fever, no systemic inflammation

**Common sites:**
- Knee OA: medial compartment most common; varus deformity; pain on stair use; difficulty rising from chair
- Hip OA: groin and anterior thigh pain; pain in buttock or lateral hip (can mimic trochanteric bursitis); reduced internal rotation
- Hand OA: DIP joints (Heberden's nodes), PIP joints (Bouchard's nodes), first CMC joint (thumb base — "base of thumb OA"); functional impact on grip, pinch, writing

**Risk factors:** Age (strongest), obesity, previous joint injury, female sex, occupational repetitive joint loading, family history

## Assessment Approach

**Diagnosis is clinical in typical presentations** — history + examination often sufficient; X-ray confirms but severity on X-ray correlates poorly with symptoms.

**Key examination findings:**
- Joint effusion (knee): patellar tap, bulge sign
- ROM: active and passive — end-range pain ± crepitus
- Alignment: varus (bow-legged) vs valgus (knock-knee) in knee OA
- Muscle wasting: quadriceps wasting with knee OA (disuse atrophy)
- Gait analysis: antalgic gait

**Investigations:**
- X-ray (AP and lateral views): joint space narrowing, osteophytes, subchondral sclerosis, subchondral cysts — confirms diagnosis; perform standing weight-bearing views for knee
- Blood tests (to exclude inflammatory arthritis): ESR, CRP, RF (rheumatoid factor), anti-CCP — these should be normal in OA; if elevated → rheumatology referral
- Urate: if gout in differential
- MRI: not routinely required for OA; reserved for meniscal pathology, surgical planning

## Key Differentials

- Rheumatoid arthritis: morning stiffness >60 minutes, symmetric small joint involvement, RF/anti-CCP positive; elevated CRP/ESR; see `rheumatoid-arthritis` entry
- Gout: acute hot swollen joint; hyperuricaemia; see `gout` entry
- Pseudogout (CPPD): calcium pyrophosphate crystals; similar to gout; aspiration distinguishes
- Trochanteric bursitis: lateral hip pain; point tenderness at greater trochanter; NOT groin pain; distinguished from hip OA
- Patellofemoral pain syndrome: anterior knee pain; young patient; no OA features on X-ray
- Meniscal tear: knee locking, pain on specific movements; MRI; younger patient or trauma history
- Malignancy: bone pain from primary or metastatic cancer — red flags: night pain, weight loss, age considerations

## Management Principles

**Non-pharmacological (first-line and most evidence-based):**

Exercise (strong evidence — most important treatment):
- Land-based exercise: strengthening exercises (quadriceps for knee OA, hip abductor/external rotator for hip OA), aerobic exercise; reduces pain and improves function
- Water-based exercise: hydrotherapy — excellent for those with difficulty exercising on land; pain reduction; widely available in Australia
- Exercise physiologist referral: structured exercise program; strong evidence; GP referral appropriate; chronic disease management plan for eligible patients (Medicare subsidised allied health)
- Physiotherapy: education, exercise supervision, taping (knee OA), manual therapy adjunct

Weight management:
- Even modest weight loss (5–10%) significantly reduces knee OA pain — every 1 kg of weight loss reduces knee joint load by 4 kg
- Dietitian referral: structured weight management; see `obesity` entry for pharmacotherapy options

Patient education and self-management:
- OA is not caused by "wearing away" cartilage from exercise — graded exercise is safe and beneficial; important education point as many patients reduce activity
- Arthritis Australia resources; community exercise programs

Assistive devices:
- Walking stick (contralateral hand to affected hip or knee)
- Orthotics: heel wedge for medial compartment knee OA; thumb splints for first CMC OA
- Bracing: unloader brace for unicompartmental knee OA

Heat and cold therapy: symptomatic relief; patient preference.

**Pharmacotherapy (adjunct to non-pharmacological measures):**

Topical (preferred initial pharmacotherapy for knee and hand OA — effective, fewer systemic side effects):
- Topical diclofenac gel (Voltaren Emulgel): highly effective for knee and hand OA; PBS-restricted; apply 3–4 times daily to affected area; minimal systemic absorption
- Topical NSAIDs preferred over oral in elderly and those with GI, cardiovascular, or renal risk

Oral NSAIDs:
- Ibuprofen, naproxen, celecoxib — effective for pain relief; significant side effect profile; limit to lowest effective dose for shortest duration
- GI protection: PPI co-prescription for all patients on regular oral NSAIDs
- Avoid in: elderly (>75), CKD (eGFR <60), heart failure, anticoagulant use; celecoxib safer for GI in high-risk patients

Paracetamol:
- Evidence for OA is more modest than previously believed; still appropriate as adjunct; 1 g four times daily maximum; reduce in liver disease; safe profile

Duloxetine: 30–60 mg daily; SNRI; evidence for knee OA pain; particularly useful if central sensitisation or concurrent depression; PBS-restricted (depression/anxiety indication — off-label for OA pain)

Corticosteroid injections (intra-articular):
- Triamcinolone (Depo-Medrol) or methylprednisolone intra-articular: short-term pain relief (6–12 weeks); useful for acute flares; performed in-person (GP procedural training or orthopaedics); not repeated more than 3–4 times/year (risk of cartilage damage with frequent injections)

Hyaluronic acid injection: limited evidence; more expensive; not PBS-listed; some orthopaedic surgeons offer for selected patients.

**Opioids:** Generally NOT recommended for OA; modest benefit; significant adverse effects; dependence risk; if used, limit to short-term in very severe pain awaiting surgery.

**Surgical referral (when conservative management fails):**
- Total knee replacement (TKR) or total hip replacement (THR): highly effective for severe OA; reduces pain, improves function; appropriate when: X-ray confirms severe OA, significant functional impairment affecting quality of life, conservative measures failed ≥6 months
- Arthroscopic surgery: NOT effective for OA (not recommended by RACGP/Arthritis Australia); debridement and lavage does not improve outcomes over placebo

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Topical diclofenac** (Voltaren Emulgel 1%, 2.32% — PBS restricted: knee and hand osteoarthritis; 3–4 applications daily; first-line pharmacotherapy)
- **Celecoxib** (PBS restricted: osteoarthritis; 100–200 mg twice daily; preferred oral NSAID in high GI risk; cardiovascular caution)
- **Ibuprofen** (PBS unrestricted — OA; 400 mg three times daily; add PPI; limit duration)
- **Naproxen** (PBS unrestricted — OA; 500 mg twice daily; longer-acting; add PPI)
- **Paracetamol** (PBS unrestricted — adjunct; 1 g four times daily maximum)
- **Triamcinolone** (Kenacort — PBS restricted: intra-articular injection; for inflammatory flare or severe pain; requires in-person procedural administration)

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent assessment:**
- Acute hot swollen joint — septic arthritis must be excluded (emergency); in-person assessment and possible aspiration
- Rapid deterioration in OA with systemic features — malignancy or inflammatory arthritis

**Non-urgent specialist referral:**
- Orthopaedic surgery: severe OA with functional impairment failing conservative management → surgical assessment for TKR/THR; refer earlier rather than later to avoid long waiting lists
- Rheumatology: diagnostic uncertainty, inflammatory arthritis features, rapid joint deterioration
- Chronic disease management plan allied health (Medicare): exercise physiologist, physiotherapist, dietitian — all available under Enhanced Primary Care plan for OA patients with eligible chronic disease criteria

## Telehealth Suitability

Osteoarthritis management is well suited to telehealth for established patients.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Symptom review and NSAID/paracetamol prescribing
- Exercise prescription and referral coordination
- Weight management counselling and dietitian referral
- Chronic disease management plan review and allied health referral
- Surgical referral for severe cases
- Medication review (NSAID safety in elderly)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial clinical examination (ROM, effusion, alignment assessment)
- X-ray ordering and review (weight-bearing views)
- Intra-articular corticosteroid injection — in-person procedural
