## Grief and Bereavement Assessment (RACGP)

Grief is the natural emotional, cognitive, behavioural, social, and physical response to loss — most commonly the death of a loved one, but also loss of relationship, role, health, function, or identity. Bereavement refers specifically to the state of having experienced a significant loss. GPs are uniquely positioned to support bereaved patients through ongoing therapeutic relationships. Most grief, while deeply painful, follows an adaptive course and does not require psychiatric treatment. The GP's role is to: provide compassionate support and validation, assess for complicated grief or prolonged grief disorder, identify concurrent depression or anxiety, provide psychoeducation, connect patients with support resources, and prescribe appropriately (sleep difficulties; depression if it develops). Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD — DSM-5-TR, ICD-11) is a distinct diagnostic category when intense grief persists beyond 12 months (6 months in ICD-11) and significantly impairs functioning. The Australian Centre for Grief and Bereavement (ACGB): grief.org.au. Telehealth is well-suited to grief support.

## Clinical Presentation

**Normal grief (adaptive mourning):**
- Emotional: sadness, yearning, longing for the deceased; waves of distress; anger; guilt; anxiety; relief (expected or prolonged illness)
- Cognitive: preoccupation with thoughts of the deceased; disbelief and confusion early; reviewing memories
- Physical: fatigue; sleep disturbance; appetite change; somatic symptoms (chest heaviness, feeling of emptiness)
- Behavioural: crying; social withdrawal; visiting places associated with the deceased; keeping mementos
- Social: isolation; role changes; financial or practical stressors (especially after loss of partner)
- Course: typically most intense in first weeks; gradually integrates over months to years; grief does not have a fixed timeline; anniversaries, holidays trigger intensification ("anniversary reactions")

**Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD — previously complicated grief):**
- DSM-5-TR criteria: after at least 12 months since death (6 months in ICD-11):
  - Intense yearning or longing for the deceased (on most days, to a disabling degree)
  - At least 3 of: identity disruption, disbelief about death, avoidance of reminders, intense emotional pain, difficulty reintegrating (relationships, activities), emotional numbness, meaninglessness, intense loneliness
  - Clinically significant distress or functional impairment
- Prevalence: approximately 10% of bereaved individuals
- Risk factors: sudden/traumatic death, suicide or violence, loss of child, prior attachment difficulties, limited social support, prior mental illness

**Special circumstances:**
- Traumatic or sudden death (accident, suicide, homicide): higher risk of PGD and PTSD; concurrent trauma processing needed; see `ptsd`
- Suicide bereavement: guilt, stigma, unanswered questions; specific support needed; Survivors of Suicide Loss (SOS) groups; SANE Australia (1800 187 263)
- Perinatal loss: miscarriage, stillbirth, neonatal death; often minimised socially; significant grief; SANDS Australia (Stillbirth and Neonatal Death Support): sands.org.au; pregnancy loss helpline
- Loss of child: parental grief; intense and prolonged; Compassionate Friends (Australia): compassionatefriends.org.au
- Anticipatory grief: before death occurs (terminal illness, dementia); validate and normalise

**Bereavement in older adults:**
- Widow/widower effect: significantly elevated mortality and morbidity in first year after spousal death
- Social isolation; loss of daily structure and meaning; increased alcohol use
- Assess: depression (overlaps with grief but treatable); suicide risk; physical health needs

## Assessment Approach

**Grief assessment:**
- Allow patient to tell their story: what happened; who died; nature of relationship; circumstances of death
- Grief symptom assessment: intensity of yearning/longing; intrusive thoughts; avoidance; ability to function (work, relationships, self-care)
- Timeline: when did death occur; has grief been gradually integrating or remaining static/worsening
- Support network: family; friends; community; religious or cultural support
- Coping mechanisms: healthy (talking, exercise, creative) vs unhealthy (alcohol, social withdrawal, self-neglect)
- Functional impact: work attendance; self-care; social participation

**Screening tools:**
- Brief Grief Questionnaire (BGQ): 5 items; screens for complicated/prolonged grief
- Inventory of Complicated Grief (ICG): 19 items; more comprehensive
- Prolonged Grief Disorder scale (PG-13): research and clinical setting
- PHQ-9: screen for comorbid depression (common in bereaved patients)
- AUDIT-C: screen for alcohol use (common coping mechanism in bereavement)

**Suicide risk assessment:**
- Passive SI: "I wish I could be with them" or "I would not mind dying" — explore; distinguish grief-related passive ideation from active intent
- Active SI: specific plan, intent, means — urgent Mental Health Plan; 000 if immediate risk
- Higher risk: suicide bereavement, social isolation, alcohol use, prior suicidality, severe functional impairment

## Key Differentials

- Major depressive episode: persistent depressed mood most of the day nearly every day; loss of all pleasure; not grief-specific; can coexist with grief; PHQ-9 ≥10; responds to antidepressants + therapy
- Prolonged Grief Disorder: grief-specific (yearning, preoccupation with deceased); functional impairment; >12 months; responds to grief-specific therapy (complicated grief treatment — CGT)
- PTSD: traumatic death; flashbacks; hypervigilance; avoidance of trauma reminders; see `ptsd`; responds to trauma-focused CBT, EMDR
- Adjustment disorder: distress in response to identifiable stressor; less severe and time-limited than PGD
- Alcohol or substance use disorder: grief-triggered but becomes autonomous; needs substance-specific treatment

## Management Principles

**Supportive approach in normal grief:**
- Validate grief: grief is a normal, healthy response to loss; it is not a medical condition requiring treatment in most cases
- Provide space and compassion: ask about the deceased (their name, relationship); listening is therapeutic
- Psychoeducation: grief is unique to each individual; there is no "right way" to grieve; grief does not follow predictable stages; anniversary reactions are normal
- Connect with support: family, friends, community, religious/spiritual supports; encourage where they are present
- Grief support groups: Australian Centre for Grief and Bereavement; community bereavement groups; BeyondBlue; Griefline (helpline 1300 845 745)

**When to refer for specialist grief support:**
- Prolonged Grief Disorder (>12 months, functional impairment): Complicated Grief Treatment (CGT) — structured evidence-based grief therapy; or grief-focused cognitive behavioural therapy
- Traumatic bereavement: concurrent PTSD treatment + grief therapy; EMDR or trauma-focused CBT
- Mental health services (GP Mental Health Treatment Plan — MHTP): clinical psychologist or mental health social worker under Better Access; 20 sessions per calendar year
- ACGB grief counsellors: grief.org.au; online and face-to-face services nationally

**Pharmacological management (adjunctive — not first-line for grief itself):**
- Sleep disturbance: melatonin 2–5 mg nocte (first-line); low-dose sedating antihistamine short-term; avoid benzodiazepines (dependence, MOH equivalent, worsen grief processing); review at 2 weeks
- Concurrent major depression (not grief per se): antidepressant (SSRI — sertraline 50–100 mg or escitalopram 10–20 mg); refer to psychology for CBT; note — antidepressants treat comorbid depression but do NOT shorten grief or treat PGD; discuss with patient
- Antidepressants do NOT hasten grief resolution; evidence for PGD does NOT support routine antidepressant use (grief-specific therapy is preferred)
- Alcohol/substance misuse: brief intervention; AUDIT-C; referral to drug and alcohol services if dependence suspected

**Practical support:**
- Practical assistance: bereavement leave entitlements (Fair Work Act Australia; 2 days compassionate leave for immediate family); financial counselling if sudden loss of income
- Centrelink: bereavement payment; widow's allowance; services for surviving spouse
- Australian Centre for Grief and Bereavement (ACGB): training, counselling, resources; grief.org.au

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Melatonin 2 mg MR (Circadin)** (PBS restricted: insomnia — age ≥55; for sleep disturbance in older bereaved patients; preferred over benzodiazepines; safe; non-habit-forming)
- **Melatonin (compounded or immediate-release)**: not PBS-listed but widely used off-label; evidence supports for insomnia; 0.5–5 mg nocte
- **Sertraline 50–100 mg (Zoloft)** (PBS restricted: major depression, PTSD; used for comorbid depression or PTSD developing after bereavement; 6–12 month minimum course)
- **Escitalopram 10–20 mg** (PBS restricted: depression and anxiety; well-tolerated; first-line SSRI)
- **Note:** Benzodiazepines (temazepam, diazepam) should be AVOIDED for sleep in grief. They impair grief processing, carry dependence risk, and mask rather than resolve underlying distress.

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate (same session or 000):**
- Active suicidal ideation with intent or plan: Mental Health Act assessment; 000 if imminent risk; same-day psychiatry if not 000
- Severe psychomotor retardation or inability to care for self: acute psychiatric assessment

**Urgent (within days):**
- Passive suicidal ideation or significant passive death wishes: Mental Health Plan + urgent psychology; contact patient next day; family involvement with consent
- Suspected concurrent severe major depression: MHTP + psychology referral; consider antidepressant

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Prolonged Grief Disorder (>12 months with functional impairment): ACGB grief counsellor; psychologist specialising in grief; MHTP under Better Access
- Traumatic bereavement or suicide bereavement: specialist grief or trauma therapy
- Social isolation and lack of support: community services; Griefline (1300 845 745); church or community groups

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- All aspects of grief support and psychoeducation
- Mental Health Treatment Plan (MHTP) development
- Medication review and management (sleep, depression)
- Follow-up for prolonged grief or concurrent depression
- Suicide risk monitoring with established patient

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- High suicide risk assessment (in-person preferred for safety planning and physical examination)
- Initial mental health assessment if significant clinical uncertainty
