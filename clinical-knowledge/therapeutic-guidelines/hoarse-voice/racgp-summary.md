## Hoarse Voice (Dysphonia) Assessment (RACGP)

Hoarseness (dysphonia) is an abnormal change in voice quality, pitch, loudness, or effort of voice production. It is a common GP presentation with causes ranging from self-limiting acute laryngitis to serious pathology including laryngeal carcinoma. The GP's critical role is to distinguish benign self-limiting causes from serious pathology requiring urgent specialist assessment. The key red flag is hoarseness persisting beyond 3 weeks — this mandates ENT referral and laryngoscopy to exclude laryngeal malignancy. Smoking and alcohol are the major risk factors for laryngeal cancer. Other important causes include vocal cord nodules (voice overuse), laryngopharyngeal reflux (LPR), recurrent laryngeal nerve palsy (from thyroid, lung, or mediastinal pathology), and hypothyroidism. Voice rest and voice therapy (speech pathology) are the cornerstone of management for benign disorders. Telehealth has limited utility for initial assessment.

## Clinical Presentation

**Acute laryngitis (most common):**
- Sudden onset hoarseness ± aphonia; URTI prodrome (coryza, sore throat); low-grade fever; vocal fatigue
- Usually self-limiting: 7–14 days; rest, hydration, voice rest
- Viral (rhinovirus, adenovirus, influenza); less commonly bacterial superinfection
- Recurrence with repeated voice use or underlying reflux

**Laryngopharyngeal reflux (LPR):**
- Hoarseness worst in morning (supine overnight reflux); throat clearing; globus sensation (lump in throat); chronic cough; dysphagia
- Distinguished from typical GORD (heartburn not always present — "silent reflux")
- Frequent in teachers, singers, public speakers
- Treatment: PPI BD × 8–12 weeks (higher dose than typical GORD); dietary modification; weight loss; elevate head of bed

**Vocal cord nodules / polyps:**
- Singers, teachers, call centre workers — voice overuse
- Nodules: bilateral; at mid-membranous vocal cord; hoarseness + vocal fatigue
- Polyps: usually unilateral; may be larger; haemorrhagic polyp after single vocal strain event
- Management: speech pathology voice therapy; surgical excision if persistent

**Recurrent laryngeal nerve (RLN) palsy:**
- Hoarseness with breathy, weak voice; left RLN more commonly affected (longer intrathoracic course)
- Causes: lung cancer (left hilar or mediastinal nodes), thyroid cancer, thyroidectomy, mediastinal tumour, aortic aneurysm, cardiovascular surgery
- Any new unexplained hoarseness in smoker or patient with neck/thyroid/chest pathology: urgent investigation
- Assessment: CXR, CT chest/neck, TFTs; ENT laryngoscopy

**Laryngeal carcinoma (RED FLAG):**
- Risk factors: smoking (major risk), alcohol use, age >50, male sex
- Hoarseness is earliest symptom (glottic cancer — affects vocal cord early)
- Progressive hoarseness; later: odynophagia, dysphagia, haemoptysis, neck mass, weight loss, stridor (late)
- Hoarseness >3 weeks in a smoker or person over 50 = URGENT ENT REFERRAL; laryngoscopy required

**Hypothyroidism:**
- Husky, low-pitched voice; myxoedematous vocal cords; weight gain, fatigue, cold intolerance, constipation
- TFT: TSH elevated; T4 low; treat with levothyroxine

**Functional dysphonia:**
- No structural laryngeal abnormality; psychogenic or muscular tension
- Muscle tension dysphonia: harsh, strained voice; laryngeal tension on palpation; excessive vocal effort; voice therapy effective
- Functional aphonia: sudden complete voice loss in context of psychological stressor; often young women; whispered speech but normal cough

**Other causes:**
- Reinke's oedema: diffuse vocal cord oedema; heavy smokers; bilateral; characteristic "whisky voice"
- Spasmodic dysphonia: neurological; strangled voice; staccato breaks; botulinum toxin injection (ENT)
- Presbylaryngis (age-related vocal cord atrophy): older adults; thin, bowed cords; voice therapy

## Assessment Approach

**History (key questions):**
- Duration: <3 weeks (likely acute laryngitis); >3 weeks (require ENT laryngoscopy)
- Onset: acute (infection, trauma, sudden overuse) vs gradual (nodules, cancer, LPR)
- Risk factors: smoking, alcohol, voice overuse occupation, GORD symptoms
- Associated symptoms: throat clearing, globus, chronic cough (LPR); weight loss, neck mass, haemoptysis, dysphagia (malignancy); fatigue, weight gain (hypothyroidism)
- Voice characteristics: breathy (RLN palsy), strained (MTD), low-pitched (hypothyroidism), progressive worsening

**Examination:**
- Oral cavity and oropharynx: inspect tonsils, palate, pharyngeal walls, tongue base
- Neck: palpate thyroid (goitre, nodule), cervical lymph nodes, laryngeal cartilages
- Indirect laryngoscopy (in-office with mirror or flexible nasoendoscopy): direct visualisation of vocal cords — restricted to ENT and trained practitioners
- Respiratory: stridor (airway compromise = urgent)
- GP assessment: TFTs, CXR, neck ultrasound — based on clinical suspicion

**Investigations:**
- TFTs (TSH, T4): hypothyroidism causing hoarseness
- CXR: lung cancer, mediastinal mass, aortic aneurysm causing RLN palsy
- CT chest/neck with contrast: if RLN palsy suspected; evaluate mediastinal and neck pathology
- Thyroid ultrasound: thyroid nodule or mass causing RLN palsy or tracheal compression
- ENT referral: laryngoscopy is definitive assessment of vocal cords — mandatory for hoarseness >3 weeks

## Key Differentials

- Acute laryngitis: <3 weeks; URTI context; self-limiting
- LPR: chronic; morning hoarseness; throat clearing; no heartburn; trial PPI
- Vocal cord nodules/polyps: voice users; bilateral symmetrical (nodules) vs unilateral (polyp)
- RLN palsy: breathy voice; underlying cause (lung/thyroid pathology); CT chest/neck
- Laryngeal carcinoma: >50 years; smoker; progressive; >3 weeks → urgent ENT
- Hypothyroidism: husky low pitch; systemic features; TFT
- Functional dysphonia: no structural abnormality on laryngoscopy; psychosocial history; voice therapy

## Management Principles

**Acute laryngitis:**
- Voice rest: complete voice rest for 24–48 hours; then reduced voice use for 1 week; avoid whispering (increases vocal cord tension)
- Hydration: drink 2 L water daily; steam inhalations (not menthol — irritant)
- Avoid: smoking, alcohol, caffeine, throat clearing, dry environments
- Antibiotics: NOT indicated (viral aetiology); only if bacterial superinfection (green purulent secretions, high fever, not improving at 7 days) — amoxicillin
- Simple analgesia: paracetamol, honey-lemon drinks; throat lozenges

**Laryngopharyngeal reflux (LPR):**
- PPI twice daily (omeprazole 20 mg or pantoprazole 40 mg BD, 30 minutes before meals) × 8–12 weeks: higher dose and longer than standard GORD
- Dietary changes: avoid caffeine, alcohol, fatty foods, spicy foods, chocolate, carbonation; avoid eating within 3 hours of lying down
- Weight loss; elevation of head of bed; voice hygiene
- Review at 8–12 weeks: ENT laryngoscopy if no improvement to confirm LPR and exclude malignancy

**Vocal cord nodules/polyps:**
- Speech pathology referral: voice therapy (voice hygiene, vocal exercises, resonant voice technique) is primary treatment for nodules; ENT laryngoscopy to confirm diagnosis
- Voice rest: especially for acute haemorrhagic polyp
- ENT referral: if not resolving after 6–8 weeks of voice therapy; surgical microsuspension laryngoscopy for persistent polyp or nodules

**RLN palsy:**
- Investigate underlying cause (CXR, CT chest/neck) urgently — exclude malignancy
- ENT: voice injection augmentation (hyaluronic acid or calcium hydroxyapatite) or thyroplasty for permanent palsy
- Speech pathology: voice therapy for compensatory strategies

**Hypothyroidism:**
- Levothyroxine replacement: voice often improves with euthyroid state restoration; takes months

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Pantoprazole 40 mg BD (Somac)** (PBS restricted: GORD; use BD before meals for LPR — higher dose than standard; 8–12 week course before ENT review if not improving)
- **Omeprazole 20 mg BD (Losec)** (PBS restricted: GORD; standard dose 20 mg daily; LPR requires BD; consider continuing maintenance after acute treatment if recurrent)
- **Note:** No evidence-based pharmacological treatment for vocal cord nodules or functional dysphonia. Voice therapy (speech pathology) is the evidence-based treatment for these conditions.

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / hospital:**
- Stridor (respiratory compromise): indicates severe laryngeal or tracheal obstruction; 000; airway management
- Acute angio-oedema causing voice change + throat swelling: anaphylaxis; 000; adrenaline

**Urgent ENT referral (within 2 weeks):**
- Hoarseness persisting >3 weeks: URGENT laryngoscopy to exclude laryngeal carcinoma — particularly if smoker, drinker, or age >50
- RLN palsy (new breathy voice): urgent CT chest/neck to exclude lung cancer or mediastinal mass; ENT
- Haemoptysis + hoarseness: urgent investigation for malignancy

**Non-urgent ENT referral:**
- Vocal cord nodules or polyps after speech pathology trial
- Spasmodic dysphonia: botulinum toxin injection
- Reinke's oedema: smoking cessation + ENT surgical assessment

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Acute laryngitis: advice on voice rest, hydration, symptom management
- LPR management: PPI prescribing, dietary advice
- Follow-up for known vocal cord pathology under ENT management
- Hypothyroidism management and levothyroxine dose adjustment

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Hoarseness >3 weeks: ENT laryngoscopy mandatory (cannot assess vocal cords via telehealth)
- Neck examination for lymphadenopathy or thyroid pathology
- Any suspicion of malignancy
