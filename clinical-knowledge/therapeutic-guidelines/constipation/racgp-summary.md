## Constipation Assessment (RACGP)

Constipation is defined as unsatisfactory defaecation characterised by infrequent stools (fewer than 3 per week), difficult stool passage, or both. It affects approximately 16–20% of Australians and is more common in women, the elderly, and those with low dietary fibre or physical inactivity. The majority of constipation in general practice is functional (idiopathic or slow-transit); however, identifying secondary causes and alarm features (red flags) that require urgent investigation is essential. Telehealth is suitable for most constipation assessments and management.

## Clinical Presentation

**Rome IV criteria for functional constipation (≥2 in last 3 months, onset ≥6 months):**
- Straining during ≥25% of defaecations
- Lumpy or hard stools ≥25% of the time
- Sensation of incomplete evacuation ≥25% of the time
- Sensation of anorectal obstruction/blockage ≥25%
- Manual manoeuvres required ≥25%
- Fewer than 3 spontaneous bowel movements per week

**Subtypes:**
- Slow-transit constipation: reduced colonic propulsion; common in women; responds to laxatives
- Defaecatory disorder (obstructed defaecation): difficulty with rectal evacuation; paradoxical sphincter contraction; requires pelvic floor physiotherapy
- IBS with constipation (IBS-C): constipation with associated abdominal pain/bloating; see IBS entry

**Common secondary causes:**
- Medications (very common): opioids, antidepressants (TCAs), antipsychotics, iron supplements, antacids (calcium-based), verapamil, antidiarrhoeal agents
- Metabolic/endocrine: hypothyroidism, hypercalcaemia, diabetes (autonomic neuropathy)
- Neurological: Parkinson's disease, multiple sclerosis, spinal cord lesion
- Structural: colorectal cancer, stricture, pelvic organ prolapse, anal fissure, rectocoele
- Dehydration, low fibre diet, immobility

**Alarm features (require urgent investigation):**
- New onset constipation after age 50 (without prior bowel habit change)
- Rectal bleeding or haematochezia
- Unexplained weight loss
- Family history of colorectal cancer or IBD
- Iron deficiency anaemia
- Palpable abdominal or rectal mass
- Change in bowel habit in patient over 50

## Assessment Approach

- Detailed history: bowel frequency, stool consistency (Bristol Stool Chart — type 1–2 = constipation), duration, straining, digital manoeuvres, rectal bleeding, pain on defaecation
- Medication review — identify constipating drugs
- Dietary assessment: fibre intake (<25 g/day is inadequate), fluid intake, physical activity
- Rectal examination (in-person): assess anal tone, prostate (men), rectocoele, faecal impaction, rectal mass; important for excluding faecal impaction before prescribing laxatives
- Digital rectal exam assesses for structural causes and paradoxical puborectalis contraction (defaecatory disorder)

**Investigations:**
- Routine: not required for simple chronic constipation; TFTs if hypothyroid suspected; serum calcium if hypercalcaemia suspected
- Colonoscopy: indicated if alarm features present — colorectal cancer, mass, significant rectal bleeding
- Colonic transit study or defaecography: specialist investigation for refractory constipation

## Key Differentials

- Colorectal cancer — alarm features; colonoscopy; urgent referral if suspected
- Hypothyroidism — check TSH in new-onset constipation with other hypothyroid features
- Hypercalcaemia — constipation + polyuria + confusion + weakness; check serum calcium
- Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS-C) — constipation + abdominal pain; see IBS entry
- Faecal impaction — elderly, immobile; rectal examination required; treatment with disimpaction before laxatives
- Medication side effect — very common; review medication list
- Parkinson's disease — constipation often precedes motor symptoms by years

## Management Principles

**Lifestyle (first-line for all functional constipation):**
- Dietary fibre: increase to 25–30 g/day gradually; sources — wholegrains, fruits, vegetables, legumes, psyllium; increase slowly to avoid bloating
- Fluid intake: 1.5–2 L water daily; dehydration worsens constipation
- Physical activity: regular aerobic exercise promotes colonic motility
- Toilet posture: feet on stool (squatting-like position) facilitates defaecation; reduce time straining
- Bowel habit training: respond to urge; regular scheduled toilet time after meals (gastrocolic reflex)

**Laxatives:**

Bulk-forming (fibre supplements):
- Psyllium (Metamucil), methylcellulose (Citrucel): increase stool bulk and water content; require adequate fluid intake; first-line for mild constipation; may worsen if dehydrated or slow-transit

Osmotic laxatives:
- Macrogol/PEG (Movicol, Osmolax): first-line pharmacotherapy; well tolerated; effective for all ages; 1–3 sachets daily in water; safe long-term
- Lactulose: osmotic; effective; may cause bloating and flatulence; less preferred than macrogol
- Milk of magnesia (magnesium hydroxide): effective; caution in renal impairment (magnesium toxicity risk)

Stimulant laxatives:
- Senna, bisacodyl: stimulate colonic propulsion; effective for short-term or as adjunct; concern about long-term use — melanosis coli (cosmetic, not clinically significant); safe for regular use in most patients
- Docusate (stool softener): weak evidence; mainly used for faecal impaction or post-operative

Rectal treatments:
- Glycerol suppositories: stimulate rectal defaecation reflex; good for evacuation difficulty
- Phosphate enemas: for faecal impaction disimpaction; in-person assessment required
- Microlax (sodium citrate enema): soften hard stools; useful in acute impaction

Opioid-induced constipation:
- Methylnaltrexone (Relistor): peripheral mu-opioid receptor antagonist; SC injection; PBS-restricted for opioid-induced constipation in palliative care
- Naloxegol: oral peripheral opioid antagonist; not PBS-listed in Australia

**Defaecatory disorder:** Biofeedback pelvic floor physiotherapy — first-line; highly effective; specialist pelvic floor physio referral.

**Referral for refractory constipation:** Colorectal surgeon or gastroenterologist — for investigation (colonoscopy, transit studies, defaecography) and specialist management.

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Macrogol/PEG** (Movicol, Osmolax — PBS restricted: chronic constipation; 1–3 sachets daily; first-line pharmacotherapy)
- **Lactulose** (PBS unrestricted — constipation; 15–30 mL twice daily)
- **Senna** (Senokot — PBS unrestricted — stimulant laxative; 2–4 tablets at night)
- **Bisacodyl** (PBS unrestricted — stimulant laxative; 5–10 mg oral or 10 mg suppository)
- **Docusate sodium** (PBS unrestricted — stool softener; 120–480 mg/day)
- **Methylnaltrexone** (Relistor — PBS restricted: opioid-induced constipation in palliative care)
- **Psyllium** (Metamucil — available OTC; not PBS-listed; bulk-forming; require 2 glasses water per dose)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Complete large bowel obstruction — absolute constipation, abdominal distension, vomiting, pain; surgical emergency
- Faecal impaction with bowel obstruction or perforation — medical emergency

**Urgent in-person or hospital assessment:**
- Alarm features present (rectal bleeding, weight loss, anaemia, palpable mass) — urgent colonoscopy referral within 2 weeks
- Faecal impaction in elderly — manual disimpaction or enema; in-person management required
- Hypercalcaemia with confusion or cardiac symptoms — IV fluids, hospital admission

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Gastroenterology or colorectal surgery: refractory constipation not responding to ≥2 laxative classes; colonoscopy for structural exclusion; specialist investigation
- Pelvic floor physiotherapist: suspected defaecatory disorder or obstructed defaecation

## Telehealth Suitability

Constipation assessment and management is well suited to telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Symptom assessment using Rome IV criteria and Bristol Stool Chart (patient can complete before consultation)
- Laxative prescribing and titration for functional constipation
- Dietary and lifestyle advice
- Medication review for constipating drug identification
- Safety netting for alarm features and return criteria

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Digital rectal examination — assessment for faecal impaction, mass, anal fissure, defaecatory disorder
- Suspected faecal impaction — disimpaction cannot be performed remotely
- Alarm features requiring physical examination and urgent investigation
