## Malaria Prophylaxis Assessment (RACGP)

Malaria is a life-threatening mosquito-borne parasitic disease caused by Plasmodium species (P. falciparum most dangerous — causes cerebral malaria; P. vivax, P. ovale, P. malariae — less severe but still serious). Globally, approximately 240 million cases occur annually; >600,000 deaths, predominantly sub-Saharan African children. In Australia, malaria is not endemic (rare local transmission in far north Queensland from introduced cases only). GPs manage malaria in two contexts: (1) prescribing chemoprophylaxis for Australians travelling to endemic regions; and (2) investigating and treating post-travel fever. This entry focuses on chemoprophylaxis; the `pre-travel-consultation` and `dengue-fever` entries address the broader travel context. Malaria prophylaxis is one of the most important, evidence-based, potentially life-saving GP interventions for travellers. Missing or stopping chemoprophylaxis causes preventable deaths annually in Australian travellers.

## Clinical Presentation

**Malaria risk determination (key questions before prescribing prophylaxis):**
- Destination: country AND specific region within country (malaria risk varies markedly — e.g., Vietnam coast vs highlands vs Mekong Delta)
- Duration: longer stay = higher cumulative exposure
- Accommodation: air-conditioned hotel vs rural camp vs volunteering in village
- Activities: rural, outdoor, night-time activities; agriculture; healthcare volunteering
- Season: rainy season = higher transmission
- Species prevalence: P. falciparum (Africa, some SE Asia) vs P. vivax (South Asia, Americas)
- Drug resistance: P. falciparum chloroquine resistance — widespread; mefloquine resistance — Mekong area; check CDNA (Communicable Diseases Network Australia) or ISTM destination-specific advice

**High-risk destinations for P. falciparum:**
- Sub-Saharan Africa: highest malaria transmission worldwide; P. falciparum predominates; high mortality risk; all travellers need chemoprophylaxis
- Papua New Guinea and Solomon Islands: very high risk; Australian travellers; P. falciparum + P. vivax
- South/Southeast Asia: variable risk by country and region; lower than Africa overall; P. vivax and P. falciparum; mefloquine resistance in Mekong region
- South America (Amazon region): moderate risk; P. vivax + P. falciparum
- Indian subcontinent: moderate risk; P. vivax predominates; P. falciparum in some areas

**Malaria post-travel (see also `pre-travel-consultation` entry):**
- ALWAYS consider malaria in any febrile traveller within 28 days of return from endemic area
- P. falciparum can present within days; P. vivax may present weeks to months later
- Malaria can be fatal within hours once cerebral malaria develops; no delay in testing

## Assessment Approach

**Pre-travel assessment for chemoprophylaxis:**
- Medical history: pregnancy (mefloquine and doxycycline contraindicated; atovaquone/proguanil relatively safe); psychiatric history (mefloquine contraindicated in depression, anxiety, PTSD, psychotic disorders); renal function (atovaquone/proguanil reduced efficacy in severe CKD); G6PD deficiency (primaquine contraindicated); epilepsy (mefloquine contraindicated); cardiac arrhythmia (mefloquine)
- Current medications: drug interactions (mefloquine + beta-blockers; atovaquone/proguanil + rifampicin; doxycycline + warfarin)
- Previous malaria or prophylaxis experience: tolerability; contraindications
- Check CDNA or ISTM maps for destination-specific risk and resistance patterns

**Post-travel malaria assessment:**
- Urgent malaria film + PCR: MUST perform on ALL febrile patients within 28 days of endemic area travel — do NOT defer to outpatient; this is a same-day urgent investigation
- Thick and thin blood films: thick film for sensitivity; thin film for species identification
- Malaria PCR: more sensitive than film; same-day result in most Australian hospitals
- If strongly suspected but film negative: repeat every 12–24 hours × 3 (P. vivax may have low parasitaemia)

## Key Differentials (post-travel fever)

- Dengue fever: most common travel fever after malaria; mosquito-borne; no prophylaxis; see `dengue-fever` entry; thrombocytopenia + leukopenia; NS1 antigen
- Typhoid fever: contaminated food/water; sustained fever; relative bradycardia; blood culture
- Chikungunya: mosquito-borne; severe arthralgia; rash; self-limiting
- Rickettsial infection: tick/mite bite; eschar; serology
- Viral haemorrhagic fever: Ebola, Lassa (rare; contact with infected patients; isolation and specialist management if suspected)
- Hepatitis A or E: jaundice; food/water; serology
- Leptospirosis: animal/water contact; serology

## Management Principles

**Malaria chemoprophylaxis options:**

1. Atovaquone/proguanil (Malarone 250/100 mg):
   - Regimen: 1 tablet daily; start 1–2 days before entering endemic area; continue 7 days AFTER leaving
   - Efficacy: >95% for P. falciparum; also some activity vs P. vivax
   - Advantages: well-tolerated; short post-travel course; can be used for short trips
   - Side effects: GI upset (nausea, abdominal pain) — take with food and full glass of water to minimise; rare: mouth ulcers, elevated LFTs
   - Contraindications: severe renal impairment (eGFR <30); weight <40 kg use paediatric tablets; pregnancy/breastfeeding (avoid if possible — limited data; only use if no alternative and risk is high)
   - Drug interactions: rifampicin (reduces levels); tetracycline (reduces levels); antiretrovirals (some)
   - Cost: expensive (~$10–15/day); most important consideration for budget travellers; no PBS subsidy for prophylaxis

2. Doxycycline 100 mg daily:
   - Regimen: 100 mg daily; start 1–2 days before; continue 4 weeks AFTER leaving endemic area (longer post-travel)
   - Efficacy: >95% for P. falciparum; also protects against some other tropical infections (rickettsial, leptospirosis)
   - Advantages: cheapest option (~$0.50/day); additional broad antimicrobial protection; can also be used as standby for travellers' diarrhoea
   - Side effects: photosensitivity (ALWAYS counsel — use SPF50+ sunscreen, protective clothing; risk of severe sunburn); GI upset (take with food and full glass of water); oesophageal ulceration if taken lying down (take upright before meals); vaginal candidiasis (yeast infections more common)
   - Contraindications: pregnancy (harm to fetal bone/teeth); children <8 years (tooth discolouration); tetracycline allergy; avoid breastfeeding
   - Suitable for: Mekong area (mefloquine resistance); budget travellers; all ages ≥8 years not pregnant

3. Mefloquine (Lariam 250 mg):
   - Regimen: 1 tablet weekly; start 2–3 weeks before travel (to detect side effects before departure); continue 4 weeks AFTER leaving
   - Efficacy: ~90% for most P. falciparum
   - Advantages: once weekly (adherence); long post-travel course means more protection
   - Disadvantages: neuropsychiatric side effects — anxiety, depression, vivid dreams, sleep disturbance, psychosis, suicidal ideation; may persist after stopping; not recommended for deployment or people requiring precise judgment
   - Contraindications: psychiatric history (depression, anxiety, PTSD, schizophrenia); epilepsy; cardiac arrhythmias; cardiac conduction disorders; use with beta-blockers or antiarrhythmics
   - Resistance: widespread resistance in Mekong region (Thailand-Cambodia border, Vietnam highland); ineffective there — use atovaquone/proguanil or doxycycline instead
   - Mandatory pre-prescription psychiatric screening: ask specifically about depression, anxiety, suicidal thoughts; contraindicate if positive history
   - Declining use: neuropsychiatric side effect profile makes this a third-choice option for most travellers

4. Chloroquine (no longer recommended for most destinations):
   - P. falciparum resistance widespread; only limited areas where chloroquine-sensitive malaria remains (parts of Central America above Panama Canal, Caribbean)
   - Not appropriate for Africa, SE Asia, South America (most regions)

**Special populations:**

Pregnancy:
- Avoid non-essential travel to high-risk malaria areas during pregnancy
- If travel unavoidable: atovaquone/proguanil if absolutely needed (limited data but increasingly used; discuss risk-benefit)
- Mefloquine: historical limited use in 2nd/3rd trimester when no alternative; consult travel medicine specialist
- Doxycycline: absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy
- Fetal malaria: risk of miscarriage, stillbirth, premature birth, low birth weight from P. falciparum

Children:
- Atovaquone/proguanil paediatric tablets (62.5/25 mg): weight-based dosing ≥5 kg; very effective; short post-travel course; preferred for children
- Doxycycline: ≥8 years only (tooth and bone development)
- Mefloquine: ≥5 kg; only if no alternatives

P. vivax relapse prevention (radical cure):
- P. vivax and P. ovale can form dormant hepatic stages (hypnozoites) that cause relapse months-years later
- Primaquine 30 mg daily × 14 days: required after return from vivax/ovale malaria endemic area for travellers with significant exposure (not all travellers need this)
- G6PD testing MANDATORY before primaquine: primaquine causes haemolytic anaemia in G6PD deficiency (very common in populations from endemic areas)
- Specialist decision usually: radical cure for vivax relapse prevention typically coordinated by travel medicine or infectious disease

**Mosquito bite prevention (ALWAYS concurrent with chemoprophylaxis):**
- DEET (20–50%) repellent: most effective; apply every 4–6 hours; reapply after swimming; safe in children >2 months (not <2 months); apply AFTER sunscreen
- Anopheles mosquitoes bite from DUSK TO DAWN (opposite to Aedes/dengue): evening/night protection critical
- Permethrin-treated clothing: long sleeves, trousers; especially after dusk
- Bed nets: insecticide-treated bed nets (ITN) — essential for rural accommodation; provided by some NGOs
- Accommodation: air-conditioned, screened rooms preferred for high-risk destinations

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Atovaquone/proguanil (Malarone 250/100 mg)** (PBS restricted S4: malaria prevention; private prescription at patient cost; 1 tablet daily; start 1–2 days before; continue 7 days after; most convenient but most expensive option)
- **Doxycycline 100 mg** (PBS unrestricted: multiple indications including malaria; cheapest; 1 tablet daily; start 1–2 days before; continue 4 WEEKS after — critical counselling point; photosensitivity counselling essential)
- **Mefloquine (Lariam) 250 mg** (PBS restricted: malaria prophylaxis; weekly; start 2–3 weeks before; continue 4 weeks after; MANDATORY psychiatric screening before prescribing; if neuropsychiatric symptoms develop — cease immediately)
- **Primaquine 15/30 mg** (PBS restricted: malaria treatment and radical cure; G6PD testing mandatory before prescribing; specialist initiation recommended for radical cure protocols)
- **Note:** All malaria prophylaxis drugs are taken at patient's expense (no PBS rebate for prophylaxis); cost is a genuine barrier and should be discussed at pre-travel consultation; doxycycline is the most cost-effective option for most adults

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Post-travel fever within 28 days of endemic area return: malaria film + PCR URGENTLY — do NOT wait; P. falciparum can be fatal within hours; if GP cannot arrange same-day testing, direct to ED
- Malaria confirmed: hospitalise all P. falciparum; IV artesunate or IV quinine in hospital for severe disease
- Suspected cerebral malaria: severe fever + confusion, seizures, altered consciousness — 000

**Same-day urgent:**
- Febrile returned traveller from Africa, PNG, Pacific: malaria film and PCR same day; arrange directly

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Travel medicine clinic: complex itineraries; immunocompromised travellers; pregnancy and malaria risk; specialist guidance on non-standard regimens
- Infectious disease specialist: post-travel malaria treatment; vivax radical cure; resistant or severe malaria

## Telehealth Suitability

Malaria prophylaxis counselling and prescribing are suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Pre-travel risk assessment and prophylaxis selection
- Mefloquine psychiatric screening
- Prescribing and dosing counselling (all three options)
- DEET and bed net prevention education
- Destination-specific risk advice

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Any post-travel fever: examination, blood tests (malaria film + PCR) — do NOT manage acute malaria via telehealth; direct to ED or urgent pathology
- G6PD testing before primaquine (blood test)
- Severe or complicated malaria — hospitalisation
