## Vitamin D Deficiency Assessment (RACGP)

Vitamin D deficiency is prevalent in Australia, affecting approximately 1 in 4 adults, despite the country's high sun exposure. It is more common in southern states, winter months, and in people who avoid sun exposure (skin cancer concerns, cultural practices, indoor occupations). Most vitamin D in humans is synthesised in skin on exposure to UVB radiation; dietary sources provide only a small contribution. Deficiency causes impaired calcium absorption, bone disease (osteomalacia in adults, rickets in children), and muscle weakness. Evidence for non-skeletal benefits of vitamin D supplementation (cardiovascular, immune, cancer prevention) remains inconsistent. Telehealth is highly suitable for results review and supplementation management.

## Clinical Presentation

Severe deficiency is often asymptomatic or causes non-specific symptoms: fatigue, bone pain, muscle weakness, mood changes. Proximal myopathy (difficulty rising from chair or climbing stairs) occurs with severe or prolonged deficiency.

Symptomatic presentations of osteomalacia: diffuse bone pain and tenderness (especially lower back, pelvis, hips), proximal muscle weakness, antalgic gait.

Risk factors for deficiency:
- Limited sun exposure: indoor lifestyle, sun-avoidance behaviour, sun-protective clothing, veil or hijab, southern latitude in winter (Melbourne, Hobart, Adelaide)
- Dark skin pigmentation — higher melanin reduces cutaneous synthesis
- Obesity (BMI ≥30) — vitamin D sequestered in adipose tissue
- Malabsorption: coeliac disease, inflammatory bowel disease, bariatric surgery
- Chronic kidney or liver disease — impaired hydroxylation to active form
- Medications: anticonvulsants (phenytoin, carbamazepine), corticosteroids, rifampicin — accelerate catabolism
- Older age — reduced skin synthesis capacity and reduced sun exposure
- Exclusively breastfed infants — breast milk is low in vitamin D

## Assessment Approach

Serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D [25(OH)D] — the appropriate test to assess vitamin D status:
- Deficiency: <50 nmol/L
- Insufficiency: 50–74 nmol/L (some guidelines use <75 nmol/L as the threshold for insufficiency)
- Sufficiency: ≥50 nmol/L (RACGP and Endocrine Society of Australia)
- Toxicity: >200–250 nmol/L (rare except with very high supplementation doses)

Note: routine population screening is not recommended — test in symptomatic patients or those with significant risk factors.

Check calcium and phosphate when vitamin D is low — may reveal primary hyperparathyroidism (elevated calcium and PTH, low-normal phosphate) or osteomalacia (low calcium, low phosphate, elevated ALP, elevated PTH).

Assess bone health context: DEXA scan for osteoporosis risk in patients with prolonged deficiency, older age, or fracture history.

## Key Differentials

- Osteoporosis — low bone density on DEXA; vitamin D deficiency contributes but is not the sole cause
- Primary hyperparathyroidism — elevated calcium with elevated PTH; often incidental; refer to endocrinology
- Hypomagnesaemia — can cause functional hypoparathyroidism and resistance to vitamin D therapy; check Mg if vitamin D not responding to supplementation
- Malabsorption syndromes — coeliac, IBD, pancreatic insufficiency; investigate if unexpected deficiency or inadequate response to supplementation
- Chronic kidney disease — impaired activation of vitamin D; requires alfacalcidol or calcitriol (active forms), not standard cholecalciferol

## Management Principles

**Identify and address the cause:** Avoid excessive sun-avoidance; address malabsorption if present; review precipitating medications.

**Vitamin D supplementation:**

For deficiency (25(OH)D <50 nmol/L):
- Loading dose: cholecalciferol (vitamin D3) 1,000–2,000 IU daily for 6–12 weeks, then maintenance; OR high-dose loading — cholecalciferol 50,000 IU weekly for 6–8 weeks (prescription product, though some brands available OTC) followed by maintenance
- Maintenance: 1,000–2,000 IU daily ongoing for high-risk groups; 400–800 IU daily for the general population

For insufficiency (50–74 nmol/L) with risk factors:
- 1,000 IU daily is generally sufficient

Route and formulation:
- Cholecalciferol (vitamin D3) preferred over ergocalciferol (D2) — superior at raising serum levels
- Oral drops, tablets, and capsules are equivalent in efficacy
- Combined calcium + vitamin D preparations available (useful in osteoporosis management)

Calcium supplementation: required in osteomalacia or if dietary calcium is inadequate (<1,200 mg/day). Dietary calcium is preferred over supplementation for most patients (calcium supplements may increase cardiovascular risk at high doses — evidence debated).

Target 25(OH)D: 50–150 nmol/L. Recheck 25(OH)D at 3–6 months after initiating supplementation to confirm response.

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Cholecalciferol** (vitamin D3 — widely available OTC as Ostelin, Bio-D, Healthy Essentials; various strengths 400 IU to 1,000 IU; PBS-listed only for specific conditions including osteoporosis, malabsorption — most supplementation is OTC or self-funded)
- **Cholecalciferol 50,000 IU** (D-Cure, available OTC in some states; prescription strength for loading)
- **Calcium carbonate** (Caltrate, Calcichew — OTC and PBS-listed for osteoporosis)
- **Calcium + vitamin D3 combination** (Caltrate Plus, Os-Cal — OTC)
- **Alfacalcidol / calcitriol** (PBS restricted: renal osteodystrophy, hypoparathyroidism — active vitamin D forms; not for standard deficiency management)
- Note: PBS subsidy for cholecalciferol is limited to specific indications; most patients fund supplementation themselves at low cost

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 — rare with vitamin D deficiency alone:**
- Severe hypocalcaemia with symptomatic tetany, seizures, laryngospasm, or cardiac arrhythmia (can result from rapid correction of vitamin D deficiency mobilising calcium into bone)

**Urgent in-person review:**
- Hypercalcaemia discovered on blood tests — may suggest primary hyperparathyroidism, sarcoidosis, malignancy, or vitamin D toxicity (very rare)
- Severe symptomatic myopathy limiting mobility or causing falls

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Endocrinology: primary hyperparathyroidism, severe or refractory deficiency, suspected granulomatous disease (sarcoidosis — 1-alpha-hydroxylase in macrophages; vitamin D supplementation may worsen hypercalcaemia)
- Gastroenterology: malabsorption as underlying cause
- Rheumatology / metabolic bone disease: osteomalacia, complex bone disease

## Telehealth Suitability

Vitamin D deficiency is highly suitable for telehealth management.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Results review and diagnosis discussion
- Supplementation initiation and dosing guidance
- Dietary and sun exposure counselling
- Follow-up pathology review at 3–6 months
- Identification of risk factors and addressing underlying causes

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Suspected severe osteomalacia or proximal myopathy (gait assessment)
- Hypercalcaemia investigation
- DEXA scan referral (requires radiology)
