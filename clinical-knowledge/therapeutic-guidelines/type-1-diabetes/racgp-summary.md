## Type 1 Diabetes — GP Monitoring (RACGP)

Type 1 diabetes (T1D) is an autoimmune condition resulting in destruction of pancreatic beta cells, causing absolute insulin deficiency. It affects approximately 130,000 Australians and can present at any age, though peak incidence is in childhood and adolescence. T1D requires lifelong insulin replacement — there is no oral hypoglycaemic alternative. The GP's role in established T1D includes: monitoring glycaemic control, preventing and managing complications, reviewing insulin regimens in stable patients, managing intercurrent illness, and coordinating with endocrinology and diabetes educators. Telehealth is highly suitable for T1D review in stable patients.

## Clinical Presentation

**New or undiagnosed T1D (acute presentation):**
- Polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss, fatigue — often rapid onset over days to weeks
- Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA): vomiting, abdominal pain, rapid breathing (Kussmaul), fruity breath, confusion — medical emergency; call 000
- Diagnosis: random glucose ≥11.1 mmol/L with symptoms; or fasting glucose ≥7.0 mmol/L confirmed twice; or HbA1c ≥48 mmol/mol; + positive islet autoantibodies (anti-GAD, anti-IA2) or C-peptide deficiency confirm T1D vs T2D

**Ongoing T1D management — GP focuses on:**
- Glycaemic control: HbA1c target typically <53 mmol/mol (7%) in adults; individualise for hypoglycaemia risk, life expectancy, pregnancy
- Hypoglycaemia: the primary risk of insulin therapy; assess frequency, severity (mild self-treated; severe = impaired consciousness requiring assistance), hypoglycaemia unawareness
- Diabetic complications screening: retinopathy, nephropathy, neuropathy, macrovascular disease
- Diabetes technology: insulin pump (CSII), continuous glucose monitor (CGM) — increasing use in T1D; GP should understand basic function
- Intercurrent illness: sick day management — never stop insulin; insulin requirements increase with illness; risk of DKA

**Sick day rules (critical patient education):**
- Never stop insulin even if not eating (risk of DKA); may need to increase doses
- Monitor blood glucose every 2–4 hours; test for ketones if glucose >15 mmol/L
- Seek urgent medical attention if: vomiting, unable to keep down fluids, moderate-large ketones, blood glucose not responding, confusion — hospital assessment required

## Assessment Approach

**3–6 monthly review in stable T1D:**
- HbA1c: target <53 mmol/mol (7%); check at each review
- Continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) data review: time in range (TIR) 70–180 mg/dL target >70%; time below range <4 mmol/L target <4%; hypo events; CGM devices — Dexcom G6/G7, Libre 2/3 (partially PBS-subsidised for T1D)
- Insulin dose review: total daily dose, basal-bolus regimen; injection sites (lipohypertrophy from repeated injection in same site reduces absorption)
- Hypoglycaemia assessment: frequency, severity, unawareness; driving safety (must not drive within 30 min of eating after hypo)
- Weight, blood pressure, BMI
- Foot examination: annually (or more frequently if peripheral neuropathy or vascular disease)
- Injection site inspection: lipohypertrophy causes erratic insulin absorption

**Annual complications screening:**
- Retinal screening: ophthalmology or accredited retinal photography service; annually from 5 years duration of T1D (or at diagnosis in adults)
- Kidney: spot urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (ACR) + eGFR — annually; ACR >3 mg/mmol = microalbuminuria; commence ACE inhibitor/ARB
- Peripheral neuropathy: 10 g monofilament, vibration (tuning fork), ankle reflexes — annually
- Blood pressure: target <130/80 mmHg in T1D; ACE inhibitor/ARB first-line if hypertensive or microalbuminuric
- Lipid panel: annual; LDL-C target <2.5 mmol/L; statin if established CVD or high risk
- Thyroid: autoimmune thyroid disease associated with T1D; TSH annually
- Coeliac antibodies: anti-tTG IgA; increased prevalence coeliac disease in T1D

**PBS-subsidised CGM for T1D:**
- Libre sensor (Abbott FreeStyle Libre) — PBS-subsidised for T1D adults and children; reduces hypoglycaemia, improves HbA1c
- Dexcom G6/G7: PBS-subsidised for T1D adults and children; real-time CGM with alarms; integrates with insulin pumps for closed-loop systems (artificial pancreas)

## Key Differentials

- Type 2 diabetes — usually older, overweight, insulin-resistant; C-peptide positive initially; anti-GAD negative; see `type-2-diabetes` entry
- MODY (maturity-onset diabetes of the young): monogenic diabetes; autoantibody negative; genetic testing; specialist referral
- Secondary diabetes: pancreatitis, haemochromatosis, corticosteroid use; distinguish from T1D
- Latent autoimmune diabetes of adults (LADA): adult onset with slow beta cell destruction; may initially respond to oral agents; confirm with anti-GAD; progresses to insulin-dependence

## Management Principles

**Insulin regimens in T1D (GP monitoring role — initiation and major changes to endocrinologist):**

Basal-bolus regimen:
- Basal insulin: long-acting; provides background insulin; insulin glargine (Lantus, Toujeo) or detemir (Levemir) or degludec (Tresiba) once or twice daily
- Bolus insulin: rapid-acting at mealtimes; insulin lispro (Humalog), aspart (NovoRapid), glulisine (Apidra), or faster-acting aspart (Fiasp); carbohydrate counting to calculate dose
- Correction bolus: additional rapid-acting insulin for hyperglycaemia correction

Insulin pump (CSII):
- Continuous SC infusion of rapid-acting insulin via pump; no long-acting insulin; delivers basal and bolus
- Closed-loop system (hybrid artificial pancreas): CGM-integrated pump that adjusts basal automatically (e.g., Omnipod 5, Medtronic 780G); increasingly standard of care for T1D in Australia

**Hypoglycaemia management:**
- Mild-moderate (conscious, self-treating): 15 g fast-acting carbohydrate (glucose tablets, 150 mL regular soft drink, 200 mL fruit juice); wait 15 min; recheck; repeat if still low; then have a snack
- Severe (impaired consciousness, unable to swallow): glucagon IM/SC — Gvoke (readymix SC pen) or GlucaGen (IM injection); or buccal glucose gel; call 000; DO NOT give fluids orally if impaired consciousness
- Driver: must not drive if blood glucose <5.0 mmol/L; recheck 15 min after hypo treatment; document

**Driving regulations for T1D:**
- Passenger car licence (Group 1): inform licensing authority of T1D; annual medical review; must have CGM or test BGL before driving and every 2 hours; must not drive if BGL <5.0 mmol/L; GP role in AUSTROADS medical standards documentation

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Insulin glargine** (Lantus, Toujeo — PBS unrestricted for T1D: basal insulin; glargine 100 units/mL or 300 units/mL for Toujeo)
- **Insulin degludec** (Tresiba — PBS restricted: T1D and T2D basal insulin; longer duration; once daily)
- **Insulin aspart** (NovoRapid — PBS unrestricted: rapid-acting bolus insulin; T1D; also Fiasp — faster-acting aspart)
- **Insulin lispro** (Humalog — PBS unrestricted: rapid-acting bolus)
- **Glucagon** (GlucaGen — PBS restricted: severe hypoglycaemia; IM injection kit; Gvoke — PBS restricted: SC auto-injector pen; prescribe and ensure all T1D patients have one at home)
- **CGM sensors** (FreeStyle Libre, Dexcom — PBS-subsidised for T1D via Department of Health CGM subsidy program — not standard PBS; patient registers for subsidy separately)
- **Note:** Insulin dose adjustments in T1D typically require endocrinologist or diabetes educator guidance; GP role is monitoring, complication screening, and managing intercurrent illness

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA): vomiting, abdominal pain, altered consciousness, rapid breathing, blood glucose >15 mmol/L + ketones — life-threatening; IV insulin, fluids, electrolyte replacement
- Severe hypoglycaemia with impaired consciousness or seizure — administer glucagon; call 000
- Hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS — uncommon in T1D): extreme hyperglycaemia without DKA; hospitalisation

**Urgent endocrinology referral or contact:**
- New diagnosis T1D — endocrinologist + diabetes educator for insulin initiation
- DKA recovery — specialist review and regimen optimisation
- Recurrent severe hypoglycaemia or hypoglycaemia unawareness — intensive CGM and regimen review
- Poorly controlled T1D (HbA1c >75 mmol/mol) despite engagement — specialist intensification

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Endocrinologist: annual or as needed for complex T1D; insulin pump initiation; closed-loop setup
- Diabetes educator: carbohydrate counting, sick day management, insulin adjustment education
- Ophthalmologist: retinal screening (≥5 years T1D in adults; at diagnosis in children)
- Dietitian: carbohydrate counting, nutritional planning
- Podiatrist: foot care, neuropathy — annual or more frequent if high risk

## Telehealth Suitability

T1D monitoring is highly suitable for telehealth in stable patients.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- HbA1c and complication result review
- CGM data download and review (FreeStyle LibreLink, Dexcom Clarity — shared remotely)
- Insulin dose discussion and review
- Hypoglycaemia assessment and education
- Sick day management education
- Driving and lifestyle counselling

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Foot examination (monofilament, pulse palpation)
- Injection site assessment for lipohypertrophy
- Blood pressure measurement
- Blood tests — pathology collection
