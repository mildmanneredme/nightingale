## Recurrent UTI Assessment (RACGP)

Recurrent urinary tract infection (RUTI) is defined as ≥2 culture-proven UTIs in 6 months, or ≥3 in 12 months. It is predominantly a condition affecting women — lifetime risk of at least one UTI is 50–60% in women; approximately 25–30% of women who have had one UTI will have a recurrence. RUTI in women is most commonly due to re-ascending infection from periurethral flora (Escherichia coli causes 75–85% of cases) rather than treatment failure. RUTI in men is uncommon and usually indicates underlying structural or functional urological pathology requiring investigation. In older women, RUTI is associated with post-menopausal oestrogen deficiency causing atrophic urogenital tissue. GPs play a central role in: confirming true RUTI (culture-proven), identifying modifiable risk factors, selecting appropriate antibiotic prophylaxis strategies, and referring to urology for investigation of structural abnormalities. Telehealth is well-suited to RUTI management and prophylaxis review.

## Clinical Presentation

**UTI symptoms (acute uncomplicated lower UTI):**
- Dysuria: burning or pain during urination — most specific symptom
- Urinary frequency and urgency: increased urge to urinate; small frequent voids
- Suprapubic pain or discomfort
- Cloudy or malodorous urine
- Haematuria: blood in urine — common with UTI; requires investigation if persisting after treatment

**Recurrent UTI patterns:**
- Relapse: recurrence with the same organism within 2 weeks of completing treatment; suggests treatment failure, resistant organism, or structural issue (renal stones, obstructive uropathy)
- Reinfection: most common pattern; different organism or same organism after negative culture interval; normal bladder/kidney anatomy; most female RUTI falls here

**Complex vs uncomplicated UTI:**
- Uncomplicated: healthy non-pregnant adult woman; no structural or functional abnormality
- Complicated: males; pregnancy; catheter-associated; abnormal urinary tract (stone, obstruction, vesicoureteric reflux); immunocompromised; diabetes; recent urological procedure; symptoms of upper tract (loin pain, fever)

**Risk factors for recurrent UTI in women:**
- Sexual activity: post-coital UTI is very common; relationship between intercourse and UTI onset
- Spermicide use and diaphragm: alter vaginal flora
- Post-menopausal: oestrogen deficiency → atrophic urethral and vaginal mucosa → reduced colonisation resistance
- Anatomical: short urethra; bladder prolapse; pelvic floor dysfunction
- Incomplete bladder emptying: post-void residual >50 mL
- Previous UTI history: strongest risk factor for recurrence
- Constipation: in children especially; impacted stool compresses bladder
- Urinary catheters: catheter-associated UTI

## Assessment Approach

**Confirming true RUTI:**
- Mid-stream urine (MSU) culture: essential before diagnosing RUTI; confirm each episode with culture — NOT just dipstick; avoid overdiagnosis
- Dipstick leucocytes and nitrites: high sensitivity for UTI but not specific; asymptomatic bacteriuria (common in elderly) does NOT require treatment
- Urinalysis: haematuria, protein
- Significant bacteriuria: ≥10⁸ cfu/L on clean MSU with consistent symptoms

**Investigations to identify modifiable causes:**
- MSU C&S: document organisms and sensitivities for each episode
- Post-void residual (bladder scan or in-office ultrasound): if voiding dysfunction suspected
- Pelvic floor assessment: pelvic organ prolapse — cystocoele can impair bladder emptying
- Renal ultrasound: assess kidneys (stones, hydronephrosis, anatomical abnormality); outpatient ordering
- Urinalysis: persistent haematuria after treatment → cystoscopy to exclude bladder cancer
- STI screen (NAAT for chlamydia, gonorrhoea): in sexually active women with recurrent symptoms — STI can mimic UTI

**Indications for urological investigation:**
- RUTI in men: any recurrence — urology referral
- RUTI with haematuria (after UTI treated): cystoscopy mandatory to exclude bladder cancer
- Suspected structural abnormality: stones, obstruction, diverticulum
- Failure of antibiotic prophylaxis

## Key Differentials

- Overactive bladder (OAB): frequency and urgency WITHOUT infection (negative MSU); not UTI; anticholinergics; physiotherapy
- Interstitial cystitis / painful bladder syndrome: chronic pelvic pain + urinary symptoms; negative culture; cystoscopy; urology
- STI: chlamydia, gonorrhoea, herpes — dysuria; urethral discharge; NAAT testing
- Atrophic vaginitis/urethritis (post-menopause): urinary symptoms from oestrogen deficiency; vaginal examination; responds to topical oestrogen — may present as apparent RUTI
- Vulvodynia: vestibular pain mimicking UTI; localised pain on examination; no infection
- Bladder cancer: haematuria; recurrent apparent UTI without culture growth; cystoscopy
- Urinary incontinence with frequency: voiding diary; pelvic floor assessment

## Management Principles

**Non-pharmacological prevention (first-line for all women with RUTI):**

Behavioural strategies:
- Adequate fluid intake: 1.5–2 L/day; dilutes urine; promotes bladder flushing
- Void after sexual intercourse: reduces post-coital UTI risk significantly
- Front-to-back wiping
- Avoid spermicides and diaphragms: switch contraceptive method if using
- Avoid prolonged bladder holding: void every 3–4 hours
- Treat constipation: particularly relevant in children and elderly

Cranberry:
- Cranberry products (juice, tablets, capsules): modest evidence for reducing UTI recurrence in women; safe; consider as adjunct; Ural brand cranberry sachets available; not as effective as antibiotic prophylaxis but may be tried first for mild RUTI

**Topical vaginal oestrogen (post-menopausal women):**
- Most effective non-antibiotic prevention for post-menopausal RUTI
- Restores vaginal pH and Lactobacillus colonisation; reduces colonisation with uropathogens
- Ovestin cream (oestriol 1 mg/g): apply intravaginally daily × 2 weeks, then 2–3× weekly for maintenance; or
- Vagifem (oestradiol 10 µg vaginal tablet): BD × 2 weeks, then twice weekly
- PBS-listed (Ovestin and Vagifem): vaginal atrophy indication
- Minimal systemic absorption: generally safe even in women with history of hormone-sensitive breast cancer (discuss with oncologist for known hormone-sensitive cancer); no breast cancer risk from topical vaginal oestrogen per evidence
- No progestogen required with topical vaginal oestrogen (no systemic HRT needed)

**Antibiotic prophylaxis (when behavioural + non-antibiotic measures insufficient):**

Options:

1. Continuous low-dose prophylaxis (nightly or every other night):
   - Trimethoprim 150 mg nocte: most commonly used; PBS-listed; simple; low resistance rates in Australia (check local data); avoid if >3 months' continuous use → resistance risk
   - Nitrofurantoin 50–100 mg nocte: PBS-listed; effective; avoid in eGFR <30 (reduced excretion); avoid in elderly (pulmonary toxicity risk with prolonged use)
   - Cephalexin 125–250 mg nocte: PBS-listed; alternative; broad spectrum (higher resistance risk than trimethoprim)
   - Duration: 6–12 months; reassess need; attempt to wean

2. Post-coital prophylaxis (if UTI clearly triggered by intercourse):
   - Single dose antibiotic within 2 hours of intercourse: trimethoprim 150 mg or cephalexin 500 mg or nitrofurantoin 100 mg
   - Less antibiotic exposure than continuous; preferred if UTIs clearly post-coital
   - Equally effective as continuous for post-coital RUTI

3. Self-start therapy (patient-initiated):
   - Prescription for antibiotic course provided in advance; patient initiates treatment at first symptoms
   - MSU collected before treatment and sent; results reviewed by GP
   - Suitable for: reliable motivated patients; infrequent episodes; good access to pathology

**Treatment of acute episodes:**
- Trimethoprim 300 mg daily × 3 days (uncomplicated lower UTI in women): first-line in areas with <20% trimethoprim resistance
- Nitrofurantoin 100 mg BD modified-release × 5 days: alternative; more GI side effects
- Cephalexin 500 mg BD × 5 days: alternative; broader spectrum (avoid if not needed)
- Ciprofloxacin: NOT first-line for uncomplicated UTI (fluoroquinolone stewardship); reserve for complicated UTI or resistance
- Always send MSU C&S with each acute episode — guides prophylaxis choice

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Trimethoprim 150 mg nocte** (PBS unrestricted: UTI prophylaxis; check local E. coli resistance rates; avoid if recent trimethoprim use or known resistant cultures)
- **Nitrofurantoin (Macrobid) 100 mg MR BD × 5 days** (PBS unrestricted: UTI treatment; or 50 mg nocte for prophylaxis; avoid in eGFR <30; avoid in elderly >75 years for long-term use — pulmonary/hepatic toxicity risk with prolonged use; not suitable for pyelonephritis — inadequate tissue levels)
- **Cephalexin 500 mg BD** (PBS unrestricted: treatment; or 125–250 mg nocte prophylaxis; broader spectrum — use when trimethoprim-resistant organism identified on culture)
- **Ovestin cream (oestriol 0.1% cream)** (PBS restricted: urogenital atrophy — post-menopausal; intravaginal; first-choice non-antibiotic prevention for post-menopausal RUTI; safe; highly effective)
- **Note:** Urine culture (MSU C&S) is required to confirm each UTI episode in RUTI — do not treat recurring symptoms without culture; asymptomatic bacteriuria in non-pregnant women does NOT require treatment; common cause of unnecessary antibiotic overuse

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / urgent ED:**
- Urosepsis: fever >38.5°C + rigors + loin pain + systemically unwell — IV antibiotics (ceftriaxone or meropenem if resistant); blood cultures; hospitalisation
- Acute pyelonephritis not responding to oral antibiotics: hospital

**Urgent urology:**
- RUTI in any male patient: structural pathology likely; cystoscopy and imaging
- RUTI with persistent haematuria: cystoscopy mandatory to exclude bladder cancer
- Suspected urolithiasis on ultrasound: stone management
- Post-void residual >150 mL: urological evaluation for outflow obstruction

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Urology: female RUTI refractory to 6–12 months antibiotic prophylaxis; suspected structural abnormality; haematuria
- Gynaecology or urogynaecology: pelvic organ prolapse contributing to incomplete bladder emptying
- Pelvic floor physiotherapy: bladder dysfunction, voiding symptoms, OAB

## Telehealth Suitability

RUTI management is well-suited to telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Prophylaxis review and duration planning
- MSU result review and antibiotic selection
- Post-menopausal topical oestrogen initiation and review
- Self-start therapy education and prescription
- Cranberry and behavioural strategy counselling
- Referral coordination

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial pelvic examination (post-void residual, prolapse, atrophic vaginitis)
- Blood tests and MSU collection — pathology arranged
- Renal ultrasound referral
- Urology cystoscopy
