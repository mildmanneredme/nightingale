## Palliative Care — GP Role (RACGP)

Palliative care is active, total care of patients with life-limiting illness, focusing on relief of pain and other distressing symptoms, and on support of the patient and family. GPs are ideally placed to deliver high-quality primary palliative care for patients with cancer, organ failure (cardiac, respiratory, renal, hepatic), dementia, and other life-limiting conditions — particularly in the community and residential aged care settings. Australia has a well-developed specialist palliative care system, but the majority of symptom management, goals of care discussions, prescribing of comfort medications, and after-hours support is provided by GPs. MBS items 3001–3005 (Palliative Care Planning Items) were introduced in 2022 to support GP-delivered palliative care planning. The GP role encompasses: recognition of the palliative phase, goals of care discussions, symptom management, advance care planning, end-of-life medication prescribing, carer support, and after-death care. Telehealth is suitable for many palliative care consultations.

## Clinical Presentation

**Recognising the palliative phase — triggers for early palliative care integration:**

The "surprise question": "Would I be surprised if this patient died within 12 months?" — if no, initiate palliative care planning
- Cancer: metastatic or locally advanced disease not amenable to curative treatment; declining performance status; weight loss
- Heart failure: NYHA class III–IV; recurrent hospitalisations; declining despite optimal therapy (see `heart-failure` entry)
- COPD: FEV1 <30%; MRC dyspnoea scale 4–5; increasing oxygen dependence; frequent exacerbations (see `copd` entry)
- Renal failure: eGFR <15; decision to withdraw or not initiate dialysis (see `chronic-kidney-disease` entry)
- Dementia: advanced — unable to walk, eat, or communicate; recurrent aspiration infections (see `dementia-assessment` entry)
- Frailty: clinical frailty scale ≥7 (severely frail); multiple falls, hospitalisation

**Australia-modified Karnofsky Performance Status (AKPS) / ECOG Performance Status:**
- ECOG 3–4 / AKPS <40%: significant functional impairment; palliative focus appropriate
- Palliative Performance Scale (PPPS): specific to palliative care; guides prognosis

**Common palliative symptoms requiring management:**
- Pain: most feared; WHO analgesic ladder; opioids are appropriate and necessary
- Dyspnoea: most distressing symptom in last days; opioids effective (not just for pain)
- Nausea and vomiting: multiple causes; metoclopramide, haloperidol, ondansetron, hyoscine
- Constipation: opioid-induced constipation nearly universal — always prescribe laxatives with opioids
- Agitation and delirium (terminal restlessness): haloperidol, midazolam SC in last hours/days
- Oral secretions ("death rattle"): hyoscine hydrobromide SC; repositioning
- Anorexia: explain to family as expected; not starvation; feeding tubes not indicated in last months
- Depression and anxiety: treat aggressively — poor quality of life; sertraline, mirtazapine (fewer drug interactions in palliative care)

## Assessment Approach

**Goals of care discussion (essential — GP-led):**

Framework for conversation:
- Establish what the patient already understands about their condition
- Invite discussion of what matters most to them: "What are you most hoping for?" "What worries you most?"
- Explore preferences: preferred place of death (home vs hospital vs hospice vs residential care); resuscitation preferences; wishes regarding hospitalisation; artificial nutrition, hydration
- Document in advance care directive (ACD): legally recognised in all Australian states/territories; varies by jurisdiction — RACGP provides jurisdiction-specific resources
- Enduring power of attorney (medical/personal): designate a substitute decision-maker while patient has capacity
- DNRCPR / DNRO (Do Not Resuscitate/Do Not Resuscitate Order): discuss sensitively; document; available to paramedics via ambulance systems in most states (Medical Order, Ambulance DNR)

**Pain assessment:**
- Assess: site, character, severity (NRS 0–10), radiation, aggravating/relieving factors, temporal pattern (constant vs episodic)
- Breakthrough pain: always prescribe breakthrough opioid alongside regular opioid (dose = 1/6 of 24-hour total opioid dose)
- Neuropathic component: burning, shooting, allodynia — add adjuvant (gabapentin, amitriptyline, duloxetine)
- Bone pain (metastatic): NSAIDs (if tolerated) + opioid; palliative radiotherapy for localised bone pain

**Prognosis estimation:**
- PiPS model, PPS, AKPS: non-specific prognostic tools; weeks-months vs days prognosis changes management
- Signs of last hours/days: mottling, Cheyne-Stokes breathing, cold peripheries, reduced consciousness, altered breathing pattern, inability to swallow medications

## Key Differentials

- Reversible acute illness vs disease progression: pneumonia, UTI, medication toxicity, dehydration — treatable if appropriate to patient's goals; assess in context of goals of care
- Delirium vs terminal agitation: delirium in palliative patients is common (50–85%); may be reversible (treat cause if appropriate); terminal restlessness in last days managed with SC haloperidol ± midazolam
- Depression vs adjustment disorder vs existential distress: distinguish and treat depression if impacting quality of life
- Opioid-induced constipation vs bowel obstruction: clinical assessment; laxatives vs surgery/stenting

## Management Principles

**WHO analgesic ladder — adapted for palliative care:**

Step 1 (mild pain): paracetamol 1 g QID; NSAIDs if tolerated
Step 2 (moderate pain): low-dose opioid (codeine/tramadol or low-dose morphine); consider moving directly to Step 3 in palliative care
Step 3 (severe pain): strong opioid — morphine (first-line); oxycodone; hydromorphone; fentanyl patch (for stable pain; cannot titrate rapidly)

Opioid prescribing in palliative care:
- Morphine: oral immediate-release (Kapanol capsules, Ordine liquid) for titration; modified-release (MS Contin) for maintenance
- Start low, titrate to effect; in opioid-naive patients with cancer pain: morphine 5 mg oral Q4H + 5 mg PRN; increase 30–50% if >3 PRN doses needed in 24 hours
- Opioid rotation: if side effects (myoclonus, hallucinations, sedation at analgesic dose) — rotate to alternative opioid (equianalgesic dose with 20–30% dose reduction for incomplete cross-tolerance)
- Subcutaneous syringe driver: when patient can no longer swallow; morphine, hydromorphone, methadone SC; specialist palliative care team arranges

Symptom management (key medications):
- Haloperidol (SC/oral): nausea, agitation, delirium in palliative phase; 0.5–1.5 mg SC 8-hourly or syringe driver
- Metoclopramide: nausea; avoid in complete bowel obstruction (may worsen colic)
- Ondansetron (SC or sublingual): nausea refractory to metoclopramide
- Hyoscine butylbromide (Buscopan SC): bowel colic in obstruction; secretion drying
- Midazolam SC: terminal agitation, breathlessness refractory to opioids; syringe driver
- Dexamethasone: raised ICP, SVC obstruction, anorexia stimulation, bowel obstruction (reduces inflammation); short-term benefit
- Mirtazapine: depression + insomnia + nausea + anorexia in palliative care (multiple benefits, fewer interactions)

**Just-in-case medications (comfort pack for home palliative care):**
- GP prescribes and leaves at home for PRN use by patient, carers, or after-hours nurse
- Typically includes: morphine (or oxycodone) SC, haloperidol SC, midazolam SC, hyoscine butylbromide SC, buscopan SC
- All require S8 authority in most states; emergency access provisions exist under Therapeutic Goods Act
- Palliative care nurse or specialist to advise on specific state requirements

**MBS Palliative Care Planning Items (2022):**
- Item 3001: Palliative care planning consultation (GP); >45 minutes; requires specific components (goals of care, ACP)
- Items 3003, 3004, 3005: Ongoing review and case conference items
- Document goals of care discussions in medical records; share with family and care team

**After-death care:**
- Death certificate: GP who had clinical relationship with deceased signs MCCD (Medical Certificate Cause of Death)
- If death unexpected or cause uncertain: refer to coroner (state-specific criteria); do not sign MCCD
- Bereavement support: offer family a post-death review consultation; refer to grief counselling (see `grief-bereavement` entry)

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Morphine IR (Kapanol, Ordine — oral)** (PBS restricted: moderate-severe pain including cancer; authority required for chronic use)
- **Morphine SR (MS Contin, Kapanol capsules)** (PBS restricted: moderate-severe chronic pain; authority)
- **Oxycodone IR/CR (Endone, OxyContin)** (PBS restricted: moderate-severe pain; authority; alternative if morphine poorly tolerated)
- **Fentanyl transdermal patch (Durogesic)** (PBS restricted: chronic cancer pain; stable pain only; 72-hourly patch; not for rapid titration)
- **Haloperidol** (PBS unrestricted: psychosis, nausea — palliative use; SC or oral; 0.5–5 mg)
- **Midazolam** (S8; PBS restricted: sedation; SC in palliative care; state-specific authority for palliative sedation)
- **Note:** PDMS (Prescription Drug Monitoring System) registration required for Schedule 8 opioids; palliative care patients are an important exception category — discuss with state PDMS for appropriate management in terminal phase

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED (consider goals of care — may not be appropriate for all patients):**
- Hypercalcaemia of malignancy (confusion, nausea, polyuria, severe weakness) — IV bisphosphonate
- Spinal cord compression: sudden back pain + neurological deficit — dexamethasone 16 mg IV; urgent oncology; only if within patient's goals
- Pathological fracture: pain, deformity, inability to weight-bear — orthopaedics if appropriate to goals

**Urgent specialist palliative care:**
- Refractory pain not responding to opioid titration
- Complex symptoms in last days/hours — palliative care team or hospice support
- Request for voluntary assisted dying (VAD): discuss eligibility and process (VAD is legal in all Australian states as of 2023 — state-specific laws apply; refer to RACGP VAD resources)

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Specialist palliative care team: early referral improves quality of life; does not hasten death; can be concurrent with active treatment
- Palliative care nurse: community-based symptom management and carer support
- Social worker: financial support, family communication
- Chaplain/spiritual care: spiritual distress
- Bereavement services: offer to family after patient's death

## Telehealth Suitability

Palliative care consultations are often highly suitable for telehealth, especially for patients who cannot travel.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Goals of care discussions and advance care planning
- Symptom review and medication adjustment counselling
- MBS palliative care planning items (items 3001–3005 — rebatable via telehealth)
- Carer support and family communication
- Case conference participation
- Referral coordination (specialist palliative care, social work)
- After-hours advice for carers with just-in-case medications

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Physical examination for new or changing symptoms
- Prescribing of just-in-case medications (in-person visit preferred; prescription can be mailed)
- Death certification (must have viewed body or examined patient recently)
