## Skin Rash Assessment (RACGP)

Skin rashes encompass a broad differential diagnosis ranging from benign self-limiting conditions to life-threatening emergencies. Assessment via telehealth has limitations and a significant proportion of skin presentations will require in-person examination, dermoscopy, or dermatology referral.

## Common presentations in primary care

Conditions commonly presenting as skin rash include:
- Atopic dermatitis (eczema): chronic, pruritic, often flexural; may flare with irritants or allergens
- Contact dermatitis: localised rash at site of allergen or irritant exposure
- Tinea (dermatophyte infections): scaly, ring-shaped, often pruritic; confirmed by skin scraping/culture
- Urticaria (hives): raised, itchy wheals; may indicate allergic reaction
- Viral exanthems: widespread rashes associated with systemic viral illness
- Psoriasis: well-demarcated, scaly plaques, often on extensor surfaces

## Urgent red flags requiring immediate escalation

The following features require emergency assessment:
- Rapidly spreading rash with fever and systemic illness (possible meningococcal disease, toxic shock, necrotising fasciitis)
- Non-blanching purpuric or petechial rash — meningococcal septicaemia until proven otherwise
- Throat or tongue swelling with rash (anaphylaxis)
- Blistering or skin peeling affecting mucous membranes (possible Stevens-Johnson Syndrome)
- Rash with severe systemic symptoms (hypotension, confusion)

## Telehealth limitations

Skin assessment via telehealth is significantly limited. Photograph quality, lighting, and patient description may not capture features critical for accurate assessment. AI-assisted assessment of skin rashes should be considered preliminary and must be reviewed by the GP. Many presentations will require in-person examination.

## Management considerations

Management is highly condition-specific and requires GP assessment. General principles include:
- Identifying and removing aggravating factors (irritants, allergens)
- Emollients for dry, irritated skin conditions
- Topical treatments as guided by diagnosis (corticosteroids, antifungals, etc.) — prescribing requires GP sign-off
- Patient education on skin care and avoidance of triggers
