## Childhood Otitis Assessment (RACGP)

Otitis media (OM) is the most common childhood infection for which antibiotics are prescribed in Australia, and the most common cause of paediatric hearing loss. It encompasses a spectrum: acute otitis media (AOM — symptomatic middle ear infection), otitis media with effusion (OME/"glue ear" — fluid without acute infection), and chronic suppurative otitis media (CSOM — perforated TM with discharge). Australian Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children have among the world's highest rates of otitis media and associated hearing loss — early identification and management is a priority. Most AOM is viral; antibiotics are reserved for specific clinical criteria. Conductive hearing loss from OME is a major cause of language, speech, and learning delay in children. GPs play a central role in: diagnosing OM with pneumatic otoscopy, applying evidence-based antibiotic stewardship, monitoring hearing and language development, and coordinating ENT referral for recurrent or complicated disease. All children's management requires guardian involvement and weight-based dosing.

## Clinical Presentation

**Acute Otitis Media (AOM):**
- Otalgia: ear pain; young children hold/pull ear; irritability, unexplained crying, disturbed sleep
- Fever: common; often <39°C
- Bulging tympanic membrane: key diagnostic sign; red, opaque, or yellow; loss of landmarks; bulging outward (hallmark of AOM — distinguishes from OME)
- Hearing loss: conductive; temporary during infection
- Otorrhoea: spontaneous perforation and discharge — AOM with perforation; relieves pain; treat with antibiotics

**Otitis Media with Effusion (OME — "glue ear"):**
- Conductive hearing loss: often only symptom; child may not complain of ear pain
- Dull, retracted, or amber-coloured TM: fluid behind TM; air-fluid level or bubbles visible
- Flat tympanogram (type B): effusion confirmed on tympanometry
- May follow AOM or arise de novo
- Parental concern: speech delay, inattention, poor school performance, TV volume increased
- Usually resolves spontaneously within 3 months (70–80%)

**Chronic Suppurative Otitis Media (CSOM):**
- Persistent discharge (>2 weeks) from perforated TM
- Chronic conductive hearing loss
- Painless — distinguishes from AOM
- Very common in Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children
- Requires ENT assessment; ciprofloxacin ear drops + suction

**Severity classification (AOM):**
- Mild: unilateral; temperature <39°C; mild symptoms; age ≥2 years
- Severe: bilateral AOM; <2 years old; temperature ≥39°C; severe otalgia; AOM with perforation; immunocompromised; Aboriginal/Torres Strait Islander child (higher complication risk)

**Complications (rare but important):**
- Mastoiditis: post-auricular redness, swelling, tenderness; displaced pinna forward; urgent ENT/ED
- Meningitis: severe headache, neck stiffness, photophobia — 000
- Sensorineural hearing loss: rare; persistent conductive loss → developmental impact

## Assessment Approach

**Diagnosis:**
- Otoscopy: mandatory; pneumatic otoscopy (gold standard) — assess TM mobility (poor mobility = effusion); visualise TM colour, opacity, position (bulging = AOM; retracted = OME or chronic)
- Tympanometry: assesses middle ear pressure; type B = effusion; type C = negative pressure; type A = normal; available in some GP practices; audiological assessment
- Audiogram: formal hearing assessment by audiologist; required if OME >3 months or speech concerns

**Investigations:**
- Ear swab for C&S: if CSOM (chronic discharge); guides antibiotic choice; typically Pseudomonas aeruginosa or S. aureus in CSOM
- FBC, CRP: rarely required; if meningitis or mastoiditis suspected — hospitalise

**Hearing assessment referral:**
- All children with OME >3 months: audiological assessment; speech and language assessment
- All children with ≥3 AOM episodes in 6 months or ≥4 in 12 months: audiological assessment + ENT referral
- Language and speech concerns: hearing assessment + speech pathology

## Key Differentials

- Otitis externa: external ear canal inflammation; pain with tragus pressure; NOT middle ear; no fever (usually); topical treatment
- Foreign body: unilateral; visible on otoscopy; removal by GP
- Eustachian tube dysfunction: ear fullness, popping; type C tympanogram; not true OM
- Cholesteatoma: retraction pocket with keratin debris; may present as chronic discharge; ENT surgery
- Mastoiditis: post-auricular swelling/redness/tenderness — emergency

## Management Principles

**Antibiotic stewardship (AOM):**

Antibiotic NOT routinely required for all AOM (watchful waiting appropriate in selected cases):

Watchful waiting (observation without antibiotics) for ≥24–48 hours:
- Criteria: age ≥2 years + mild unilateral AOM + symptoms <48 hours + no otorrhoea
- Provide safety netting: return if worsening (fever ≥39°C, severe pain, signs of complications) or no improvement at 48 hours
- Delayed prescription strategy: give prescription but instruct to only fill if no improvement in 48 hours
- Rationale: most AOM resolves spontaneously; antibiotics shorten illness by 1 day; increase antimicrobial resistance

Antibiotics REQUIRED for (treat immediately):
- Age <2 years (bilateral AOM especially)
- Severe symptoms: high fever ≥39°C, severe pain, marked toxicity
- AOM with perforation and discharge
- Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander children: lower threshold for antibiotics — higher complications
- Immunocompromised
- No improvement with 48-hour watchful waiting

**Antibiotic regimens:**

First-line:
- Amoxicillin 80–90 mg/kg/day in 3 divided doses (oral) × 5 days for mild-moderate AOM; × 7–10 days for severe or age <2 years; maximum 3 g/day
- Note: higher dose (80–90 mg/kg/day) covers S. pneumoniae — higher penicillin-resistant pneumococcal rates in Australia

Amoxicillin allergy (non-anaphylactic):
- Cefalexin 25 mg/kg BD–TDS × 5–7 days

Amoxicillin failure (48–72 hours no improvement) or recurrence:
- Amoxicillin-clavulanate (Augmentin Duo Forte) 22.5/3.2 mg/kg BD × 5–10 days: adds beta-lactamase coverage; PBS-listed

Penicillin anaphylaxis:
- Azithromycin 10 mg/kg day 1, then 5 mg/kg/day days 2–5 (max 500/250 mg): cotrimoxazole alternative

AOM with perforation:
- Amoxicillin-clavulanate + ciprofloxacin 0.3% ear drops: ciprofloxacin ear drops safe even if TM perforated (unlike aminoglycoside drops)

CSOM:
- Ciprofloxacin 0.3% ear drops (Ciloxan): 2–3 drops BD–TDS × 7–14 days; suction microsuction of debris; ENT referral; topical ciprofloxacin far superior to systemic antibiotics for CSOM

**OME management:**
- Most cases: watchful waiting for 3 months — 70–80% spontaneous resolution
- Autoinflation: Otovent balloon device; child inflates balloon via nose; improves TM mobility; evidence in OME
- NO antibiotics: antibiotics not indicated for OME without acute infection
- No decongestants or antihistamines: no evidence; not recommended
- ENT referral if: OME >3 months with significant hearing loss (>25 dB) + speech/language delay or educational impact; recurrent OME
- Grommet insertion (ventilation tubes): most common paediatric ENT surgery in Australia; resolves effusion; restores hearing; inserted under general anaesthetic; indications: persistent OME >3 months + documented hearing loss + speech/language impact

**Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children — specific considerations:**
- Higher burden of severe AOM, CSOM, and hearing loss than general population
- Lower threshold for antibiotic treatment
- CSOM in Aboriginal/Torres Strait Islander children: priority for ENT assessment; ciprofloxacin drops + suction; grommets if indicated
- Hearing loss impact on language, school, and social functioning is profound in this population
- Refer early and follow up closely; ear health is a significant social determinant of health

**Pain management:**
- Paracetamol 15 mg/kg 4-hourly PRN: first-line for ear pain; important for child comfort
- Ibuprofen 5–10 mg/kg TDS PRN: alternative/adjunct; anti-inflammatory; can alternate with paracetamol
- Warm pad to ear: comfort measure

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Amoxicillin suspension (125 mg/5 mL, 250 mg/5 mL)** (PBS unrestricted: AOM; 80–90 mg/kg/day in 3 divided doses; calculate dose by child's weight; confirm weight at each consultation; dispense with oral syringe)
- **Amoxicillin-clavulanate (Augmentin Duo Forte 400/57 mg/5 mL suspension)** (PBS restricted: AOM second-line or failure; 22.5/3.2 mg/kg BD; diarrhoea common side effect — probiotics may help; PBS authority for AOM)
- **Cefalexin suspension** (PBS unrestricted: AOM alternative; 25 mg/kg BD–TDS)
- **Ciprofloxacin 0.3% ear drops (Ciloxan)** (PBS restricted: CSOM — specifically indicated; 2–3 drops BD; safe if TM perforated; far superior to systemic antibiotics for CSOM; NOT aminoglycoside drops which are ototoxic with perforation)
- **Paracetamol suspension** (PBS unrestricted: analgesia; weight-based dosing — essential; 15 mg/kg/dose)
- **Note:** Aminoglycoside ear drops (gentamicin, framycetin) are CONTRAINDICATED if TM is perforated — ototoxicity risk; always use ciprofloxacin drops for CSOM or AOM with perforation

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000:**
- Meningitis: severe headache, neck stiffness, photophobia, altered consciousness in child with AOM — immediate hospital
- Mastoiditis: post-auricular swelling, displaced pinna, high fever — ENT emergency; IV antibiotics; mastoidectomy if abscess

**Urgent ENT:**
- Mastoiditis (non-acute): post-auricular tenderness/erythema without swelling — urgent ENT review
- Complications of AOM: persistent facial nerve palsy, labyrinthitis, petrous apex involvement

**Non-urgent ENT:**
- Recurrent AOM: ≥3 episodes in 6 months or ≥4 in 12 months — ENT assessment; grommets consideration
- Persistent OME >3 months with hearing loss: ENT for grommet decision
- CSOM: all cases need ENT assessment; chronic perforation; disease control + hearing rehabilitation

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Audiologist: all children with OME >3 months; speech/language concerns; hearing assessment
- Speech pathologist: language delay secondary to hearing loss

## Telehealth Suitability

Childhood otitis management requires in-person assessment; follow-up partially suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Follow-up after AOM treatment (symptom resolution assessment)
- Parent counselling and watchful waiting guidance for OME
- Hearing test result discussion and ENT referral planning
- Safety netting education for parents

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Otoscopy (essential for AOM and OME diagnosis — cannot assess TM via telehealth)
- Pneumatic otoscopy (TM mobility assessment)
- Tympanometry
- Ear swab collection for CSOM
- Suction microsuction of debris

## Paediatric Context and Guardian Involvement

- ALL prescriptions and administration: guardian responsibility; confirm understanding of dose and schedule
- Weight-based dosing: document child's weight at every consultation; dose changes as child grows
- Ear drops administration: lay child on side; warm drops to body temperature (hold bottle in hands 1 minute); administer drops; keep head still for 2–3 minutes; guardian education is critical for compliance
- Language and learning: explain to guardians the connection between OME/hearing loss and speech, language, and school performance; motivates early referral
- School notification: if significant bilateral hearing loss — inform school for classroom accommodations (front seating, FM systems)
