## Falls Prevention Assessment (RACGP)

Falls are the leading cause of injury-related hospitalisation in older Australians, with one in three people aged 65 and over falling each year. Falls are associated with significant morbidity (hip fracture, head injury, fear of falling, functional decline) and are a major driver of premature residential care placement. The GP plays a central role in multifactorial falls risk assessment, medication review (polypharmacy and high-risk medications are the most modifiable risk factor), and coordinating targeted interventions including exercise, vision correction, and home safety modification. ACSQHC Falls Prevention Clinical Care Standard 2021 guides best practice. Falls risk assessment should be integrated into every annual health assessment for patients aged ≥65.

## Clinical Presentation

**Risk factors for falls (multifactorial assessment required):**

Intrinsic factors:
- Previous falls (strongest predictor of future falls)
- Muscle weakness and sarcopenia (lower limb weakness)
- Gait and balance impairment: shuffling gait, broad-based stance, inability to rise from chair without arms
- Dizziness: orthostatic hypotension, vestibular disorders
- Cognitive impairment: dementia significantly increases fall risk
- Vision impairment: cataracts, macular degeneration, uncorrected refractive error
- Foot problems: bunions, neuropathy, ill-fitting footwear
- Urinary urgency/incontinence: rushing to toilet

Extrinsic/medication factors (most modifiable):
- Polypharmacy (≥5 medications): each additional medication increases fall risk
- High-risk medications (STOPP criteria):
  - Psychotropics: benzodiazepines, Z-drugs, antipsychotics, antidepressants (especially TCAs)
  - Antihypertensives: excessive BP lowering, orthostatic hypotension
  - Opioids: dizziness, sedation, orthostatic hypotension
  - Alpha-blockers (tamsulosin, prazosin): orthostatic hypotension
  - Anticholinergics: cognitive impairment, sedation
  - Cardiovascular: antiarrhythmics, digoxin, nitrates
  - Antihistamines (sedating): promethazine, chlorpheniramine

Environmental hazards:
- Poor lighting, loose rugs, uneven surfaces, absence of handrails (bathroom, stairs)
- Home safety assessment: occupational therapist (OT) home visit

**Consequences of falls:**
- Hip fracture: 30-day mortality 5–8%; 1-year mortality 20–30%; residential care placement high
- Head injury: subdural haematoma in anticoagulated patients (low-threshold CT brain)
- Rib fractures, wrist fractures, soft tissue injuries
- Fear of falling (post-fall syndrome): avoidance of activity → deconditioning → more falls
- Long lie (>1 hour on floor): hypothermia, dehydration, rhabdomyolysis, pressure injury

## Assessment Approach

**Annual falls risk assessment (MBS item 701 — Health Assessment for Adults 75+; item 718 for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people):**

Falls risk screening:
- Ask directly: "Have you had any falls in the past 12 months?" "Do you feel unsteady when walking or standing?"
- 1 fall in past year → full multifactorial assessment
- Timed Up and Go Test (TUG): time to rise from chair, walk 3 m, return, sit; >12 seconds = increased fall risk
- 4-Stage Balance Test: side-by-side, semi-tandem, tandem, single-leg stance; inability = significant risk
- STRATIFY falls risk tool: validated for hospital and community; quick

**Multifactorial assessment:**
- Gait and balance: TUG, Berg Balance Scale
- Orthostatic hypotension: lying/standing BP; ≥20 mmHg systolic drop (or ≥10 mmHg diastolic) = orthostatic hypotension — medication review, hydration, compression stockings
- Vision: Snellen chart; referral to optometrist if <6/12 corrected vision; cataracts — ophthalmology
- Cognitive assessment: MMSE or MoCA
- Medication review: apply STOPP/START criteria; identify and reduce falls risk medications
- Feet and footwear: examination; refer podiatrist; recommend firm-soled supportive shoes
- Environmental assessment: refer to OT for home safety visit
- Vitamin D: deficiency increases fall and fracture risk; supplement to normal levels (see `vitamin-d-deficiency` entry)
- Bone density: DEXA if not recent; fracture risk assessment (see `osteoporosis` entry)

## Key Differentials

- Syncope causing fall: loss of consciousness precedes fall (vasovagal, cardiac); distinguish from fall with secondary head strike; ECG, Holter, tilt table testing
- Epileptic seizure causing fall: post-ictal confusion, urinary incontinence, tongue bite; neurology
- Drop attack (vertebrobasilar TIA): sudden lower limb weakness without warning in elderly; Doppler/MRA posterior circulation
- Mechanical tripping or slipping: environmental cause; no underlying medical risk factor necessarily
- Parkinson's disease: gait disorder, postural instability; examine for motor features

## Management Principles

**Multifactorial intervention (most effective — evidence from PROFET, OTAGO trials):**

Exercise (most important single intervention):
- Progressive balance and strength training: reduces falls by 23–40% (Cochrane review)
- Otago Exercise Programme: evidence-based home-based programme for older adults; physiotherapist-delivered or self-guided with handout
- Tai chi: strong evidence for balance improvement in community-dwelling elderly
- Referral to physiotherapist: exercise prescription (MBS Chronic Disease Management plan)
- Exercise physiologist: structured strength and balance program

Medication review:
- Deprescribe benzodiazepines and Z-drugs: gradual tapering plan; discuss with patient and family
- Reduce antihypertensives if orthostatic hypotension detected: target SBP ≥120 in frail elderly
- Eliminate sedating medications where possible
- STOPP/START tool: systematic medication review; consider geriatrician referral for complex polypharmacy

Home safety modification:
- OT home assessment: bathroom rails, shower chair, non-slip surfaces, removal of trip hazards, improved lighting
- MBS-funded: refer via Chronic Disease Management plan or aged care package

Vision correction:
- Refer to optometrist for annual review; cataract surgery reduces fall risk significantly
- Advise caution with new multifocal glasses (adapt with single focal for outdoor use initially)

Footwear:
- Firm heel counter, low heel, thin non-slip sole; avoid slippers
- Podiatrist referral: foot pain or deformity contributing to falls

Vitamin D supplementation:
- Target >50 nmol/L (many elderly have levels <30 nmol/L); 1000–2000 IU/day
- Reduces falls when combined with exercise; less evidence when used alone

Hip protectors:
- Reduce hip fracture impact force in residential care settings; compliance is the limiting factor; reasonable in high-risk fallers

Ambulatory aids:
- Walking stick, quad stick, rollator: individualise; OT/physiotherapist assessment for appropriate aid

**Post-fall assessment:**
- Any fall requiring medical attention: assess for injury (hip fracture — X-ray; head injury — consider CT brain especially if anticoagulated)
- Anticoagulated patients who fall: counsel on risk; do not routinely cease anticoagulation after fall without specialist review (bleeding risk from fall vs stroke/PE risk without anticoagulation)
- Long lie (>1 hour): FBC, electrolytes, CK (rhabdomyolysis), creatinine, ECG, temperature

**Residential aged care:**
- Higher-risk population: multifactorial falls risk assessment mandatory on admission
- Hip protectors, bed alarms, call bells, night lighting, non-slip surfaces
- Physiotherapy for balance and strength
- Medication review by clinical pharmacist

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Vitamin D (colecalciferol)** (PBS unrestricted: vitamin D deficiency; 1000–2000 IU daily maintenance; ergocalciferol 3000 IU weekly also used)
- **Calcium + Vitamin D (Caltrate Plus, Ostelin)** (PBS unrestricted: OTC; combined supplement in osteoporosis prevention)
- **Note:** Falls risk medications to DEPRESCRIBE: benzodiazepines (taper; Z-drugs equivalent risk), antipsychotics (taper; increase fall risk in dementia), sedating antihistamines, anticholinergics, excessive antihypertensives
- **Melatonin** (PBS restricted: insomnia in patients ≥55; safer alternative to benzodiazepines for sleep in elderly; reduces fall risk compared with Z-drugs and benzodiazepines)
- **Note:** There is no single pharmacological agent to prescribe for falls prevention; intervention is primarily non-pharmacological plus medication reduction

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Fall with suspected hip fracture (pain + inability to weight-bear + shortened, externally rotated limb) — 000; X-ray; orthopaedic surgery
- Fall with head injury in anticoagulated patient — CT brain urgently even if initially asymptomatic (subdural haematoma may be delayed)
- Long lie with hypothermia, confusion, rhabdomyolysis — 000
- Fall with loss of consciousness (syncope cause) — ECG, cardiac monitoring

**Urgent review:**
- Multiple recurrent falls (≥3/year) — multifactorial assessment immediately; geriatrician or falls clinic referral
- Fall with rib fractures — pain management; respiratory physiotherapy; risk of pneumonia

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Physiotherapist: balance and strength exercise program (Otago, Tai chi)
- Exercise physiologist: structured falls prevention exercise
- Occupational therapist: home safety assessment and modification
- Optometrist: annual vision assessment; cataract referral if indicated
- Podiatrist: foot care, footwear advice
- Geriatrician: complex polypharmacy, recurrent falls, frailty assessment
- Falls clinic: hospital-based multidisciplinary falls clinic (where available)

## Telehealth Suitability

Falls prevention is partially suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Falls risk history and medication review
- Deprescribing counselling (benzodiazepines, Z-drugs, antipsychotics)
- Vitamin D result review and supplementation prescribing
- Referral coordination (physio, OT, optometrist, podiatrist)
- Exercise and lifestyle counselling
- Carer support and education

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- TUG test and balance assessment
- Lying/standing blood pressure
- Physical examination
- Vision assessment — optometrist
- Home safety assessment — OT in-person visit
