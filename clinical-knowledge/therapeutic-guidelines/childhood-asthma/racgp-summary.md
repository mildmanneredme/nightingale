## Childhood Asthma Assessment (RACGP)

Asthma is the most common chronic disease in Australian children, affecting approximately 1 in 9 children under 15 years of age. Australia has among the highest asthma prevalence rates globally. It is the most common reason for paediatric emergency department presentations and hospitalisations. Childhood asthma has distinct clinical features from adult asthma — particularly in preschool-age children where episodic viral wheeze (triggered predominantly by colds) is the most common pattern. GPs play a central role in: diagnosing childhood asthma, developing written asthma action plans, prescribing and reviewing controller therapy, educating families in inhaler technique and trigger avoidance, and managing acute exacerbations. GPs must involve parents/guardians and use weight-based dosing. Telehealth is suitable for review and ongoing management; acute severe exacerbations require 000 and emergency attendance. This entry covers childhood asthma specifically; adult asthma is covered in the `asthma` entry.

## Clinical Presentation

**Diagnostic features:**
- Episodic wheeze: recurrent expiratory wheeze — bilateral, polyphonic
- Cough: particularly nocturnal; dry, persistent
- Shortness of breath: exercise limitation; child may avoid physical activity
- Chest tightness
- Symptoms triggered by: viral URTI (most common trigger in children), exercise, allergens, cold air, cigarette smoke, pet dander, dust mites
- Symptom pattern: typically episodic with periods of no symptoms between episodes (distinguishes from other causes)
- Partial or complete response to bronchodilator (salbutamol trial): important diagnostic feature

**Age-specific patterns:**

Under 5 years (preschool — viral episodic wheeze most common):
- Viral-induced wheeze: wheeze occurring with cold/URTI only; symptom-free between episodes; many outgrow by school age
- Multiple-trigger wheeze: wheeze with multiple triggers (exercise, allergens, cold, viral); more likely to persist as asthma
- Spirometry not reliable under 5 years — diagnosis based on clinical pattern and salbutamol response

5 years and older (school-age — classic asthma pattern):
- More consistent exercise-induced symptoms; allergen triggers (dust mite, pets, grass pollen)
- Spirometry feasible (FEV1 <80% predicted; FEV1/FVC ratio <0.75; reversibility with salbutamol — ≥12% increase)
- FeNO (fractional exhaled nitric oxide): high FeNO suggests eosinophilic airway inflammation; predicts steroid response

**Severity (in-between flares — assess on preventer therapy or without treatment):**
- Intermittent: symptoms ≤2 days/week; ≤2 nights/month; no limitation between episodes
- Mild persistent: symptoms 3–6 days/week; ≥3 nights/month; some limitation
- Moderate persistent: daily symptoms; ≥1 night/week; moderate limitation
- Severe persistent: continuous symptoms; frequent nocturnal; severely limited

**Acute exacerbation severity:**
- Mild: SpO2 ≥95%; talking normally; mild wheeze; salbutamol responsive; no use of accessory muscles
- Moderate: SpO2 92–95%; limited speech; marked wheeze; accessory muscles; partial response to salbutamol
- Severe: SpO2 <92%; unable to speak; severe wheeze or silent chest; severe accessory muscle use; poor salbutamol response
- Life-threatening: SpO2 <90%; cyanosis; exhaustion; altered consciousness; silent chest

## Assessment Approach

**Diagnosis:**
- Clinical history: symptom pattern, triggers, atopy history (eczema, allergic rhinitis), family history of asthma, response to salbutamol
- Spirometry (≥5 years): FEV1, FVC, reversibility post-salbutamol (≥12% improvement)
- Salbutamol trial (any age): prescribe 4–6 puffs via spacer; assess response at 20 minutes; positive response supports diagnosis
- Chest X-ray: hyperinflation in acute attack; not routine; exclude foreign body (sudden onset, unilateral wheeze) or pneumonia
- Allergy testing (RAST or skin prick): if allergic triggers suspected (cat, dog, house dust mite, grass pollen); relevant for allergen avoidance planning
- FeNO: specialised centres; predicts inhaled corticosteroid response

**Review at each visit:**
- Symptom control (Childhood Asthma Control Test — c-ACT for 4–11 years; ACT for ≥12 years): validated scores
- Inhaler technique: demonstrate and observe at EVERY visit; most common cause of treatment failure is poor technique
- Spacer use: essential for children; reduces oropharyngeal deposition; improves lung delivery; Volumatic, Able Spacer, AeroChamber
- Adherence: frequency of reliever use; number of controller doses missed
- Written Asthma Action Plan: review and update; available at nationalasthma.org.au
- Trigger identification: exposure to smoke, allergens, exercise
- School impact: days missed, physical education participation

**Acute exacerbation assessment:**
- Vital signs: respiratory rate, heart rate, SpO2, conscious state
- Accessory muscle use, nasal flaring, subcostal retractions
- Air entry, wheeze quality, silent chest (severe — no airflow)
- Peak expiratory flow (PEF): >5 years; % predicted compared to personal best

## Key Differentials

- Viral bronchiolitis: <2 years; RSV (respiratory syncytial virus); rhinitis, cough, wheeze; preceding URTI; no response to salbutamol (not asthma); supportive care
- Croup (laryngotracheobronchitis): inspiratory stridor (not expiratory wheeze); barking cough; hoarse voice; dexamethasone/nebulised adrenaline
- Foreign body inhalation: sudden onset wheeze, often unilateral; history of aspiration; chest X-ray; bronchoscopy
- Tracheomalacia / laryngomalacia: infant wheeze; stridor; structural; ENT assessment
- Cardiac cause (congenital): wheeze unresponsive to salbutamol; murmur; poor feeding; echocardiogram
- Cystic fibrosis: recurrent chest infections, failure to thrive, fatty stools; sweat test
- Vocal cord dysfunction: inspiratory stridor; adolescent girls; anxiety association; spirometry flattened inspiratory loop; speech therapy

## Management Principles

**Preventer (controller) therapy — stepwise approach:**

Step 1 — Intermittent mild symptoms (<5 years):
- Salbutamol reliever as needed only
- Written Asthma Action Plan

Step 2 — Low-dose ICS (inhaled corticosteroid):
- Budesonide 200 µg/day (100 µg BD via Turbuhaler or spacer) — first-line for children ≥5 years with mild persistent asthma
- Fluticasone propionate 100 µg/day (50 µg BD via MDI with spacer)
- Montelukast (Singulair): PBS-listed leukotriene receptor antagonist; alternative first-line for mild persistent asthma especially in young children unable to use inhaler device; 4 mg nocte (<5 years), 5 mg nocte (5–11 years); PBS-listed for asthma
- Note: montelukast black box warning (FDA 2020): neuropsychiatric events (nightmares, behaviour changes, suicidal thoughts) — discuss with parents; cease if occurs; RACGP recommends discussing this at initiation

Step 3 — Medium-dose ICS or low-dose ICS + LABA:
- Increase ICS dose: budesonide 400 µg/day or fluticasone 200 µg/day
- Add salmeterol (Seretide combination) in children ≥5 years if low-dose ICS insufficient
- LABA NOT recommended in children <5 years as monotherapy

Step 4 — High-dose ICS + LABA ± specialist referral:
- Specialist (paediatric respiratory) involvement recommended
- Consider: omalizumab (anti-IgE; PBS-listed for severe allergic asthma ≥6 years), mepolizumab (anti-IL5; specialist)

**Reliever therapy:**
- Salbutamol (Ventolin) MDI with spacer: 4–6 puffs (100 µg/puff) via spacer; wait 30 seconds between puffs; use spacer for ALL children with MDI
- Dose by age/weight for acute use: 2.5 mg nebulised or 4–6 puffs MDI for mild-moderate exacerbation; 10 puffs every 20 minutes in severe; nebuliser in severe
- Continue until symptoms resolve; if using reliever >3 times/week — review preventer therapy

**Spacer use — essential for children:**
- ALL children using MDI must use a spacer
- AeroChamber (with mask for <4 years; without for ≥4 years) or Able Spacer
- Monthly washing with warm water; allow to air dry; replace every 6–12 months
- Demonstrate technique at every consultation — common cause of treatment failure

**Written Asthma Action Plan (WAP):**
- All patients must have a WAP
- Three zones (green/amber/red): based on symptoms and/or peak flow
- Specifies: trigger avoidance, preventer dose, reliever dose for flares, when to seek medical care, when to call 000
- Available free at nationalasthma.org.au; GP completes and provides to patient and school/childcare

**Acute exacerbation management:**
- Mild: 4–6 puffs salbutamol via spacer; reassess in 20 minutes; repeat if partial response; education and action plan review
- Moderate: 4–6 puffs salbutamol every 20 minutes × 3 doses; add prednisolone 1 mg/kg/day (max 50 mg) × 3–5 days; admit if poor response; call ahead to ED
- Severe/life-threatening: 000; high-flow oxygen; continuous salbutamol nebulisation; IV/IM adrenaline if failing; parenteral magnesium sulfate; ICU

**Trigger avoidance:**
- Tobacco smoke: absolute avoidance; smoking cessation for household members
- House dust mite: mattress covers, pillow covers; wash bedding weekly ≥60°C; reduce soft toys in bedroom
- Pet allergens: reduce exposure if sensitised; ideally remove pet from home
- Pollen: limit outdoor activity during high pollen season; change clothes after outdoor play
- Exercise-induced: pre-exercise salbutamol 2 puffs 15 minutes before; warm-up; swimming excellent choice

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Salbutamol (Ventolin) 100 µg MDI** (PBS unrestricted: asthma reliever; for all ages with spacer; 4–6 puffs per dose; Accuhaler alternative for ≥7 years who can use correctly)
- **Budesonide (Pulmicort) 200 µg/dose Turbuhaler** (PBS restricted: persistent asthma; ≥5 years; 200–400 µg/day; spacer required for MDI formulations in children)
- **Fluticasone propionate (Flixotide) 50–125 µg MDI** (PBS restricted: persistent asthma; with spacer; 100–250 µg/day)
- **Montelukast (Singulair) 4 mg granules (2–5 years), 5 mg chewable (6–14 years)** (PBS restricted: asthma; alternative or add-on to ICS; discuss neuropsychiatric black box warning; 4–5 mg nocte by age)
- **Prednisolone (1 mg/kg/day, max 50 mg)** (PBS unrestricted: oral prednisolone for acute exacerbation; liquid formulation for young children; 3–5 day course; no tapering required for short courses)
- **Fluticasone/salmeterol (Seretide) combination**: step 3; ≥5 years; paediatric doses; not first-line in children; add-on to ICS only

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000:**
- Severe or life-threatening asthma: SpO2 <92%, silent chest, cyanosis, altered consciousness, exhausted child, poor response to repeat salbutamol — 000; high-flow oxygen; continuous nebulisation; paramedic treatment en route
- Child not responding after 6 puffs salbutamol × 3 doses: call 000; do not delay

**Urgent ED:**
- Moderate exacerbation not responding to 2 rounds of 6 puffs salbutamol: oral prednisolone + ED
- SpO2 <95% not improving

**Urgent paediatric respiratory specialist:**
- Poorly controlled asthma on Step 3 therapy: paediatric respiratory physician
- Suspected severe or difficult-to-treat asthma: specialist assessment; consider alternative diagnoses

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Paediatric respiratory specialist: if poorly controlled on Step 2–3; diagnostic uncertainty; severe asthma
- Paediatric allergist/immunologist: severe allergic asthma; consideration of omalizumab; allergy desensitisation
- Paediatric physiotherapist: breathing pattern disorders, vocal cord dysfunction co-existing

## Telehealth Suitability

Childhood asthma monitoring is suitable for telehealth; acute assessment requires in-person or emergency.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Routine review: symptom control assessment (c-ACT), medication compliance, trigger review
- Asthma Action Plan review and update
- Montelukast initiation and neuropsychiatric monitoring
- Preventer dose adjustment in stable patients
- Parent and carer education (spacer use demonstrated via video)
- Referral coordination

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Acute exacerbation assessment (SpO2, respiratory rate, auscultation)
- Spirometry (≥5 years)
- Initial diagnosis in young or atypical presentations
- Inhaler technique observation (can be partially assessed via video)
- Blood tests and FeNO testing

## Paediatric Context and Guardian Involvement

- All asthma management requires guardian engagement: prescriptions, administration, action plans
- Inhaler administration for children <5 years: parent/guardian administers all doses
- Children 5–12 years: supervised administration; parent checks technique regularly
- Written Asthma Action Plan: copy to school/childcare, parent, and child's file; key contact number on plan
- Weight-based dosing for acute presentations: prednisolone 1 mg/kg/day (max 50 mg); salbutamol nebulisation (2.5 mg <20 kg; 5 mg ≥20 kg)
- Developmental appropriateness: assess understanding at each age; involve child in management discussions from ~8 years; self-management from ~12 years with supervision
