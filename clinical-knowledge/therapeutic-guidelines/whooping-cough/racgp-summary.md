## Whooping Cough (Pertussis) Assessment (RACGP)

Pertussis (whooping cough) is a highly contagious respiratory infection caused by Bordetella pertussis. Despite widespread vaccination, pertussis remains endemic in Australia with cyclical outbreaks every 3–4 years. It is one of the most common vaccine-preventable diseases in Australia and is notifiable in all states and territories. Disease severity is greatest in infants <6 months (too young to be fully vaccinated) who are at risk of life-threatening complications including apnoea, pneumonia, seizures, and death. Adults and adolescents are the primary reservoir — often presenting with atypical or mild illness — and are the major source of infection for vulnerable infants. The GP plays a central role in diagnosis, notification, antibiotic treatment, contact tracing, and vaccination promotion. Telehealth may be used for follow-up and vaccination counselling.

## Clinical Presentation

**Classic pertussis (infant and child — fully susceptible):**
- Catarrhal phase (1–2 weeks): non-specific URTI symptoms — runny nose, mild cough, low-grade fever; highly infectious
- Paroxysmal phase (2–6 weeks): severe paroxysms of rapid coughing ending in an inspiratory whoop; post-tussive vomiting; cyanosis during paroxysms; apnoea in infants; night-time cough worse
- Convalescent phase: gradual reduction in cough severity over weeks to months ("100-day cough")

**Atypical pertussis (adults and partially immune vaccinees — most GP presentations):**
- Persistent cough >2–3 weeks without whoop
- Post-tussive vomiting (suggestive)
- Paroxysmal cough (classically worsens at night)
- Cough syncope
- Absence of fever or other URTI features by paroxysmal phase
- No inspiratory whoop in most adults
- Classic features insufficient to exclude pertussis — laboratory confirmation required

**Danger signs requiring 000 / hospital admission:**
- Any infant <6 months with confirmed or suspected pertussis — hospital admission
- Apnoea, cyanosis, or severe respiratory distress in any age
- Very young infant with coughing paroxysms even without confirmed pertussis

## Assessment Approach

**Diagnostic testing:**
- Pertussis PCR (nasopharyngeal swab or nasal wash): most sensitive; positive within first 3–4 weeks of cough onset; preferred test in acute phase
- Pertussis serology (IgA or IgG anti-PT): useful after 4 weeks of illness (when PCR often negative); single high titre or rising titre; less useful in recently vaccinated patients (elevated antibody from vaccine)
- Culture: high specificity, low sensitivity; not routinely used
- Clinical diagnosis alone insufficient; laboratory confirmation required for notification and contact tracing
- Do not delay treatment if clinical suspicion is high — treat empirically while awaiting results

**Notification:**
- Pertussis is a notifiable disease in all Australian states and territories
- Notify state/territory health authority immediately upon confirmed diagnosis and clinical suspicion in high-risk settings (maternity wards, childcare)
- Contact tracing — public health unit will assist; GPs should identify household contacts and close contacts with unvaccinated infants

## Key Differentials

- Mycoplasma pneumoniae: "walking pneumonia"; persistent cough; low-grade fever; bilateral infiltrates on CXR; doxycycline or azithromycin
- COVID-19: PCR or RAT testing; cough ± fever; contact/travel history
- Viral URTI post-infectious cough: resolves within 4–8 weeks; no whoop; pertussis serology to distinguish in prolonged cough
- Asthma: episodic wheeze; bronchodilator responsive; peak flow variability; see `asthma` entry
- GORD: chronic cough triggered by acid reflux; worse supine; see `gord` entry
- ACE inhibitor cough: dry persistent cough; medication history; resolves with cessation (4–6 weeks)
- Foreign body aspiration: sudden onset in children; unilateral wheeze; CXR; urgent bronchoscopy
- Croup: barking cough, stridor in young children; see `croup` entry

## Management Principles

**Antibiotic treatment:**

Azithromycin (first-line — all age groups):
- Adults: azithromycin 500 mg day 1, then 250 mg daily days 2–5 (5-day course)
- Children: azithromycin 10 mg/kg/day (max 500 mg) for 5 days
- Reduces infectivity and duration if started in catarrhal phase; minimal benefit on cough duration if started in paroxysmal phase, but still recommended to reduce transmission

Clarithromycin (alternative):
- Adults: 500 mg BD × 7 days
- Children: 7.5 mg/kg/dose BD × 7 days

Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (TMP-SMX — alternative if macrolide contraindicated):
- Adults: TMP 160 mg / SMX 800 mg BD × 14 days
- Do not use azithromycin in neonates <1 month — risk of infantile hypertrophic pyloric stenosis (IHPS); use trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole

Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) for close contacts:
- Same antibiotic regimen as treatment
- Recommended for: household contacts with infants <12 months, pregnant women in third trimester, immunocompromised people, healthcare workers in high-risk settings
- PEP within 21 days of exposure reduces risk of disease

**Supportive care:**
- Pertussis cough cannot be significantly reduced by any medication once in paroxysmal phase
- Avoid cough suppressants (ineffective and potentially harmful in children)
- Adequate hydration; small frequent meals to reduce vomiting
- Limit triggers: cold air, exercise, feeding (which precipitate paroxysms)
- Hospital admission: infants <6 months; any infant with apnoea or cyanosis; severe respiratory compromise

**Exclusion from childcare and school:**
- Exclude until 5 days of antibiotic treatment completed OR (if untreated) 21 days after onset of cough

**Vaccination — critical public health measure:**
- Childhood NIP immunisation schedule: dTpa (diphtheria-tetanus-acellular pertussis) at 2, 4, 6 months; 18 months; 4 years; 11–13 years
- Adult booster: dTpa (Boostrix/Adacel) single dose for adults who have not had one in last 10 years; included in free NIP for adults in outbreak areas
- Pregnancy: dTpa vaccine at 20–32 weeks gestational age (ideally 28 weeks) — NIP-funded; provides maternal antibody transfer to infant before infant can be vaccinated — most important single intervention to prevent infant pertussis deaths
- Cocooning: vaccinate all household members (grandparents, siblings) of newborns who have not had recent dTpa

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Azithromycin** (PBS unrestricted: respiratory tract infections; pertussis is a listed indication; 500 mg stat then 250 mg daily × 4 days, or as per guidance)
- **Clarithromycin** (PBS unrestricted: respiratory infections; 500 mg BD × 7 days)
- **Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (Bactrim)** (PBS unrestricted: bacterial infections; 160/800 mg BD × 14 days; avoid in neonates)
- **dTpa vaccine (Boostrix, Adacel)** (NIP-funded: pregnancy 20–32 weeks; free for household contacts of infants in some states; private script at cost if not NIP-eligible)
- **Note:** Early treatment is most effective; treat empirically in high clinical suspicion while awaiting PCR; notify public health unit

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Any infant <6 months with confirmed or suspected pertussis — hospital admission for monitoring
- Apnoea or cyanosis during coughing paroxysm
- Respiratory distress, inability to feed, severe dehydration
- Suspected pertussis encephalopathy (seizures, altered consciousness)

**Urgent same-day assessment:**
- High clinical suspicion in household with unvaccinated infant — urgent pertussis testing and prophylaxis coordination
- Failure to improve on antibiotics in first 48 hours

**Public health:**
- Notify state/territory health authority: pertussis is notifiable; notify upon confirmed diagnosis and upon suspected clinical diagnosis in high-risk settings
- Close contact management: household contacts with at-risk infants — coordinate PEP and vaccination catch-up with public health unit

## Telehealth Suitability

Pertussis consultations are partially suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Follow-up of confirmed pertussis in adults — symptom monitoring, treatment completion
- Post-exposure prophylaxis prescribing for low-risk contacts
- Vaccination counselling (pregnancy, household contacts, catch-up)
- Notification coordination discussion

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Nasopharyngeal swab collection (PCR) — clinic or collection centre
- Assessment of infants — in-person examination; consider hospital referral
- Severe respiratory symptoms — in-person assessment mandatory
