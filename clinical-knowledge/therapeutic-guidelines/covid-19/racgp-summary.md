## COVID-19 Management (RACGP)

COVID-19 (coronavirus disease 2019) is caused by SARS-CoV-2 and has been the dominant respiratory viral infection since 2020. In 2023 and beyond, COVID-19 is increasingly managed as an endemic seasonal respiratory illness in Australian general practice, with ongoing surveillance and treatment of high-risk patients. Most COVID-19 infections in immunised Australians are mild and self-limiting; however, high-risk populations (older adults, immunocompromised, severe chronic disease) remain at risk of serious illness including hospitalisation and death. GPs have a central role in: identifying high-risk patients eligible for antiviral therapy, managing acute mild illness, assessing for Long COVID, and coordinating COVID-19 vaccination. Telehealth is highly suitable for COVID-19 assessment and management.

## Clinical Presentation

**Acute COVID-19 (Omicron-era predominant — 2023 onwards):**
- Upper respiratory symptoms: sore throat, nasal congestion, rhinorrhoea, hoarse voice
- Systemic: fever, fatigue, myalgia, headache
- Cough: dry or productive
- Loss of taste and smell (anosmia/ageusia): less common than earlier variants but still occurs
- GI symptoms: nausea, diarrhoea — more common with Omicron subvariants

**Severity classification:**
- Mild: no dyspnoea or hypoxia; home management appropriate for low-risk patients
- Moderate: dyspnoea or SpO2 90–93%; close monitoring ± antiviral; some may require hospital
- Severe: SpO2 <90%, severe dyspnoea, altered consciousness — hospital admission required

**High-risk patients (eligible for antiviral therapy):**
- Adults ≥70 years (≥50 for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples)
- Severely immunocompromised (haematological malignancy, transplant, high-dose immunosuppressants, HIV with low CD4)
- Disability with significant functional impairment
- Some complex chronic conditions with high risk of progression

**Long COVID / post-COVID syndrome:** Symptoms persisting or arising >4 weeks after acute infection; see `long-covid` entry.

## Assessment Approach

**Diagnosis:**
- Rapid antigen test (RAT): home testing; widely available; sensitivity ~80–90% (lower sensitivity in Omicron with RAT); positive RAT is diagnostic and sufficient to initiate treatment
- PCR: higher sensitivity; available via pathology or hospital; required for some clinical contexts (immunocompromised, pre-hospitalisation)
- Clinical diagnosis: during periods of high community prevalence in symptomatic patient, clinical diagnosis acceptable if RAT unavailable

**Risk stratification:**
- Age: ≥70 years is single strongest risk factor for severe disease; ≥50 in ATSI
- Vaccination status: 3+ doses significantly reduce severe disease risk
- Comorbidities: chronic respiratory disease, cardiovascular disease, diabetes, obesity, CKD, immunosuppression
- Functional status: disability, cognitive impairment — relevant to prognosis and monitoring capacity

**Assessment in high-risk patients:**
- SpO2 (pulse oximetry): essential; SpO2 <93% warrants urgent review; home oximeter recommended for high-risk patients during illness
- Symptom monitoring: breathlessness on minimal exertion; confusion; persistent fever >5 days; inability to maintain oral hydration
- Blood tests (selected cases): FBC, CRP, LFT, UEC, D-dimer — may be performed to guide management in moderate-severe illness

## Key Differentials

- Influenza: clinically indistinguishable; combined COVID-19/influenza RAT available; management of antivirals applies to both
- Streptococcal pharyngitis: predominantly throat features; throat swab; treat with amoxicillin if confirmed
- Other viral URTI: rhinovirus, RSV — no specific treatment; COVID-19 RAT negative
- Bacterial pneumonia: consolidation on CXR; antibiotics required; see `community-acquired-pneumonia` entry

## Management Principles

**Mild COVID-19 in low-risk patients:**
- Supportive care: rest, fluids, antipyretics (paracetamol, ibuprofen)
- Isolation: current state/territory health department guidance on isolation period (may vary — generally while symptomatic + 24 hours after fever resolution; guidance evolves — check health.gov.au for current recommendation)
- No specific antiviral required
- Monitor for deterioration: SpO2 daily if patient has home oximeter; warning signs education (dyspnoea, SpO2 drop, confusion)

**Antiviral therapy for high-risk patients (start as early as possible — ideally within 5 days of symptom onset):**

Nirmatrelvir-ritonavir (Paxlovid):
- Oral antiviral; nirmatrelvir 300 mg + ritonavir 100 mg twice daily for 5 days
- Significant drug interactions: ritonavir is a potent CYP3A4 inhibitor — interactions with statins (especially simvastatin — must hold during course), immunosuppressants (tacrolimus, cyclosporine — dose management critical), antiarrhythmics, many others; use drug interaction checker (Liverpool COVID-19 Drug Interaction Checker at covid19-druginteractions.org) before prescribing
- Contraindicated: severe hepatic impairment; CYP3A4-dependent drugs that cannot be safely withheld; eGFR <30 (dose reduction required at eGFR 30–60)
- PBS restricted: high-risk patients meeting criteria; authority required

Molnupiravir (Lagevrio):
- Oral antiviral; 800 mg twice daily for 5 days
- Mutagenic mechanism; avoid in pregnancy and breastfeeding; not for children
- Fewer drug interactions than Paxlovid
- PBS restricted: high-risk patients not suitable for Paxlovid; authority required

Remdesivir: IV antiviral — hospital setting; outpatient use in selected severe high-risk cases; specialist supervised.

**COVID-19 vaccination:**
- Annual COVID-19 vaccine recommended for all Australians ≥6 months; particularly important in high-risk groups
- Bivalent/updated vaccines targeting current circulating strains recommended
- NIP-funded for priority groups; GP can administer and record on AIR (Australian Immunisation Register)
- Discuss and offer at every relevant consultation

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Nirmatrelvir-ritonavir** (Paxlovid — PBS restricted: COVID-19 treatment for high-risk patients; 5-day course; authority required; check drug interactions before prescribing — multiple significant interactions with common medications)
- **Molnupiravir** (Lagevrio — PBS restricted: COVID-19 treatment for high-risk patients not suitable for Paxlovid; 5-day course; authority required; avoid in pregnancy)
- **Paracetamol / ibuprofen** (PBS unrestricted — symptomatic management)
- **COVID-19 vaccines** (NIP-funded for eligible groups; not PBS; administer and record via AIR; annual recommendations updated)
- **Note:** Drug interactions with Paxlovid are complex and potentially serious. Always use an interaction checker (Liverpool COVID-19 Drug Interactions Checker) before prescribing. Common interactions include: statins (hold simvastatin/lovastatin during course; can continue rosuvastatin at lower dose), blood thinners, some antiepileptics, some cardiac drugs.

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- SpO2 <90% or progressive hypoxia
- Dyspnoea at rest or with minimal activity
- Altered consciousness, confusion
- Chest pain
- Persistent vomiting preventing medication/fluid intake
- Signs of sepsis (rigors, haemodynamic instability)

**Urgent hospital referral or same-day assessment:**
- SpO2 90–93% in high-risk patient — close monitoring and consider hospitalisation
- Immunocompromised patient with moderate symptoms — lower threshold for hospitalisation
- Unable to take Paxlovid due to interactions + high-risk — IV remdesivir assessment

**Telehealth follow-up (24–48 hours):**
- All high-risk patients started on antiviral therapy — check for side effects (Paxlovid: dysgeusia "metallic taste"), treatment completion, symptom trajectory
- Any patient with worsening SpO2 — escalate

## Telehealth Suitability

COVID-19 management is highly suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- RAT-confirmed diagnosis and risk stratification
- Paxlovid or molnupiravir prescribing (drug interaction check performed by prescriber before prescription)
- Supportive care advice and monitoring instructions
- Isolation guidance (current state/territory rules — check health.gov.au)
- SpO2 monitoring education (home oximeter use)
- COVID-19 vaccination counselling and referral
- Long COVID assessment (see separate entry)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- SpO2 measurement if patient without home oximeter and dyspnoea suspected
- Moderate or severe presentations — clinical examination and hospital referral
- Blood tests if indicated
