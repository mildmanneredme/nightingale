## Acute Gastroenteritis Assessment (RACGP)

Acute gastroenteritis (AGE) is a common self-limiting condition characterised by acute-onset diarrhoea with or without nausea, vomiting, and abdominal cramps. It is predominantly caused by viral pathogens in Australia (norovirus and rotavirus being the most common). Bacterial and protozoal causes are less frequent but more likely with foodborne exposure, travel history, or bloody diarrhoea. The majority of cases resolve within 1–5 days without specific treatment. Management focuses on hydration, symptom control, and identifying the minority of patients who require further investigation or hospitalisation. Telehealth is well-suited to assessment and management advice for uncomplicated presentations.

## Clinical Presentation

Key features: acute onset (hours to days) of loose or watery diarrhoea (≥3 loose stools in 24 hours), nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramps, and low-grade fever. Symptoms are typically self-limiting.

Viral gastroenteritis (norovirus, rotavirus): vomiting often prominent; diarrhoea watery; resolves in 1–3 days; highly contagious.

Bacterial gastroenteritis: fever more prominent; bloody diarrhoea more common (Campylobacter, Salmonella, Shigella, EHEC); onset typically 6–48 hours after contaminated food or water.

Food poisoning (Staphylococcal, Bacillus cereus): very short incubation (1–6 hours); prominent vomiting; short duration.

Traveller's diarrhoea: onset during or within 2 weeks of return from travel to endemic region (SE Asia, Indian subcontinent, Africa, South America); most commonly enterotoxigenic E. coli; consider Giardia, Cryptosporidium with prolonged diarrhoea (>2 weeks).

Assess dehydration severity: mild (thirst, slightly reduced urine), moderate (dry mucous membranes, reduced skin turgor, postural dizziness), severe (significant hypotension, tachycardia, confusion, oliguria).

## Assessment Approach

Clinical diagnosis in most cases; stool investigations are not routinely required for uncomplicated AGE.

Indications for stool MCS (microscopy, culture, and sensitivity) and parasitology:
- Bloody or mucoid diarrhoea
- Immunocompromised patient
- Symptoms persisting >7 days
- Recent overseas travel (include ova and parasites, Giardia antigen, Cryptosporidium)
- Suspected outbreak (foodborne illness) — notify public health unit
- Healthcare worker or food handler with occupational exposure concern

Assess for dehydration: in-person examination is preferred for moderate-to-severe dehydration assessment; telehealth clinical assessment can estimate mild dehydration from history.

Consider alternative diagnoses if: no vomiting and diarrhoea is chronic or progressive (IBD flare, coeliac, microscopic colitis), severe abdominal pain disproportionate to diarrhoea (appendicitis, bowel obstruction, ischaemia), bloody diarrhoea without infectious contact or travel history (IBD, ischaemic colitis).

Special populations: elderly patients are at higher risk of dehydration, electrolyte disturbance, and complications — lower threshold for in-person assessment or hospitalisation.

## Key Differentials

- Inflammatory bowel disease flare — known IBD history, bloody diarrhoea, systemic features, longer duration
- Irritable bowel syndrome — no fever, no blood, symptoms linked to stress or diet, chronic history
- Appendicitis — periumbilical pain migrating to RIF, anorexia, fever, localised tenderness — 000 if suspected
- Antibiotic-associated diarrhoea / C. difficile — recent antibiotic use; profuse watery diarrhoea; test stool for C. diff toxin
- Mesenteric ischaemia — severe pain disproportionate to exam findings; older patient with cardiovascular risk — emergency
- Overflow diarrhoea from constipation — especially in elderly

## Management Principles

**Oral rehydration is the cornerstone of treatment:**
- Oral rehydration solution (ORS) — preferred for replacing fluid and electrolytes lost with diarrhoea; e.g., Gastrolyte, HydraLyte (widely available OTC in Australia)
- Sports drinks and fruit juice are not ideal ORS (incorrect sodium/glucose ratio)
- Broth, water, and dilute apple juice are reasonable alternatives for mild dehydration in adults
- Regular fluids throughout the day; small sips if vomiting; avoid dehydrating beverages (alcohol, caffeinated drinks)

**Diet:**
- Continue eating as tolerated — fasting is not recommended
- Plain, easily digestible foods initially (dry toast, crackers, rice, banana) — BRAT diet acceptable but not essential
- Avoid high-fat, spicy, or fibre-rich foods acutely
- Lactose avoidance is generally not required in adults with AGE unless known lactose intolerance

**Antiemetics (symptomatic relief):**
- Ondansetron 4 mg orally/sublingually (PBS-listed) — highly effective for vomiting; enables oral rehydration
- Metoclopramide 10 mg oral (PBS-listed) — alternative; avoid in young adults and children (dystonia risk)
- Prochlorperazine (PBS-listed) — useful for nausea/vomiting in adults

**Antidiarrhoeals:**
- Loperamide 2 mg (Imodium — OTC) — reduces stool frequency in non-bloody diarrhoea; do NOT use in bloody diarrhoea or suspected bacterial dysentery (may worsen)
- Not recommended in children under 12 years

**Antibiotics:**
- Not routinely indicated for uncomplicated viral gastroenteritis
- Consider if: moderate-to-severe traveller's diarrhoea, bloody diarrhoea with systemic illness (Campylobacter, Salmonella), confirmed bacterial aetiology in at-risk patient, or immunocompromised patient
- Azithromycin 500 mg daily for 3 days — preferred empirical therapy for traveller's diarrhoea and Campylobacter
- Norfloxacin or ciprofloxacin: used for Salmonella or severe traveller's diarrhoea, noting increasing fluoroquinolone resistance in some regions
- Tinidazole 2 g single dose or metronidazole for confirmed Giardia

**Probiotics:** Evidence supports some benefit from Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG and Saccharomyces boulardii in reducing duration; not essential but reasonable adjunct.

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Ondansetron** (PBS restricted: prevention/treatment of nausea and vomiting; 4 mg ODT or tablet) — requires prescription
- **Metoclopramide** (PBS restricted: as antiemetic; 10 mg oral) — avoid in young patients (extrapyramidal reactions)
- **Azithromycin** (PBS restricted: bacterial gastroenteritis — Campylobacter indication; traveller's diarrhoea)
- **Norfloxacin** (PBS restricted: enteric infections)
- **Metronidazole** (PBS restricted: Giardia, anaerobic infections) — 400 mg three times daily for 5–7 days for Giardia
- **Tinidazole** (PBS restricted: Giardia — 2 g single dose; preferred single-dose option)
- **Loperamide** (OTC — not PBS-listed; widely available)
- **Oral rehydration solutions** (Gastrolyte, HydraLyte — OTC; not PBS-listed)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Signs of severe dehydration: hypotension, tachycardia, confusion, inability to tolerate any oral fluids
- Bloody diarrhoea with high fever, systemic toxicity (haemolytic uraemic syndrome — particularly in children after E. coli O157:H7 infection)
- Severe abdominal pain suggesting surgical emergency (appendicitis, bowel ischaemia, perforation)
- Altered conscious state, seizure

**Urgent in-person GP review:**
- Unable to maintain oral hydration despite antiemetics and ORS guidance
- Elderly, immunocompromised, or pregnant patient with moderate dehydration
- Diarrhoea >7 days without improvement
- Bloody diarrhoea — requires stool MCS and assessment for bacterial dysentery or IBD
- Diarrhoea in a healthcare worker or food handler — occupational health and public health notification may be required

**Non-urgent follow-up:**
- Post-infectious IBS (develops in up to 10% after AGE — symptoms of IBS starting after gastroenteritis episode)
- Persistent loose stools >4 weeks — consider post-infectious IBS, IBD, coeliac disease, parasitic infection

## Telehealth Suitability

Uncomplicated acute gastroenteritis is suitable for telehealth in adults without severe dehydration.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Symptom history and risk stratification (mild vs moderate dehydration)
- Oral rehydration guidance and dietary advice
- Antiemetic prescription to enable oral rehydration
- Loperamide guidance for non-bloody diarrhoea
- Antibiotic prescribing for traveller's diarrhoea or confirmed bacterial aetiology based on history

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Signs suggesting moderate-to-severe dehydration (assessment requires clinical examination)
- Bloody diarrhoea with systemic illness
- Severe abdominal pain
- Any concern about surgical emergency
- Children with AGE and any signs of moderate dehydration
