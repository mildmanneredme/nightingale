## Tinnitus Assessment (RACGP)

Tinnitus is the perception of sound without an external source — ringing, buzzing, hissing, whooshing, or clicking. It affects approximately 15–20% of Australians to some degree, with 3–4% experiencing tinnitus that significantly impacts quality of life. Tinnitus is a symptom, not a diagnosis — it requires investigation to exclude treatable underlying causes. The most common causes are sensorineural hearing loss (especially noise-induced and presbycusis), noise exposure, and medications. Most tinnitus is subjective (heard only by the patient); objective tinnitus is rare but has identifiable causes. GPs play a central role in: assessing tinnitus, identifying treatable causes, referring for audiological assessment, providing reassurance and education, and identifying red flags requiring urgent ENT or neurological review. Telehealth is suitable for ongoing counselling and management review.

## Clinical Presentation

**Types of tinnitus:**

Subjective tinnitus (most common — >99%):
- Heard only by patient; no external sound source
- Quality: ringing, buzzing, hissing, whistling, humming, roaring, cricket-like
- Pitch: varies; often matches frequency of hearing loss (commonly high-pitched in noise-induced/presbycusis)
- Unilateral vs bilateral: unilateral raises suspicion of acoustic neuroma or Menière's; bilateral more common in systemic or noise exposure causes
- Pulsatile tinnitus: rhythmic, often synchronous with heartbeat — indicates vascular or pulsatile cause; requires investigation

Objective tinnitus (rare — <1%):
- Audible to examiner (stethoscope placed near ear)
- Causes: arteriovenous malformation (AVM), glomus tumour, palatal myoclonus, middle ear muscle spasm, patulous Eustachian tube

**Clinical features:**
- Impact: assess severity — Tinnitus Handicap Inventory (THI); impact on sleep, concentration, work, relationships, mental health
- Associated hearing loss: very common — audiogram essential
- Hyperacusis (sound sensitivity): often accompanies tinnitus; oversensitivity to normal environmental sounds
- Associated vertigo: suggests Menière's disease — triad of tinnitus + hearing loss + episodic vertigo
- Psychological impact: depression and anxiety extremely common — tinnitus distress is the primary clinical problem in many patients

**Common causes by mechanism:**
- Sensorineural hearing loss: noise-induced, presbycusis, ototoxic medications — most common
- Noise exposure: acute (concerts, machinery) or chronic occupational
- Cerumen impaction: conductive; resolves with wax removal
- Otitis media: conductive; resolves with treatment
- Ototoxic medications: aminoglycosides, cisplatin, high-dose loop diuretics, salicylates (aspirin), quinine
- Pulsatile: hypertension, arteriovenous malformation, glomus tumour, carotid artery stenosis, benign intracranial hypertension
- Neurological: acoustic neuroma, MS, head injury
- Systemic: anaemia (high-output cardiac state), hypertension, hyperthyroidism, Menière's disease
- Idiopathic: no identifiable cause in 30–40%

## Assessment Approach

**Clinical history:**
- Character: type of sound, pitch, rhythm (pulsatile vs non-pulsatile)
- Duration and onset: sudden onset vs gradual; trigger events (noise exposure, medication change, infection)
- Laterality: unilateral (red flag — acoustic neuroma) vs bilateral
- Associated symptoms: hearing loss, vertigo, aural fullness, facial weakness
- Impact: THI score; sleep, concentration, mental health, work
- Medication review: ototoxic drugs (aminoglycosides, cisplatin, loop diuretics, aspirin, NSAIDs, quinine)
- Noise exposure history: occupational, recreational (concerts, headphone use, shooting)

**Physical examination:**
- Otoscopy: cerumen, tympanic membrane perforation, otitis media, cholesteatoma
- Tuning fork: Weber and Rinne — screen for conductive vs sensorineural loss
- Cranial nerve examination: facial nerve, trigeminal — assess for central pathology
- Auscultation over ear/neck: objective tinnitus (rare); carotid bruit (pulsatile)
- Blood pressure: hypertension — pulsatile tinnitus
- Neck: lymphadenopathy, thyroid

**Investigations:**
- Audiogram: mandatory — most tinnitus patients have underlying hearing loss even if not subjectively noticed
- FBC, iron studies: anaemia
- TSH: hyper/hypothyroidism
- Fasting glucose: diabetic neuropathy affects auditory system
- MRI internal auditory meatus: if unilateral or asymmetric tinnitus/hearing loss — exclude acoustic neuroma; urgent referral to ENT for MRI ordering

**Tinnitus severity assessment:**
- Tinnitus Handicap Inventory (THI): 25-item validated questionnaire; scores tinnitus handicap; grades mild/moderate/severe/catastrophic; guides management intensity

## Key Differentials

- Acoustic neuroma (vestibular schwannoma): unilateral tinnitus + asymmetric SNHL ± vertigo; MRI IAM urgent — see `hearing-loss` entry
- Menière's disease: episodic triad — tinnitus + unilateral SNHL + vertigo + aural fullness; audiogram shows low-frequency SNHL; ENT referral
- Pulsatile tinnitus: vascular causes — AVM, carotid stenosis, glomus tympanicum, benign intracranial hypertension; MRI/MRA; ENT/neurology
- Drug-induced tinnitus: salicylates (bilateral, dose-dependent, reversible); aminoglycosides; cisplatin; review medication list
- Benign intracranial hypertension (idiopathic intracranial hypertension): obese women; pulsatile tinnitus + headache + papilloedema + visual changes; MRI, LP; neurology
- Temporomandibular joint (TMJ) dysfunction: jaw pain + tinnitus + facial pain; worsened by chewing; dental referral

## Management Principles

**Treat underlying cause:**
- Cerumen removal: often completely resolves tinnitus — check and clear wax first
- Treat otitis media: antibiotics if bacterial; decongestants; OME typically resolves
- Cease ototoxic medications: if alternative available; discuss risk-benefit with prescribing specialist for chemotherapy agents
- Treat anaemia, hypertension, thyroid disease: may improve tinnitus
- Menière's disease: salt restriction, diuretics, betahistine; ENT; see `dizziness-vertigo` entry

**Education and reassurance (essential first step):**
- Explain that tinnitus is very common and for most people does not worsen or cause hearing loss
- Many people habituate over 6–18 months — the brain learns to filter the tinnitus signal
- Avoid silence: background sound (fans, music) reduces tinnitus prominence; habituation aided by enriched sound environment
- Avoid excessive focus on tinnitus — rumination worsens distress

**Sound therapy (enriched sound environment):**
- Background noise masking: white noise generators, nature sounds, fan, radio; reduces contrast between tinnitus and silence
- Sound generators: wearable devices (similar to hearing aids); provide low-level broadband noise
- Hearing aids: if underlying hearing loss — amplification of external sound reduces tinnitus prominence; most effective intervention in those with hearing loss

**Tinnitus retraining therapy (TRT):**
- Combines structured sound therapy and directive counselling
- Aims to habituate the emotional and perceptual response to tinnitus
- 12–24 months; delivered by audiologist specialising in tinnitus
- High evidence for significant and sustained improvement

**Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT):**
- Strongest psychological evidence for tinnitus distress
- Changes maladaptive thoughts and reactions to tinnitus; does not reduce loudness but reduces distress
- Refer to psychologist (GP Mental Health Care Plan); self-help CBT apps (Tinnitus Relief app — Australian)
- Particularly for high THI scores or comorbid depression/anxiety

**Sleep management:**
- Sound enrichment at night (gentle background music or white noise)
- Sleep hygiene education
- Melatonin (2 mg MR): may improve sleep in tinnitus patients; PBS-restricted ≥55 years

**Pharmacological:**
- No medications proven to treat tinnitus itself
- Treat comorbid depression and anxiety: SSRIs or SNRIs — treat comorbidity, not tinnitus directly
- Avoid benzodiazepines: tinnitus distress can drive requests; not recommended for tinnitus management; dependency risk
- Ginkgo biloba: not recommended — evidence does not support efficacy for tinnitus

**Hearing aids (Hearing Australia):**
- Funded for eligible patients (Pensioner Concession Card, DVA, Healthcare Card) — see `hearing-loss` entry
- Significantly reduce tinnitus perception in those with concurrent hearing loss

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **SSRIs (sertraline, escitalopram)** (PBS restricted: depression/anxiety; if comorbid depression or anxiety driving tinnitus distress; do not treat tinnitus directly)
- **Melatonin (Circadin 2 mg MR)** (PBS restricted: insomnia ≥55 years; off-label for younger; improves sleep disrupted by tinnitus)
- **Note:** No PBS-listed medications are approved or effective for tinnitus itself; treatment is multimodal — education, audiological assessment, hearing aids, TRT, CBT
- **Note:** Avoid prescribing benzodiazepines for tinnitus distress — no long-term evidence; high dependency risk; worsens sleep quality

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent ENT (within 1–2 weeks):**
- Unilateral or asymmetric tinnitus: exclude acoustic neuroma with MRI IAM
- Sudden onset tinnitus with simultaneous sudden SNHL: co-management with sudden SNHL protocol (see `hearing-loss` entry)
- Pulsatile tinnitus: requires vascular imaging to exclude AVM, glomus tumour, intracranial hypertension

**Urgent neurology:**
- Pulsatile tinnitus + headache + papilloedema: suspected benign intracranial hypertension — MRI, LP, ophthalmology review

**Non-urgent ENT or audiology:**
- All patients with tinnitus: audiological assessment for hearing evaluation, hearing aids if loss, TRT if indicated
- Menière's disease: ENT for diagnosis and management

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Audiologist specialising in tinnitus: TRT, sound therapy
- Psychologist (GP Mental Health Care Plan): CBT for tinnitus distress; comorbid depression/anxiety

## Telehealth Suitability

Tinnitus ongoing management is suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Education and reassurance (majority of initial management)
- THI questionnaire and severity monitoring
- Depression and anxiety screening (PHQ-9, GAD-7)
- Medication review
- Referral coordination (audiologist, psychologist)
- Sound therapy strategy discussion and review
- Sleep management counselling

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Otoscopy and tympanoscopy
- Tuning fork examination
- Cerumen removal if indicated
- Audiogram — audiologist
- MRI IAM — if unilateral or pulsatile
