## Growth Concerns Assessment (RACGP)

Growth monitoring is a core component of child health surveillance in Australian general practice. The GP manages growth concerns by systematic plotting on WHO or CDC growth charts, identifying faltering growth or abnormal growth patterns, and determining appropriate investigation and referral pathways. Key growth parameters: height (length in under 2 years), weight, BMI, and head circumference (under 2 years). Growth charts used in Australia: WHO growth standards (birth to 2 years); CDC charts (2–18 years). Faltering growth (failure to thrive) in infants and toddlers is most often non-organic (feeding difficulties, nutritional inadequacy, psychosocial factors) and requires a multidisciplinary approach. Short stature (height below 2nd centile or >2 SDS below mean for age and sex) and tall stature each have distinct differential diagnoses. Parental height (mid-parental height calculation) is critical for interpretation. Growth velocity matters as much as absolute size: crossing centile lines downward is more concerning than consistently low centile. Paediatric endocrinology referral for pathological causes. Telehealth is suitable for growth result review; accurate measurements require in-person assessment.

## Clinical Presentation

**Faltering growth (failure to thrive — FTT) in infants/toddlers:**
- Weight gain less than expected for age; crossing centile lines downward in first 2 years
- Definition: weight consistently below 2nd centile OR weight crossing two or more major centile lines downward
- Most common: non-organic (80%): inadequate caloric intake; feeding difficulties; parental feeding anxiety; psychosocial issues
- Organic: chronic illness (coeliac disease, IBD, CHD, cystic fibrosis, renal tubular acidosis, hypothyroidism)
- Red flags: weight crossing down while height is maintained → nutritional deprivation; weight AND height both crossing down → chronic systemic disease or growth hormone deficiency
- Developmental delay accompanying FTT: concerning for chromosomal abnormality or severe neglect

**Short stature:**
- Definition: height below 2nd centile (<-2 SDS) for age and sex
- Familial short stature (most common benign cause): height below mean but appropriate for mid-parental height; normal growth velocity; bone age normal
- Constitutional delay of growth and puberty (CDGP): delayed bone age; puberty delayed; will reach normal adult height; family history often positive; "late bloomer"
- Secondary to chronic illness: coeliac disease, IBD, cystic fibrosis, hypothyroidism, CKD, type 1 diabetes — exclude with screening investigations
- Growth hormone deficiency (GHD): rare; height velocity slow; below -2 SDS AND slowing velocity; bone age delayed; GH stimulation testing required
- Turner syndrome (females): short stature + primary amenorrhoea + characteristic features (webbed neck, cubitus valgus, shield chest); karyotype
- Skeletal dysplasia: disproportionate short stature; body proportions abnormal; radiology

**Tall stature:**
- Usually familial: parental height guides expectation
- Marfan syndrome: tall + arachnodactyly + scoliosis + lens dislocation + aortic root dilatation; FBN1 gene; echocardiogram
- Klinefelter syndrome (males, 47,XXY): tall stature + small firm testes + gynaecomastia; karyotype
- Precocious puberty causing early growth spurt then early epiphyseal fusion → final height below expected
- Pituitary gigantism (rare): excess GH from pituitary adenoma; headache; visual field defect

**Obesity and overweight:**
- BMI ≥95th centile (age/sex) = obese; BMI 85–94th centile = overweight
- Rapidly increasing problem in Australian children; see `obesity` for adult management
- Comorbidities: insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes, dyslipidaemia, hypertension, NAFLD, sleep apnoea, orthopaedic problems (Blount's, slipped capital femoral epiphysis)
- Simple obesity (exogenous): most common; dietary excess + sedentary behaviour
- Endocrine obesity: hypothyroidism, Cushing's, growth hormone deficiency — suspect if height velocity is also slow (obese AND short = endocrine cause until proven otherwise)

## Assessment Approach

**Growth chart plotting (accurate measurements required):**
- Infants <2 years: length (supine with length board); weight (naked); head circumference (occipital-frontal)
- Children >2 years: standing height (bare feet; stadiometer; wall-mounted); weight; calculate BMI
- Compare to previous measurements: growth velocity is key; plot ALL measurements on same chart
- WHO charts (0–2 years): breastfed infant reference standard; used in Australia
- CDC charts (2–18 years): used in Australian clinical practice

**Mid-parental height (MPH) calculation:**
- Boys: [(father's height + mother's height) / 2] + 6.5 cm
- Girls: [(father's height + mother's height) / 2] − 6.5 cm
- Target range: MPH ± 8 cm (85% probability); the child's expected adult height centile should align with this

**Investigations (targeted to presentation):**
- Faltering growth in infancy: FBC (anaemia), EUC, LFTs (hepatic disease), TFTs (hypothyroidism), anti-tTG IgA + total IgA (coeliac), urinalysis (renal tubular acidosis — alkaline urine), bone age X-ray (left wrist), 3-day dietary diary
- Short stature screen: FBC, EUC, LFTs, TFTs, anti-tTG IgA (coeliac), IGF-1 and IGFBP-3 (GH axis screening — low = refer for GH stimulation testing), bone age X-ray (left wrist), karyotype (all girls below -2 SDS stature — exclude Turner syndrome)
- Bone age interpretation: delayed = constitutional delay or hypothyroidism or GHD; advanced = precocious puberty; normal = familial short stature
- Obesity screen: FBG/HbA1c, fasting lipids, LFTs (NAFLD), TFTs, blood pressure, uric acid

## Key Differentials

- Familial short stature: MPH consistent; normal velocity; normal bone age; no investigation needed
- Constitutional delay: delayed bone age; family history; normal velocity for bone age; observe
- Coeliac disease: most common treatable cause of growth failure; anti-tTG IgA; duodenal biopsy
- Hypothyroidism: short stature + slowing velocity + weight gain; TFTs
- GH deficiency: slow height velocity; below -2 SDS; low IGF-1; GH stimulation test; paediatric endocrinology
- Turner syndrome: any girl with unexplained short stature; karyotype
- Neglect or nutritional deprivation: weight crosses down first; height later; psychosocial assessment

## Management Principles

**Faltering growth (non-organic — most cases):**
- Dietitian referral: comprehensive dietary assessment; caloric requirement for catch-up growth; high-calorie foods; feeding advice
- Parental counselling: responsive feeding; structured meal times; avoid force-feeding or food as reward
- Lactation support: if exclusively breastfed infant with inadequate weight gain — lactation consultant
- Social work: if food insecurity, neglect, or domestic violence contributing
- Monitor weight weekly initially; fortnightly once improving; aim for catch-up growth
- Oral nutritional supplements (Infatrini, Pediasure): if dietary intake still inadequate after dietitian input; review at 4–6 weeks

**Short stature — treating underlying cause:**
- Coeliac: strict gluten-free diet → significant catch-up growth expected
- Hypothyroidism: levothyroxine replacement; monitor growth closely
- GH deficiency: growth hormone therapy (Genotropin, Norditropin — daily subcutaneous injection) — paediatric endocrinology; PBS Authority required for GHD
- Turner syndrome: growth hormone therapy + oestrogen replacement at puberty; paediatric endocrinology + gynaecology
- Constitutional delay: reassurance; most reach normal adult height; testosterone in boys with significant psychosocial distress from delayed puberty (paediatric endocrinology short course IM testosterone)

**Obesity in children:**
- Family-centred lifestyle intervention: whole family dietary change + physical activity; screen time reduction
- Refer to paediatric obesity service if: BMI >99th centile; comorbidities; age <6 years; Tier 3 programs available
- No pharmacotherapy routinely for children (<18 years); liraglutide and orlistat rarely considered in adolescents under specialist supervision

## Paediatric Context and Guardian Involvement

- Accurate measurements: critical for interpretation; poorly measured heights and weights cause unnecessary anxiety or missed pathology; standardise technique at every visit
- Growth charts: print and share with parents — visual representation helps understanding
- Puberty staging: Tanner staging required to interpret growth in pubertal children (growth spurt normally coincides with Tanner 2–3 in girls, 3–4 in boys)
- Parent height: always ask; document; calculate MPH at first assessment of short stature
- Psychological impact: short stature may cause significant bullying and self-esteem issues in older children; address at consultation; GP Mental Health Treatment Plan if needed
- Breastfeeding guidance: WHO charts specifically validated for breastfed infants; breastfed babies grow differently from formula-fed (normal on WHO charts; "slow" on older charts); avoid unnecessary formula supplementation based on old charts

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Levothyroxine (Oroxine, Eutroxsig)** (PBS restricted: hypothyroidism; weight-based dosing in children; recheck TFTs 6 weeks after dose change; growth recovery expected after 6–12 months of treatment)
- **Growth hormone (Genotropin, Norditropin)** (PBS restricted: growth hormone deficiency, Turner syndrome, Prader-Willi, chronic renal failure; paediatric endocrinologist prescribes and monitors; Authority required; daily subcutaneous injection)
- **Oral nutritional supplements (Pediasure, Infatrini)**: not PBS-listed for general use; NDIS funding may apply for specific conditions; dietitian-guided
- **Note:** Growth hormone must only be initiated by a paediatric endocrinologist after formal GH stimulation testing confirming GHD. GP role: refer, monitor growth, co-manage comorbidities.

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent:**
- Severe acute malnutrition (kwashiorkor/marasmus): hospital admission; nutritional rehabilitation
- Suspected neglect or child abuse: mandatory report; Child Protection Services (state)
- Turner syndrome with cardiac complications (coarctation, aortic root dilatation): cardiology

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Paediatric endocrinology: height below -2 SDS with low growth velocity or IGF-1; suspected GHD or Turner syndrome; precocious puberty
- Paediatric gastroenterology: coeliac disease; IBD; non-coeliac malabsorption causing FTT
- Dietitian: all cases of faltering growth or significant obesity
- Paediatric dietitian + social work: faltering growth with psychosocial concerns

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Growth monitoring result review (using home-measured heights and weights with parent technique guidance)
- Dietary advice and supplement recommendations
- Referral coordination and results discussion

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Accurate growth measurements (stadiometer, calibrated scales, length board for infants)
- Pubertal staging (Tanner stage assessment)
- Physical examination for dysmorphic features, organomegaly (in suspected systemic disease)
