## Hepatitis B Assessment (RACGP)

Hepatitis B virus (HBV) infection is a major global health challenge, with Australia having approximately 218,000 people with chronic HBV. Chronic HBV disproportionately affects people born in high-prevalence countries (Asia, Pacific Islands, Sub-Saharan Africa, Middle East) and Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples. Chronic HBV causes cirrhosis in 20–30% of infected individuals over 20–30 years and significantly increases hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) risk (20–25× general population). Australia has had an effective HBV vaccination program since 1988 (universal infant vaccination from 2000), greatly reducing new infections. GPs play a critical role in: screening at-risk populations, interpreting serology, assessment of liver disease, HCC surveillance, antiviral treatment coordination, vaccination of susceptibles, and partner/household notification. Telehealth is suitable for stable chronic HBV monitoring.

## Clinical Presentation

**Phases of chronic HBV infection:**

Immune tolerant (HBeAg positive, high viral load, normal ALT, minimal liver damage):
- Common in perinatally acquired HBV (Asian-born patients)
- Not usually treated; close monitoring essential
- HCC risk still present despite normal ALT — surveillance required

Immune active / immune clearance (elevated ALT, active liver damage):
- Target for antiviral treatment
- May be HBeAg positive or negative (pre-core mutant strains)

Inactive carrier (HBeAg negative, low viral load <2000 IU/mL, normal ALT, minimal damage):
- Low risk of progression; still surveillance required for HCC
- Annual monitoring; antiviral treatment not usually required

Reactivation (may occur spontaneously or with immunosuppression):
- HBsAg-positive patients starting immunosuppressants (chemotherapy, biologics, corticosteroids): screen all for HBV; give prophylactic antiviral (tenofovir or entecavir) to HBsAg-positive patients; also consider for anti-HBc positive patients at high risk

**Serological interpretation:**
- HBsAg positive, anti-HBs negative: current HBV infection (acute or chronic)
- HBsAg negative, anti-HBs positive, anti-HBc negative: vaccinated — immune
- HBsAg negative, anti-HBs positive, anti-HBc positive: past infection, now immune
- HBsAg negative, anti-HBc positive, anti-HBs negative: past infection (isolated anti-HBc); low-level occult HBV possible; reactivation risk with immunosuppression
- All seronegative (all negative): susceptible — vaccinate

## Assessment Approach

**Screening — who to test (HBsAg, anti-HBs, anti-HBc):**
- All people born in high-prevalence countries (Asia, Pacific, Africa, Middle East, Eastern Europe)
- Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples
- Sexual partners or household contacts of HBsAg-positive people
- People who inject drugs (PWID)
- Men who have sex with men (MSM)
- Healthcare workers
- Pregnant women (universal antenatal screening — mandatory in Australia)
- Immunosuppression candidates (check before starting)

**Baseline assessment of newly diagnosed chronic HBV:**
- HBV DNA (viral load): quantitative PCR — key for staging and treatment decisions
- HBeAg and anti-HBe: determines phase of infection
- LFTs (ALT/AST): active hepatic inflammation
- FBC, coagulation (PT/INR): liver synthetic function
- Renal function (eGFR): relevant for tenofovir choice
- Hepatitis A serology: vaccinate if non-immune (HAV + chronic HBV = worse outcomes)
- HIV and HCV co-infection testing
- FIB-4 score (age × AST / [platelets × √ALT]): non-invasive fibrosis assessment; >3.25 = advanced fibrosis; refer to gastroenterologist
- Liver ultrasound: baseline; cirrhosis features; HCC screening (if not already specialist-managed)

**HCC surveillance (mandatory for all with chronic HBV — regardless of cirrhosis status):**
- Liver ultrasound every 6 months: recommended by GESA/ASHM for all chronic HBV patients
- AFP (alpha-fetoprotein): used in conjunction with ultrasound; not specific alone
- Higher HCC risk: males >40 years, cirrhosis, family history HCC, high viral load, prolonged immune-active phase, sub-Saharan African patients (HCC risk higher at younger age)
- GP can order ultrasound and AFP; arrange if patient not under specialist surveillance

**Ongoing monitoring (non-treatment phase):**
- ALT every 3–6 months; HBV DNA annually; HBsAg quantification 6-monthly
- LFTs, FBC, renal function 6-monthly to annually
- FIB-4 score annually

## Key Differentials

- Acute HBV: jaundice, acute liver failure; self-limiting in adults (>95% clear); manage supportively; hospital if severe; anti-HBc IgM positive distinguishes from chronic
- Alcoholic liver disease: alcohol history; AST:ALT ratio >2; MCV elevated; no HBV markers
- NAFLD: obesity, metabolic syndrome; HBV serology negative; ultrasound shows steatosis — see `nafld` entry
- HCV: anti-HCV antibody positive; HCV RNA confirms active infection — see `hepatitis-c` entry
- Autoimmune hepatitis: ANA, ASMA positive; elevated IgG; liver biopsy
- Wilson's disease: young patient; Kayser-Fleischer rings; low ceruloplasmin

## Management Principles

**Antiviral treatment (gastroenterologist/hepatologist initiates and manages — GP co-manages):**

Treatment indications (GESA/ASHM guidelines):
- HBV DNA >2000 IU/mL + ALT >2× ULN: treat
- Any cirrhosis with detectable HBV DNA: treat regardless of ALT
- HCC with detectable HBV DNA: treat
- Immune suppression planned (chemotherapy, biologics, corticosteroids, transplant): prophylactic antiviral

Preferred antivirals (high resistance barrier — oral, once daily):
- Tenofovir disoproxil fumarate (TDF — Viread, generic): 300 mg daily; highly effective; low resistance; monitor renal function (nephrotoxic — annual eGFR, urine ACR); bone density effects (annual monitoring in prolonged use)
- Tenofovir alafenamide (TAF — Vemlidy): 25 mg daily; less nephrotoxic and less bone density impact; preferred if renal impairment or osteoporosis; cost may be higher
- Entecavir: 0.5 mg daily (1 mg if prior lamivudine use); alternative; very low resistance; avoid in decompensated cirrhosis

Duration of treatment:
- In most cases: indefinite (stopping antiviral in HBeAg-negative patients leads to high reactivation rate)
- HBeAg-positive patients: may stop after HBeAg seroconversion + 12 months; specialist decision
- Cirrhosis: indefinite

**Vaccination of susceptibles (household and sexual contacts):**
- HBV vaccine (3-dose course: 0, 1, 6 months): Engerix-B, H-B-Vax II, Twinrix (HAV+HBV)
- Check post-vaccine anti-HBs at 4–8 weeks after last dose: >10 mIU/mL = immune
- Non-responders (anti-HBs <10): repeat 3-dose course; check HBsAg (may be chronically infected)
- NIP-funded for children; funded for high-risk adults in some jurisdictions; otherwise PBS-listed for at-risk

**Pregnancy:**
- Screen all pregnant women for HBsAg in first trimester
- HBsAg-positive mother: infant receives HBV vaccine + HBIG (hepatitis B immunoglobulin) at birth within 12 hours
- High maternal HBV DNA (>200,000 IU/mL): antiviral (TDF from 28 weeks) reduces perinatal transmission
- All prophylaxis arranged in conjunction with obstetrics and neonatology

**HBV notification:**
- Notifiable in all Australian states and territories: notify state/territory health authority for newly diagnosed HBV
- Partner/contact notification: health department assists; NSW Sexual Health Infolink 1800 451 624

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Tenofovir disoproxil fumarate (Viread, generic)** (PBS restricted: chronic HBV with active disease; authority; specialist initiation; 300 mg daily; monitor eGFR and bone density)
- **Tenofovir alafenamide (Vemlidy)** (PBS restricted: chronic HBV; preferred if renal impairment or osteoporosis; specialist initiation)
- **Entecavir (Baraclude)** (PBS restricted: chronic HBV; specialist initiation; 0.5 mg daily; 1 mg if lamivudine-experienced)
- **HBV vaccines (Engerix-B, H-B-Vax II)** (NIP-funded at birth and for infants; PBS-funded for at-risk adults; 3-dose schedule)
- **HBIG (hepatitis B immunoglobulin)** (hospital supply for newborns of HBsAg-positive mothers; post-exposure prophylaxis)
- **Note:** Lamivudine should NOT be used for new HBV treatment (high resistance rates); entecavir and tenofovir-based regimens are standard

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Acute liver failure: jaundice + coagulopathy + encephalopathy — rare but life-threatening; liver transplant consideration

**Urgent hepatology/gastroenterology:**
- New HCC detected on surveillance ultrasound — multidisciplinary oncology review urgently
- Decompensated cirrhosis: ascites, variceal haemorrhage, hepatic encephalopathy
- HBV reactivation in immunosuppressed patient: rapidly rising ALT and HBV DNA on antiviral prophylaxis — intensify antiviral; hepatology review

**Non-urgent gastroenterology/hepatology:**
- All newly diagnosed chronic HBV: baseline specialist review for staging and treatment decision
- FIB-4 >3.25 or clinical cirrhosis features — specialist urgently for HCC surveillance and treatment
- Antiviral initiation and dose titration — specialist managed

## Telehealth Suitability

Stable chronic HBV monitoring is highly suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- HBV DNA, LFT, and renal function result review
- FIB-4 calculation and fibrosis risk counselling
- Medication adherence review
- HCC surveillance coordination (ultrasound referral, AFP result review)
- Vaccination of household contacts
- Pregnancy planning counselling
- Referral coordination (gastroenterology, hepatology)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Physical examination if cirrhosis suspected
- Blood tests — pathology arranged
- Liver ultrasound — radiology referral
