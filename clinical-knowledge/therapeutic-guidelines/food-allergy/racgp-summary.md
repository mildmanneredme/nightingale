## Food Allergy Assessment (RACGP)

Food allergy is an adverse immune response to a food protein. IgE-mediated food allergy (immediate hypersensitivity) is the most clinically important form — it can cause anaphylaxis. Food allergy affects approximately 10% of Australian infants and 2–4% of adults. The "Big 9" allergens responsible for the majority of reactions in Australia: peanut, tree nuts, shellfish, fish, cow's milk, egg, wheat, soy, sesame. Peanut allergy is the most common cause of fatal anaphylaxis. The GP's roles are: accurately diagnose food allergy (not food intolerance), prescribe EpiPen and provide ASCIA anaphylaxis action plans, refer to allergy clinic for formal testing and management, and support families in safe food introduction practices. CRITICAL: food intolerance (eg lactose intolerance, FODMAP sensitivity) is NOT allergy — no anaphylaxis risk. Early allergen introduction (from 4–6 months) now recommended to PREVENT food allergy — ASCIA Infant Feeding Guideline. Allergy Helpline: 1300 728 000. Telehealth is suitable for follow-up; initial allergy workup requires in-person testing.

## Clinical Presentation

**IgE-mediated (immediate) food allergy:**
- Onset: within minutes to 2 hours of ingestion (most within 30 minutes)
- Symptoms (any combination): urticaria (hives), angioedema (swelling), vomiting, abdominal pain, rhinitis, wheeze/cough, anaphylaxis
- Severity: mild (urticaria only) → moderate (urticaria + abdominal pain or vomiting) → severe/anaphylaxis (cardiovascular, respiratory collapse)
- Reproducible: symptoms occur consistently on exposure to the same food
- Dose-dependent: threshold varies; hidden allergens (eg peanut in sauces, sesame in dips) can trigger reactions

**Non-IgE-mediated (delayed) food allergy:**
- Onset: 2–72 hours after ingestion
- Conditions: Food protein-induced enterocolitis syndrome (FPIES) — infants; profuse vomiting ± diarrhoea 1–4 hours after cow's milk or soy; can cause shock
- Food protein-induced allergic proctocolitis (FPIAP): infants; blood in stool; usually cow's milk protein via breastmilk; resolves with maternal elimination diet
- Eosinophilic oesophagitis (EoE): dysphagia; food bolus impaction; eosinophil-predominant oesophageal inflammation; food triggers; endoscopy + biopsy required

**Food intolerance (NOT allergy — no anaphylaxis risk):**
- Lactose intolerance: diarrhoea, bloating, wind after dairy — enzyme deficiency; NOT immune mediated; NO anaphylaxis risk
- FODMAP sensitivity (IBS): bloating, cramps; multiple foods; NOT allergy
- Non-coeliac gluten sensitivity: GI symptoms with gluten; no autoimmune; normal serology; see `coeliac-disease`
- Sulphite sensitivity: asthma worsening with wine, dried fruit; NOT classical IgE allergy

**Anaphylaxis from food:**
- Most common trigger in children; peanut most common fatal trigger
- Signs: DIVA mnemonic:
  - **D**ifficulty breathing (wheeze, stridor, respiratory distress)
  - **I**nvoluntary urination/defecation or incontinence (severe hypotension)
  - **V**ascular collapse (hypotension, pallor, loss of consciousness)
  - **A**ngioedema of face/throat (laryngeal oedema — hoarse voice, stridor)
- Biphasic anaphylaxis: second wave 4–12 hours later in 5–20% of cases; reason for 4–6 hour observation post-anaphylaxis

## Assessment Approach

**Clinical history (allergy assessment):**
- Which food triggered reaction?
- Time from ingestion to symptoms (immediate vs delayed)?
- Symptoms (skin, GI, respiratory, cardiovascular)?
- Severity of reaction (required adrenaline? hospital admission?)
- Prior exposures tolerated vs reactions consistent?
- Age of onset; family history of atopy, food allergy
- Concurrent illness at time of reaction (cofactors: exercise, alcohol, NSAIDs — lower threshold for anaphylaxis)
- Breastfed infants: maternal diet; timing of complementary feeding introduction

**Testing (allergy clinic — not primary care):**
- Skin prick testing (SPT): gold standard for IgE-mediated food allergy; performed by allergist; wheal ≥3 mm above control = positive; highly specific; false negatives possible
- Specific IgE (sIgE RAST): blood test; useful when SPT not feasible; RAST levels correlate with probability of allergy but do NOT predict severity
- Total IgE: non-specific; not diagnostic alone; elevated in atopy
- Oral food challenge (OFC): gold standard for definitive diagnosis; gradual incremental doses under medical supervision; definitive confirmation or exclusion; performed by allergist only
- CRITICAL: DO NOT perform oral food challenge in GP office — anaphylaxis risk; refer to allergy clinic

**Primary care investigations:**
- Specific IgE (RAST) for common allergens: can be ordered by GP; results require specialist interpretation; useful to support referral; a low RAST does NOT exclude clinical allergy
- Refer to allergy clinic for definitive workup including SPT and OFC

## Key Differentials

- Food intolerance: GI symptoms only; no urticaria or systemic features; NOT allergy; lactose intolerance most common
- Oral allergy syndrome (pollen-food allergy): raw fruits and vegetables cross-react with pollen proteins; itch/tingling mouth only; cooked forms usually tolerated; benign; distinguish from true allergy
- Scombroid poisoning (histamine toxicity): spoiled fish (tuna, mackerel); flushing, headache, rash; within 30 minutes; mimics food allergy; not immune-mediated; multiple people affected by same batch
- Gustatory flushing: spicy foods; NOT allergy
- Coeliac disease: chronic GI symptoms + malnutrition; gluten autoimmune; see `coeliac-disease`
- Anxiety or panic attacks: somatic symptoms; no objective signs; normal tryptase

## Management Principles

**Acute anaphylaxis:**
- Adrenaline IM immediately: EpiPen 0.3 mg (adults and children ≥30 kg) or EpiPen Jr 0.15 mg (children 10–20 kg); anterolateral thigh
- Call 000; lay patient flat; repeat adrenaline every 5–10 minutes if no improvement
- Antihistamines and steroids: ADJUNCT only; do NOT delay adrenaline
- Hospital: 4–6 hours observation minimum post-anaphylaxis (biphasic reaction risk)
- See `anaphylaxis-management`

**EpiPen prescription and anaphylaxis action plan:**
- Prescribe 2 EpiPens: one to carry; one spare at home/school/work
- ASCIA anaphylaxis action plan: MANDATORY for all patients with food-triggered anaphylaxis; colour-coded; available at allergy.org.au; personalise with patient's photo, triggers, threshold, emergency contacts
- Train patient, family, school, workplace in EpiPen use
- MedicAlert bracelet: strongly recommended for severe food allergy
- PBS: EpiPen is PBS-listed for patients at risk of anaphylaxis; two pens per prescription

**Allergen avoidance:**
- Read food labels: all packaged foods must declare "Big 9" allergens in plain language (Australian Food Standards — FSANZ); "may contain traces" = advisory statement; patient guidance on threshold
- Cross-contamination risk: shared cooking surfaces; eating out; airlines; social events
- ASCIA's "Allergy Aware" resources for patients, schools, and workplaces

**Referral to allergy clinic (ASCIA member allergist):**
- All children and adults with anaphylaxis or uncertain food allergy label
- Prior to food reintroduction (supervised oral food challenge)
- Considering oral immunotherapy (OIT) for peanut allergy (Palforzia now available in Australia — PBS pending) or desensitisation programs
- Complex multiple food allergy

**Early allergen introduction (prevention — ASCIA guidelines):**
- All infants: introduce allergenic foods from 4–6 months (once complementary foods started); NO need to wait or delay; early introduction reduces peanut allergy prevalence by 80% (LEAP trial)
- Infants with severe eczema or existing food allergy: allergy clinic assessment before introduction; may require supervised OFC
- ASCIA "How to introduce solid foods" guide: allergy.org.au

**Oral immunotherapy (OIT) for peanut allergy:**
- PALFORZIA (standardised peanut OIT): TGA-approved; gradually desensitises to peanut; reduces severity of reactions; NOT a cure; daily maintenance dose required; specialist only; hospital facility required for initial doses

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Adrenaline 0.3 mg EpiPen** (PBS restricted: anaphylaxis; prescribe 2 pens at all times; check that old pens (expired) are replaced annually; EpiPen price under PBS is approximately $7.30 for each pen with concession; approx $41 full price)
- **Adrenaline 0.15 mg EpiPen Jr** (PBS restricted: children 10–20 kg; peanut allergy most common paediatric indication; school management plan required)
- **Promethazine or cetirizine**: adjunct antihistamine for mild reactions (urticaria without systemic involvement); cetirizine 10 mg oral preferred (non-sedating); NOT a substitute for adrenaline in anaphylaxis
- **Note:** Allergy skin prick testing is a specialist procedure and should NOT be performed in primary care without appropriate training, equipment, and adrenaline availability on-site. RAST (specific IgE blood tests) can be requested in primary care to support referral, but do not constitute definitive allergy diagnosis.

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000:**
- Anaphylaxis: adrenaline immediately; 000; 4–6 hour hospital observation

**Urgent allergy clinic (within weeks):**
- Any child or adult after first significant food-triggered allergic reaction
- Prior to any food reintroduction challenge
- Uncertain food allergy diagnosis affecting quality of life or nutrition

**School and daycare notification:**
- Mandatory ASCIA action plan for all children with food allergy attending school or childcare; provide copy to school principal and teachers; Australian state/territory education department guidelines apply

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Allergy history review and documentation
- EpiPen prescription renewal
- Nutritional impact of food avoidance (dietitian referral)
- ASCIA action plan review and update
- Parent/patient education on label reading and cross-contamination

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial food allergy assessment (skin prick testing — allergy clinic)
- Any acute reaction assessment
- Oral food challenge: specialist facility with resuscitation equipment
