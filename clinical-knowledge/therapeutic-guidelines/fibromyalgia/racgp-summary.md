## Fibromyalgia Assessment (RACGP)

Fibromyalgia is a chronic widespread pain condition characterised by diffuse musculoskeletal pain, fatigue, sleep disturbance, cognitive dysfunction ("fibro fog"), and multiple somatic symptoms. It affects approximately 2–5% of Australians, predominantly women (female:male ratio 7:1). Fibromyalgia is a central sensitisation disorder — not a peripheral joint or muscle disease — and reflects amplified pain processing in the central nervous system. It is frequently misunderstood and underdiagnosed; patients often receive extensive and costly investigations before diagnosis. The GP plays a central role in: making a positive diagnosis (not one of exclusion), providing psychoeducation, coordinating multimodal treatment, and preventing over-investigation and opioid prescribing. Telehealth is suitable for routine fibromyalgia management.

## Clinical Presentation

**Diagnostic criteria (ACR 2010/2016 — used in Australian practice):**

Widespread Pain Index (WPI): Number of painful regions in past week (0–19 areas assessed)
Symptom Severity Scale (SSS): Severity of fatigue, unrefreshed sleep, cognitive symptoms (each 0–3); somatic symptom burden (0–3); total 0–12

Fibromyalgia diagnosis: WPI ≥7 AND SSS ≥5; OR WPI 4–6 AND SSS ≥9; symptoms present for ≥3 months; no other disorder explaining symptoms

**Core clinical features:**
- Widespread pain: above and below waist, bilateral, axial; classically "I hurt all over"
- Fatigue: profound, not relieved by rest; disproportionate to activity
- Unrefreshed sleep: waking unrestored; non-restorative sleep pattern; frequent association with restless legs, sleep apnoea
- Cognitive dysfunction: word-finding difficulty, poor concentration, memory lapses — "fibro fog"
- Multiple somatic symptoms: headache, IBS, bladder urgency, dysmenorrhoea, temporomandibular joint pain, restless legs, paraesthesiae (without neurological deficit)

**Psychological comorbidities (extremely common — must assess and address):**
- Major depression: 30–50% prevalence; anxiety: 40–60%
- Post-traumatic stress disorder: significant association with adverse childhood experiences and trauma
- Central sensitisation syndrome overlap: also seen in IBS, interstitial cystitis, tension headache, TMJ — look for pattern

**What is absent in fibromyalgia (helps confirm diagnosis):**
- No objective joint swelling or synovitis
- No muscle weakness (not myopathy)
- No abnormal investigations: CRP/ESR normal; ANA may be weakly positive in general population but not diagnostic; CK normal; imaging normal

## Assessment Approach

**Positive diagnosis — not exclusion:**
- Apply ACR 2010/2016 criteria after history and examination
- The diagnosis is clinical; normal investigations support it (do not over-investigate)
- Tender point exam (ACR 1990 criteria): 11/18 tender points no longer required; WPI/SSS criteria preferred

**Targeted investigations to exclude alternative diagnoses:**
- FBC, CRP, ESR: to exclude inflammatory arthritis, systemic disease
- TSH: hypothyroidism (fatigue, myalgia, cognitive slowing — mimics fibromyalgia)
- CK: exclude inflammatory myopathy
- Vitamin D: deficiency causes myalgia and fatigue; correct if low
- ANA: if clinical features of connective tissue disease (photosensitivity, Raynaud's, oral ulcers, rash)
- HbA1c: diabetic peripheral neuropathy can cause diffuse pain
- Do NOT order: extensive MRI whole body, PET scan, multiple rheumatology panels — low yield and reinforce illness behaviour

**Sleep assessment:**
- Screen for obstructive sleep apnoea (STOP-BANG): treating OSA can substantially improve fibromyalgia symptoms
- Assess restless legs syndrome: dopamine agonist treatment may improve sleep and pain

**Functional assessment:**
- Fibromyalgia Impact Questionnaire (FIQ): patient-reported; useful for monitoring treatment response
- Work capacity: many patients at risk of work loss; early occupational therapy referral

## Key Differentials

- Inflammatory arthritis (RA, SLE, spondyloarthropathy): synovitis on examination; elevated CRP/ESR; positive RF/ANA/HLA-B27; urgent rheumatology if suspected
- Inflammatory myopathy (polymyositis, dermatomyositis): proximal muscle weakness; elevated CK; heliotrope rash; rheumatology
- Polymyalgia rheumatica: shoulder and hip girdle stiffness; markedly elevated ESR/CRP; age >50; dramatic response to prednisolone
- Hypothyroidism: TSH elevated; fatigue, myalgia, cognitive slowing; treat and reassess
- Vitamin D deficiency: profound myalgia and fatigue; supplement and reassess before fibromyalgia label
- Sleep disorders (OSA, UARS): unrefreshed sleep; treat and reassess — may resolve fibromyalgia-like symptoms
- Chronic fatigue syndrome/ME-CFS: significant overlap; fatigue > pain; post-exertional malaise is defining feature; management differs (pacing, not graded exercise)

## Management Principles

**Multimodal biopsychosocial approach — most effective:**

Psychoeducation (essential first step):
- Explain central sensitisation: pain is real but originates in amplified pain processing, not ongoing tissue damage
- Validate the patient's experience; explicitly acknowledge the pain is real
- Discuss the bidirectional relationship between sleep, mood, stress, and pain
- This Way Up online CBT programs; Arthritis Australia fibromyalgia resources

Exercise (most evidence-based non-pharmacological treatment):
- Aerobic exercise: low-impact (hydrotherapy, walking, cycling, swimming); start very low and build slowly
- Resistance training: moderate benefit; twice weekly
- Avoid "boom-bust" pattern: gradual pacing with consistent activity; overdoing leads to flares
- Exercise physiologist referral: MBS-funded via Chronic Disease Management plan

Psychological therapies:
- CBT: pain catastrophising, sleep hygiene, activity pacing; GP Mental Health Care Plan (MBS 2713)
- Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT): highly effective in chronic pain
- Pain clinic referral: multidisciplinary pain management program (occupational therapy, psychology, physiotherapy) — most effective for severe or refractory fibromyalgia

Sleep management:
- Sleep hygiene education; treat OSA if present; restless legs treatment
- Avoid benzodiazepines and Z-drugs for chronic sleep in fibromyalgia — dependency risk; not effective long-term

**Pharmacotherapy:**

Low-dose amitriptyline (most evidence; off-label in fibromyalgia):
- 10–25 mg nocte; improves sleep quality and pain; sedating; not an antidepressant dose; warn about dry mouth, morning sedation
- Review after 4–6 weeks; if no benefit, cease

Duloxetine (SNRIs — PBS-restricted for MDD; off-label for fibromyalgia):
- 30–60 mg daily; reduces pain and improves function; also treats comorbid depression
- Pregabalin (Lyrica — PBS-restricted for fibromyalgia in Australia; 75–300 mg/day): reduces pain, improves sleep; weight gain, sedation, dependency potential — careful patient selection
- Gabapentin: off-label; similar to pregabalin; less evidence in fibromyalgia specifically

What NOT to prescribe:
- Opioids: not effective in central sensitisation pain; increase central sensitisation over time; risk of dependency; AVOID or cease existing opioids where possible
- NSAIDs: limited efficacy in fibromyalgia (not an inflammatory condition); GI and renal risk without significant benefit
- Corticosteroids: not effective; not indicated

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Amitriptyline** (PBS unrestricted: depression; 10–25 mg nocte in fibromyalgia — off-label but evidence-based; sedating; dry mouth; morning hangover)
- **Duloxetine** (Cymbalta — PBS restricted: MDD and GAD; concurrent depression/anxiety diagnosis supports prescribing; 30–120 mg daily)
- **Pregabalin** (Lyrica — PBS restricted: fibromyalgia is a listed PBS indication; authority required; 75–150 mg twice daily; monitor for sedation, weight gain, dependency)
- **Gabapentin** (PBS restricted: epilepsy and neuropathic pain; off-label in fibromyalgia; monitor carefully)
- **Note:** Opioids are NOT recommended for fibromyalgia; if patient already on opioids, develop tapering plan; refer to pain medicine specialist if complex

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent review:**
- Suicidal ideation: high prevalence in fibromyalgia; screen routinely; same-day assessment if active suicidal ideation
- Sudden objective new joint swelling, weakness, rash, fever — consider concurrent inflammatory condition; rheumatology

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Rheumatologist: diagnostic uncertainty; suspected concurrent inflammatory arthritis; first presentation for diagnosis confirmation
- Pain medicine specialist: refractory fibromyalgia; complex opioid dependency; consideration for multidisciplinary pain clinic
- Psychologist: CBT and ACT via GP Mental Health Care Plan
- Exercise physiologist: graded exercise program (MBS Chronic Disease Management plan)
- Occupational therapist: workplace accommodation, functional aids, activity pacing
- Physiotherapist: hydrotherapy, manual therapy, pacing education

## Telehealth Suitability

Fibromyalgia management is highly suitable for telehealth for established patients.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Symptom monitoring and FIQ review
- Medication review and adjustment
- Psychoeducation, sleep hygiene counselling
- Mental Health Care Plan (psychology referral) — MBS 2713
- Chronic Disease Management plan review (exercise physiologist, OT referral)
- Sleep assessment and management counselling
- Opioid tapering discussion and planning

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial physical examination to apply ACR criteria and exclude inflammatory arthritis
- Blood tests (pathology arranged)
- Tender point examination for diagnosis confirmation
- Intra-articular injection if concurrent joint pathology identified
