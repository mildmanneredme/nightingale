## Sexually Transmitted Infections Assessment (RACGP)

Sexually transmitted infections (STIs) are a significant public health concern in Australia, with rates of chlamydia, gonorrhoea, and syphilis rising substantially over the past decade. Chlamydia is the most commonly notified STI in Australia. This knowledge base file covers the three most common bacterial STIs managed in Australian general practice: chlamydia (Chlamydia trachomatis), gonorrhoea (Neisseria gonorrhoeae), and bacterial vaginosis (BV — Gardnerella vaginalis and polymicrobial). Viral STIs (herpes simplex, HPV) and bloodborne viruses (HIV, hepatitis B/C) are covered in separate knowledge base entries. Telehealth is highly suitable for results review, treatment prescribing, and partner notification support; initial testing may require a pathology or in-person component.

## Clinical Presentation

**Chlamydia:** Most infections are asymptomatic (70–80% of women, 50% of men). When symptomatic: urethral discharge, dysuria, pelvic pain, intermenstrual bleeding, epididymo-orchitis (male), or conjunctivitis. Rectal chlamydia: rectal discharge, discomfort (in men who have sex with men — MSM). Complications: pelvic inflammatory disease (PID), tubal factor infertility, ectopic pregnancy risk.

**Gonorrhoea:** Urethral discharge (purulent, yellow/green), dysuria (males), often asymptomatic in females. Pharyngeal and rectal infections often asymptomatic. Disseminated gonococcal infection (rare): septic arthritis, skin pustules, sepsis.

**Bacterial vaginosis (BV):** Characteristic thin grey-white homogeneous vaginal discharge with an offensive "fishy" odour (amine test positive). Vaginal pH >4.5. Often asymptomatic. Not sexually transmitted per se but associated with sexual activity. Diagnosed by Amsel's criteria or Nugent score on microscopy. Complications in pregnancy: preterm labour, chorioamnionitis.

## Assessment Approach

Comprehensive sexual health history: number and gender of partners, type of sexual activity (vaginal, anal, oral), condom use, last sexual contact, STI history, symptoms, last STI screen, contraceptive method, pregnancy status.

Who to screen: ASHM recommends annual chlamydia and gonorrhoea testing in all sexually active people aged <30 years; more frequent if multiple partners, no barrier contraception, or symptomatic. Opportunistic testing at any GP visit for eligible patients.

Testing approach:
- Chlamydia + gonorrhoea NAAT (nucleic acid amplification test): preferred; high sensitivity/specificity. First-void urine (male), self-collected vaginal swab (female, preferred by many patients — can be done at home and mailed). Throat and rectal swabs in MSM or if relevant exposure.
- Additional screening for at-risk populations: syphilis serology, HIV Ag/Ab, hepatitis B sAg and anti-HBs, hepatitis C antibody.
- Microscopy for BV diagnosis: wet prep or Gram stain — requires in-person swab collection; BV can sometimes be treated empirically based on clinical history and symptoms.

Self-collection kits: available through pathology labs (patient posts samples); appropriate for telehealth-initiated testing.

## Key Differentials

- Chlamydia vs gonorrhoea: clinically indistinguishable; NAAT testing differentiates; treat for both empirically if gonorrhoea suspected or confirmed in syndromic management
- Trichomonas vaginalis: vaginal discharge with irritation; NAAT or microscopy; treat with metronidazole
- Candidal vulvovaginitis: white clumpy discharge, itch, external dysuria; not STI; treat with antifungal
- Urethritis without STI: Mycoplasma genitalium, Ureaplasma; test for M. genitalium if urethritis without chlamydia/gonorrhoea
- PID: chlamydia or gonorrhoea ascending infection; bilateral lower pelvic pain, cervical motion tenderness; treat urgently

## Management Principles

**Chlamydia treatment:**
- Doxycycline 100 mg twice daily for 7 days — preferred first-line (higher microbiological cure rate than azithromycin)
- Azithromycin 1 g single dose — alternative if adherence to 7-day course is a concern; less effective for rectal chlamydia
- Abstain from sexual contact for 7 days after treatment completion (and until partner treated)
- Test of cure: not routinely required after uncomplicated genital chlamydia treated with doxycycline; test of cure recommended for rectal or pharyngeal chlamydia, pregnant women, or if azithromycin used

**Gonorrhoea treatment (important: dual therapy due to rising resistance):**
- Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM single dose (first-line) — requires in-person injection; may be arranged via GP clinic or sexual health centre
- Azithromycin 1 g oral added to reduce resistance risk (dual therapy standard)
- Culture and sensitivity: collect gonococcal culture BEFORE treating whenever possible — resistance monitoring imperative
- Test of cure at 2 weeks post-treatment: recommended for all gonorrhoea cases
- Report as notifiable condition (all Australian states/territories)

**Bacterial vaginosis treatment:**
- Metronidazole 400 mg twice daily for 7 days — first-line oral treatment
- Metronidazole 2 g single dose — alternative (lower cure rate but better adherence)
- Metronidazole 0.75% vaginal gel for 5 days — effective topical alternative; less systemic side effects
- Clindamycin 2% vaginal cream for 7 days — alternative if metronidazole intolerant
- Avoid alcohol during and for 24 hours after oral metronidazole (disulfiram-like reaction)
- Recurrent BV: consider repeat course; long-term suppressive therapy (boric acid vaginal suppositories, vaginal lactic acid gel) — specialist input for recurrent BV

**Partner notification:**
- All STI-positive patients must be advised to notify recent partners. In Australia, timeframes: chlamydia — contact tracing back 6 months (or last partner if <6 months); gonorrhoea — back 2 months; syphilis — varies by stage.
- Options: patient-led notification, GP-assisted notification, or anonymous digital notification (e.g., Let Them Know — ASHM platform)
- Contacts should be tested and treated empirically

**Notifiable conditions:** Chlamydia and gonorrhoea are notifiable in all Australian states/territories — report through state/territory health department; automated laboratory reporting also occurs.

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Doxycycline** (PBS unrestricted — chlamydia treatment; 100 mg twice daily for 7 days)
- **Azithromycin** (PBS restricted: chlamydial infection — 1 g single dose; also dual therapy with ceftriaxone for gonorrhoea)
- **Ceftriaxone** (PBS restricted: gonococcal infection; 500 mg IM — requires in-person administration or prescribing for sexual health clinic)
- **Metronidazole** (PBS unrestricted — BV; 400 mg twice daily for 7 days; 0.75% vaginal gel not PBS-listed)
- **Clindamycin vaginal cream** (Dalacin — PBS restricted: BV when metronidazole fails or intolerant)
- **Metronidazole 2 g single dose** (PBS unrestricted)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Suspected septic arthritis or disseminated gonococcal infection: fever, polyarthralgia, skin pustules in sexually active young person — IV antibiotics required
- Suspected gonococcal sepsis or meningitis — rare but medical emergency

**Urgent in-person assessment:**
- Suspected PID: lower abdominal or pelvic pain + fever + vaginal discharge + tenderness — same-day oral or IV antibiotics required; in-person examination essential
- Suspected epididymo-orchitis: unilateral testicular swelling and pain — same-day treatment; exclude testicular torsion (emergency)
- First-trimester pregnant patient with positive chlamydia or gonorrhoea — same-day treatment (risks to pregnancy)

**Notifiable disease management:**
- Report chlamydia and gonorrhoea diagnoses to state/territory health department (usually automated via laboratory)
- Provide partner notification counselling and support

## Telehealth Suitability

STI management is highly suitable for telehealth, with pathology arranged remotely.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Sexual health history and screening indication assessment
- Self-collection pathology kit arrangement (postal kit, home collection)
- Results review and treatment prescribing (chlamydia, BV via oral agents)
- Partner notification counselling
- Contact tracing guidance
- Safe sex counselling and condom provision

**Requires in-person assessment or arrangement:**
- Ceftriaxone IM injection for gonorrhoea treatment — requires clinical setting or referral to sexual health clinic
- Pelvic examination for suspected PID or STI complications
- Gonorrhoea culture collection (genital swab — may require in-person component for culture vs NAAT)
- Suspected disseminated infection or systemic complications
