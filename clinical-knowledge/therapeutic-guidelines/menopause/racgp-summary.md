## Menopause Assessment (RACGP)

Menopause is defined as the permanent cessation of menstruation due to loss of ovarian follicular activity, diagnosed retrospectively after 12 months of amenorrhoea in a woman aged ≥45 years. The average age of natural menopause in Australian women is 51–52 years. Perimenopause (menopausal transition) typically begins 4–6 years before the final menstrual period. Vasomotor symptoms (hot flushes and night sweats) are the hallmark of the menopausal transition and affect up to 80% of women. Menopausal hormone therapy (MHT) remains the most effective treatment for vasomotor symptoms and genitourinary syndrome of menopause. Telehealth is highly suitable for menopause assessment, MHT initiation, and ongoing review.

## Clinical Presentation

**Vasomotor symptoms:** Hot flushes (sudden sensation of heat, often with sweating and flushing, lasting 1–5 minutes) and night sweats. Frequency ranges from occasional to many per hour. Severity impacts sleep, mood, work, and relationships.

**Genitourinary syndrome of menopause (GSM):** Vulvovaginal atrophy, vaginal dryness, dyspareunia, urinary urgency, recurrent UTIs. Chronic and progressive — does not resolve spontaneously without treatment.

**Sleep disturbance:** Often secondary to night sweats; also independent menopausal effect.

**Mood changes:** Irritability, low mood, anxiety — common during perimenopause; distinguish from depressive disorder.

**Cognitive symptoms:** Brain fog, memory and concentration difficulties — common; usually transient.

**Musculoskeletal:** Joint aches and pains, reduced muscle mass.

**Premature ovarian insufficiency (POI):** Menopause before age 40; requires investigation (chromosomal, autoimmune causes); MHT recommended until average age of menopause to protect bone and cardiovascular health.

## Assessment Approach

Diagnosis: clinical in women ≥45 years with typical symptoms and menstrual irregularity or amenorrhoea. Hormone testing (FSH, oestradiol) not required for diagnosis in this group — highly variable and not reliable.

FSH/oestradiol testing useful for: women <45 years with menopausal symptoms (exclude POI), women with hysterectomy and uncertain menopausal status, women on hormonal contraception masking menstrual changes.

Assess at every menopause consultation:
- Vasomotor symptom severity (frequency, impact on daily life) — Menopause Rating Scale or Greene Climacteric Scale
- GSM symptoms — vaginal dryness, dyspareunia, urinary symptoms
- Sleep quality
- Mood — PHQ-9 and GAD-7 if depression or anxiety suspected
- Cardiovascular risk factors (hypertension, lipids, diabetes, smoking, BMI) — MHT decision incorporates CVD risk
- Bone health — DEXA scan if risk factors for osteoporosis (early menopause, POI, prolonged amenorrhoea, corticosteroid use, family history of hip fracture, low BMI, smoking)
- Breast cancer history or family history — affects MHT counselling and choice
- VTE history — oral oestrogen increases VTE risk; transdermal oestrogen does not

Contraindications to systemic MHT (absolute): current oestrogen-sensitive breast cancer or history of breast cancer (relative — discuss with oncologist), unexplained vaginal bleeding, active VTE or high VTE risk, active liver disease, uncontrolled hypertension.

## Key Differentials

- Depressive disorder — distinguish menopausal mood changes from major depression; PHQ-9; both may coexist
- Thyroid disease — hypothyroidism or hyperthyroidism may cause hot flushes, mood changes, irregular periods; check TSH
- Primary ovarian insufficiency / premature menopause — age <40; FSH elevated; requires investigation and early MHT
- Secondary amenorrhoea from other causes — hypothalamic (weight loss, excessive exercise, stress), prolactinoma
- Carcinoid tumour or phaeochromocytoma — rare causes of flushing; consider if atypical features (diarrhoea, hypertension, no sweating pattern)

## Management Principles

**Lifestyle:** Regular aerobic and resistance exercise reduces severity of vasomotor symptoms, improves mood, maintains bone density, and reduces cardiovascular risk. Cooling strategies: fans, cool clothing, identify triggers (alcohol, caffeine, spicy food, hot beverages, stress). Mindfulness-based stress reduction has modest evidence for vasomotor symptoms.

**Menopausal hormone therapy (MHT) — most effective treatment:**

For women with uterus: combined MHT (oestrogen + progestogen) — progestogen required to protect endometrium from oestrogen-stimulated hyperplasia. Options: sequential (cyclical progestogen — withdrawal bleed occurs) or continuous combined (no bleed — preferred for postmenopause ≥1 year).

For women without uterus (hysterectomy): oestrogen alone — no progestogen required.

Route of oestrogen:
- Transdermal oestrogen (patch, gel, spray) — preferred; does not increase VTE risk (unlike oral oestrogen); preferred for women with cardiovascular risk, VTE history, migraine with aura, hypertension, obesity
- Oral oestrogen (tablets) — effective; small increased VTE and stroke risk; convenient

Progestogen options:
- Micronised progesterone (Prometrium, Utrogestan) — most physiological; favourable breast cancer risk profile compared to synthetic progestogens; also available as pessary
- Norethisterone, medroxyprogesterone acetate — synthetic progestogens; effective but may be associated with slightly higher breast cancer risk

Duration: MHT has no mandatory time limit; continue as long as benefits outweigh risks with annual review. Risk-benefit reassessment at each annual review.

MHT and breast cancer: current evidence (RACGP, AMS): combined MHT with synthetic progestogen is associated with a small increase in breast cancer risk (approximately 1 extra case per 1,000 women over 5 years). MHT with micronised progesterone appears to have a lower risk. Oestrogen-only MHT (for hysterectomised women) may not increase breast cancer risk. These risks are small and must be weighed against significant benefits in symptomatic women.

**Genitourinary syndrome of menopause (GSM):** Local vaginal oestrogen (cream, pessary, ring) — highly effective for GSM; minimal systemic absorption; safe long-term; safe in most women with history of breast cancer (confirm with oncologist); does not require progestogen even in women with uterus. Ospemifene (selective oestrogen receptor modulator — oral) — PBS-listed for dyspareunia from GSM.

**Non-hormonal options for vasomotor symptoms (for women with contraindication to MHT):**
- SSRIs/SNRIs: escitalopram, venlafaxine — reduce hot flush frequency by ~50%; off-label use
- Gabapentin — effective for nocturnal hot flushes; off-label
- Clonidine — modest effect; side effects limit use
- Cognitive behavioural therapy for menopause — evidence-based non-hormonal option

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Estradiol transdermal patch** (Estradot, Climara — PBS restricted: menopausal vasomotor symptoms; various strengths 25–100 mcg/24h)
- **Estradiol gel** (Oestrogel — PBS restricted: menopausal symptoms; 0.06% gel 1–2 pumps daily)
- **Estradiol oral tablets** (Estrofem, Zumenon — PBS restricted)
- **Micronised progesterone** (Prometrium — PBS restricted: endometrial protection in combined MHT; also indicated for luteal phase support in IVF; 100 mg or 200 mg capsules)
- **Tibolone** (PBS restricted: menopausal symptoms in postmenopausal women ≥1 year post-LMP; combined oestrogenic/progestogenic/androgenic activity)
- **Local vaginal oestrogen** (Ovestin cream, Vagifem pessaries, Estring ring — PBS restricted: atrophic vaginitis/GSM)
- **Ospemifene** (Senshio — PBS restricted: moderate-to-severe dyspareunia from GSM in postmenopausal women)
- **Escitalopram, venlafaxine** (PBS unrestricted for depression/anxiety; off-label for vasomotor symptoms)

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent in-person assessment:**
- Postmenopausal bleeding — any vaginal bleeding ≥12 months after last period; requires endometrial assessment (transvaginal ultrasound, endometrial biopsy if indicated) to exclude endometrial cancer
- Unexplained bleeding while on continuous combined MHT after 6 months of use

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Gynaecology: PMB, complex MHT management, POI, surgical menopause
- Oncology/breast surgeon: guidance on MHT use after breast cancer treatment
- Endocrinology: POI investigation, complex hormonal issues

## Telehealth Suitability

Menopause assessment and MHT management is highly suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Symptom assessment (vasomotor, GSM, mood, sleep)
- MHT initiation counselling and prescribing (transdermal or oral oestrogen + progestogen)
- MHT review, dose adjustment, and switching
- Local vaginal oestrogen initiation
- Non-hormonal option initiation and review
- Risk-benefit discussions including breast cancer and VTE risk
- Bone health assessment and osteoporosis referral

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Postmenopausal bleeding — transvaginal ultrasound and possible endometrial biopsy
- Pelvic examination if clinical concern about uterine or ovarian pathology
- BP measurement if starting oral oestrogen (can be arranged at pharmacy or with home monitor)
