## Bipolar Disorder Assessment (RACGP)

Bipolar disorder is a chronic recurrent mood disorder characterised by episodes of mania (or hypomania) alternating with depression, with periods of euthymia. It affects approximately 2% of Australians. Bipolar I disorder is defined by at least one manic episode; Bipolar II by hypomania plus major depressive episodes. Bipolar disorder is frequently misdiagnosed — most patients present with depressive episodes and may receive an incorrect unipolar depression diagnosis for years. Premature prescribing of antidepressants without mood stabilisers in unrecognised bipolar disorder can precipitate mania or rapid cycling. The GP plays a critical role in: recognising bipolar disorder, screening with validated tools, coordinating psychiatrist care, monitoring mood stabiliser medications and their side effects, managing physical health comorbidities, and supporting patients and families. Telehealth is suitable for stable monitoring visits.

## Clinical Presentation

**Bipolar I — manic episode features (DSM-5):**

Manic episode (≥7 days, or any duration if hospitalisation required):
- Elevated or expansive mood OR irritability (not consistent with bipolar depression alone)
- Grandiosity (inflated self-esteem)
- Decreased need for sleep (not fatigue — patient feels rested after 3 hours)
- Pressured speech (racing, difficult to interrupt)
- Racing thoughts / flight of ideas
- Distractibility
- Goal-directed activity increase OR psychomotor agitation
- Impulsive high-risk behaviour: gambling, sexual indiscretions, financial recklessness, drug use

Psychotic features in mania: hallucinations, delusions — common in severe mania; psychiatric emergency if present

**Bipolar II — hypomanic episode:**
- Same features as mania but ≥4 days; NO significant functional impairment; NO psychosis
- Subjectively positive in early phase — often patient-denied; informant history crucial
- Followed by major depressive episodes (often more severe and prolonged than manic episodes)

**Bipolar depressive phase (most time spent here):**
- Clinically indistinguishable from MDD on cross-section
- Features suggesting bipolar depression: hypersomnia (sleeping excessively), psychomotor retardation, psychotic features in depression, young age of first depression, atypical features (mood reactivity, leaden paralysis), family history of bipolar disorder, previous "hypomania" on antidepressants
- MDQ (Mood Disorder Questionnaire): validated bipolar screening tool; administer when prescribing antidepressants for depression in young patients or those with atypical features

**Rapid cycling:**
- ≥4 distinct mood episodes in 12 months; associated with antidepressant use in bipolar disorder; lithium less effective; valproate, lamotrigine, quetiapine preferred

## Assessment Approach

**Screening and diagnosis:**
- MDQ (Mood Disorder Questionnaire): screen when depression presentation has atypical features or bipolar indicators; 13-item; sensitivity 70%, specificity 90%; positive if ≥7 items + "yes" to clustering + moderate/serious impact
- Collateral history from partner or family: critical for identifying hypomanic periods patient does not recall or dismisses
- Psychiatric review: diagnosis should be confirmed or significantly guided by psychiatrist before commencing mood stabilisers

**GP monitoring (established bipolar disorder on mood stabilisers):**

Lithium monitoring (most critical — narrow therapeutic index):
- Serum lithium level: target 0.6–0.8 mmol/L (maintenance); 0.8–1.0 mmol/L (acute mania); TOXIC >1.2–1.5 mmol/L
- Sampling: 12-hour trough (taken 12 hours after last dose); frequency: monthly until stable, then 3–6 monthly
- Renal function (eGFR, creatinine): lithium is renally excreted; nephrotoxic with long-term use; 3–6 monthly
- Thyroid function (TSH): lithium causes hypothyroidism in 20–40%; 6–12 monthly
- Parathyroid / calcium: lithium causes hyperparathyroidism; annual calcium
- Lithium toxicity signs: coarse tremor, ataxia, confusion, slurred speech, nausea/vomiting — check level urgently; hold lithium; aggressive hydration; severe toxicity — hospital
- Drug interactions: NSAIDs (raise lithium levels — potentially toxic), ACE inhibitors, thiazide diuretics, metronidazole — all increase lithium levels; alert patients

Valproate (sodium valproate, Epilim) monitoring:
- Serum valproate level: 50–100 mg/L (trough); useful to confirm adherence and guide dosing
- LFTs: hepatotoxicity (rare but serious); FBC: thrombocytopenia; polycystic ovarian morphology in women
- Weight gain: very common; metabolic monitoring
- ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED in pregnancy: major teratogen (neural tube defects 1–2%, neonatal valproate syndrome); contraception MANDATORY in all women of reproductive age; REMS-equivalent program Australia

Quetiapine monitoring:
- Metabolic monitoring: weight, waist circumference, fasting glucose, lipids — annually
- QTc: baseline ECG; caution with QT-prolonging medications

Lamotrigine monitoring:
- Rash (Stevens-Johnson syndrome risk) — slow titration essential; cease and refer if rash with systemic features
- Interactions: valproate doubles lamotrigine levels (halve dose); carbamazepine halves lamotrigine levels

**Annual GP review for bipolar disorder:**
- Mood stability (PHQ-9 for depression; Young Mania Rating Scale not routinely used in GP)
- Medication adherence — bipolar patients frequently self-discontinue during euthymia
- Substance use: alcohol, cannabis (precipitates episodes); screen at every review
- Cardiovascular risk: antipsychotics and mood stabilisers increase metabolic risk; annual fasting glucose, lipids, BP, weight
- Sleep: disrupted sleep precipitates episodes; assess and treat sleep disorders
- Stressors and life events: precipitate episodes; psychosocial review

## Key Differentials

- Unipolar MDD: most common misdiagnosis; MDQ screening crucial; SSRI monotherapy in bipolar disorder can precipitate mania
- Borderline personality disorder: affective instability but reactive to interpersonal events; unstable identity; self-harm; episodic impulsivity; psychiatric assessment
- ADHD: inattention, impulsivity, emotional dysregulation; can co-occur with bipolar; complex; psychiatric assessment; stimulants can worsen bipolar mania
- Substance use disorder: alcohol, stimulant, and cannabis use can cause bipolar-like presentations; careful timeline elicitation; urine drug screen
- Cyclothymic disorder: subsyndromal hypomanic and depressive symptoms for ≥2 years; not meeting full episode criteria
- Schizophrenia and schizoaffective disorder: prominent psychosis; psychiatric assessment; risperidone or clozapine management

## Management Principles

**Psychiatrist-initiated and co-managed — GP supports:**

Acute mania (hospital-level care if severe):
- Lithium + antipsychotic (quetiapine, risperidone, olanzapine, aripiprazole) OR valproate + antipsychotic
- Benzodiazepine short-term for agitation
- DO NOT use antidepressants in mania — worsen or prolong episode
- Hospital admission for: psychosis, severe functional impairment, safety risk, self-neglect

Maintenance (relapse prevention — GP co-manages):
- Lithium: most evidence for relapse prevention (both poles); antisuicidal effect; narrow therapeutic index
- Valproate: effective for mania prevention; contraindicated in women of reproductive age
- Lamotrigine: most evidence for bipolar depression prevention; less effective for mania
- Quetiapine (Seroquel): approved for bipolar maintenance; both poles; metabolic monitoring required
- Lurasidone: newer atypical antipsychotic; evidence for bipolar depression; PBS-listed

Bipolar depression treatment:
- DO NOT use antidepressants without a mood stabiliser or antipsychotic — risk of triggering mania or rapid cycling
- Quetiapine: strong evidence for bipolar depression; PBS-listed for bipolar disorder
- Lamotrigine + mood stabiliser: effective for bipolar depression maintenance
- Lurasidone: PBS-listed for bipolar depression
- Lithium: moderate effect on bipolar depression; may augment with quetiapine

Psychological therapies:
- CBT adapted for bipolar disorder: relapse signatures, lifestyle regulation
- Family-focused therapy: carer education and support
- IPSRT (Interpersonal and Social Rhythm Therapy): sleep-wake cycle stabilisation; evidence-based
- Not recommended as sole treatment without pharmacotherapy

Lifestyle:
- Sleep hygiene: maintain consistent sleep-wake cycles; sleep disruption is a major precipitant
- Alcohol and cannabis: significant risk for relapse; cessation counselling
- Exercise: mood-stabilising effect; moderate evidence
- Stress management and psychoeducation: recognise early warning signs (mood diary)

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Lithium carbonate (Lithicarb, Priadel)** (PBS restricted: bipolar disorder; authority required; narrow therapeutic index; monthly monitoring initially)
- **Sodium valproate (Epilim)** (PBS restricted: bipolar disorder; ABSOLUTE CONTRAINDICATION in pregnancy; mandatory contraception in women of reproductive age; weight gain, tremor, hair loss)
- **Quetiapine (Seroquel)** (PBS restricted: bipolar disorder mania and depression; metabolic monitoring; sedating)
- **Lamotrigine (Lamictal)** (PBS restricted: bipolar disorder; slow titration required; 25 mg daily increasing every 2 weeks; rash — cease immediately if systemic)
- **Olanzapine** (PBS restricted: mania; significant metabolic adverse effects; avoid long-term monotherapy)
- **Lurasidone (Latuda)** (PBS restricted: bipolar I depression; newer; metabolic profile better than olanzapine; specialist initiation)
- **Note:** NEVER prescribe antidepressant monotherapy in known or suspected bipolar disorder; if diagnosis uncertain — psychiatry consultation before SSRI initiation

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Acute mania with psychosis — psychiatric emergency; hospital admission
- Severe depression with active suicidal ideation or plan — 000 or ED
- Lithium toxicity (level >1.5 mmol/L with symptoms, or >2.0 mmol/L) — IV fluids + hospital; severe toxicity → haemodialysis

**Urgent psychiatric review:**
- Any suspected first manic episode (even mild) — needs psychiatric assessment and diagnosis confirmation
- Breakthrough mood episode on established therapy — medication review with psychiatrist
- Postpartum bipolar episode — high-risk period; urgent psychiatric review; mother and infant safety assessment
- Antidepressant-triggered hypomania or mania in patient previously thought to have unipolar depression

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Psychiatrist: all new diagnoses; medication initiation; ongoing co-management
- Psychologist: CBT for bipolar, IPSRT (GP Mental Health Care Plan MBS 2713)
- SANE Australia: bipolar peer support and resources
- Carers Australia (1800 422 737): support for family members

## Telehealth Suitability

Stable bipolar disorder monitoring is well suited to telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Mood monitoring and medication adherence review
- Blood result interpretation (lithium level, TSH, renal function)
- Metabolic monitoring counselling
- Psychoeducation — relapse prevention, sleep hygiene, substance use
- Mental Health Care Plan (MBS 2713) for psychology referral
- Carer support review

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- New mood episode assessment — safety assessment requires in-person or ED
- Physical examination if lithium toxicity suspected
- Blood tests (pathology arranged)
- ECG (baseline and if QTc concerns with antipsychotics)
