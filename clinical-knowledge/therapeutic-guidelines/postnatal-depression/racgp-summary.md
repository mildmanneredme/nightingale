## Postnatal Depression Assessment (RACGP)

Postnatal depression (PND) is the most common medical complication of childbirth, affecting approximately 1 in 7 women in Australia in the first year after birth. It is distinct from the normal "baby blues" (brief, self-limiting tearfulness peaking at day 3–5). PND significantly impacts maternal wellbeing, infant attachment, child development, and family relationships. GPs play a central role in universal screening using the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS), early diagnosis, and managing mild-moderate PND with psychological therapies and, when indicated, pharmacotherapy. Partners and fathers are also at risk (prevalence ~10%). Perinatal depression and anxiety (during pregnancy) also merit early identification. Telehealth is highly suitable for postnatal depression assessment and management.

## Clinical Presentation

**Baby blues vs postnatal depression:**
- Baby blues: extremely common (50–80%); onset day 3–5; tearfulness, emotional lability, anxiety, fatigue; self-limiting by 10–14 days; reassurance and support
- Postnatal depression: onset within 12 months of birth (usually 4–6 weeks); persistent; impairs function; requires clinical management

**PND symptoms:**
- Persistent low mood, tearfulness, hopelessness
- Loss of interest in the baby or difficulty bonding (may cause profound guilt)
- Excessive anxiety and worry about the baby's health or safety
- Fatigue (distinct from normal new parent fatigue — persistent, disproportionate)
- Irritability and anger
- Sleep disturbance beyond normal infant care needs
- Poor appetite, weight change
- Difficulty concentrating, poor memory
- Thoughts of harming self or the baby — must be assessed at every visit (infanticide thoughts, though rare, require urgent assessment)

**Perinatal anxiety (during pregnancy and postnatally):** Anxiety is as prevalent as depression perinatally; overlaps with PND; intrusive thoughts about the baby's safety, health anxiety, OCD-like features (checking, reassurance-seeking); often missed; GAD-7 supplement to EPDS.

**Risk factors for PND:**
- Previous history of depression or PND (strongest risk factor)
- Anxiety during pregnancy
- Life stressors (relationship difficulties, financial stress, loss, isolation)
- Lack of social support
- History of trauma or abuse
- Complicated pregnancy or birth, neonatal complications
- Infant with feeding difficulties, colic, poor sleep

## Assessment Approach

**Routine universal screening (mandatory in Australian perinatal care):**
- Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS): 10-item validated self-report questionnaire; designed for perinatal use; validated in Australian populations; administered at:
  - 6–8 week postnatal check
  - Antenatal visits (screen once per trimester or at-risk assessment)
  - Any other postnatal contact when clinically indicated
- EPDS scoring: ≥13 = probable depression (PPV ~70%); ≥10 = possible depression (warrants clinical assessment); question 10 (self-harm) — any positive response = immediate safety assessment regardless of total score
- GAD-7 supplement for anxiety: screen concurrently with EPDS

**Clinical assessment after positive screen:**
- Confirm diagnosis: duration, severity, impact on functioning, bonding with infant
- Suicidal ideation and risk: assess at every review; passive ("I'd be better off dead") vs active (plan, intent, means)
- Thoughts about harming the baby: rare but ask directly; any positive response requires urgent assessment
- Social support assessment: partner, family support, isolation
- Substance use: alcohol and other drugs in postpartum period
- Medical screen: exclude thyroid disease (postpartum thyroiditis — common cause of mood disturbance at 6 weeks and 6 months postpartum; check TSH); anaemia (FBC, iron)
- Safeguarding assessment: infant wellbeing and safety

## Key Differentials

- Baby blues: transient, self-limiting by 2 weeks; reassurance and support only
- Postpartum psychosis: rare (1:1000); rapid onset; severe; hallucinations, delusions, severe disorganised behaviour; psychiatric emergency — hospital admission required urgently
- Hypothyroidism (postpartum thyroiditis): mood changes, fatigue, cold intolerance; TSH, FT4; treat if symptomatic hypothyroidism
- Anaemia: fatigue, low mood; FBC, iron studies; treat
- Adjustment disorder with depressed mood: identifiable stressor; less severe; psychological support; antidepressant not usually indicated

## Management Principles

**Mild PND (EPDS 10–12, adequate functioning, good social support):**
- Psychoeducation: normalise; explain PND as medical condition; reduce stigma; explain treatment options
- Social support facilitation: partner involvement; family; community programs (PANDA — Perinatal Anxiety and Depression Australia — 1300 726 306; beyondblue; Home Visiting Program)
- Psychological strategies: CBT-based (structured problem-solving, behavioural activation); self-help resources; This Way Up postnatal anxiety/depression online program
- Regular GP review: 2-weekly; monitor with EPDS; safety assessment at each visit
- Peer support: playgroup, mothers' groups, local council programs

**Moderate-severe PND (EPDS ≥13, significant functional impairment):**

Psychological therapies (first-line or concurrent with pharmacotherapy):
- CBT: most evidence-based; 8–12 sessions; Medicare GP Mental Health Care Plan (up to 10 sessions — MBS Item 2713)
- Interpersonal therapy (IPT): specifically adapted for postnatal period; focuses on role transitions and relationships
- Psychologist referral: GP Mental Health Care Plan

Pharmacotherapy (moderate-severe PND or failure of psychological therapy alone):
- Antidepressants compatible with breastfeeding: all antidepressants are excreted in breast milk in small amounts; risks from untreated PND generally outweigh risks of antidepressant in breast milk; discuss with patient; consult LactMed or Mothersafe (NSW) for specific drug data
  - Sertraline: most studied in breastfeeding; low breast milk levels; preferred first-line in breastfeeding
  - Paroxetine: also low levels but discontinuation syndrome concern
  - Escitalopram: reasonable data; low milk levels
  - Fluoxetine: longer half-life; higher infant exposure; less preferred in breastfeeding; reasonable option if formula feeding or non-breastfeeding
  - Venlafaxine: reasonable data; small amounts in milk; option if SSRI ineffective
- Start at low dose; discuss that benefit takes 4–6 weeks; reassess at 6 weeks
- Duration: at least 6–12 months after recovery to prevent relapse

**Partner/father PND:** Screen with EPDS or PHQ-9; refer to psychologist and beyondblue PANDA for support.

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Sertraline** (PBS restricted: major depressive disorder; preferred SSRI in breastfeeding; 25–200 mg daily; start 25–50 mg)
- **Escitalopram** (PBS restricted: major depressive disorder; 5–20 mg daily; lower milk transfer — reasonable breastfeeding option)
- **Paroxetine** (PBS restricted: MDD, GAD; 10–40 mg; low milk levels; avoid abrupt cessation — severe discontinuation syndrome)
- **Fluoxetine** (PBS restricted: MDD; 20–60 mg; higher infant exposure via milk; less preferred in breastfeeding; reasonable if not breastfeeding)
- **Note:** Mothersafe NSW (1800 647 848): free evidence-based medication in pregnancy and breastfeeding advice service; use for any uncertainty about specific medications; consult LactMed database (free online, US NIH) for breast milk transfer data

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / mental health emergency:**
- Postpartum psychosis: rapid onset confusion, hallucinations, delusions, extreme agitation — psychiatric emergency; mother AND infant safety; hospital admission; call 000
- Active suicidal ideation with plan or intent
- Thoughts of harming the baby with intent

**Urgent same-day psychiatric or perinatal mental health team review:**
- Severe PND with inability to care for self or infant
- Any thoughts of harming the baby (even passive) — immediate safety assessment; safeguarding notification if baby at risk
- Passive suicidal ideation with escalating severity

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Psychologist: CBT via GP Mental Health Care Plan (MBS 2713)
- PANDA (Perinatal Anxiety and Depression Australia): free peer support and counselling 1300 726 306
- Perinatal psychiatrist: severe PND, postpartum psychosis recovery, medication complexity, bipolar disorder in pregnancy/postpartum
- Child and Family Health Nurse: infant health, parenting support, development monitoring
- Social worker: social support, financial stress, domestic violence screening (always screen in PND assessment)

## Telehealth Suitability

Postnatal depression management is highly suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- EPDS administration and scoring (patient completes before consultation)
- Mental health assessment, diagnosis confirmation
- Safety assessment (suicidal ideation, thoughts about baby) — document carefully
- Antidepressant prescribing and monitoring
- GP Mental Health Care Plan (MBS 2713) for psychology referral
- PANDA referral and psychoeducation
- Partner/father assessment and support

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- 6-week postnatal check (ideally includes physical examination of mother)
- Blood tests (TSH, FBC, iron — pathology arranged)
- If safeguarding concerns about infant or mother's safety — in-person assessment preferred
