## ADHD in Adults Assessment (RACGP)

Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) in adults is a neurodevelopmental condition characterised by persistent inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity that significantly impairs daily functioning. Adult ADHD is substantially underdiagnosed in Australia, with prevalence estimated at 2–5% of adults. The 2022 AADPA guidelines markedly expanded the GP's role in ADHD assessment and prescribing in Australia. In most Australian states/territories, GPs with appropriate training may now initiate stimulant medications (shared care or independent prescribing) following confirmation of diagnosis, though regulatory arrangements vary by state. Telehealth is highly suitable for ADHD assessment, monitoring, and prescribing reviews.

## Clinical Presentation

**Predominantly inattentive presentation:** Difficulty sustaining attention, frequently losing items, forgetfulness in daily activities, failing to follow through on tasks, poor organisation, distractibility, avoiding tasks requiring sustained mental effort.

**Predominantly hyperactive-impulsive presentation:** Fidgeting, difficulty remaining seated, excessive talking, interrupting others, difficulty waiting, acting without thinking, restlessness (in adults may be internal restlessness rather than overt hyperactivity).

**Combined presentation:** Most common in adults — features of both inattention and hyperactivity-impulsivity.

Adult ADHD presentations are often subtler than childhood presentations. Common complaints include: chronic procrastination, poor time management, difficulty completing projects, emotional dysregulation (frustration, irritability), relationship difficulties, low self-esteem, and underachievement relative to intellectual potential. Many adults have developed compensatory strategies masking impairments.

**Comorbidities:** Extremely common — anxiety disorders (up to 50%), depression (30–40%), substance use disorders (20–30%), autism spectrum disorder, learning disabilities, sleep disorders.

## Assessment Approach

Diagnosis is clinical — requires DSM-5 criteria: ≥5 symptoms from inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity domains, present for ≥6 months, in ≥2 settings (home, work, relationships), with impairment in social, academic, or occupational functioning, and symptoms present before age 12 years (may be confirmed retrospectively via history or childhood records).

**Key assessment steps:**
- Structured clinical interview: onset of symptoms, childhood history (school reports, parental accounts if available), functional impairment across multiple life domains
- Validated rating scales: Adult ADHD Self-Report Scale (ASRS v1.1) — 6-item screener (≥4 positive = high suspicion); Conners' Adult ADHD Rating Scale (CAARS) — full clinical assessment; DIVA-5 interview (online, free) structured DSM-5 diagnostic interview
- Rule out medical causes: thyroid function (TSH), iron studies (iron deficiency worsens attention), sleep study if sleep apnoea suspected
- Mental health assessment: PHQ-9, GAD-7 — comorbid depression and anxiety common and must be addressed concurrently
- Substance use assessment: AUDIT, DAST — active substance use complicates stimulant prescribing

**Differential diagnosis consideration:** Requires careful exclusion of primary anxiety, depression, bipolar disorder, autism spectrum disorder, and sleep disorders before ADHD diagnosis — these can all mimic ADHD and frequently coexist.

**Collateral history:** Obtain where possible (partner, family member). Particularly useful to confirm childhood-onset symptoms.

## Key Differentials

- Anxiety disorder — attentional difficulties from rumination and worry; anxiety can co-occur with ADHD
- Major depressive disorder — poor concentration, fatigue, psychomotor slowing; PHQ-9
- Bipolar disorder — periods of increased activity and distractibility during hypomania; mood history essential
- Autism spectrum disorder — organisational difficulties, sensory issues; consider ASD assessment if clinical features present
- Sleep disorders — obstructive sleep apnoea, insomnia; significant attentional impairment from poor sleep
- Substance use disorders — stimulant or alcohol misuse causes attentional symptoms; assess AUDIT/DAST
- Thyroid disease — hyperthyroidism causes irritability, concentration difficulties; check TSH
- Iron deficiency — inattention worsened by iron deficiency even without anaemia; check ferritin

## Management Principles

**Non-pharmacological (first-line, adjunct, or where stimulants contraindicated):**
- Psychoeducation: explaining ADHD neurobiology, validating patient experience; core component of management
- ADHD coaching and CBT: targeted to executive function skills — time management, planning, prioritisation, working memory strategies
- Organisational tools: calendars, reminders, task management apps
- Exercise: regular aerobic exercise has evidence for improving attention and executive function
- Sleep hygiene: sleep disorders bidirectional with ADHD; address concurrently

**Pharmacotherapy — stimulant medications (first-line for moderate-severe ADHD):**

Methylphenidate (Ritalin, Concerta, Ritalin LA):
- Short-acting methylphenidate (Ritalin): 5–20 mg 2–3 times daily; rapid onset; useful for targeted use
- Long-acting methylphenidate (Concerta XL, Ritalin LA): 18–72 mg once daily; preferred for consistent coverage
- Monitor: appetite suppression, sleep disturbance, increased BP/HR, mood changes

Dexamfetamine (Dexedrine):
- 5–20 mg 2–3 times daily; more potent per mg than methylphenidate; used when methylphenidate insufficient
- PBS-restricted: Authorised prescriber or shared care arrangement required in most states

Lisdexamfetamine (Vyvanse):
- 20–70 mg once daily; prodrug — reduced misuse potential; smooth onset and offset
- TGA-approved for adults in Australia; PBS authority required; preferred in adults with substance misuse history

**Non-stimulant medications (second-line or adjunct):**
- Atomoxetine (Strattera): 40–100 mg daily; SNRI mechanism; slower onset (4–6 weeks); suitable for comorbid anxiety, substance use, or tic disorder; PBS-restricted for adults
- Bupropion: off-label; evidence modest; useful if comorbid depression
- Guanfacine XR: TGA-approved for paediatric ADHD; off-label in adults; sedating

**Prescribing in adults — state regulatory requirements:**
- Schedule 8 stimulants require authority prescribing under state health regulations — requirements vary (SA, WA have GP prescriber registration schemes; NSW, VIC require specialist initiation with GP shared care or GP authority under specific circumstances)
- GPs should confirm current state requirements before initiating stimulant pharmacotherapy
- AADPA 2022 guidelines support expanded GP prescribing rights; regulatory landscape continues to evolve

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Methylphenidate** (Ritalin, Concerta, Ritalin LA — PBS restricted: ADHD; authority required; Schedule 8 controlled drug; state regulations apply)
- **Dexamfetamine** (Dexedrine — PBS restricted: ADHD; authority required; Schedule 8; state prescriber registration required in some states)
- **Lisdexamfetamine** (Vyvanse — PBS restricted: ADHD adults; authority required; Schedule 8; preferred in adults with misuse risk due to prodrug design)
- **Atomoxetine** (Strattera — PBS restricted: ADHD in adults and children; authority required; non-stimulant; non-controlled drug)
- **Note:** Stimulant medications are Schedule 8 controlled substances — adhere to state-specific permit and authority requirements; do not prescribe to patients with active stimulant misuse disorder; monitor for cardiovascular effects (BP, HR) and psychiatric side effects (agitation, psychosis)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Stimulant-induced psychosis or severe agitation — cease stimulant; emergency psychiatric assessment
- Cardiovascular emergency (hypertensive crisis, arrhythmia) in patient on stimulants — cease stimulant; emergency review

**Urgent psychiatric review:**
- Active suicidal ideation — ADHD and comorbid depression carry significant suicide risk
- Emergence of psychotic symptoms or severe mood instability on stimulants
- Suspected bipolar disorder — stimulants can trigger manic episodes; psychiatric assessment essential before continuing

**Non-urgent specialist referral:**
- Psychiatrist or paediatrician (adult transition): complex diagnostic uncertainty, significant comorbidities (bipolar, ASD, active substance use disorder), failure of multiple medication trials
- Psychologist: CBT for ADHD, treatment of comorbid anxiety/depression
- Occupational therapist: workplace accommodation assessment, executive function rehabilitation

## Telehealth Suitability

Adult ADHD management is highly suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- ADHD symptom assessment, rating scale administration (ASRS, CAARS via shared screen or patient-completed)
- Collateral history gathering
- Medication initiation and titration review (where GP is authorised prescriber)
- Monitoring for side effects (appetite, sleep, mood, BP/HR — BP can be patient-reported or pharmacy-measured)
- Repeat prescriptions for stable patients (Schedule 8 prescription handling varies by state)
- Psychoeducation, coaching referral, and CBT referral

**Requires in-person assessment or arrangement:**
- Physical examination for cardiovascular assessment before stimulant initiation (BP, HR, cardiac history)
- Schedule 8 prescription regulatory requirements — some states require in-person consultation for initial prescription
- Patients with suspected bipolar disorder or psychosis — in-person psychiatric assessment preferred
