## Psoriasis Assessment (RACGP)

Psoriasis is a common chronic immune-mediated inflammatory skin condition affecting approximately 2–3% of Australians. It is characterised by accelerated keratinocyte turnover resulting in well-demarcated erythematous plaques with silvery-white scale. Psoriasis has significant systemic associations — particularly psoriatic arthritis (affecting 10–30% of patients with psoriasis), cardiovascular disease, metabolic syndrome, and depression. Mild-to-moderate psoriasis is managed in general practice with topical therapy; moderate-to-severe disease requires dermatology referral for phototherapy or systemic therapy. Telehealth with photo assessment is suitable for psoriasis review.

## Clinical Presentation

**Morphological variants:**
- Plaque psoriasis (psoriasis vulgaris — >80%): sharply demarcated erythematous plaques with silvery-white adherent scale; Auspitz sign (pinpoint bleeding on scale removal); Koebner phenomenon (psoriasis in areas of skin trauma); common sites: elbows, knees, scalp, sacral area
- Guttate psoriasis: small (<1 cm) drop-shaped lesions; trunk and limbs; often triggered by streptococcal URTI; common in young adults; may resolve spontaneously
- Inverse (flexural) psoriasis: affects skin folds (groin, axillae, submammary, perineum); minimal scale; can be difficult to distinguish from candidal intertrigo or eczema
- Scalp psoriasis: very common; erythema and thick scale; extends to hairline; can cause hair loss in severe cases
- Nail psoriasis: pitting, oil spots, onycholysis (nail separation), subungual hyperkeratosis; associated with psoriatic arthritis
- Palmoplantar psoriasis: affects palms and soles; can be hyperkeratotic or pustular; significantly impacts function
- Erythrodermic psoriasis: rare; >90% body surface area involvement; erythroderma + systemic toxicity; medical emergency
- Generalised pustular psoriasis: rare; widespread sterile pustules; fever; medical emergency

**Psoriasis severity assessment:**
- Psoriasis Area and Severity Index (PASI): research/dermatology tool; rates erythema, induration, scale, and area involvement; PASI ≥10 = moderate; PASI ≥20 = severe
- Body surface area (BSA): ≤10% = mild; 10–30% = moderate; >30% = severe
- Dermatology Life Quality Index (DLQI): functional impact; ≥10 = large effect on quality of life
- Simplified assessment: face, hands, feet, or genital involvement with significant functional impact = likely warrants referral regardless of BSA

## Assessment Approach

**History:**
- Age of onset, trigger factors (streptococcal infection, medications — beta-blockers, lithium, ACE inhibitors, NSAIDs can worsen psoriasis; antimalarials)
- Previous treatments and responses
- Joint symptoms: psoriatic arthritis assessment (morning stiffness, joint pain and swelling, dactylitis — "sausage digits", enthesitis — heel pain, back pain)
- Family history of psoriasis
- Mental health: depression and anxiety comorbidity; impact on relationships, work, social life
- Cardiovascular risk factors (psoriasis is an independent CVD risk factor)

**Assessment of severity and impact:**
- Calculate BSA or DLQI
- Assess location: face, hands, genitals, feet — "high-impact" sites regardless of BSA

**Systemic screening:**
- Blood pressure, BMI, waist circumference: metabolic syndrome
- Fasting glucose, lipids: diabetes and CVD risk
- PHQ-9, GAD-7: mental health comorbidity

## Key Differentials

- Eczema (atopic dermatitis): less well-demarcated; more excoriated; flexural distribution; atopy history; no silver scale; see `eczema-atopic` entry
- Seborrhoeic dermatitis: scalp and face; less thick scale; involves nasolabial folds, eyebrows; overlaps with scalp psoriasis
- Tinea (fungal infection): tinea corporis can mimic guttate psoriasis; examine edge; scrapings for fungal microscopy; KOH prep
- Pityriasis rosea: herald patch then secondary rash along skin lines; self-limiting weeks; young adults; post-viral
- Lichen planus: violaceous flat-topped papules; Wickham's striae; oral involvement common; different distribution
- Secondary syphilis: "money spots" on palms and soles; sexual history; RPR/VDRL testing

## Management Principles

**Mild psoriasis (BSA <10%, not high-impact sites, DLQI <10):**

Emollients and moisturisers: reduce scaling and itch; enhance efficacy of topical treatments; use liberally and frequently.

Topical corticosteroids: mainstay of treatment; apply to plaques; use potency appropriate to body site:
- Mild steroids (hydrocortisone 1%): face, groin, axillae, flexures — thinner skin; avoid potent steroids
- Moderate steroids (betamethasone valerate 0.02–0.05%): body plaques; short courses
- Potent steroids (betamethasone dipropionate 0.05%, methylprednisolone aceponate): thick plaques on trunk/limbs; 4-week courses then rest; risk of thinning with prolonged use
- Limit use on face, flexures, and in children — risk of skin atrophy

Vitamin D analogues:
- Calcipotriol (Daivonex): applied once or twice daily; reduces scaling and erythema; no tachyphylaxis (unlike steroids); may be irritating on face and flexures
- Calcipotriol + betamethasone dipropionate (Daivobet, Enstilar foam): highly effective combination; PBS-listed; convenient once-daily application; first-line for plaque psoriasis

Topical calcineurin inhibitors:
- Tacrolimus ointment (Protopic), pimecrolimus cream (Elidel): face and flexures only; effective; do not cause skin atrophy; off-label for psoriasis; PBS-listed for eczema (see label restrictions)

Tar preparations: coal tar shampoo for scalp psoriasis; older, less convenient; effective; used as adjunct.

Scalp psoriasis: combination scalp treatment (calcipotriol + betamethasone scalp solution — Xamiol); coal tar shampoo; salicylic acid shampoo to remove scale.

**Moderate-to-severe psoriasis (BSA ≥10%, or high-impact sites, or DLQI ≥10):**
- Dermatology referral for phototherapy (narrowband UVB — effective, safe, no systemic toxicity) or systemic therapy

Systemic agents (initiated by dermatologist — GP may monitor):
- Methotrexate (MTX): oral or SC; effective; monitor FBC, LFTs; folic acid supplementation mandatory; contraindicated in pregnancy and significant liver disease
- Acitretin: oral retinoid; effective for pustular and severe plaque; teratogenic — strict pregnancy prevention; blood lipid monitoring
- Cyclosporine: rapid onset; renal and BP toxicity — BP and creatinine monitoring; 1–2 year maximum courses; used for flares
- Biologics (TNF inhibitors, IL-12/23, IL-17, IL-23 inhibitors): highly effective; PBS-restricted; hospital initiation; examples: adalimumab, secukinumab, risankizumab; GP monitors for infections, tuberculosis reactivation (pre-treatment IGRA), cardiovascular events

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Calcipotriol + betamethasone dipropionate** (Daivobet ointment, Enstilar foam — PBS restricted: plaque psoriasis; once daily; first-line combination therapy)
- **Calcipotriol** (Daivonex — PBS restricted: psoriasis; monotherapy where calcipotriol alone appropriate)
- **Betamethasone dipropionate** (PBS restricted: psoriasis and other inflammatory skin conditions; various formulations)
- **Coal tar shampoo** (OTC; not PBS-listed; adjunct for scalp psoriasis)
- **Xamiol** (calcipotriol + betamethasone gel for scalp — PBS restricted: scalp psoriasis)
- **Methotrexate** (PBS restricted: severe psoriasis — dermatologist or GP with experience; authority required; folic acid 5 mg 5 days/week or 1 mg daily concurrently)
- **Note:** Biologics for psoriasis require PBS authority from dermatologist; GP role is monitoring for infection (including TB), blood pressure, and routine health maintenance in patients on biologic therapy

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Erythrodermic psoriasis: widespread erythroderma + systemic toxicity (fever, haemodynamic instability) — thermoregulatory failure risk
- Generalised pustular psoriasis (GPP): widespread pustules + fever + systemic distress

**Urgent dermatology referral:**
- Moderate-to-severe psoriasis (BSA ≥10% or high-impact) — systemic therapy or phototherapy required
- High-impact site psoriasis (face, hands, genitals) affecting quality of life significantly
- Guttate psoriasis — throat swab; if streptococcal, treat; dermatology if extensive

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Dermatologist: diagnosis uncertain, treatment-resistant psoriasis, biologic therapy consideration
- Rheumatologist: psoriatic arthritis assessment and management (DMARDs, biologics)
- Psychologist/psychiatrist: significant depression or anxiety from psoriasis impact

## Telehealth Suitability

Psoriasis management is suitable for telehealth with photo assessment.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Photo-based assessment of plaque morphology, extent, and severity (patient uploads photos)
- Topical treatment prescribing and titration for mild psoriasis
- Trigger identification and lifestyle advice
- Mental health screening (PHQ-9) and referral
- Dermatology referral coordination for moderate-severe psoriasis
- Monitoring for patients on systemic therapy (results review, medication refill)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial clinical diagnosis (if uncertain — skin examination, dermatoscopy)
- Biopsy if diagnosis uncertain
- Psoriatic arthritis examination (joint swelling, dactylitis, enthesitis)
- Intralesional corticosteroid injection for resistant plaques
