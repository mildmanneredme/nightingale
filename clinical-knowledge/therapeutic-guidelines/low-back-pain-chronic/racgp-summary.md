## Chronic Low Back Pain Assessment (RACGP)

Chronic low back pain (CLBP) — defined as low back pain persisting ≥3 months — is the leading cause of years lived with disability in Australia and worldwide. It affects approximately 16% of Australians and costs the healthcare system over $4.8 billion annually. The vast majority of CLBP has no identifiable structural cause and is best understood through the biopsychosocial model — involving central sensitisation, psychological factors, and social context in addition to physical pathology. The ACSQHC Low Back Pain Clinical Care Standard 2022 emphasises: a positive diagnosis, early return to activity, avoiding harmful imaging and treatments, psychosocial risk assessment (STarT Back tool), and prioritising non-pharmacological and psychological management. Opioids are NOT first-line and provide minimal benefit with significant harm in CLBP. Telehealth is suitable for chronic low back pain management.

## Clinical Presentation

**Non-specific CLBP (95% of presentations):**
- Low back pain ± radiation into buttock or thigh; radiation below knee suggests disc herniation with radiculopathy
- No consistent relationship to imaging findings; MRI commonly shows age-related disc changes, disc bulges — not pathology in most cases
- Pain disproportionate to structural findings; flares with physical and psychological stress
- Deconditioning cycle: pain → avoidance → deconditioning → more pain

**Specific low back pain causes (5% — require targeted management):**

Red flags (serious pathology — must exclude):
- Cancer (spinal metastases): age >50, history of cancer, unexplained weight loss, pain worse at rest/night, failure to improve after 6 weeks; MRI spine urgently
- Infection (discitis, epidural abscess): fever, IV drug use, immunosuppression, recent spinal procedure; ESR/CRP, MRI
- Cauda equina syndrome (surgical emergency): bilateral leg weakness, urinary retention or incontinence, saddle anaesthesia — 000 immediately
- Vertebral fracture: osteoporosis + minor trauma; age >70, corticosteroid use; X-ray or MRI

Radiculopathy (nerve root compression):
- Shooting pain to below the knee in dermatomal distribution; L4, L5, S1 most common
- Positive straight leg raise test (SLR) if L4-S1 involvement; reverse SLR if L2-L4
- Most radiculopathy resolves within 6–12 weeks with conservative management
- MRI if progressive neurological deficit, failure of conservative management, or surgical consideration

Inflammatory back pain (axial spondyloarthropathy):
- Age <45; morning stiffness ≥30 minutes; improves with exercise (unlike mechanical back pain); nocturnal pain; may respond to NSAIDs; HLA-B27, MRI sacroiliac joints — rheumatology referral

**Psychosocial risk factors for chronicity ("yellow flags" — STarT Back Screen):**
- Fear-avoidance beliefs (catastrophising, belief that pain = damage)
- Low mood, distress, anxiety, depression
- Poor workplace relationship or job dissatisfaction
- Passive coping strategies
- High-risk STarT Back score: intensified psychological intervention needed

## Assessment Approach

**History and examination:**
- Characterise pain: onset, duration, distribution, radiation, aggravating/relieving factors, night pain, rest pain
- Screen for red flags (see above)
- Neurological examination: sensation, power, reflexes in lower limbs; straight leg raise test
- Functional assessment: what activities affected; disability level; fear-avoidance
- Psychosocial screening: STarT Back Screening Tool (9-item; validated; identifies low, medium, high psychosocial risk); PHQ-9 for depression

**Imaging — do not image by default:**
- X-ray/MRI low back pain without red flags: NOT recommended in first 6–8 weeks (ACSQHC Clinical Care Standard 2022)
- Imaging findings often incidental; disc degeneration, bulging discs on MRI are near-universal over age 40 and correlate poorly with pain
- Routine imaging does NOT improve outcomes and increases unnecessary procedures
- Image if: red flags present, failure of conservative management at 6 weeks, neurological deficit, consideration of surgery or intervention

## Key Differentials

- Sacroiliac joint dysfunction: pain over SI joint; provocation tests (FABER, FADIR); physiotherapy
- Piriformis syndrome: sciatic-like pain; piriformis stretch; physiotherapy
- Hip pathology: groin pain, limited hip internal rotation; X-ray hip; refer orthopaedics if OA
- Aortic aneurysm: pulsatile abdominal mass; vascular emergency if rupture — 000; ultrasound screening in men >65 who have ever smoked
- Endometriosis: cyclical back pain, dysmenorrhoea — gynaecology referral (see `endometriosis` entry)
- Renal colic (nephrolithiasis): flank pain, colicky, haematuria; CT KUB — see `nephrolithiasis` entry

## Management Principles

**ACSQHC Low Back Pain Clinical Care Standard 2022 — key messages:**

Non-pharmacological (first-line — most effective):

Exercise:
- Any form of exercise is effective: walking, swimming, Pilates, yoga, physiotherapy exercises
- Exercise is the single most effective intervention for CLBP — prescribe specifically
- Exercise physiologist: MBS-funded under Chronic Disease Management plan
- Graded return to activity: avoid bed rest (harmful); reassure that activity is not damaging

Physiotherapy:
- Manual therapy (manipulation, mobilisation): short-term pain relief; evidence for acute > chronic
- McKenzie method: directional preference exercises; evidence-based for specific presentations
- Referral to physiotherapist: under Chronic Disease Management plan

Psychological therapies:
- CBT: strongest evidence for psychosocial management of CLBP; pain catastrophising, fear-avoidance
- ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy): highly effective for CLBP disability reduction
- GP Mental Health Care Plan (MBS 2713) for psychology referral
- Multidisciplinary pain program: for high-disability, high-psychosocial-risk CLBP — most effective; long waitlists

Education and self-management:
- Explain that CLBP is rarely dangerous; spine is robust; movement is medicine
- Avoid reinforcing the spine-as-fragile narrative
- Pain Toolkit (www.paintoolkit.org); Painaustralia resources

**Pharmacotherapy (adjunct — not first-line):**
- NSAIDs: first-line pharmacotherapy if no contraindications; paracetamol has minimal evidence in CLBP; NSAIDs superior; use for shortest duration; PPI co-prescribe if GI risk
- Topical diclofenac gel: safer for elderly; reasonable evidence
- Duloxetine (SNRI): emerging evidence for CLBP; also addresses comorbid depression; 30–60 mg daily; PBS-listed for depression/GAD — off-label for pain but widely used
- Low-dose tricyclics (amitriptyline 10–25 mg nocte): sleep improvement and modest pain reduction; commonly used
- Muscle relaxants (diazepam, cyclobenzaprine): very short-term only for acute-on-chronic spasm; dependency risk; not long-term
- Pregabalin: evidence limited in non-neuropathic CLBP; not recommended as standard treatment; may consider if neuropathic component
- Opioids: NOT recommended for CLBP (ACSQHC, RACGP): minimal efficacy; significant harms (dependency, opioid-induced hyperalgesia, cognitive impairment, falls, constipation); if patient already on opioids — develop tapering plan

Procedural/interventional:
- Epidural corticosteroid injection: evidence for short-term relief in radiculopathy; not for non-specific CLBP; pain clinic or specialist
- Facet joint injection / radiofrequency ablation: for confirmed facetogenic pain; pain clinic
- Spinal cord stimulation: refractory neuropathic pain; specialist pain medicine
- Surgery: reserved for structural pathology (disc herniation with persistent radiculopathy, significant neurological deficit, spinal stenosis causing claudication) — specialist neurosurgery/orthopaedics

**Opioid de-prescribing (if currently prescribed):**
- Review all patients on long-term opioids for CLBP (Opioid Analgesic Risk Tool, PDMS compliance)
- Develop collaborative reduction plan; educate patient on opioid-induced hyperalgesia
- Refer to pain medicine specialist for complex cases; see `chronic-pain` entry

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Ibuprofen / Naproxen** (PBS unrestricted: musculoskeletal pain; short course; GI protection if risk factors; avoid in renal impairment, heart failure, elderly)
- **Diclofenac topical gel (Voltaren)** (PBS unrestricted: OTC; local anti-inflammatory; preferred in elderly or GI risk)
- **Duloxetine (Cymbalta)** (PBS restricted: depression/GAD — concurrent diagnosis supports prescribing for CLBP; 30–60 mg daily; may require specialist initiation)
- **Amitriptyline** (PBS unrestricted: depression; 10–25 mg nocte in CLBP — off-label; assists sleep and pain; dry mouth, morning sedation)
- **Note:** Opioids are NOT recommended for CLBP; if unavoidable — PDMS registration required; risk assessment with opioid risk tools; regular review with functional goals not pain as endpoint
- Pregabalin: limited evidence in non-neuropathic CLBP; use cautiously if neuropathic component confirmed

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Cauda equina syndrome: saddle anaesthesia, bilateral leg weakness, urinary retention/incontinence — neurosurgical emergency; 000 immediately
- Suspected spinal infection with sepsis: high fever, rigors, haemodynamic instability

**Urgent specialist:**
- Red flags suggesting malignancy, fracture, or infection — urgent imaging (MRI) and specialist referral
- Progressive neurological deficit (footdrop, worsening radiculopathy) — neurosurgery/orthopaedics urgently
- Cauda equina symptoms (even incomplete) — urgent ED

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Physiotherapist: exercise prescription, manual therapy
- Exercise physiologist: structured exercise program (Chronic Disease Management plan)
- Psychologist: CBT/ACT for CLBP (GP Mental Health Care Plan MBS 2713)
- Pain medicine specialist: opioid tapering; interventional consideration; multidisciplinary pain program
- Neurosurgery/orthopaedics: structural cause with persistent deficit after 6 weeks conservative

## Telehealth Suitability

CLBP management is highly suitable for telehealth for established patients.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Psychosocial assessment (STarT Back, PHQ-9)
- Education, reassurance and self-management counselling
- Exercise prescription review
- Medication review (including opioid review and tapering plans)
- Referral coordination (physiotherapy, exercise physiologist, psychology)
- GP Mental Health Care Plan (MBS 2713) for psychology referral
- Chronic Disease Management plan review

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial neurological examination (new or worsening leg symptoms)
- Red flag exclusion — physical examination
- Imaging referral (X-ray, MRI) — radiology
