## Hypertension Assessment (RACGP)

Hypertension is one of the most prevalent chronic conditions in Australian general practice, affecting approximately 1 in 3 adults. It is the leading modifiable risk factor for cardiovascular disease, stroke, and chronic kidney disease. Most hypertension is primary (essential) with no identifiable cause. Effective BP management substantially reduces cardiovascular mortality. Telehealth is well-suited to medication review, lifestyle counselling, and results discussion; diagnosis should be confirmed by multiple readings over time, ideally including home BP monitoring.

## Clinical Presentation

Hypertension is typically asymptomatic and detected on routine screening. Symptomatic presentations are uncommon and suggest end-organ involvement: severe headache, visual disturbance, chest pain, dyspnoea, or focal neurological symptoms may indicate hypertensive urgency or emergency (requires immediate escalation — see below).

Incidental elevated readings are common. A single elevated reading does not diagnose hypertension. Diagnosis requires elevated readings on at least 2 separate occasions, or confirmation with home BP monitoring (HBPM) or ambulatory BP monitoring (ABPM).

Risk factors: older age, obesity, physical inactivity, high sodium intake, excess alcohol, family history, ethnicity (higher risk in Pacific Islander, South Asian, and African populations in Australia), OSA, CKD, and diabetes.

## Assessment Approach

Confirm diagnosis: 2+ clinic readings ≥140/90 mmHg on 2+ occasions, or HBPM average ≥135/85 mmHg, or ABPM daytime average ≥135/85 mmHg.

Baseline investigations at diagnosis or review:
- Fasting lipids, fasting glucose / HbA1c
- eGFR and urine ACR (assess for CKD and target organ damage)
- ECG (assess for LVH, arrhythmia)
- Electrolytes (prior to commencing diuretics or RAAS agents)

Cardiovascular risk assessment: calculate absolute 5-year CVD risk using the Australian CVD risk calculator. Treatment targets and intensity are guided by total risk, not BP level alone.

Assess for secondary causes if: onset <40 years, resistant hypertension (≥3 agents at optimal doses), BP difficult to control, or clinical features suggesting renovascular disease, hyperaldosteronism, phaeochromocytoma, or OSA.

Lifestyle history: sodium intake, physical activity, alcohol, smoking, diet quality, weight.

## Key Differentials

- White coat hypertension — elevated clinic BP, normal HBPM/ABPM; confirm with home monitoring before starting treatment
- Secondary hypertension — renal artery stenosis, primary hyperaldosteronism, phaeochromocytoma, OSA, thyroid disease; consider if young, resistant, or atypical features
- Hypertensive urgency — markedly elevated BP (typically >180/120 mmHg) without end-organ damage
- Hypertensive emergency — elevated BP with acute target organ damage (AKI, hypertensive encephalopathy, aortic dissection, pulmonary oedema, STEMI)

## Management Principles

**Lifestyle modification is first-line for all patients:**
- Dietary sodium reduction to <2 g/day (5 g salt)
- DASH-style diet (high in fruit, vegetables, low-fat dairy, reduced saturated fat)
- Weight loss if overweight (each 1 kg reduction in weight lowers BP by ~1 mmHg)
- Physical activity — 150 min/week moderate intensity aerobic exercise
- Alcohol ≤2 standard drinks/day, no more than 10/week
- Smoking cessation

**BP targets:**
- General population: <140/90 mmHg; ideally <130/80 mmHg if well tolerated
- High CVD risk, DM, CKD: <130/80 mmHg
- Frail elderly: individualise; avoid symptomatic hypotension

**First-line antihypertensive agents (PBS-listed):**
- ACE inhibitors (perindopril, ramipril) or ARBs (candesartan, irbesartan, telmisartan) — preferred if diabetes, CKD, or high CVD risk
- Calcium channel blockers (amlodipine, felodipine) — well tolerated, effective across age groups
- Thiazide / thiazide-like diuretics (hydrochlorothiazide, indapamide) — effective, low cost

Combination therapy is frequently required. Single-pill combinations improve adherence.

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Perindopril** (PBS unrestricted) — 5–10 mg daily; monitor renal function and potassium
- **Ramipril** (PBS unrestricted) — 2.5–10 mg daily
- **Candesartan** (PBS — unrestricted for hypertension) — 8–32 mg daily
- **Amlodipine** (PBS unrestricted) — 5–10 mg daily; avoid in decompensated heart failure
- **Indapamide** (PBS unrestricted) — 1.25–2.5 mg daily; preferred thiazide-like diuretic in AU
- **Irbesartan, telmisartan, valsartan** (PBS — unrestricted for hypertension)
- **Metoprolol, atenolol** (beta-blockers — not first-line for uncomplicated hypertension; useful if comorbid angina, AF, or heart failure)
- **Spironolactone** (PBS — restricted for resistant hypertension; monitor potassium and renal function)
- Avoid ACE inhibitor + ARB combination (increases adverse effects without additional benefit)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / emergency department:**
- BP ≥180/120 mmHg with any of: severe headache, visual disturbance, chest pain, dyspnoea, confusion, focal neurology (hypertensive emergency)
- Symptoms of acute stroke (FAST: Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time to call 000)
- Symptoms of acute MI (chest pain, radiation to arm/jaw, diaphoresis)
- Acute aortic dissection (sudden severe tearing chest or back pain)

**Urgent in-person GP review within 24–48 hours:**
- BP ≥180/120 mmHg without symptoms (hypertensive urgency) — does not require emergency referral but requires prompt review and management
- Symptomatic hypotension after medication initiation or dose increase
- New-onset significant oedema, creatinine rise >30% above baseline, or hyperkalaemia after starting RAAS agent

**Non-urgent specialist referral:**
- Resistant hypertension (BP uncontrolled on ≥3 antihypertensives including a diuretic) — refer for secondary cause workup
- Suspected secondary hypertension
- Hypertension in pregnancy — obstetric review required

## Telehealth Suitability

Ongoing hypertension management is highly suitable for telehealth, provided the patient has a home BP monitor.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Medication review and dose adjustment based on home BP readings and recent pathology
- Lifestyle counselling and motivational support
- Results interpretation (lipids, eGFR, electrolytes)
- CVD risk review and shared decision-making on treatment targets

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial diagnosis confirmation (clinic BP measurement)
- Annual physical examination (weight, fundoscopy if indicated, auscultation)
- New symptoms suggesting secondary cause or end-organ damage
- Suspected hypertensive emergency (acute presentation — 000)
