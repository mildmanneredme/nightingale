## Plantar Fasciitis Assessment (RACGP)

Plantar fasciitis (plantar heel pain, plantar fasciopathy) is the most common cause of heel pain in adults, accounting for approximately 80% of heel pain presentations. It involves degenerative change and inflammation of the plantar fascia at its insertion on the medial calcaneal tuberosity. It affects approximately 10% of the population and is the most common foot complaint seen in Australian general practice and podiatry. Despite the common name "plantar fasciitis," histology shows degeneration (fasciopathy) rather than pure inflammation in chronic cases. Most cases (90%) resolve within 12 months with conservative management, though this can be frustrating for patients. GPs play a central role in: diagnosing plantar fasciitis, excluding other causes of heel pain, initiating conservative management, and referring to podiatry and orthopaedics for refractory cases. Telehealth is suitable for initial history and management advice; physical examination and imaging are in-person.

## Clinical Presentation

**Characteristic symptoms:**
- Plantar heel pain: worse with first steps in the morning ("post-static dyskinesia") — most characteristic symptom; stiffness and severe pain with first steps after prolonged rest
- Gradual improvement after walking a few minutes: as fascia warms up and stretches
- Returns after prolonged standing or walking: painful with prolonged activity
- Location: medial calcaneal tuberosity (medial aspect of heel pad); pain is point-tender; may radiate along plantar fascia toward toes
- No pain at rest: in early-moderate disease; pain mainly with weight-bearing
- Aggravating factors: walking barefoot on hard surfaces, first steps in morning, prolonged standing, stairs, hills

**Risk factors:**
- Biomechanical: excessive pronation (flat feet), pes cavus (high arch), tight Achilles tendon/calf, leg length discrepancy
- Overuse: sudden increase in running distance or intensity; runners, athletes
- Obesity and overweight: increased plantar load
- Occupational standing: healthcare, retail, hospitality
- Poor footwear: inadequate cushioning or arch support; high heels (shortens Achilles); thongs (no support)
- Age: peak incidence 40–60 years

## Assessment Approach

**Clinical diagnosis — physical examination:**
- Point tenderness: maximum tenderness at medial calcaneal tuberosity (medial aspect of heel, just distal to the junction of plantar and medial heel surfaces); this is pathognomonic
- Dorsiflexion of toes: increases plantar fascia tension; may reproduce pain (Windlass test)
- Ankle dorsiflexion range: limited dorsiflexion (tight Achilles/calf) — assess gastrocnemius and soleus flexibility
- Assess foot posture: pes planus (flat feet), pes cavus (high arch), excessive pronation
- Gait assessment: antalgic gait, pronation pattern

**Investigations (for typical presentation — usually not required):**
- X-ray heel (lateral view): heel spur (calcaneal spur) common finding on X-ray but NOT the cause of pain; present in 50% of asymptomatic individuals; not predictive of severity or outcome
- Ultrasound: confirms plantar fascia thickening (>4 mm) and intratendinous changes; useful if diagnosis uncertain or before injection; assesses vascularity with Doppler
- MRI heel: reserved for diagnostic uncertainty or to exclude stress fracture, nerve entrapment, tumour

## Key Differentials

- Tarsal tunnel syndrome: tibial nerve compression medial to ankle; burning/tingling along sole + heel; worse with standing; Tinel's over tarsal tunnel; NCS confirms
- Fat pad atrophy: central heel pain; older patients, thin heel pad; diffuse rather than point tenderness; cushioned heel insoles
- Stress fracture of calcaneus: acute onset; military recruits or sudden increased activity; diffuse bone tenderness; MRI or bone scan
- Heel bursitis (calcaneal bursitis): posterior heel (not plantar); pain at Achilles insertion; soft fluctuant swelling
- Spondyloarthropathy (reactive arthritis, ankylosing spondylitis, psoriatic arthritis): enthesitis at plantar fascia and Achilles; bilateral or migratory; morning stiffness >1 hour; young males; HLA-B27; refer rheumatology
- Nerve entrapment (Baxter's nerve — first branch of lateral plantar nerve): medial heel pain; specific motor findings on EMG
- Peripheral arterial disease: claudication-type heel pain; absent pulses; ABI assessment

## Management Principles

**Conservative management — most patients (90% recover in 12 months):**

Stretching (most evidence-based intervention):
- Plantar fascia stretch: seated; pull toes back toward shin to tension plantar fascia; hold 20–30 seconds; 10 repetitions × 3 times daily; especially first thing in the morning before weight-bearing
- Calf (Achilles) stretching: gastrocnemius stretch (knee straight) and soleus stretch (knee bent) against wall; both components important
- Night splint: keeps ankle in neutral dorsiflexion while sleeping; prevents overnight shortening; significantly reduces first-step pain; OT or podiatry; most effective for severe nocturnal and first-step pain

Footwear modification:
- Supportive footwear with cushioned heel and arch support; avoid hard flat shoes and thongs
- Heel cups or cushions (silicone heel pad): OTC; cushion heel; inexpensive; significant comfort improvement
- Custom orthoses (podiatrist-prescribed): for biomechanical abnormalities (excessive pronation, pes cavus); more effective than prefabricated for structural causes

Activity modification:
- Reduce high-impact activities temporarily; avoid walking barefoot on hard floors; low-impact alternatives (swimming, cycling) to maintain fitness

Ice and analgesia:
- Ice to heel 15–20 minutes after activity: anti-inflammatory; vasoconstriction
- NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen): short course for acute pain relief; limited evidence for long-term outcome; 2–4 weeks

Physiotherapy:
- Calf and plantar fascia stretching programme; manual therapy; dry needling; taping; gait retraining
- Exercise loading programme: isometric and eccentric heel raise exercises — growing evidence for fasciopathy rehabilitation

**Corticosteroid injection (second-line):**
- Ultrasound-guided methylprednisolone 20–40 mg injection at medial calcaneal tuberosity
- Rapid short-term pain relief (4–6 weeks); less effective long-term than stretching
- Maximum 2–3 injections (risk of plantar fascia rupture and fat pad atrophy with multiple injections); space 6–12 weeks
- More effective than placebo in short term; evidence supports use when conservative measures insufficient

**Other interventions:**
- Extracorporeal shockwave therapy (ESWT): ultrasound-delivered shockwave; good evidence for chronic refractory plantar fasciitis (>6 months); physiotherapy or sports medicine clinic; not PBS-funded (private ~$400–600 per course); 3 sessions weekly; alternative to surgery
- PRP (platelet-rich plasma) injection: emerging evidence; specialist sports physician or orthopaedic surgeon; not routinely recommended; no PBS funding
- Weight loss: if obesity a contributing factor — reduces plantar load significantly

**Surgical (rare — for refractory cases >12 months):**
- Plantar fasciotomy (partial release): relieves fascial tension; generally effective; risk of arch collapse; rare complication of plantar fascia rupture
- Referred to orthopaedic or foot surgeon after exhausting all conservative options including ESWT

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **NSAIDs (ibuprofen 400–600 mg TDS, naproxen 500 mg BD)** (PBS unrestricted: short course for pain relief; GI protection if prolonged course; limited evidence for modifying disease course)
- **Methylprednisolone 40 mg/mL (Depo-Medrol)** or **triamcinolone**: local injection at calcaneal insertion; not PBS-listed as injection supply; minimal direct cost; ultrasound guidance preferred for accuracy
- **Night splints**: not PBS-funded; significant evidence for first-step morning pain; OTC ~$40–80; OT or podiatry prescription
- **Custom foot orthoses**: MBS-funded under Enhanced Primary Care when podiatrist involved in CDM team care plan (up to 5 allied health visits); significant benefit for biomechanical contributors
- **Note:** Corticosteroid injections for plantar fasciitis carry a risk of plantar fascia rupture and fat pad atrophy; maximum 2–3 injections; spacing ≥6 weeks; do not use as primary treatment; preferred after initial conservative measures

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent review:**
- Acute plantar fascia rupture: sudden severe pain during activity; may be palpable defect; sudden worsening; rest, ice, elevation; boot immobilisation; orthopaedic review

**Non-urgent orthopaedics or sports medicine:**
- Refractory plantar fasciitis >12 months despite adequate conservative treatment + 2 corticosteroid injections + ESWT: surgical fasciotomy consideration
- Suspected stress fracture: MRI if localised bone tenderness and risk factors (female athlete triad, osteoporosis)

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Podiatrist: all moderate-severe presentations; biomechanical assessment, custom orthoses, taping, patient education; MBS-funded
- Physiotherapist: exercise rehabilitation programme, manual therapy, dry needling
- Rheumatologist: if bilateral enthesitis, inflammatory features, suspected spondyloarthropathy

## Telehealth Suitability

Plantar fasciitis initial assessment and management are partially suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- History-taking and clinical diagnosis based on characteristic presentation
- Stretching programme education (visual demonstration via video)
- Footwear modification advice and education
- Referral coordination (podiatry, physiotherapy)
- Follow-up after initial in-person assessment

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Physical examination: point tenderness localisation, foot posture assessment, Windlass test
- X-ray, ultrasound, or MRI if required
- Corticosteroid injection
- Custom orthosis fitting (podiatry)
