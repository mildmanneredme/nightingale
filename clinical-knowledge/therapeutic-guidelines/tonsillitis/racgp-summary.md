## Tonsillitis Assessment (RACGP)

Tonsillitis (pharyngotonsillitis) is inflammation of the palatine tonsils, usually from viral or bacterial infection. Group A beta-haemolytic Streptococcus (GAS; Streptococcus pyogenes) is the most important bacterial cause (20–30% of cases in adults, 30–40% in children). Viral causes predominate (EBV, rhinovirus, adenovirus, influenza) and do not benefit from antibiotics. The Centor (modified McIsaac) score helps predict GAS likelihood and guide antibiotic prescribing — a cornerstone of antibiotic stewardship. Most tonsillitis is self-limiting (resolves in 7 days), but GAS requires antibiotic treatment to prevent: rheumatic fever (rare in Australia — higher risk in Indigenous Australians and Pacific Islander communities), peritonsillar abscess (quinsy), and post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis. Peritonsillar abscess is the most common deep neck space infection, characterised by unilateral peritonsillar swelling, uvular deviation, and 'hot potato' voice — requires urgent ENT referral. Telehealth is suitable for low-risk tonsillitis management; severe presentations require in-person assessment.

## Clinical Presentation

**Viral pharyngotonsillitis (most common):**
- Sore throat; odynophagia; low-grade fever; coryza; cough; hoarse voice
- Adenovirus: pharyngoconjunctival fever (sore throat + conjunctivitis + fever)
- Rhinovirus, coronavirus: milder; coryza predominant
- Self-limiting; supportive management only

**Streptococcal tonsillitis (GAS):**
- Sudden onset; high fever (>38.5°C); severe odynophagia; malaise
- Tonsillar exudate (white/yellow patches on inflamed tonsils)
- Tender anterior cervical lymphadenopathy
- ABSENCE of: cough, coryza, hoarse voice (their presence suggests viral aetiology)
- Scarlet fever: GAS + characteristic fine sandpaper rash; strawberry tongue; pastia's lines in skin folds; desquamation after 1 week

**Infectious mononucleosis (glandular fever — EBV):**
- Severe pharyngotonsillitis; very large tonsils + exudate; palatal petechiae
- Generalised lymphadenopathy; splenomegaly; hepatitis; fatigue persisting weeks
- Young adults and adolescents; monospot (Paul-Bunnell heterophile antibody) test; EBV serology (VCA IgM)
- CRITICAL: DO NOT prescribe amoxicillin or ampicillin — causes widespread maculopapular rash (90% of EBV cases)
- Contact sport restriction until spleen normalises (risk of splenic rupture): 3–4 weeks minimum; ultrasound spleen before return to sport

**Peritonsillar abscess (quinsy):**
- Severe unilateral throat pain; trismus (difficulty opening mouth); 'hot potato' (muffled) voice; drooling
- Uvular deviation to contralateral side; unilateral peritonsillar bulge; inability to swallow
- Complication of GAS tonsillitis or mixed oral flora infection
- URGENT ENT referral: intraoral drainage + IV antibiotics; hospital admission

**Retropharyngeal or parapharyngeal abscess:**
- Deep neck space infection; neck stiffness; dysphagia; fever; drooling; respiratory compromise
- Children more common; CT neck with contrast to confirm; 000; airway management

## Assessment Approach

**Modified McIsaac (Centor) Score — predicts GAS likelihood:**

Score 1 point each for:
- Fever >38°C
- Absence of cough
- Tender anterior cervical lymphadenopathy
- Tonsillar swelling or exudate

Score 1 additional point if: Age 3–14 years
Score -1 point if: Age ≥45 years

**Interpretation:**
- Score ≤1: Viral — do NOT prescribe antibiotics; 97% non-GAS; reassurance and symptom management
- Score 2–3: Uncertain — throat swab for GAS culture (or rapid antigen test if available); withhold antibiotics pending result; or empirical antibiotics if clinical concern
- Score ≥4: Likely GAS — prescribe antibiotics; throat swab optional but improves antibiotic stewardship
- **Australian context**: In Indigenous Australians and Pacific Islander communities: lower threshold for antibiotics (score ≥2) given higher rheumatic fever risk; follow local primary health network guidelines

**Investigations:**
- Throat swab for GAS culture: sensitivity 90–95%; specific; takes 24–48 hours; recommended before antibiotics if uncertain; GAS culture can confirm diagnosis retrospectively
- Rapid antigen detection test (RADT) for GAS: sensitivity 70–90%; specific; result within minutes; increasingly available in general practice; negative result does not exclude GAS (low sensitivity)
- FBC + EBV serology (VCA IgM, EBV PCR): if mononucleosis suspected; WBC shows atypical lymphocytes (lymphocytosis)
- Monospot test (Paul-Bunnell): rapid test; sensitivity 85–90% in adults; less sensitive in children <12 years; false negatives in first week
- Blood cultures: if systemically unwell with suspected bacteraemia; febrile with peritonsillar or deep neck space collection

## Key Differentials

- Viral tonsillitis: coryza, cough, hoarse voice, low-grade fever — no antibiotics
- GAS tonsillitis: McIsaac ≥2, no cough, high fever, exudate — antibiotics
- EBV (glandular fever): severe tonsillitis, generalised lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly, fatigue — NO amoxicillin; EBV serology
- Peritonsillar abscess: unilateral, uvular deviation, trismus, hot potato voice — ENT urgent
- Diphtheria: rare in vaccinated populations; grey membrane; bull neck; severe systemic illness; notifiable disease
- Epiglottitis: muffled voice, drooling, severe dysphagia, high fever, tripod position, respiratory compromise — 000; CRITICAL AIRWAY EMERGENCY
- Angina (Ludwig's angina): floor of mouth cellulitis; submandibular oedema; airway risk; 000
- Peritonsillar cellulitis (not yet abscess): unilateral redness and swelling without bulge or uvular deviation; treat with IV antibiotics

## Management Principles

**Symptomatic treatment (all tonsillitis):**
- Paracetamol 1 g QID: analgesia and antipyresis; regular dosing reduces pain
- Ibuprofen 400 mg TDS: NSAIDs; anti-inflammatory; superior to paracetamol for throat pain; combine with paracetamol for severe pain; avoid in children <3 months
- Throat lozenges (benzocaine/lignocaine): topical anaesthesia; temporary relief; OTC
- Warm saltwater gargles; cold fluids and ice blocks: comfort measures
- Adequate hydration: advise adequate fluid intake to prevent dehydration

**Antibiotic therapy for GAS tonsillitis (McIsaac ≥2 or confirmed GAS):**
- First-line: phenoxymethylpenicillin (penicillin V) 500 mg QID × 10 days: PO; PBS-listed; eradicates GAS; prevents rheumatic fever; well-tolerated; take on empty stomach or with food (less critical than flucloxacillin)
- Alternative if adherence concern: amoxicillin 500 mg BD–TDS × 10 days: broader spectrum; once-daily extended release available; some prefer for tonsillitis compliance
- Penicillin allergy (non-anaphylactic): cefalexin 500 mg BD × 10 days; or roxithromycin 300 mg daily × 10 days (macrolide; increasing resistance)
- Penicillin allergy (anaphylaxis): clindamycin 300 mg TDS × 10 days; or azithromycin 500 mg day 1, then 250 mg days 2–5 (Z-pack; short course; increasing macrolide resistance)
- **CRITICAL**: Never prescribe amoxicillin or ampicillin until EBV excluded — causes extensive rash
- Course length: 10 days is required for GAS eradication and rheumatic fever prevention; shorter courses have higher failure rates

**Indigenous Australians and Pacific Islanders — rheumatic fever prophylaxis:**
- Higher risk of acute rheumatic fever (ARF) and rheumatic heart disease; even with antibiotic treatment
- Secondary prophylaxis (after confirmed ARF): benzathine penicillin G 1.2 MU IM 3–4 weekly (bicillin); for 10 years minimum (or longer depending on cardiac involvement) — follow RHD Australia guidelines

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Phenoxymethylpenicillin (penicillin V) 500 mg QID × 10 days** (PBS restricted: streptococcal pharyngitis; first-line; PBS-listed; take with a full glass of water; most important to complete full course; 250 mg for children 10–25 kg)
- **Amoxicillin 500 mg BD** (PBS unrestricted: broader spectrum than penicillin V; alternative for tonsillitis adherence; DO NOT use if EBV suspected)
- **Cefalexin 500 mg BD** (PBS restricted: penicillin allergy — non-anaphylactic; cephalosporin cross-reactivity approximately 2% with penicillin allergy)
- **Roxithromycin 300 mg daily × 10 days** (PBS restricted: macrolide; penicillin allergy; increasing GAS resistance; use only if other options not tolerated)
- **Note:** 10-day course is mandatory for GAS tonsillitis. The shorter 5-day courses used in some countries are NOT recommended in Australia given rheumatic fever risk, particularly in high-risk communities.

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / hospital:**
- Epiglottitis: muffled voice, drooling, tripod position, stridor, high fever, severe dysphagia — CRITICAL AIRWAY; 000; Do NOT examine throat without airway equipment
- Retropharyngeal or parapharyngeal abscess: neck stiffness, drooling, respiratory compromise
- Ludwig's angina: floor of mouth cellulitis; submandibular oedema, trismus, airway threat
- Severe systemic illness, sepsis, airway compromise

**Urgent ENT referral (same-day or next day):**
- Peritonsillar abscess: unilateral bulge, uvular deviation, trismus, hot potato voice; aspiration or incision and drainage + IV antibiotics + hospital

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Recurrent tonsillitis (≥7 episodes in 1 year, or ≥5/year for 2 years, or ≥3/year for 3 years): ENT for tonsillectomy consideration (Paradise criteria)
- Persistent sore throat not resolving despite antibiotics: monospot/EBV serology; throat swab; ENT review
- Suspected tonsillar malignancy (unilateral tonsillar enlargement in adult ± neck mass): urgent ENT + CT neck

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Low-risk tonsillitis (McIsaac ≤1): symptomatic management; patient education; no antibiotics
- Review of throat swab or EBV serology results
- GAS tonsillitis follow-up if responding to penicillin
- Antibiotic prescription for GAS if high McIsaac score and clinically low risk for peritonsillar abscess
- EBV management counselling; return to sport advice

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Any suspected peritonsillar abscess or deep neck space infection: in-person examination is mandatory
- Stridor or airway compromise: 000
- Moderate to severe odynophagia with inability to swallow or open mouth
