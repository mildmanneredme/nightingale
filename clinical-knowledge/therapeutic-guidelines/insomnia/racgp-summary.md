## Insomnia Assessment (RACGP)

Insomnia disorder is the most common sleep complaint in general practice, affecting approximately 1 in 3 Australian adults with symptoms and 1 in 10 meeting diagnostic criteria for insomnia disorder. It is characterised by difficulty initiating or maintaining sleep, or early morning wakening, with associated daytime impairment. Insomnia frequently coexists with anxiety, depression, chronic pain, and other medical conditions. Cognitive Behavioural Therapy for Insomnia (CBTi) is the first-line treatment and is more effective than pharmacotherapy long-term. Telehealth is highly suitable for assessment, CBTi initiation, and medication review.

## Clinical Presentation

Diagnostic criteria (DSM-5 / ICSD-3): difficulty falling asleep, staying asleep, or early awakening, occurring ≥3 nights/week, for ≥3 months, causing daytime impairment, despite adequate opportunity for sleep.

Daytime impairment: fatigue, mood disturbance (irritability, low mood), cognitive impairment (concentration, memory), reduced motivation, functional decline, safety concerns (driving, machinery).

Acute insomnia (<3 months): often precipitated by a stressor or life event; may resolve without treatment.

Comorbid insomnia: coexists with depression, anxiety, chronic pain, GORD, nocturia, restless legs, OSA, menopause, PTSD. Treating the comorbidity alone often insufficient — insomnia typically persists without targeted sleep treatment.

Screen for: mood disorder (PHQ-9, GAD-7), alcohol use (commonly used as sleep aid — worsens sleep architecture), OSA (STOP-BANG screening), restless legs syndrome (irresistible urge to move legs, worse at rest and at night, relieved by movement).

## Assessment Approach

Sleep history: sleep onset latency, number of wakings, wake time after sleep onset, total sleep time, time in bed, sleep efficiency (total sleep time ÷ time in bed × 100%; target >85%), sleep schedule consistency.

Sleep diary: 2-week prospective sleep diary is the gold standard for characterising insomnia patterns; app-based diaries (e.g., Sleep Diary app) can be used for telehealth.

Daytime function: Epworth Sleepiness Scale (ESS) — if ESS ≥10, consider OSA; chronically sleep-deprived insomnia patients often have low ESS (paradoxically alert daytime due to hyperarousal).

Assess perpetuating factors: excessive time in bed, irregular sleep-wake schedule, daytime napping, stimulant use (caffeine after noon), screen use before bed, bedroom use for non-sleep activities, conditioned arousal (lying awake in bed).

Medical history: thyroid disease, cardiac failure (paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea), chronic pain, medications (corticosteroids, stimulants, beta-blockers, SSRIs/SNRIs, diuretics causing nocturia).

## Key Differentials

- Obstructive sleep apnoea — snoring, witnessed apnoeas, daytime sleepiness, overweight; ESS elevated; requires polysomnography (in-person referral)
- Restless legs syndrome — irresistible urge to move legs, worse at rest and evening; associated with iron deficiency — check ferritin
- Circadian rhythm disorder (delayed sleep phase) — consistently late sleep onset and wake time, not distressing if allowed to follow natural schedule; common in adolescents and shift workers
- Depression with insomnia — early morning wakening predominant, low mood, anhedonia
- Anxiety disorder — sleep onset insomnia predominant, racing thoughts
- Substance-induced insomnia — alcohol, cannabis, caffeine, stimulant medications

## Management Principles

**First-line: Cognitive Behavioural Therapy for Insomnia (CBTi)**
Most effective treatment; superior to pharmacotherapy in long-term outcomes; recommended by RACGP and Sleep Health Foundation as first-line.

CBTi components:
- Sleep restriction therapy — limit time in bed to match actual sleep time; builds sleep pressure; gradually extends as efficiency improves
- Stimulus control — bed only for sleep and sex; arise at same time daily; don't lie awake in bed
- Sleep hygiene education — regular sleep schedule, dark/quiet/cool room, no screens 30 min before bed, reduce caffeine after 12pm, limit alcohol
- Relaxation techniques — progressive muscle relaxation, mindfulness-based approaches
- Cognitive restructuring — challenging unhelpful beliefs about sleep (catastrophising, unrealistic sleep expectations)

Delivery: face-to-face with GP or psychologist; digital CBTi programs (e.g., Sleepio, SleepHub) shown to be effective and appropriate for GP initiation via telehealth referral; accessible via Medicare mental health care plan (GP referral to psychologist).

**Pharmacotherapy — adjunct only, short-term:**
Not recommended as first-line. Use only when: CBTi not available or declined, acute severe insomnia requiring short-term bridge, or specific comorbid indication.

- Low-dose doxepin 3–6 mg (PBS-listed for insomnia) — improves sleep maintenance; favourable side effect profile at low doses
- Melatonin immediate-release (Circadin 2 mg — PBS-listed for primary insomnia in adults ≥55 years) — modest effect; jet lag and circadian disorders
- Temazepam (PBS Schedule 8 — short-term only ≤4 weeks; dependence risk; avoid in elderly, OSA, depression)
- Avoid long-term benzodiazepine or Z-drug use (zopiclone — not PBS-listed in Australia; not recommended as long-term treatment)

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Doxepin** (Silenor — PBS restricted: insomnia in adults ≥18 years; low-dose 3–6 mg; not the antidepressant dose formulation) — 3 mg or 6 mg taken within 30 minutes of bedtime; caution with urinary retention, glaucoma
- **Melatonin prolonged-release** (Circadin 2 mg — PBS restricted: short-term treatment of primary insomnia in patients ≥55 years) — 2 mg taken 1–2 hours before bedtime for up to 13 weeks
- **Temazepam** (PBS Schedule 8 Controlled Drug — restricted: severe insomnia; short-term only; Authorised Prescriber must comply with state S8 prescribing rules)
- Note: zopiclone, zolpidem — not PBS-listed in Australia; not recommended as they offer no advantage over benzodiazepines and carry same dependence risk

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000:**
- Insomnia is rarely a direct emergency. However: assess suicide risk in all patients with insomnia and comorbid depression — insomnia is an independent risk factor for suicide. If active suicidal ideation with intent or plan, call 000 or arrange emergency psychiatric review.

**Urgent in-person or same-day assessment:**
- Severe daytime functional impairment with safety risk (e.g., driving, operating heavy machinery with significant sleep deprivation)
- Acute onset insomnia with severe psychiatric symptoms suggesting acute decompensation

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Sleep physician / respiratory physician: suspected OSA (polysomnography), complex sleep-wake disorders, insomnia refractory to CBTi and pharmacotherapy
- Psychologist: formal CBTi program delivery under Medicare mental health care plan
- Psychiatrist: insomnia with comorbid psychiatric disorder requiring specialist management

## Telehealth Suitability

Insomnia assessment and management is highly suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Sleep history taking and sleep diary review (patient can share diary via email or app screenshot)
- CBTi initiation, psychoeducation, and follow-up
- Digital CBTi program initiation and monitoring
- Pharmacotherapy initiation, review, and deprescribing planning
- Mental health care plan generation for psychology referral

**Requires in-person or specialist assessment:**
- Polysomnography for suspected OSA (requires sleep laboratory)
- Physical examination for restless legs or periodic limb movement disorder
