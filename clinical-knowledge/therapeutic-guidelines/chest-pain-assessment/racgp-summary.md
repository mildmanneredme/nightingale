## Chest Pain Assessment — Non-Acute (RACGP)

Chest pain is one of the most common presentations in both general practice and emergency settings. The critical challenge is to rapidly identify life-threatening causes (acute coronary syndrome, aortic dissection, pulmonary embolism, tension pneumothorax, pericarditis with tamponade) and differentiate them from benign causes (musculoskeletal, gastro-oesophageal reflux, anxiety). This knowledge base entry covers the assessment and management of chest pain in an outpatient/GP context where acute emergency has been reasonably excluded. Any chest pain with features of acute emergency should prompt immediate 000. Telehealth has significant limitations for acute chest pain assessment.

## Clinical Presentation

**Cardiac causes (high priority — must exclude first):**
- Acute coronary syndrome (ACS — STEMI, NSTEMI, unstable angina): central/left-sided chest pressure, heaviness, tightness; radiation to jaw, left arm, shoulder, back; diaphoresis; nausea; exertional onset; rest pain; sudden onset; elderly women, diabetics, and Aboriginal/Torres Strait Islander peoples may have atypical presentation (dyspnoea, fatigue, nausea without chest pain)
- Stable angina: predictable exertional chest discomfort; relieved by rest or GTN; reproducible
- Pericarditis: sharp pleuritic chest pain; worse lying flat, relieved by leaning forward; pericardial friction rub; may follow viral illness
- Myocarditis: chest pain + dyspnoea after viral illness; young person; elevated troponin; ECG changes

**Pulmonary causes:**
- Pulmonary embolism (PE): pleuritic chest pain (worse on inspiration), dyspnoea, tachycardia; risk factors (immobility, DVT, malignancy, OCP, recent surgery, pregnancy)
- Pneumothorax: sudden onset pleuritic chest pain; dyspnoea; reduced breath sounds on affected side
- Pneumonia/pleuritis: pleuritic pain; cough, fever, sputum

**Non-cardiac causes:**
- Gastro-oesophageal reflux (GORD): burning, retrosternal; post-prandial; improved with antacids; may mimic cardiac pain
- Oesophageal spasm: severe crushing substernal pain; may respond to GTN (mimics ACS); often associated with GORD
- Musculoskeletal: localised, sharp, reproducible on palpation; worse with movement or position; costochondritis (Tietze's); rib fracture; intercostal muscle strain
- Anxiety / panic disorder: chest tightness, palpitations, dyspnoea; episodic; associated anxiety features; hyperventilation
- Shingles (herpes zoster): dermatomal burning/shooting pain; may precede rash

## Assessment Approach

**In general practice, a systematic approach is required:**

History (highest diagnostic yield — better than ECG or examination in many cases):
- SOCRATES: Site, Onset, Character, Radiation, Associated symptoms, Time course, Exacerbating/relieving factors, Severity
- Red flag features: radiation to arm/jaw, diaphoresis, exertional onset, rest pain, syncope, palpitations, dyspnoea at rest
- Cardiac risk factors: hypertension, diabetes, smoking, hyperlipidaemia, family history of premature CVD, prior CVD
- Wells score for PE: clinical probability assessment (DVT symptoms, alternative diagnosis, HR >100, immobility, prior PE/DVT, haemoptysis, malignancy)
- Aortic dissection risk: severe tearing back/chest pain, BP difference between arms, unequal pulses — immediate 000

**Examination (in-person):**
- Vital signs: BP (both arms if dissection suspected), HR, RR, SpO2, temperature
- Cardiovascular: pulse quality, JVP, heart sounds (murmur, rub), peripheral oedema
- Respiratory: breath sounds, percussion (pneumothorax, pneumonia)
- Musculoskeletal: chest wall palpation for tenderness (localised tenderness suggests musculoskeletal)
- Abdominal: epigastric tenderness (GORD, peptic ulcer)
- Skin: herpes zoster rash

**Investigations (directed by clinical assessment):**
- ECG: essential for any suspected cardiac or pericarditis cause; may be normal in ACS — serial ECGs
- Troponin (high-sensitivity): rules in/out ACS; single hs-troponin + 3-hour repeat; normal rules out NSTEMI with very high NPV
- D-dimer: if PE suspected; normal age-adjusted D-dimer rules out PE in low-moderate probability
- CXR: pneumonia, pneumothorax, cardiomegaly, pulmonary oedema, aortic widening
- Lipase/amylase: if pancreatitis suspected (epigastric pain radiating to back)
- FBC, UEC, CRP: supportive

**Pre-test probability tools:**
- HEART score (History, ECG, Age, Risk factors, Troponin): validated risk stratification for ACS in emergency
- Wells score for PE clinical probability

## Key Differentials

- Acute coronary syndrome — must be first exclusion; troponin + ECG; do not delay; call 000
- Pulmonary embolism — Wells score, D-dimer, CT pulmonary angiography
- Aortic dissection — tearing pain, BP asymmetry, widened mediastinum on CXR; CT aorta emergency
- Tension pneumothorax — diminished breath sounds, tracheal deviation, haemodynamic collapse; needle decompression emergency
- GORD / oesophagitis — common, burning, post-prandial; responds to PPI
- Musculoskeletal chest pain — most common cause in general practice; localised, positional, reproducible on palpation
- Pericarditis — positional pleuritic pain; pericardial rub; ECG saddle-shaped ST elevation; NSAID treatment
- Panic disorder / anxiety — important but diagnosis of exclusion after cardiac causes ruled out
- Costochondritis — local tenderness at costochondral junctions; reproduced by palpation; managed with NSAIDs and reassurance

## Management Principles

**Musculoskeletal chest pain:**
- Reassurance: explain that the pain has a mechanical cause
- NSAIDs: ibuprofen 400 mg three times daily for 5–7 days; topical diclofenac for localised costochondritis
- Heat, gentle activity modification; physiotherapy if persistent
- Follow up in 1–2 weeks

**GORD-related chest pain:**
- PPI (omeprazole or pantoprazole 20–40 mg daily) for 4–8 weeks; if responsive, confirms diagnosis
- See `gord` entry for full management

**Pericarditis:**
- NSAIDs (ibuprofen 600 mg three times daily) + colchicine 0.5 mg twice daily for 3 months — reduces recurrence
- Rest; avoid strenuous exercise until symptom-free + normalised CRP
- Cardiology referral for complicated cases, myocarditis, or haemodynamic compromise

**Stable angina:**
- Short-acting GTN (glyceryl trinitrate sublingual spray or tablet): 400 mcg spray under tongue; repeat every 5 minutes up to 3 doses; if unresolved after 3 doses — call 000
- Long-acting anti-anginal: beta-blocker (metoprolol, bisoprolol) first-line; calcium channel blocker (amlodipine) or long-acting nitrate as alternatives or adjuncts
- Cardiology referral: exercise stress testing, coronary angiography assessment
- Secondary CVD prevention: statin, aspirin 100 mg, antihypertensive (see `cardiovascular-risk` entry)

**Anxiety-related chest pain:**
- Psychoeducation: explain the physiological connection between anxiety and somatic symptoms
- CBT referral; relaxation techniques; breathing retraining for hyperventilation
- SSRI if anxiety disorder confirmed (see `anxiety` entry)

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **GTN spray / tablet** (Nitrolingual spray, Anginine — PBS restricted: angina; 400 mcg sublingual as needed; patient education on use)
- **Ibuprofen** (PBS unrestricted — musculoskeletal chest pain, pericarditis; 400–600 mg three times daily)
- **Colchicine** (PBS unrestricted — pericarditis, 0.5 mg twice daily for 3 months; reduces recurrence by 50%)
- **Metoprolol** (PBS restricted — stable angina, hypertension; 50–100 mg daily or in divided doses)
- **Omeprazole / pantoprazole** (PBS restricted — GORD-related chest pain; 20–40 mg daily)
- **Aspirin 100 mg** (PBS unrestricted — secondary prevention in established CVD; not for primary prevention)
- **Note:** All chest pain presentations require clinical assessment to exclude cardiac cause before assuming a benign cause. Troponin and ECG are the cornerstones of ACS exclusion.

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Any suspicion of ACS: central chest pain + any of radiation, diaphoresis, rest pain, exertional trigger, nausea — call 000; administer aspirin 300 mg (chewed if not contraindicated); do NOT drive patient
- Aortic dissection features: severe tearing/ripping chest or back pain, asymmetric BP or pulses — 000 immediately
- PE with haemodynamic instability: chest pain + tachycardia + hypotension + severe dyspnoea
- Tension pneumothorax: acute dyspnoea + absent breath sounds + haemodynamic collapse
- Any chest pain in patient with troponin elevation detected in general practice

**Urgent same-day assessment or ED referral:**
- Chest pain + elevated D-dimer + intermediate PE probability — CT pulmonary angiography required
- New pericarditis with suspected haemodynamic compromise or fever — cardiology or ED
- Chest pain + new ECG changes (ST depression, new LBBB, T-wave inversion) without troponin result

## Telehealth Suitability

Chest pain assessment via telehealth has significant limitations.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Review of previously assessed and investigated chest pain (confirmed musculoskeletal, GORD, stable angina with prior workup)
- Stable angina medication review and dose adjustment
- GTN patient education and use instructions
- GORD or anxiety management review where cardiac cause already excluded

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- ANY new chest pain episode: ECG, vital signs, and clinical examination cannot be adequately performed remotely
- Troponin testing requires in-person or pathology collection
- Suspected ACS, PE, pericarditis, pneumothorax, aortic dissection — in-person and likely ED
