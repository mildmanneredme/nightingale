## NAFLD/MASLD Assessment (RACGP)

Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) — now increasingly termed metabolic-associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD) — is the most common chronic liver disease in Australia, affecting approximately 25–33% of the adult population. NAFLD encompasses a spectrum from simple steatosis (fat accumulation without significant inflammation) through non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH/MASH — steatosis with inflammation and injury) to fibrosis, cirrhosis, and hepatocellular carcinoma. The majority of patients with elevated liver enzymes in primary care have NAFLD as the underlying cause. NAFLD is strongly associated with metabolic syndrome, obesity, type 2 diabetes, and dyslipidaemia. The GP plays a central role in initial diagnosis, risk stratification, lifestyle counselling, and identification of patients with advanced fibrosis requiring specialist review. Telehealth is suitable for stable NAFLD monitoring.

## Clinical Presentation

**Clinical features:**
- Usually asymptomatic — detected incidentally on abnormal liver function tests or imaging
- Right upper quadrant discomfort or fullness (non-specific; may indicate hepatomegaly)
- Fatigue (non-specific)
- Signs of advanced disease (cirrhosis): spider naevi, palmar erythema, jaundice, ascites, splenomegaly, caput medusae, hepatic encephalopathy

**Risk factors (strongly associated with NAFLD):**
- Obesity (BMI ≥30): 75–100% prevalence of NAFLD; even non-obese patients with central obesity
- Type 2 diabetes: 50–75% prevalence of NAFLD
- Dyslipidaemia (elevated TG, low HDL)
- Hypertension
- Metabolic syndrome (see `metabolic-syndrome` entry)
- Rapid weight gain or loss (surgical or dietary)
- Certain medications: corticosteroids, amiodarone, tamoxifen, antiretroviral therapy

**NAFLD spectrum:**
- Simple steatosis: fat in >5% of hepatocytes; no significant inflammation; good prognosis
- NASH/MASH: steatosis + hepatocyte ballooning + lobular inflammation; significant progression risk
- Fibrosis (F0–F4): staged on liver biopsy or non-invasive markers; F3–F4 = advanced fibrosis/cirrhosis — significant morbidity
- Cirrhosis: portal hypertension complications; HCC risk (annual ultrasound surveillance)

## Assessment Approach

**Initial evaluation:**
- Liver function tests: ALT most useful; AST; ALP; GGT; bilirubin (elevated in advanced disease)
- Alcohol history: NAFLD requires exclusion of significant alcohol use (<20 g/day women, <30 g/day men for NAFLD definition)
- Medication review: exclude drug-induced liver injury
- FBC: thrombocytopenia in cirrhosis (splenic sequestration)
- Fasting glucose, HbA1c, lipid profile: metabolic risk profiling
- TSH: hypothyroidism associated with fatty liver
- Hepatitis B serology (HBsAg, anti-HBs, anti-HBc): exclude viral hepatitis
- Hepatitis C antibody: exclude HCV
- ANA, ASMA (anti-smooth muscle antibody): exclude autoimmune hepatitis
- Serum ferritin + transferrin saturation: haemochromatosis screen (common); ferritin also elevated non-specifically in NAFLD/NASH
- Abdominal ultrasound: bright liver (steatosis); cannot reliably distinguish simple steatosis from NASH; cannot stage fibrosis accurately; detects cirrhosis and portal hypertension

**Fibrosis risk stratification (critical in NAFLD management):**

FIB-4 score (FIB-4 = [age × AST] / [platelet count × √ALT]):
- <1.30: low risk of advanced fibrosis — reassure, lifestyle intervention, annual GP review
- 1.30–2.67: indeterminate — refer for elastography (FibroScan) or gastroenterology
- >2.67: high risk of advanced fibrosis — urgent gastroenterology referral; liver biopsy often needed

NAFLD Fibrosis Score (NFS): alternative non-invasive scoring tool; incorporates age, BMI, hyperglycaemia, platelets, albumin, ALT/AST

FibroScan (transient elastography): liver stiffness measurement; non-invasive; widely available in gastroenterology clinics; accurately stages fibrosis; preferred over liver biopsy where possible

**Annual monitoring in established NAFLD:**
- FBC, LFTs (ALT, AST, bilirubin), eGFR, fasting glucose/HbA1c, lipids
- FIB-4 annually: track trajectory
- Ultrasound: every 6 months in confirmed cirrhosis for HCC surveillance; annually in advanced fibrosis

## Key Differentials

- Alcoholic fatty liver disease: significant alcohol intake (>20 g/day women, >30 g/day men); AST:ALT ratio typically >2 in alcoholic liver disease
- Viral hepatitis B or C: hepatitis serology; PCR for viral load
- Autoimmune hepatitis: elevated ALT; positive ANA/ASMA; raised IgG; histological features
- Haemochromatosis: elevated ferritin + transferrin saturation >45%; HFE gene testing; phlebotomy treatment
- Drug-induced liver injury (DILI): medication review — NSAIDs, paracetamol, herbal supplements, antibiotics
- Wilson's disease: young patient; Kayser-Fleischer rings; low ceruloplasmin; copper studies — specialist
- Primary biliary cholangitis (PBC): elevated ALP; anti-mitochondrial antibody (AMA); middle-aged women
- Thyroid disease: hypothyroidism can elevate liver enzymes; TSH

## Management Principles

**Lifestyle intervention — cornerstone of NAFLD treatment:**

Weight loss:
- 7–10% total body weight loss: significantly reduces steatosis, inflammation, and improves fibrosis
- 5% weight loss: sufficient for steatosis reduction
- Caloric deficit 500–1000 kcal/day; dietary composition — Mediterranean diet most evidence; low-refined carbohydrate and fructose (high-fructose corn syrup in processed food particularly harmful)
- Physical activity: 150–300 min/week moderate-intensity aerobic exercise; resistance training; significant hepatic benefit even without weight loss

Alcohol: abstain or minimise alcohol — even moderate alcohol accelerates progression in NAFLD

Avoid hepatotoxic medications and supplements: NSAIDs, herbal supplements (many hepatotoxic — saw palmetto, kava, green tea extract), and unnecessary medications

**Pharmacotherapy (no PBS-approved agent specifically for NAFLD in 2023-2024):**
- Vitamin E: reduces inflammation in non-diabetic NASH; 800 IU/day; not recommended long-term due to prostate cancer signal; specialist advice
- Pioglitazone (thiazolidinedione): reduces NASH inflammation; PBS-listed for T2DM; use if concurrent T2DM; weight gain risk
- GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, liraglutide): significant NASH resolution and fibrosis reduction in trials; PBS for T2DM and obesity; specialist guidance
- SGLT2 inhibitors: emerging evidence; PBS-listed for T2DM and CKD; hepatic benefit likely
- Resmetirom (thyroid hormone receptor agonist): approved by FDA 2024; not yet TGA-approved or PBS-listed in Australia at time of writing; likely to become available — liver specialist monitoring

**Component metabolic risk management:**
- Optimise T2DM control (see `type-2-diabetes` entry)
- Treat dyslipidaemia: statins safe in NAFLD — do not avoid statins due to elevated baseline liver enzymes; check LFTs before starting; monitor at 3 months
- BP control: ARBs (candesartan) may have additional hepatic benefit in NAFLD

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Pioglitazone** (PBS restricted: type 2 diabetes; off-label for NASH without T2DM; 15–45 mg daily; weight gain, fluid retention, fracture risk; not if heart failure)
- **Semaglutide (Ozempic)** (PBS restricted: type 2 diabetes; hepatic benefit in NASH; significant weight loss; nausea initially)
- **Atorvastatin / Rosuvastatin** (PBS restricted: dyslipidaemia with CVD risk; safe in NAFLD; do not avoid statins due to mildly elevated baseline ALT)
- **Note:** No TGA/PBS-approved agent specifically for NAFLD in Australia at time of publication; treatment targets underlying metabolic risk factors; lifestyle remains the primary intervention
- Refer to gastroenterologist for advanced fibrosis, NASH with FIB-4 >2.67, or diagnostic uncertainty

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Acute liver failure: jaundice + coagulopathy + encephalopathy — life-threatening emergency
- Variceal haemorrhage: haematemesis or melaena in patient with known cirrhosis — 000
- Hepatic encephalopathy: confusion, flap (asterixis), in cirrhosis patient — urgent ED

**Urgent gastroenterology:**
- FIB-4 >2.67 — high risk of advanced fibrosis; FibroScan and possible liver biopsy
- Clinical or imaging features of cirrhosis or portal hypertension
- Thrombocytopenia + liver enzyme elevation — investigate for cirrhosis
- Rapid ALT rise (>5× normal) — exclude acute hepatitis, drug injury, autoimmune

**Non-urgent gastroenterology:**
- FIB-4 1.30–2.67 — indeterminate; FibroScan referral
- Suspected NASH — specialist assessment for advanced fibrosis staging
- Consideration of clinical trials or newer pharmacotherapies

## Telehealth Suitability

NAFLD monitoring is well suited to telehealth for stable low-risk patients.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Lifestyle counselling (diet, exercise, alcohol)
- LFT/FBC result review
- FIB-4 calculation and risk stratification counselling
- Metabolic risk factor management (BP, HbA1c, lipids)
- Medication review
- Referral coordination (gastroenterology, dietitian)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Physical examination if cirrhosis suspected (stigmata of chronic liver disease)
- Blood tests — pathology arranged
- FibroScan — gastroenterology or specialist imaging centre
- Liver biopsy — specialist centre
