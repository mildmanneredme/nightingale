## Ross River Fever Assessment (RACGP)

Ross River virus (RRV) infection is the most common mosquito-borne disease in Australia, with approximately 5,000–10,000 cases notified annually. It is caused by the Ross River alphavirus, transmitted by mosquitoes (primarily Culex annulirostris and Aedes vigilax). Distribution: predominantly Queensland, Northern Territory, Western Australia, and northern parts of other states; seasonal peaks in summer and autumn. Clinical hallmarks: polyarthritis (often debilitating), arthralgia, and myalgia — often more disabling than the acute viral illness. The name "epidemic polyarthritis" reflects the prominent joint involvement. There is no specific antiviral treatment; management is supportive (NSAIDs, paracetamol, rest, exercise). Most patients recover within 3–6 months, but up to 20–30% experience symptoms for >1 year (protracted post-infection syndrome). NOTIFIABLE disease in all Australian states and territories. Prevention: mosquito bite avoidance is the only protective measure (no vaccine available). Telehealth is suitable for management after initial diagnosis.

## Clinical Presentation

**Typical presentation:**
- Incubation period: 3–21 days (usually 7–9 days) after mosquito bite
- Onset: acute; abrupt arthralgia/arthritis ± fever ± rash
- Arthritis/arthralgia: bilateral, symmetrical, polyarticular; predominantly small joints (fingers, wrists, ankles, toes); also knees, elbows, hips; migratory or additive; severe morning stiffness; swelling and erythema of affected joints; often the most debilitating feature
- Fever: low-to-moderate grade; not always present; may be absent at presentation
- Rash: maculopapular, papular or vesicular rash in 50% of cases; affects trunk and limbs; non-purpuric; typically resolves within 10 days
- Myalgia: muscle aches; fatigue; profound lethargy
- Lymphadenopathy: mild; usually cervical or inguinal

**Protracted course (post-RRV syndrome):**
- 20–30% experience symptoms beyond 3 months; some >12 months
- Persistent arthralgia/arthritis; fatigue; myalgia; difficulty with daily activities
- Pathophysiology poorly understood; no evidence of ongoing active infection after acute phase
- Management: pacing, graded activity, NSAIDs, physiotherapy — similar to chronic fatigue/fibromyalgia approach

**At-risk groups:**
- Outdoor workers, rural residents, campers, people living near wetlands or floodplains
- Travellers visiting endemic areas
- Post-flood outbreaks: flooding increases mosquito breeding grounds; community outbreaks

## Assessment Approach

**Clinical diagnosis:**
- Clinical features in endemic area at the right time of year + appropriate exposure (outdoor activity, rural, coastal) = strong clinical suspicion
- Serology to confirm

**Serological diagnosis:**
- Ross River virus IgG and IgM antibodies (indirect fluorescent antibody test or ELISA): acute and convalescent serology
- IgM: becomes detectable 7–10 days after onset; confirms acute infection; may persist up to 12 months (limits interpretation of IgM alone for recurrent symptoms)
- IgG: detectable from 2 weeks; persists for years; indicates past infection (does not confirm active disease)
- BOTH IgM and IgG positive: strongly suggests recent infection within months
- Negative serology in first 7 days does NOT exclude RRV: repeat serology 2 weeks after symptom onset if initial negative
- Send to state public health laboratory; notify local public health unit (notifiable disease)

**Investigations (to exclude other diagnoses):**
- FBC: mild lymphocytosis; CRP/ESR: mildly elevated in acute phase; not specific
- Rheumatoid factor (RF) and anti-CCP: to exclude rheumatoid arthritis (if joint symptoms persist)
- ANA: exclude connective tissue disease if prolonged course
- Dengue serology: if recent tropical travel (dengue can present similarly)
- Barmah Forest virus serology: similar disease; Australian; request both if uncertain

## Key Differentials

- Rheumatoid arthritis: persistent symmetric polyarthritis; RF/anti-CCP positive; no rash; no mosquito exposure
- Reactive arthritis: follows enteric or STI infection; asymmetric; lower limb predominance; HLA-B27
- Rubella: maculopapular rash + polyarthritis; unvaccinated women; rubella serology
- Dengue fever: Southeast Asia/Pacific travel; fever + arthralgia + rash + myalgia; NS1 antigen; see `dengue-fever`
- Barmah Forest virus: same presentation as RRV; serology differentiates; also Australian
- Parvovirus B19: polyarthritis in adults; slapped cheek rash; IgM positive; self-limiting
- Systemic lupus erythematosus: ANA; renal involvement; photosensitive rash; prolonged course

## Management Principles

**No specific antiviral treatment exists:**
- Supportive and symptomatic management is the mainstay

**Pain management:**
- NSAIDs (ibuprofen 400 mg TDS or naproxen 500 mg BD): first-line for joint pain and inflammation; take with food; avoid in renal impairment, peptic ulcer disease; cover with PPI if risk factors
- Paracetamol 1 g QID: adjunct or alternative if NSAIDs contraindicated
- Low-dose corticosteroids: occasionally used for severe symptoms; short-term only; not proven to alter course
- Hydroxychloroquine: some evidence for benefit in protracted RRV arthritis; specialist (rheumatology) prescribing; PBS-listed for rheumatic conditions

**Activity and rehabilitation:**
- Acute phase: rest affected joints; avoid high-impact exercise during active arthritis
- Subacute phase: gradual return to gentle activity; avoid deconditioning
- Physiotherapy: joint mobilisation; hydrotherapy; particularly helpful for prolonged symptoms
- Pacing: energy management if fatigue and post-exertional malaise prominent (similar to `chronic-fatigue`)
- Graded exercise therapy: gentle progressive increase in activity; do NOT exercise through severe pain

**Patient education:**
- Natural history: most recover within 3–6 months; reassurance but honesty about possible prolonged course
- Fatigue and "boom-bust" cycle: explain energy envelope concept; avoid overexertion on good days
- Return to work: often restricted by arthralgia and fatigue; planning gradual return; workplace modifications
- Notifiable disease: public health notification required; NSW Health, QLD Health etc. — forms available

**Prevention:**
- Mosquito bite avoidance: most effective preventive measure; no vaccine available
- DEET-containing repellent (20–30%): apply to exposed skin; especially dawn and dusk
- Long sleeves and long pants in endemic areas at peak mosquito activity times
- Mosquito screens; nets; remove standing water around property

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Ibuprofen 400 mg TDS** (PBS unrestricted: NSAID; most effective single agent for RRV arthritis; limit to 2–4 weeks at a time; repeat courses as needed; cover with esomeprazole or pantoprazole if risk factors)
- **Naproxen 500 mg BD** (PBS restricted: NSAID; alternative; twice daily dosing; GI protection if risk factors)
- **Hydroxychloroquine 200 mg BD (Plaquenil)** (PBS restricted: antimalarial/rheumatic; off-label for protracted RRV but used in practice; annual ophthalmology review required for maculopathy; takes 3–6 months for full effect)
- **Note:** Corticosteroids (prednisolone) may provide short-term benefit for severe acute arthritis but should be used cautiously and briefly given potential to prolong the viral phase and cause dependency. Discuss risks/benefits clearly.

## Escalation Triggers

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Rheumatology: protracted arthritis >3–6 months not responding to NSAIDs; if hydroxychloroquine consideration; exclude RA or other inflammatory arthropathy
- Physiotherapy: chronic arthralgia and fatigue; graded rehabilitation
- Occupational therapy: prolonged functional impairment; return to work planning; home modifications

**Public health notification:**
- Notifiable disease: all confirmed RRV cases reported to state/territory public health unit; provides surveillance data; assists outbreak investigation

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Acute assessment with clinical history + serology ordering (if confident of diagnosis)
- Review of serology results and management planning
- NSAIDs and analgesia prescribing
- Ongoing symptom management and activity guidance
- Follow-up for protracted course; hydroxychloroquine management

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial presentation if diagnosis uncertain (joint examination; rash assessment)
- Joint effusion assessment (consider aspiration if large, for diagnosis/relief)
- Patients requiring rheumatological examination
