## Testicular Pain Assessment (RACGP)

Scrotal and testicular pain is a common presentation in general practice with causes ranging from benign (epididymo-orchitis, varicocoele, inguinal hernia) to surgical emergencies (testicular torsion — the most critical diagnosis to exclude). Testicular cancer, while relatively rare (the most common cancer in Australian males aged 15–35), classically presents as a painless testicular mass but can occasionally cause ache or heaviness. The GP's primary role is to: exclude testicular torsion (surgical emergency), identify epididymo-orchitis (most common cause in adults), detect testicular malignancy, and refer appropriately. The mantra is: acute onset testicular pain = testicular torsion until proven otherwise; do not delay surgical referral while waiting for investigations. Scrotal ultrasound is the key imaging investigation for non-emergent evaluation. Telehealth is not appropriate for acute scrotal pain assessment.

## Clinical Presentation

**Testicular torsion (SURGICAL EMERGENCY):**
- Acute onset (within hours) severe testicular pain; may radiate to inguinal region or lower abdomen
- Nausea and vomiting common (visceral pain response)
- Affected testis: high-riding (elevation as cremasteric reflex contraction pulls upwards), horizontally oriented ("bell-clapper deformity"), tender on palpation
- Cremasteric reflex: ABSENT on affected side (normally stroking inner thigh contracts cremaster); absence highly sensitive for torsion
- Age: bimodal — neonates and peripubertal adolescents (age 12–18 most common); can occur at any age
- CRITICAL: viability of testis is time-dependent: 90–100% salvageable within 6 hours; 50% at 12 hours; near-zero beyond 24 hours; 000 or immediate ED
- Do NOT: wait for ultrasound if clinical suspicion is high — proceed directly to urology

**Epididymo-orchitis (most common cause in adults):**
- Gradual onset (24–48 hours) pain, swelling, warmth; may have fever and rigors
- Epididymis (posterior ridge of testis) palpably enlarged and tender; testis may also be involved
- Urethral discharge or dysuria may be present; urethral STI in men <35 years (chlamydia, gonorrhoea)
- E. coli and other enteric organisms in men >35 years (urinary tract source); trauma, instrumentation
- Cremasteric reflex: PRESENT (distinguishes from torsion)
- Prehn's sign (elevation of testis relieves pain): classically positive in epididymo-orchitis; negative in torsion — but unreliable; do not use to exclude torsion

**Testicular torsion of appendage (appendix testis):**
- Gradual onset pain less severe than testicular torsion; younger boys
- "Blue dot sign": visible blue/black dot through scrotal skin at upper pole of testis (torted appendage)
- Self-limiting: resolves with analgesia and NSAIDs; occasionally requires surgical excision

**Varicocoele:**
- "Bag of worms" sensation in scrotum; heaviness; worse with prolonged standing or heat; left side more common (left gonadal vein drains into left renal vein — angle effect)
- May cause testicular atrophy and subfertility
- Adolescents: new varicocoele in adolescent → urology; may indicate left renal tumour compressing renal vein (Nutcracker phenomenon) or IVC tumour — CT kidney

**Hydrocele:**
- Fluid collection around testis; smooth, transilluminates; usually non-tender; often asymptomatic; heaviness
- Primary (idiopathic) — common in newborns (usually resolves by 2 years)
- Secondary: infection, trauma, malignancy — investigate underlying cause

**Inguinal hernia:**
- Inguinal bulge ± scrotal extension; reducible or irreducible; may cause testicular ache
- Strangulated hernia: acute onset severe pain; non-reducible; absent bowel sounds; 000

**Testicular cancer (crucial to exclude):**
- Typically painless (70%) or dull ache/heaviness; firm irregular testicular mass
- Most common cancer in men aged 15–35 (seminoma, non-seminoma); good prognosis with early treatment
- Risk factors: cryptorchidism (10× risk even after orchiopexy), family history, prior testicular cancer
- AFP, β-hCG, LDH: tumour markers (elevated in non-seminoma; β-hCG in seminoma)
- Urgent scrotal ultrasound + tumour markers + urgent urology referral

**Referred pain:**
- Renal colic: ureteric calculus (lower ureteral stone refers to ipsilateral testis); flank pain; haematuria; CT urogram
- Lumbar disc herniation: L1–2 dermatome; no testicular pathology on examination

## Assessment Approach

**History:**
- Onset: acute (minutes-hours = torsion; emergency) vs gradual (hours-days = epididymo-orchitis, torsion of appendage)
- Severity: severe and sudden = torsion; moderate-gradual = epididymo-orchitis
- Associated symptoms: nausea/vomiting (torsion), dysuria/urethral discharge (STI), fever (epididymo-orchitis), haematuria (renal calculus)
- Sexual history: STI risk; age <35 (chlamydia/gonorrhoea)
- Trauma: minor trauma can trigger torsion in susceptible individuals
- Age: neonatal and peripubertal → highest torsion risk; 15–35 → testicular cancer most prevalent

**Examination:**
- Vital signs: fever in epididymo-orchitis
- Cremasteric reflex: absent = strong evidence for torsion → urgent surgery
- Testis position: high-riding = torsion
- Palpate: testis, epididymis, spermatic cord — location of maximum tenderness; mass separate from testis
- Transillumination: fluid-filled = hydrocele; solid = testis tumour
- Inguinal rings: hernia
- Skin: blue dot sign (appendix testis torsion)

**Investigations:**
- MSU (MCS): pyuria and bacteriuria (epididymo-orchitis from urinary source)
- First-catch urine or urethral swab: chlamydia/gonorrhoea NAAT (men <35 years with epididymo-orchitis)
- Scrotal ultrasound with Doppler: most useful investigation for non-emergent evaluation; blood flow (absent in torsion — but NORMAL FLOW does NOT exclude torsion; clinical decision takes priority); mass characterisation; epididymal swelling
- Tumour markers: AFP, β-hCG, LDH — if testicular mass on ultrasound or clinically suspected cancer; urgent urology
- FBC, CRP: infection severity

## Key Differentials

- Testicular torsion: acute onset, severe, nausea, absent cremasteric reflex, high-riding testis → 000; surgical emergency
- Epididymo-orchitis: gradual onset, fever, urethral discharge, epididymis tender posteriorly; STI or urinary source
- Torsion of appendix testis: gradual onset in young boys; blue dot sign; self-limiting
- Testicular cancer: painless mass; firm; ultrasound + tumour markers; urgent urology
- Varicocoele: left-sided; "bag of worms"; worse with heat/standing; fertility concern
- Hydrocele: transilluminates; non-tender; check underlying testis on ultrasound
- Inguinal hernia: inguinal bulge; scrotal extension; strangulation emergency

## Management Principles

**Testicular torsion:**
- 000 immediately / direct transfer to emergency department with urology coverage
- Manual detorsion: "open book" manoeuvre may be attempted by trained clinician only if surgery will be delayed; does NOT replace surgical exploration
- Surgery: bilateral orchidopexy (fixation of both testes) under general anaesthetic; within 6 hours for best outcomes

**Epididymo-orchitis:**
- STI-related (age <35 or STI risk factors):
  - Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM single dose (gonorrhoea) PLUS doxycycline 100 mg BD × 14 days (chlamydia)
  - Or: if gonorrhoea unlikely — doxycycline 100 mg BD × 14 days alone
  - Concurrent STI screening; partner notification (ASHM guidelines)
- Non-STI-related (enteric organisms, age >35):
  - Doxycycline 100 mg BD × 14 days: first-line (covers atypical organisms including chlamydia if not excluded)
  - Ciprofloxacin 500 mg BD × 14 days: if enteric gram-negative organisms likely; urology guidance
  - Cefalexin + doxycycline: combined cover if uncertain aetiology
- Supportive: scrotal support (snug underwear or jockstrap); NSAIDs for pain; rest; ice

**Torsion of appendix testis:**
- Analgesia: paracetamol + ibuprofen; NSAIDs
- No antibiotics required; no surgery in most cases; reassure parents
- Resolves in 5–14 days; if not → surgical excision

**Testicular cancer:**
- Urgent urology referral (same-day or next-day): radical orchidectomy via inguinal approach (not trans-scrotal biopsy — risk of disrupting lymphatic drainage)
- Do NOT delay for tumour markers (request urgently in parallel)
- Excellent prognosis: overall >95% 5-year survival; seminoma extremely chemosensitive

**Varicocoele:**
- Asymptomatic: no treatment required
- Symptomatic or subfertility: urology referral for ligation/embolisation; evidence for improved fertility in selected patients
- Adolescent with new varicocoele: urology referral; exclude left renal vein/IVC pathology (nutcracker)

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Doxycycline 100 mg BD × 14 days** (PBS restricted: STI and non-STI epididymo-orchitis; take with food and water; do NOT take with dairy or antacids — chelation; avoid in pregnancy)
- **Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM** (PBS restricted: gonorrhoea; single IM dose; for STI-related epididymo-orchitis under 35; follow current ASHM recommendations — doses have increased due to resistance)
- **Ibuprofen 400 mg TDS** (PBS unrestricted: analgesia and anti-inflammatory for epididymo-orchitis and torsion of appendix testis; take with food)
- **Note:** Fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin, norfloxacin) are no longer recommended as first-line empirical therapy for STI-related epididymo-orchitis in Australia due to high rates of quinolone-resistant Neisseria gonorrhoeae. Follow current ASHM guidelines.

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / surgical emergency:**
- Testicular torsion: acute onset severe pain, absent cremasteric reflex, high-riding testis — do NOT delay; 000 / direct ED transfer; viability time-dependent
- Strangulated inguinal hernia: irreducible inguinal swelling + severe pain + nausea

**Urgent urology (same-day):**
- Testicular mass: urgent scrotal ultrasound + AFP, β-hCG, LDH; same-day urology referral
- Any clinical uncertainty between torsion and epididymo-orchitis: surgical exploration is preferred over ultrasound alone
- Acute epididymo-orchitis with systemic sepsis: IV antibiotics; hospital admission

**Non-urgent urology:**
- Varicocoele: symptomatic or subfertility concerns; ligation consideration
- Hydrocele: secondary hydrocele (confirm no underlying pathology on ultrasound); primary hydrocele if symptomatic

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Epididymo-orchitis follow-up (after in-person diagnosis and swabs)
- STI result review; partner notification counselling
- Chronic scrotal pain follow-up
- Varicocoele management review

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- ALL acute scrotal pain presentations: cremasteric reflex, testis position, palpation — cannot exclude torsion via telehealth
- Any testicular mass: requires physical examination and urgent scrotal ultrasound
- Epididymo-orchitis initial assessment: swabs, urinalysis
