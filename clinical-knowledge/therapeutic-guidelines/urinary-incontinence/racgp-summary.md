## Urinary Incontinence Assessment (RACGP)

Urinary incontinence (UI) — the involuntary loss of urine — affects approximately 38% of Australian women and 10% of men. It is significantly underreported; patients are often embarrassed and may not raise it voluntarily. GPs should proactively ask about UI, particularly in at-risk groups (postmenopausal women, post-prostatectomy men, older adults, women post-childbirth). There are three main types: stress UI (leakage with physical effort — sneezing, coughing, exercise), urgency UI (sudden compelling urge followed by leakage — overactive bladder), and mixed UI (combination of stress and urgency). Overflow incontinence (from incomplete bladder emptying) is less common. First-line management is conservative: pelvic floor muscle training (PFMT) supervised by a continence physiotherapist is the most effective intervention. The Continence Foundation of Australia helpline: 1800 330 066. Telehealth is suitable for management of established UI.

## Clinical Presentation

**Stress urinary incontinence (SUI):**
- Involuntary urine leakage with increased intra-abdominal pressure: coughing, sneezing, laughing, exercise, lifting
- No urgency or urge to void before leakage
- Risk factors: multiparity, vaginal delivery (especially large baby, prolonged second stage), high BMI, menopause, post-prostatectomy in men, chronic cough (COPD, smoking)
- Mechanism: urethral sphincter weakness or urethral hypermobility (inadequate pelvic floor support)

**Urgency urinary incontinence (UUI) / overactive bladder (OAB):**
- Sudden, compelling urge to void immediately followed by uncontrollable leakage
- Urgency (key symptom); urinary frequency (>8 micturitions/day); nocturia
- OAB without incontinence: urgency + frequency + nocturia but no leakage
- Risk factors: age, neurological disease (stroke, MS, Parkinson's, spinal cord injury), bladder irritants (caffeine, alcohol), UTI-triggered
- Mechanism: detrusor overactivity — involuntary bladder contractions

**Mixed urinary incontinence:**
- Both stress and urgency components; very common; treat dominant component first

**Overflow incontinence:**
- Incomplete bladder emptying → overdistension → continuous dribbling
- Men: bladder outlet obstruction from BPH (see `bph`); causes hesitancy, poor stream, terminal dribbling
- Women: pelvic mass, prolapse, cystocele; neurogenic bladder (diabetes, MS)
- Post-void residual (PVR) >150 mL on ultrasound
- Caution: acute urinary retention is a surgical emergency; 000

**Functional incontinence:**
- Normal bladder function but inability to reach the toilet due to mobility/cognitive impairment
- Common in frail elderly; falls prevention (see `falls-prevention`); environmental modifications

## Assessment Approach

**History:**
- Type characterisation: stress (effort-related), urgency (compelling urge first), mixed, overflow (dribbling + retention symptoms)
- Severity: pads used per day; social impact (activities restricted); Urogenital Distress Inventory (UDI-6) or ICIQ-SF questionnaire
- Bladder diary (3-day diary): time and volume of voids; leakage episodes; urge scores; fluid intake — most useful diagnostic tool
- Medications causing or worsening UI: diuretics (urgency), alpha-blockers (stress), anticholinergics (overflow), sedatives (functional)
- Bowel: constipation exacerbates UI; fibre and stool management
- Menopausal status: oestrogen deficiency causes urogenital atrophy → urgency and stress symptoms
- Neurological history: stroke, MS, Parkinson's, spinal cord disease → neurogenic bladder

**Examination:**
- Abdominal palpation: bladder distension (overflow); pelvic mass
- Pelvic examination (women): pelvic organ prolapse (cystocele, rectocele, uterine prolapse); vaginal atrophy (pale, dry, thin tissue)
- Urogenital atrophy: pale, dry, atrophied vaginal mucosa; loss of rugae; reduced oestrogen
- DRE (men): BPH assessment; prostate nodule (if present → PSA + urology referral)
- Neurological: bulbocavernosus reflex, perineal sensation, lower limb neurology (if neurogenic suspected)

**Investigations:**
- MSU (MCS): exclude UTI before initiating management (UTI can cause urgency symptoms and mimic/worsen UUI)
- Post-void residual urine (PVR) bladder scan: if overflow incontinence suspected; >150 mL abnormal; refer for urodynamics
- Urodynamic studies: specialist-level investigation; if diagnosis uncertain; before surgical intervention
- Pelvic ultrasound: pelvic mass; detrusor wall thickness (OAB); prolapse severity

## Key Differentials

- UTI: acute onset urgency + frequency + dysuria + pyuria; treat first before assessing UI
- Urogenital atrophy (GSM): postmenopausal; urgency + dysuria + vaginal dryness; responds to topical oestrogen
- Prolapse: cystocele causing symptoms of stress UI or voiding difficulty
- BPH (men): overflow and urgency symptoms; see `bph`
- Interstitial cystitis / bladder pain syndrome: pelvic pain + urgency + frequency + sterile urine; urology referral
- Neurogenic bladder: any neurological condition; complex management; continence nurse + urology

## Management Principles

**Conservative (first-line for all types):**
- Pelvic floor muscle training (PFMT): most effective intervention for stress UI and beneficial for urgency UI; referral to continence physiotherapist is strongly recommended; minimum 3-month programme
- Bladder training (for urgency UI): scheduled voiding; gradual extension of voiding intervals; urge suppression techniques (stop, relax, contract pelvic floor, wait for urge to subside then walk to toilet)
- Fluid management: adequate (1.5–2 L/day); avoid caffeine and alcohol (bladder irritants); avoid fluid restriction (concentrates urine and worsens urgency)
- Continence Foundation of Australia: resources at continence.org.au; helpline 1800 330 066
- Weight loss: BMI reduction of 5–10% significantly reduces stress UI episodes
- Treat constipation: straining exacerbates pelvic floor weakness

**Urgency UI / OAB — pharmacological:**
- First-line (if conservative fails after 3 months):
  - Solifenacin (Vesicare) 5–10 mg daily: most commonly used; PBS-restricted for OAB; anticholinergic (dry mouth, constipation); avoid in cognitive impairment and elderly — see anticholinergic burden in `polypharmacy-review`
  - Oxybutynin 5 mg BD–TDS: older; more side effects (anticholinergic); extended-release or patch reduces side effects; PBS-restricted
  - Tolterodine 4 mg MR daily: similar to solifenacin; PBS-restricted
  - Mirabegron (Betmiga) 25–50 mg daily: beta-3 adrenoceptor agonist; NO anticholinergic effects — preferred for elderly and cognitively impaired; PBS-restricted for OAB; do not use if uncontrolled hypertension; can combine with antimuscarinics if partial response
- Second-line (specialist):
  - Botulinum toxin A (Botox) intradetrusor injection: cystoscopic procedure; urologist; effective for refractory OAB; 6-monthly intervals
  - Percutaneous tibial nerve stimulation (PTNS): office-based; 12-week course; urologist or continence clinic

**Stress UI — treatment pathway:**
- Conservative: PFMT (continence physiotherapist) × 3 months — most effective
- Continence pessary: silicone ring pessary supports bladder neck; reduces leakage; try before surgery; inserted by continence nurse or gynaecologist
- Duloxetine 20–40 mg BD: SNRI; increases urethral sphincter tone; modest efficacy for stress UI; not PBS-listed for UI; side effects (nausea, dizziness); specialist consideration
- Surgical (specialist — urogynaecology or urology):
  - Mid-urethral sling (tension-free vaginal tape — TVT/TOT): highly effective for stress UI; urogynaecological or urological surgery; refer after conservative management failure

**Urogenital atrophy (GSM) — topical oestrogen:**
- Post-menopausal urgency and stress UI component significantly improved by topical oestrogen
- Vaginal oestradiol 10 µg pessary (Vagifem) or oestradiol cream (Ovestin 0.1%) inserted vaginally
- PBS-listed for vulvovaginal atrophy; minimal systemic absorption; safe even in women with history of breast cancer (in consultation with oncologist)
- Significant improvement in urgency, frequency, and dysuria within 3–6 weeks

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Solifenacin (Vesicare) 5 mg or 10 mg** (PBS restricted: overactive bladder/UUI; anticholinergic; dry mouth, constipation; AVOID in cognitive impairment and frail elderly; increase dose to 10 mg if 5 mg insufficient after 4 weeks)
- **Mirabegron (Betmiga) 25–50 mg** (PBS restricted: OAB; beta-3 agonist; NO anticholinergic effects; preferred in elderly; contraindicated if BP >180/110 mmHg; check BP before initiating; may be combined with anticholinergic for partial response)
- **Oxybutynin 5 mg** (PBS restricted: OAB; more side effects; extended-release formulation better tolerated; patch available)
- **Oestradiol vaginal pessaries 10 µg (Vagifem)** (PBS restricted: vulvovaginal atrophy; 1 pessary nightly × 2 weeks then twice weekly maintenance; minimal systemic absorption; effective for urogenital atrophy and associated UI)
- **Continence aids**: Continence Foundation of Australia provides information on Australian Government Continence Aids Payment Scheme (CAPS) — annual payment for eligible people with permanent incontinence; apply via continence.org.au

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent:**
- Acute urinary retention (unable to void; painful distended bladder): 000 or urgent ED; urethral catheterisation; urology review
- New neurological symptoms with incontinence (cauda equina syndrome — back pain + saddle anaesthesia + acute onset incontinence): 000; MRI spine urgently

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Continence physiotherapist: ALL patients with UI before pharmacological treatment — PFMT is first-line
- Urogynaecology (women) or urology (men): stress UI considering surgical treatment; refractory OAB; prolapse + UI; post-void residual >200 mL; haematuria with UI; suspected neurogenic bladder
- Continence nurse specialist: bladder diary review; pessary fitting; complex incontinence management

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Bladder diary review and type classification
- Antimuscarinics or mirabegron initiation and dose titration
- Continence physiotherapy referral and PFMT explanation
- Topical oestrogen prescribing for urogenital atrophy
- Follow-up and ongoing management

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial pelvic examination: prolapse, urogenital atrophy, DRE (men)
- MSU collection and post-void residual measurement
- Pessary fitting or review
- Haematuria assessment
