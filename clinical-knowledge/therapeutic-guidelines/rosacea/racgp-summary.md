## Rosacea Assessment (RACGP)

Rosacea is a chronic relapsing inflammatory skin condition primarily affecting the central face — cheeks, nose, chin, and forehead. It affects approximately 10% of the Australian population, predominantly Caucasians (Celtic/Northern European descent), women (though men develop more severe nose involvement), and adults aged 30–50 years. Rosacea is characterised by flushing, persistent erythema, papules, pustules, telangiectasia, and in advanced cases phymatous skin changes (most notably rhinophyma — bulbous nose). It is a clinical diagnosis — no diagnostic test required. GPs manage the majority of rosacea; specialist referral for severe, treatment-refractory, or ocular disease. Telehealth is well-suited to initial assessment and ongoing review. Rosacea significantly impacts quality of life and is associated with significant psychological distress.

## Clinical Presentation

**Rosacea subtypes (ROSCO classification — phenotype-based approach):**

Erythematotelangiectatic rosacea (ETR):
- Flushing (transient erythema): episodic, triggered; lasts minutes to hours
- Persistent central facial erythema: chronic background redness
- Telangiectasias: dilated small blood vessels; "broken capillaries" on cheeks and nose
- Burning/stinging sensation; dry, sensitive skin; dermatitis may coexist

Papulopustular rosacea (PPR):
- Central facial papules and pustules on background of erythema
- NO comedones (distinguish from acne)
- Most common type presenting to GP

Phymatous rosacea:
- Skin thickening and enlargement of nose (rhinophyma), chin, cheeks, forehead
- Predominantly men; develops after years of untreated rosacea
- Surgical treatment (dermabrasion, CO2 laser, electrosurgery)

Ocular rosacea:
- Blepharitis, meibomian gland dysfunction, conjunctivitis, keratitis
- Gritty, burning, red eyes; foreign body sensation; photophobia
- Present in 50% of rosacea patients; often precedes or accompanies skin disease
- Important: keratitis can lead to corneal scarring and visual loss — ophthalmology if severe

**Common triggers:**
- Sun exposure (UV radiation) — most common trigger
- Heat, hot beverages, hot food
- Spicy food, alcohol (particularly red wine, spirits)
- Exercise
- Emotional stress
- Cold wind
- Topical products containing alcohol, acetone, witch hazel
- Certain medications: vasodilators (CCBs, GTN), systemic steroids (topical steroids cause steroid rosacea)
- Demodex folliculorum mite overgrowth: associated with rosacea (causal role uncertain)

**Psychological impact:**
- Significant quality of life impairment: embarrassment, social withdrawal, depression
- Rosacea is strongly associated with depression — screen with PHQ-9
- Patient education and self-management support are central to management

## Assessment Approach

**Clinical diagnosis — no investigations required in typical presentation:**

Diagnostic features:
- Fixed centrofacial erythema + one or more of: flushing, papules/pustules, telangiectasia, ocular changes, phymatous change
- Location: central face (nose, cheeks, forehead, chin)
- Absence of comedones: key distinction from acne

**Assessment:**
- Duration, frequency, and severity of flares
- Identify triggers specific to this patient
- Ocular symptoms: gritty eyes, redness, photophobia — assess for ocular rosacea
- Medication history: systemic and topical steroids (cause perioral dermatitis / steroid rosacea)
- Psychological impact: PHQ-9; quality of life

**Grading severity:**
- Mild: occasional flushing, mild erythema, minimal papules
- Moderate: persistent erythema, multiple papules, telangiectasia
- Severe: intense erythema, numerous papules and pustules, rhinophyma, ocular involvement

**Investigations (only if atypical features or uncertain diagnosis):**
- Skin swab: if superimposed infection suspected
- Biopsy: rarely required; non-specific findings; only if diagnosis uncertain
- Lupus serology (ANA): if SLE lupus malar rash suspected (distribution similar but different quality)

## Key Differentials

- Acne vulgaris: comedones (blackheads, whiteheads) present; younger patients; different distribution; see `acne` entry
- Seborrhoeic dermatitis: scaling, dandruff in scalp and nasolabial folds; overlaps with rosacea (common comorbidity)
- Perioral dermatitis: papulopustular rash around mouth; associated with topical steroid use; treat by cessation of steroids + topical metronidazole
- Lupus malar rash (SLE): butterfly distribution; spares nasolabial folds; systemic symptoms; ANA
- Contact dermatitis: history of allergen exposure; patch testing
- Polycythaemia: persistent plethoric red face; elevated Hb/haematocrit; FBC
- Carcinoid syndrome: episodic flushing + diarrhoea + bronchospasm; 5-HIAA in urine; specialist

## Management Principles

**Trigger avoidance (essential — foundational):**
- Sun protection: SPF 50+ broad-spectrum daily; physical sunscreens (zinc oxide, titanium dioxide) better tolerated than chemical; hats; shade
- Identify and avoid personal triggers: food and drink diary; avoid known triggers
- Gentle skin care: fragrance-free, hypoallergenic, non-comedogenic moisturiser; avoid products with alcohol, acetone, witch hazel; gentle cleansers (Cetaphil, QV Gentle Wash)
- Avoid topical steroids: exacerbate and cause steroid rosacea

**Topical treatments (first-line for papulopustular rosacea):**
- Azelaic acid 15–20% gel/cream (Finacea, Skinoren): anti-inflammatory + antikeratinising; once or twice daily; effective for erythema and papules; good safety; mild stinging initially; PBS-listed
- Metronidazole 0.75–1% cream or gel (Rozex, MetroGel): anti-inflammatory; twice daily; well-tolerated; first-line option; PBS-listed
- Ivermectin 1% cream (Soolantra): targets Demodex mite + anti-inflammatory; once daily; highly effective for PPR; PBS-listed for rosacea; newer agent; well-tolerated
- Brimonidine 0.33% gel (Mirvaso): alpha-2 adrenergic agonist; topical vasoconstrictor; for persistent erythema (not papules); once daily; rapid but temporary effect (~8 hours); rebound redness possible; PBS-listed

**Systemic treatments (moderate-severe papulopustular rosacea):**
- Doxycycline 40 mg modified-release (Oracea): anti-inflammatory (sub-antimicrobial dose); not antibiotic at this dose; first-line systemic for moderate-severe PPR; 40 mg daily with food; PBS-listed for rosacea; treat for 3 months then reassess
- Doxycycline 100 mg (standard): if 40 mg MR unavailable; sun sensitivity; avoid in pregnancy and children
- Tetracycline 500 mg BD: older alternative; less used; same cautions as doxycycline
- Low-dose isotretinoin (0.1–0.3 mg/kg/day): specialist-initiated; for severe refractory rosacea especially with phyma; teratogenic; PBS restricted

**Ocular rosacea:**
- Warm compresses + eyelid massage: meibomian gland dysfunction
- Artificial tears: lubricants; preservative-free preferred for frequent use
- Azithromycin 1% eye drops or topical cyclosporine: specialist (ophthalmologist)
- Oral doxycycline: treats ocular as well as skin rosacea
- Ophthalmology referral: if keratitis, visual symptoms, or poor response to initial treatment

**Laser and procedural treatments (specialist/dermatologist):**
- Pulsed dye laser (PDL) or intense pulsed light (IPL): for telangiectasia and persistent erythema; not PBS-funded; private cost
- Rhinophyma: CO2 laser, electrosurgery, surgical shave — plastic/ENT surgeon or dermatologist
- Not acute-phase treatments — use after inflammation controlled

**Psychological support:**
- Depression screening PHQ-9 at diagnosis and review: rosacea-related depression common
- Patient education and support: RosaRed Australia (patient support organisation)
- Psychology referral if significant distress (GP Mental Health Care Plan)

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Azelaic acid (Finacea 15% gel)** (PBS restricted: rosacea — specifically listed; apply BD; mild stinging initially settles; effective for erythema + papules)
- **Metronidazole 0.75% cream (Rozex)** (PBS restricted: rosacea — specifically listed; apply BD; first-line topical; well-tolerated)
- **Ivermectin 1% cream (Soolantra)** (PBS restricted: rosacea — specifically listed; apply once daily; particularly effective for inflammatory papules; better compliance than BD applications)
- **Brimonidine 0.33% gel (Mirvaso)** (PBS restricted: rosacea erythema — specifically listed; once daily; temporary vasoconstriction; warn patients about rebound redness; avoid in cardiovascular disease)
- **Doxycycline 40 mg MR (Oracea)** (PBS restricted: rosacea — specifically listed; 40 mg daily; sub-antimicrobial anti-inflammatory dose; 3-month course initially; avoid in pregnancy; sun sensitivity)
- **Note:** Avoid systemic antibiotics at full antimicrobial doses for rosacea long-term — antibiotic stewardship; doxycycline 40 mg MR is sub-antimicrobial; topical agents preferred when possible

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent ophthalmology:**
- Keratitis: eye pain, photophobia, reduced visual acuity, corneal changes — corneal scarring risk; urgent ophthalmology review
- Suspected corneal ulcer from ocular rosacea complications

**Non-urgent dermatology:**
- Refractory rosacea: not responding to adequate trial (3 months) of topical therapy + doxycycline
- Rhinophyma: phymatous change requiring surgical or laser intervention
- Diagnostic uncertainty: atypical features; suspected SLE
- Isotretinoin initiation: required for severe disease; dermatologist prescribes (iPLEDGE monitoring in women of childbearing potential)

## Telehealth Suitability

Rosacea management is well-suited to telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Initial assessment based on clinical description and photos (if patient can share via platform)
- Trigger identification and lifestyle counselling
- Topical treatment initiation and review
- Doxycycline prescribing and monitoring
- Psychological impact assessment (PHQ-9)
- Referral coordination

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Formal clinical skin examination for uncertain diagnosis
- Differentiation from lupus or other conditions requiring laboratory testing
- Rhinophyma assessment (surgical planning)
- Ocular examination if rosacea eye symptoms
