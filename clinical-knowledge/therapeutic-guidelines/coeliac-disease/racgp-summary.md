## Coeliac Disease Assessment (RACGP)

Coeliac disease is an autoimmune condition triggered by gluten ingestion in genetically susceptible individuals (HLA-DQ2 or HLA-DQ8 carriers), causing small intestinal mucosal damage and malabsorption. It affects approximately 1 in 70 Australians, though up to 80% remain undiagnosed. Coeliac disease has become increasingly recognised as a multisystem condition with both gastrointestinal and extraintestinal manifestations. The only effective treatment is strict lifelong gluten-free diet (GFD). The GP plays a central role in case-finding, diagnosis coordination (serology + gastroenterology referral for duodenal biopsy), monitoring for complications, and managing comorbidities. Telehealth is suitable for coeliac management and monitoring once diagnosis established.

## Clinical Presentation

**Gastrointestinal presentations:**
- Classical: chronic diarrhoea, steatorrhoea, bloating, abdominal distension, weight loss — less common in adults at presentation
- Malabsorption symptoms: fatigue (from iron or B12/folate deficiency), bone pain (vitamin D deficiency → osteoporosis), failure to thrive in children
- IBS-like presentation: abdominal pain, bloating, altered bowel habit; increasing recognition that coeliac disease underlies a proportion of "IBS" — serological screening appropriate in IBS

**Extraintestinal manifestations (often predominant in adults):**
- Iron deficiency anaemia: most common presentation in adults; unexplained or poorly responsive to iron supplementation
- Folate and vitamin B12 deficiency: neurological sequelae (peripheral neuropathy)
- Osteoporosis / osteopenia: vitamin D and calcium malabsorption; DEXA screening indicated
- Dermatitis herpetiformis: intensely itchy vesicular rash on elbows, knees, buttocks; pathognomonic of gluten sensitivity; skin biopsy confirms; treat with dapsone + GFD
- Elevated liver enzymes: unexplained transaminase elevation (up to 50% of coeliac); resolves on GFD
- Neurological: peripheral neuropathy, cerebellar ataxia (gluten ataxia)
- Subfertility, recurrent miscarriage, delayed puberty
- Aphthous ulcers (mouth ulcers)

**Risk groups (opportunistic serology recommended):**
- First-degree relatives of confirmed coeliac disease (10–15% risk)
- Type 1 diabetes (5–10%)
- Autoimmune thyroid disease (Hashimoto's, Graves')
- Down syndrome and Turner syndrome (higher risk)
- IBS-like symptoms
- Unexplained iron deficiency or malabsorption
- Osteoporosis without clear risk factors

## Assessment Approach

**Serology (must be performed while on gluten-containing diet — critical):**
- Anti-tissue transglutaminase IgA (anti-tTG IgA): most sensitive and specific first-line test; sensitivity >95%, specificity >95%; must be paired with total IgA to exclude IgA deficiency
- Total serum IgA: IgA deficiency (1 in 500) causes false negative anti-tTG IgA; if IgA deficient, use IgG-based tests (anti-DGP IgG or anti-tTG IgG)
- Anti-endomysial IgA (EMA): high specificity; second-line confirmation; more expensive
- Anti-deamidated gliadin peptide (DGP) IgG: useful in IgA deficiency

**CRITICAL: Patient must be eating gluten regularly (≥3 g/day for ≥6–8 weeks) at the time of serology — testing on a self-started gluten-free diet will give false negative results. Advise gluten challenge (reintroduction) if already on GFD before testing.**

**Histological confirmation (gastroenterology referral for duodenal biopsy):**
- Upper GI endoscopy with multiple duodenal biopsies: required for diagnosis confirmation (except children with high anti-tTG IgA >10× upper normal limit + positive EMA — can diagnose without biopsy per international guidelines)
- Marsh classification: grade of villous atrophy (Marsh 3 = partial to total villous atrophy = confirmed coeliac)
- HLA-DQ2/DQ8 testing: negative result rules out coeliac disease (high negative predictive value); positive does not confirm (60–70% of general population carries HLA-DQ2 or DQ8); useful in equivocal cases

**Follow-up monitoring (after confirmed diagnosis and on GFD):**
- Anti-tTG IgA at 6 months and 12 months — should normalise with adherence; persistent elevation suggests ongoing gluten exposure
- FBC, iron studies, vitamin D, B12, folate: baseline and monitoring
- DEXA scan: at diagnosis for osteoporosis assessment; repeat at 2 years if osteoporotic or if GFD poorly adhered
- Annual review: symptom assessment, dietary adherence review, nutritional status, screening for complications

## Key Differentials

- Irritable bowel syndrome: overlapping symptoms; coeliac serology appropriate in IBS before labelling
- Inflammatory bowel disease: Crohn's disease may affect small bowel; distinguish with biopsy findings and ileoscopy
- Microscopic colitis: watery diarrhoea; normal colonoscopy; biopsy required
- Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO): diarrhoea, bloating; breath test; responds to antibiotics; may coexist with coeliac
- Lactose intolerance: very common comorbidity in coeliac (secondary to villous atrophy); assess separately; often improves with GFD
- Wheat allergy: IgE-mediated; different mechanism; skin prick testing; not the same as coeliac
- Non-coeliac gluten sensitivity (NCGS): symptomatic improvement on GFD without positive serology or histology; diagnosis of exclusion; management same (GFD)

## Management Principles

**Strict lifelong gluten-free diet (only treatment):**
- Eliminate: wheat, rye, barley, and commonly contaminated oats (unless certified gluten-free oats); read food labels carefully
- Allowed: rice, corn, potato, quinoa, certified gluten-free oats, all unprocessed meats/fish/dairy/vegetables
- Cross-contamination must be avoided (shared cooking surfaces, utensils, bread toasters)
- Dietitian referral: essential for all newly diagnosed coeliac patients; education on label reading, hidden gluten sources, nutritional completeness of GFD
- Coeliac Australia membership: resources, peer support, restaurant cards, product guides

**Nutritional supplementation (address deficiencies at diagnosis):**
- Iron supplements: if iron deficiency anaemia; replete before starting GFD (GFD often resolves deficiency over months)
- Vitamin D: supplement if deficient; see `vitamin-d-deficiency` entry
- Folate and B12: supplement if deficient; investigate cause if B12 very low (intrinsic factor antibodies — coeliac + pernicious anaemia coexistence)
- Calcium: adequate dietary calcium on GFD; supplement if needed

**Monitoring for complications:**
- Refractory coeliac disease (persistent symptoms + villous atrophy despite strict GFD >12 months): rare; gastroenterology referral; risk of enteropathy-associated T-cell lymphoma
- Intestinal lymphoma: rare but increased risk in long-standing uncontrolled coeliac; alarm features (weight loss, abdominal pain, night sweats) → urgent gastroenterology
- Dermatitis herpetiformis: dapsone for rapid skin symptom control; strict GFD allows gradual cessation of dapsone over months

**Vaccinations:**
- Increased pneumococcal disease risk in hyposplenism (functional asplenia associated with coeliac): offer 23vPPV and 13vPCV per RACGP guidelines

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Iron supplements** (PBS restricted: iron deficiency; ferrous sulfate 325 mg twice daily; or IV iron infusion for severe deficiency or oral intolerance)
- **Cholecalciferol (vitamin D)** (PBS unrestricted — 1000–2000 IU daily; or 50,000 IU weekly loading dose if severely deficient)
- **Folic acid** (PBS restricted — folate deficiency; 0.5 mg daily or 5 mg daily)
- **Cyanocobalamin / hydroxocobalamin** (vitamin B12 — PBS restricted; 1000 mcg IM monthly if malabsorption-related B12 deficiency)
- **Dapsone** (PBS restricted — dermatitis herpetiformis; specialist supervised; baseline G6PD deficiency test; haemolysis monitoring)
- **Note:** Gluten-free foods are not PBS-subsidised in Australia; Coeliac Australia and specialist dietitians can assist patients with food labelling and sourcing

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent gastroenterology referral:**
- Suspected refractory coeliac disease: persisting symptoms + positive serology despite strict GFD >12 months
- New alarming symptoms (weight loss, lymphadenopathy, abdominal mass): small bowel lymphoma exclusion
- Severely symptomatic at diagnosis with significant malnutrition: hospital assessment and specialist induction

**Non-urgent specialist referral:**
- Gastroenterologist: all newly diagnosed coeliac — confirmation biopsy via endoscopy
- Dietitian: all newly diagnosed — essential first-line specialist referral
- Endocrinologist / osteoporosis specialist: if significant osteoporosis identified
- Dermatologist: suspected dermatitis herpetiformis — skin biopsy for DIF (direct immunofluorescence)

## Telehealth Suitability

Coeliac disease monitoring is well suited to telehealth once diagnosis established.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Dietary adherence review and GFD counselling
- Anti-tTG IgA result interpretation and monitoring
- Nutritional supplementation review
- Symptom monitoring for refractory disease or complications
- Dietitian referral and coordination
- Vaccination status review (pneumococcal)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial diagnostic workup — blood tests and gastroenterology referral
- DEXA scan for osteoporosis
- Clinical examination if alarming symptoms present
