## Chronic Kidney Disease Monitoring (RACGP)

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) is defined as abnormalities of kidney structure or function for >3 months, with implications for health. CKD affects approximately 10% of Australians — up to 1.7 million people — most of whom are undiagnosed. The most common causes are diabetic nephropathy, hypertensive nephrosclerosis, and glomerulonephritis. CKD is a major independent risk factor for cardiovascular disease; cardiovascular death is more common than end-stage kidney disease (ESKD) in most CKD patients. The GP role includes: identifying at-risk patients, annual CKD screening, managing progression risk factors (BP, proteinuria, glucose), monitoring for complications, medication dose adjustment, and coordinating nephrology referral. Telehealth is well-suited for CKD monitoring and medication management.

## Clinical Presentation

CKD is largely asymptomatic until advanced stages (Stage 4–5). Symptoms of uraemia (advanced CKD): fatigue, anorexia, nausea, fluid retention, dyspnoea, pruritus, confusion. Most CKD is detected incidentally through blood or urine testing.

**CKD staging (KDIGO 2012):**
- G1: eGFR ≥90 mL/min/1.73m² + kidney damage markers
- G2: eGFR 60–89 + kidney damage markers
- G3a: eGFR 45–59
- G3b: eGFR 30–44
- G4: eGFR 15–29
- G5: eGFR <15 (kidney failure)

**Albuminuria categories (A):**
- A1: ACR <3 mg/mmol (normal)
- A2: ACR 3–30 mg/mmol (moderately increased)
- A3: ACR >30 mg/mmol (severely increased)

**Risk stratification uses both GFR and Albuminuria categories** — "heat map" in KDIGO guidelines.

**At-risk populations to screen annually:**
- Type 1 and 2 diabetes: annual eGFR + ACR from diagnosis (T1D) or annually (T2D)
- Hypertension: annual eGFR + ACR
- Cardiovascular disease
- Obesity (BMI ≥30)
- Age ≥60 years
- Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples ≥18 years — significantly elevated CKD risk; 3–4× general population; earlier and more aggressive CKD
- Family history of CKD or kidney disease
- Previous acute kidney injury (AKI)
- Obstructive uropathy, recurrent UTIs, kidney stones

## Assessment Approach

**Annual monitoring in established CKD:**
- eGFR: estimated from serum creatinine using CKD-EPI equation; NB — eGFR based on creatinine may overestimate in patients with low muscle mass (elderly, malnutrition); cystatin C-based eGFR may be more accurate in some populations
- Spot urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (ACR): morning void preferred; repeat if ACR elevated to confirm (two of three elevated results = persistent albuminuria = CKD)
- Blood pressure: target <130/80 mmHg in CKD with albuminuria; both in-person and home BP monitoring valuable
- Electrolytes: sodium, potassium (hyperkalaemia risk especially with ACE inhibitor/ARB + CKD), bicarbonate (metabolic acidosis in CKD), calcium, phosphate (hyperphosphataemia in advanced CKD)
- Haemoglobin: anaemia of CKD (normochromic normocytic) — check from eGFR <45
- PTH, vitamin D: CKD-mineral bone disorder — from eGFR <45

**Medication review (CKD stage-specific dose adjustment required for many drugs):**
- Metformin: reduce dose at eGFR <45; stop at eGFR <30 (lactic acidosis risk)
- NSAIDs: avoid in CKD eGFR <30; caution <60; risk of acute deterioration
- Antibiotics: trimethoprim, nitrofurantoin, aminoglycosides, vancomycin — renal dose adjustment
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs: continued benefit in proteinuric CKD; accept up to 25% eGFR decline on initiation; monitor potassium
- SGLT2 inhibitors: reduced efficacy at eGFR <45; however, dapagliflozin and empagliflozin have demonstrated CKD progression reduction independent of diabetes; may continue down to eGFR 20 for renoprotective effect (per updated guidelines)
- Radiocontrast (iodinated): withheld 24–48 hours before procedure and adequate hydration required; risk of contrast-induced nephropathy in CKD <45

## Key Differentials

- Acute kidney injury (AKI) — acute, reversible; serum creatinine rising over days to weeks; distinguish from CKD (look for prior eGFR values); AKI requires urgent assessment
- Pre-renal azotaemia: dehydration, heart failure, NSAID use; reversible with volume/medication change
- Obstructive uropathy: hydronephrosis from prostate, stones, pelvic mass; renal ultrasound; reversible if treated
- Chronic interstitial nephritis: analgesic overuse (NSAIDs), lithium, calcineurin inhibitors
- Glomerulonephritis: haematuria + proteinuria ± hypertension; nephrology referral for biopsy

## Management Principles

**Blood pressure control (most important modifiable factor):**
- Target: <130/80 mmHg in CKD (particularly if proteinuric)
- ACE inhibitor or ARB: first-line in diabetic and non-diabetic proteinuric CKD — reduces proteinuria and slows progression; do NOT use ACE inhibitor + ARB together (increased adverse effects without additional benefit — ONTARGET trial)
- Check potassium 1–2 weeks after initiation or dose change; accept up to 25% eGFR decline (if stable thereafter, continue)
- Add calcium channel blocker or diuretic as second/third agent for BP control

**Glycaemic control in diabetic CKD:**
- HbA1c target ~53 mmol/mol (7%); may individualise (less stringent in advanced CKD and elderly with hypoglycaemia risk)
- SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin): preferred add-on in diabetic CKD — reduce CV events, heart failure, and slow CKD progression; dapagliflozin PBS Authority for CKD with albuminuria ≥300 mg/g regardless of diabetes
- Metformin: dose-adjust at eGFR <45; stop at <30
- Sulfonylureas: hypoglycaemia risk in CKD (reduced renal clearance of active metabolites — especially gliclazide); use gliclazide (shorter-acting metabolites) cautiously; avoid glibenclamide in CKD
- Insulin: dose reduction as CKD progresses (reduced insulin clearance)

**Proteinuria reduction:**
- ACE inhibitor or ARB: first-line for all CKD with ACR ≥3 mg/mmol; titrate to maximum tolerated dose
- Finerenone: new mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist; PBS-restricted for diabetic CKD with albuminuria — reduces CKD progression and cardiovascular events; used in addition to SGLT2 inhibitor and RAS blockade

**Lifestyle:**
- Smoking cessation: slows CKD progression; see `smoking-cessation` entry
- Dietary protein restriction: 0.8 g/kg/day in advanced CKD (eGFR <45) — may slow progression; dietitian guidance essential
- Sodium restriction: <2 g/day sodium reduces BP and proteinuria
- Potassium restriction: required in hyperkalaemia (ACR >3 + hyperkalaemia on ACE inhibitor/ARB)
- Fluid management: not restricted in early CKD; restricted in advanced CKD or heart failure

**Anaemia management:**
- Check iron first: iron deficiency common; treat with oral or IV iron before erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESA)
- Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESA — darbepoetin, epoetin): initiated by nephrologist; target Hb 100–115 g/L; avoid excess Hb (CVD risk)

**CKD-mineral bone disorder:**
- Phosphate restriction (dietary); phosphate binders if hyperphosphataemia — specialist supervised
- Vitamin D supplementation: active vitamin D (calcitriol or alfacalcidol) — specialist supervised for CKD stages 3b–5

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Ramipril / perindopril / lisinopril** (ACE inhibitors — PBS restricted: hypertension, heart failure, diabetes; renoprotective in CKD with albuminuria; monitor eGFR and potassium)
- **Irbesartan / candesartan / losartan** (ARBs — PBS restricted; alternative to ACE inhibitor if intolerant)
- **Dapagliflozin** (Forxiga — PBS restricted: CKD with albuminuria ≥300 mg/g — authority for CKD indication regardless of diabetes status; 10 mg daily; reduces CKD progression)
- **Finerenone** (Kerendia — PBS restricted: diabetic CKD with albuminuria; 10–20 mg daily; monitor potassium)
- **Iron supplements** (PBS restricted — iron deficiency in CKD; oral or IV iron infusion)
- **Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents** (PBS restricted: dialysis patients; specialist initiated)
- **Note:** Review all medications for renal dose adjustment at each eGFR monitoring; use online resources such as mims.com.au or Therapeutic Guidelines for dosing at specific eGFR values; avoid nephrotoxic agents (NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, contrast without precautions) in CKD

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Acute severe hyperkalaemia (K+ >6.0 mmol/L with ECG changes or symptoms): cardiac emergency
- AKI (rapid creatinine rise) in CKD: causes include dehydration, NSAID, infection, contrast — hospital assessment urgently
- Uraemic encephalopathy (confusion, seizure in CKD) — dialysis emergency

**Urgent nephrology referral (within weeks):**
- eGFR <30 (Stage 4) — nephrology for dialysis preparation planning, including fistula creation
- Rapidly declining eGFR (>25% decline over 1 year without identifiable cause)
- Heavy proteinuria (ACR >100 mg/mmol) — may require biopsy
- Refractory hypertension in CKD
- Hyperkalaemia unresponsive to conservative management

**Non-urgent nephrology referral:**
- CKD Stage 3b (eGFR 30–44) with progressive disease, proteinuria, or diagnostic uncertainty
- All Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander patients with CKD — lower threshold for specialist input

## Telehealth Suitability

CKD monitoring is highly suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- eGFR and ACR result review
- BP result review and antihypertensive management
- Medication review for CKD-appropriate prescribing
- SGLT2 inhibitor or finerenone initiation and monitoring
- Lifestyle counselling and dietitian referral
- Anaemia monitoring and iron supplementation
- Nephrology referral coordination

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Blood pressure measurement (home BP monitoring or pharmacy can supplement)
- Blood tests (eGFR, electrolytes, HbA1c, iron studies — pathology arranged)
- Renal ultrasound (radiology referral if obstructive cause or diagnostic uncertainty)
