## Anaphylaxis — Action Plan Review (RACGP)

Anaphylaxis is a severe, potentially life-threatening generalised allergic reaction that requires immediate treatment with intramuscular adrenaline. In Australia, approximately 1 in 1000 Australians will experience anaphylaxis, and the rate is increasing. Common triggers include foods (peanuts, tree nuts, milk, egg, wheat, seafood, sesame), medications (penicillin, NSAIDs, contrast media), insect stings (bee, wasp, jack jumper ant), and latex. The GP plays a central role in: post-anaphylaxis management, prescription of adrenaline autoinjectors (EpiPen), ASCIA Action Plan completion, allergen identification, and coordinating immunology/allergy referral. Telehealth is suitable for post-anaphylaxis follow-up and action plan review.

## Clinical Presentation

**ASCIA diagnostic criteria for anaphylaxis (any one of the following following likely allergen exposure):**

Criterion 1: Acute onset of illness (minutes to hours) with involvement of SKIN and/or MUCOSA (urticaria, erythema, angioedema, generalised itching) PLUS at least one of:
- Respiratory compromise (dyspnoea, wheeze, bronchospasm, stridor, hypoxaemia)
- Cardiovascular compromise (hypotension, shock, syncope, collapse)
- Severe GI symptoms (severe cramping, vomiting)

Criterion 2: TWO OR MORE of the following within minutes to hours of likely allergen exposure:
- Skin-mucosal involvement
- Respiratory compromise
- Cardiovascular compromise
- Severe GI symptoms

Criterion 3: Hypotension alone after exposure to known allergen

**Severity grading (ASCIA):**
- Mild: urticaria, angioedema, rhinitis, nausea only — likely allergic reaction, not yet anaphylaxis
- Moderate: wheeze, persistent cough, chest tightness, vomiting, presyncope — anaphylaxis; treat with IM adrenaline
- Severe: cyanosis, SpO2 <92%, hypotension, collapse, loss of consciousness — severe anaphylaxis; IM adrenaline + 000

**Biphasic anaphylaxis:** Second reaction 1–72 hours after initial response (typically 4–8 hours); occurs in up to 20% of cases; all patients require ≥4–6 hours observation post-treatment in hospital.

**Common allergen triggers (Australia-specific):**
- Food: peanuts most common; tree nuts (cashew most common in AU); cow's milk; egg; wheat; seafood (crustacea, fish); sesame; lupin (common in AU — found in flour, bread, some foods)
- Medications: beta-lactam antibiotics (penicillin, cephalosporins), NSAIDs, aspirin, ACE inhibitors (angioedema — bradykinin-mediated, NOT IgE-mediated), chemotherapy
- Insect venom: honey bee, wasp, jack jumper ant (Myrmecia pilosula — Australian-specific; found in southeastern Australia and Tasmania)
- Latex: less common; healthcare workers, spina bifida patients; cross-reactivity with kiwi, avocado, banana
- Exercise-induced anaphylaxis: exercise alone or with cofactor (food, NSAID, alcohol)

## Assessment Approach

**Post-anaphylaxis follow-up (GP consultation after ED discharge):**
- Review what happened: timeline, trigger, symptoms, treatment given (adrenaline, antihistamines, steroids); effectiveness of treatment
- Confirm diagnosis: was it anaphylaxis? (distinguish from vasovagal, panic attack, urticaria alone, vocal cord dysfunction)
- Prescribe/review adrenaline autoinjector: ensure patient has current EpiPen (0.3 mg adrenaline for adults ≥20 kg; EpiPen Jr 0.15 mg for children 15–20 kg); review correct technique; prescribe second device (carry 2 at all times)
- Complete ASCIA Action Plan for Anaphylaxis: personalised; identifies trigger; clear instructions for recognition and treatment; completed and signed by GP or specialist; available on ascia.org.au
- Allergen identification: serum-specific IgE testing or skin prick testing — via allergy specialist (ASCIA recommends specialist allergy testing in most cases)
- Allergen avoidance counselling: food labelling, cross-reactive foods, hidden allergens (sesame in bread, lupin in flour, fish sauce in Asian food)
- Referral to allergist/immunologist: for testing, confirmation, venom immunotherapy (for insect venom anaphylaxis — highly effective, reduces risk by >80%)

**Tryptase testing:**
- Serum tryptase: taken 1–4 hours after anaphylaxis onset (peaks at 1–2 hours); correlates with severity; useful in diagnostic uncertainty; elevated >11.4 µg/L supports anaphylaxis diagnosis; some hospitals test routinely; can be arranged via pathology in ED or immediately post-event

## Key Differentials

- Vasovagal syncope: loss of consciousness without urticaria, angioedema, wheeze; triggered by pain, fear, procedure; bradycardia; pallor; no treatment with adrenaline required
- Panic attack: anxiety, hyperventilation, feeling of doom; no objective allergic features; no hypotension or wheeze
- Urticaria alone (no systemic features): not anaphylaxis; treat with antihistamine; does not require adrenaline
- Angioedema from ACE inhibitor: bradykinin-mediated; not IgE; antihistamines and adrenaline may have limited effect; cetirizine + icatibant (bradykinin receptor antagonist) ± tranexamic acid; stop ACE inhibitor; switch to ARB
- Vocal cord dysfunction (VCD): laryngospasm; stridor on inspiration; associated with anxiety; speech pathology; not anaphylaxis
- Hereditary angioedema: C1 esterase inhibitor deficiency; episodes of angioedema without urticaria; triggers include trauma, stress, infection; treat with C1 esterase inhibitor concentrate or icatibant (not adrenaline)

## Management Principles

**Acute anaphylaxis treatment (not primary GP responsibility — ensure patient/carer education):**

1. Call 000 (immediately — do not wait)
2. Adrenaline (epinephrine) IM: FIRST-LINE; administer with EpiPen to lateral mid-thigh (through clothing); dose: 0.3 mg adult (≥20 kg); 0.15 mg child 15–20 kg; 0.01 mg/kg if <15 kg (hospital)
3. Lie flat with legs elevated (if not respiratory distress); if respiratory distress — allow patient to sit; if pregnant — on left side
4. Repeat adrenaline at 5 minutes if no improvement; maximum 2 EpiPen doses before ambulance
5. Second-line: antihistamines (cetirizine or promethazine — NOT instead of adrenaline; adjunct only); corticosteroids (do NOT reliably prevent biphasic reaction despite common use)
6. All patients — observe ≥4–6 hours in hospital for biphasic reaction

**Ongoing GP management:**

Adrenaline autoinjectors:
- EpiPen 0.3 mg: adults and children ≥20 kg; PBS-listed; one device per script; prescribe at least 2 (keep one at school/work, one home)
- EpiPen Jr 0.15 mg: children 15–20 kg; PBS-listed
- Expiry: 12–18 months; check at each review; replace before expiry
- Storage: room temperature; avoid extremes; do not refrigerate
- Training: patient and carers; ASCIA training video; trainer device for practice; review technique at each visit

ASCIA Action Plan:
- Mandatory for every patient with confirmed or suspected anaphylaxis
- Personalised with: patient's name, known triggers, symptoms, when to give EpiPen, when to call 000
- Sign and provide copy to patient, school/childcare, workplace
- Download from ascia.org.au/anaphylaxis-resources

**Venom immunotherapy (VIT) — referral to allergist:**
- Jack jumper ant, bee, wasp venom allergy: highly effective (reduces systemic reaction risk from >50% to <5%); 3–5 year course; subcutaneous injections; specialist administered
- Refer all patients with confirmed insect venom anaphylaxis

**Food immunotherapy:**
- Peanut oral immunotherapy (OIT): ASCIA guidance; specialist-supervised; reduces severe reactions in food allergy
- Not yet mainstream in general practice — specialist referral for interest

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **EpiPen 0.3 mg** (adrenaline autoinjector — PBS restricted: confirmed anaphylaxis or high risk of anaphylaxis; adults ≥20 kg; authority required; prescribe 2 per consultation for PBS subsidy; 12-monthly refill)
- **EpiPen Jr 0.15 mg** (PBS restricted: children 15–20 kg; as per adult criteria)
- **Cetirizine** (Zyrtec, generic — PBS unrestricted: urticaria, allergic reaction; 10 mg daily; adjunct to adrenaline — NOT replacement)
- **Promethazine** (PBS unrestricted — injectable antihistamine; second-line in anaphylaxis only after adrenaline)
- **Prednisolone** (PBS unrestricted — adjunct in anaphylaxis; does not reliably prevent biphasic reaction but commonly prescribed; 50 mg for 3 days)
- **Icatibant** (Firazyr — PBS restricted: hereditary angioedema and ACE inhibitor-induced angioedema; specialist-supervised)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 for all anaphylaxis:**
- Any anaphylaxis — call 000 immediately AFTER administering adrenaline autoinjector; do NOT delay 000 call while waiting to see if adrenaline works
- Biphasic reaction: return of anaphylaxis symptoms 1–72 hours after initial treatment
- Failed to improve after 2 EpiPen doses — IV adrenaline by paramedics or ED

**Non-urgent allergy referral (all confirmed anaphylaxis):**
- Allergist/immunologist: all patients with confirmed anaphylaxis — for specific IgE testing, skin prick testing, trigger confirmation, and VIT discussion
- Paediatric allergist: children with food-triggered anaphylaxis — multidisciplinary team (dietitian, psychologist)

## Telehealth Suitability

Anaphylaxis management is well suited to telehealth for follow-up and action plan review.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Post-anaphylaxis follow-up review (typically 1 week after ED discharge)
- EpiPen prescribing renewal (confirmed diagnosis)
- ASCIA Action Plan review and update
- Allergen avoidance counselling
- Allergy referral coordination

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- First presentation with suspected anaphylaxis — acute management (000 + ED)
- EpiPen technique training — ideally done in person at least once; video review supplementary
- Allergy testing (skin prick test, intradermal) — specialist clinic
