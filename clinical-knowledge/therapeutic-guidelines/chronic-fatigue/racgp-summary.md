## Chronic Fatigue Assessment (RACGP)

Myalgic encephalomyelitis / chronic fatigue syndrome (ME/CFS) is a complex, serious, and disabling multi-system disease characterised by post-exertional malaise (PEM), profound fatigue, cognitive impairment, sleep dysfunction, and orthostatic intolerance. It is not simply "tiredness" — it is a neurological condition with significant objective impairment. Approximately 250,000 Australians are affected. ME/CFS is frequently misunderstood and underdiagnosed; average diagnostic delay is 5 years. The term "chronic fatigue" also encompasses secondary fatigue from identifiable treatable causes — GPs must first systematically exclude these. The NICE 2021 guideline (updated based on evidence) recommends against graded exercise therapy (GET) and cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) as primary treatments for ME/CFS; this represents a major change from prior guidelines. Management focuses on energy management (pacing), symptom control, and specialist referral for complex presentations. Post-COVID syndrome (Long COVID) shares significant overlap with ME/CFS. Telehealth is well-suited to ME/CFS management — minimises exertion for patients.

## Clinical Presentation

**ME/CFS diagnostic criteria (Institute of Medicine 2015 — all 3 required):**

1. Substantial reduction or impairment in ability to engage in pre-illness level of activities (lasting >6 months) accompanied by fatigue
2. Post-exertional malaise (PEM): worsening of all symptoms after physical, cognitive, emotional, or orthostatic exertion — hallmark symptom
   - PEM may be delayed by 12–48 hours after exertion
   - Recovery takes days to weeks
   - Distinguishes ME/CFS from other fatigue conditions
3. Unrefreshing sleep: sleep does not restore; sleeping any amount of time leaves patient still exhausted

PLUS one of:
- Cognitive impairment ("brain fog"): difficulty thinking, concentrating, remembering; word-finding difficulties; slow processing
- Orthostatic intolerance: symptoms worsen on standing/sitting upright (palpitations, lightheadedness, pre-syncope); postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome (POTS) common in ME/CFS

**Other common symptoms:**
- Pain: widespread musculoskeletal pain, headaches, myalgia, arthralgia without arthritis
- Flu-like symptoms: sore throat, tender lymph nodes (frequently)
- Sensory sensitivities: light, sound, smell sensitivity
- Temperature dysregulation: intolerance to heat and cold
- GI symptoms: nausea, IBS-like symptoms, bloating

**ME/CFS severity:**
- Mild: reduced activity 50% from pre-illness; still mobile and may work/study with significant effort
- Moderate: mainly housebound; multiple symptoms; significant functional impairment
- Severe: bedbound much of the time; severe cognitive impairment; may not tolerate light, sound, visitors
- Very severe: fully bedbound; requiring assistance with basic care; may be tube-fed

**Post-COVID ME/CFS:**
- Up to 2–10% of COVID-19 survivors develop ME/CFS-like syndrome (Long COVID)
- PEM is a distinguishing feature from other Long COVID presentations
- Manage with same pacing principles as ME/CFS

## Assessment Approach

**Systematic exclusion of secondary causes of fatigue (essential before ME/CFS diagnosis):**

Investigations to perform in all patients with significant fatigue (>6 weeks duration):
- FBC: anaemia, leucopenia/leucocytosis (infection, haematological malignancy)
- ESR, CRP: chronic inflammatory or autoimmune disease
- Thyroid function (TSH): hypothyroidism — very common treatable cause
- Fasting glucose, HbA1c: diabetes
- LFTs: hepatitis, liver disease
- eGFR, creatinine: renal disease
- Vitamin B12, folate: deficiency causes fatigue and cognitive impairment
- Vitamin D (25-OH): severe deficiency causes fatigue and myalgia
- Iron studies (ferritin, iron, TIBC): iron deficiency without anaemia causes fatigue
- Calcium: hypercalcaemia (malignancy, hyperparathyroidism)
- Coeliac serology (anti-tTG IgA): coeliac disease causes fatigue, malabsorption
- EBV (Epstein-Barr) serology: acute infectious mononucleosis — may trigger ME/CFS
- Cortisol/short Synacthen test: if adrenal insufficiency suspected
- ANA, RF: if systemic autoimmune disease suspected (SLE, rheumatoid arthritis)
- HIV serology: if risk factors

**Psychiatric/psychological assessment:**
- PHQ-9 (depression): depression causes fatigue; treat if present; but depression does NOT explain PEM — if PEM present, consider concurrent ME/CFS
- GAD-7 (anxiety): similar considerations
- Sleep disorders: sleep apnoea assessment (Epworth Sleepiness Scale); consider polysomnography

**Orthostatic testing:**
- Lying and standing BP and HR: after 3 minutes lying, take measurements; then stand and repeat at 2, 5, 10 minutes
- POTS: HR increase ≥30 bpm on standing without hypotension — common in ME/CFS; refer for tilt table test if suspected
- Neurally mediated hypotension: BP drop on prolonged standing

**Referral for diagnosis:**
- ME/CFS diagnosis is clinical; no single diagnostic test exists
- Complex patients: refer to ME/CFS specialist or clinic (Emerge Australia maintains national directory)

## Key Differentials

- Depression: fatigue, low mood, anhedonia; no PEM; PHQ-9; treat depression; reassess; PEM distinguishes ME/CFS
- Hypothyroidism: fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance; TSH elevated; treat with thyroxine
- Sleep apnoea: unrefreshing sleep, daytime hypersomnolence, snoring; Epworth Sleepiness Scale; polysomnography
- Anaemia (iron deficiency, B12 deficiency): FBC, iron studies; treat underlying cause
- Coeliac disease: fatigue, GI symptoms, malabsorption; anti-tTG IgA; gluten-free diet
- Chronic infection: hepatitis B/C, HIV, Lyme serology (if overseas exposure); treat if found
- Connective tissue disease (SLE): ANA, complement; multisystem; fatigue + joint pain + rash
- Malignancy: unexplained weight loss, night sweats, lymphadenopathy; BPAC investigation protocol
- Fibromyalgia: significant overlap with ME/CFS; widespread pain + fatigue; may coexist; see `fibromyalgia` entry

## Management Principles

**Pacing and energy management (cornerstone of ME/CFS management):**
- Energy envelope theory: remain within available energy limit (envelope); do not push through fatigue — this causes PEM and relapse
- Heart rate monitoring: stay below anaerobic threshold (~60% age-predicted maximum HR) — provides objective pacing guide
- Occupational therapist referral: activity analysis; workplace and home modifications; aids and equipment
- Avoid boom-bust cycle: identifying "good days" and overdoing it leads to PEM crash

**DO NOT recommend:**
- Graded exercise therapy (GET): CONTRAINDICATED in ME/CFS per NICE 2021; increases PEM; worsens outcomes; formerly recommended, now strongly advised against
- CBT as primary treatment aiming to correct "unhelpful illness beliefs": no longer recommended; ME/CFS is not a psychosomatic illness
- CBT for coping and adjustment (not cure): may be used to address secondary depression/anxiety from living with ME/CFS — different goal

**Sleep management:**
- Sleep hygiene education: consistent sleep/wake times, dark cool room, reduce blue light
- Melatonin: 0.5–2 mg nocte; may help circadian dysregulation; PBS-listed for >55 years; off-label for younger
- Avoid sedating antihistamines and benzodiazepines: impair sleep architecture; worsen cognitive symptoms
- Low-dose tricyclic (amitriptyline 10–25 mg nocte): may improve pain and sleep in low doses; assess risk-benefit

**Pain management:**
- Paracetamol: first-line for musculoskeletal pain
- Low-dose amitriptyline (10–25 mg nocte): neuropathic and widespread pain; also aids sleep
- Low-dose naltrexone (LDN): 1.5–4.5 mg daily; emerging evidence for ME/CFS pain and fatigue; not PBS-listed; can be compounded; discussed with patients seeking options
- Avoid strong opioids: not recommended for ME/CFS pain; worsen fatigue and cognitive impairment

**Orthostatic intolerance (POTS) management:**
- Increased salt and fluid intake: 3–5 g sodium/day + 2–3 L fluid/day — first-line for POTS
- Compression stockings (waist-high grade 2): increases venous return
- Fludrocortisone 0.1 mg daily: volume expansion; specialist initiation
- Beta-blockers (propranolol, bisoprolol): low-dose for POTS-related tachycardia — specialist or experienced GP

**Cognitive and psychological support:**
- Occupational therapy: cognitive pacing strategies; aids for brain fog (notebooks, phone reminders)
- Psychology: adjustment, coping, secondary depression/anxiety (not aimed at curing ME/CFS)
- Peer support: Emerge Australia support groups; online communities

**Multidisciplinary care:**
- CDM plan (MBS 721/723): GP Management Plan allows up to 5 allied health sessions per year — OT, psychologist, dietitian, physiotherapist (gentle; pacing-based only)
- Social work: Centrelink DSP applications if severe; NDIS if applicable

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Amitriptyline** (PBS unrestricted: depression; off-label for pain and sleep in ME/CFS; 10–25 mg nocte; caution in elderly — falls risk)
- **Melatonin (Circadin 2 mg MR)** (PBS restricted: insomnia in ≥55 years; off-label for younger patients; safe; helps circadian rhythm)
- **Fludrocortisone 0.1 mg** (PBS restricted: adrenal insufficiency; off-label for orthostatic intolerance/POTS; increases salt/fluid retention; specialist initiation)
- **Duloxetine or pregabalin**: if neuropathic pain component — see `peripheral-neuropathy` entry for dosing
- **Note:** Low-dose naltrexone (LDN) is not PBS-listed for ME/CFS; can be prescribed as compounded preparation; discuss with patient regarding evidence base (limited but growing); available at compounding pharmacies
- **Note:** Avoid graded exercise programs framed as treatment for ME/CFS — confirm with physiotherapist that management is pacing-based, not progressive exertion-based

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent review:**
- Rapid functional decline: rule out new medical comorbidity (thyroid, Addison's, malignancy)
- Severe malnutrition or inability to eat/drink: hospital assessment; nutritional support
- Suicidal ideation: common in severe ME/CFS (chronic illness, isolation); same-day psychiatric review
- Severe resting symptoms: may indicate severe ME/CFS requiring specialist coordination

**Non-urgent referral:**
- ME/CFS specialist: confirm diagnosis; manage complex presentations; POTS, severe cases; Emerge Australia maintains directory of ME/CFS-knowledgeable clinicians
- Cardiologist or autonomic neurologist: for confirmed POTS — tilt table test, management
- Immunologist: if immune dysfunction suspected (recurrent infections, low immunoglobulins)
- Occupational therapist: activity pacing, workplace accommodation
- Social worker: Centrelink DSP, NDIS

## Telehealth Suitability

ME/CFS management is highly suitable for telehealth — reduces exertion burden significantly.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Symptom monitoring and pacing review
- Medication review and titration
- Orthostatic intolerance management review
- Depression and anxiety monitoring (PHQ-9, GAD-7)
- Referral coordination
- CDM plan review (MBS 721)
- Patient education, pacing strategies, activity diary review

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial comprehensive assessment and physical examination
- Orthostatic BP and HR measurement (lying and standing)
- Blood tests — pathology arranged
- Spirometry, sleep study if indicated
- Review of new or changing symptoms requiring examination
