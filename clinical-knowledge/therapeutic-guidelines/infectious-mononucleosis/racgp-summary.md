## Infectious Mononucleosis Assessment (RACGP)

Infectious mononucleosis (IM — "mono" or "glandular fever") is caused by primary Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) infection in >90% of cases, with Cytomegalovirus (CMV) and other viruses causing an indistinguishable syndrome in the remainder. EBV infects over 90% of adults worldwide, typically in childhood in developing countries (usually subclinical) or in adolescence and young adulthood in higher-income countries (more likely symptomatic). In Australia, IM predominantly affects teenagers and young adults aged 15–25. The illness is characterised by the classic triad of fever, exudative pharyngitis, and lymphadenopathy. Most cases are self-limiting over 2–4 weeks, though fatigue may persist for months. The GP plays a central role in: diagnosis, differentiating from bacterial tonsillitis, managing complications (splenomegaly, airway obstruction, hepatitis), advising on activity restriction (splenomegaly and contact sport), and supporting return to school or work.

## Clinical Presentation

**Classic clinical triad:**
- Fever: 38–40°C; typically high-grade; may last 1–2 weeks
- Exudative pharyngitis/tonsillitis: severe sore throat; white-grey tonsillar exudate; markedly enlarged tonsils (may cause airway compromise in severe cases)
- Lymphadenopathy: bilateral posterior cervical lymphadenopathy (distinctive feature — anterior cervical is common in any tonsillitis; posterior = EBV); may include axillary and inguinal

**Additional features:**
- Splenomegaly: present in ~50% by week 2; risk of spontaneous rupture (rare but life-threatening) with trauma or vigorous contact sport
- Hepatomegaly and hepatitis: mild LFT elevation in ~80%; jaundice in 5–10%; hepatitis typically self-limiting
- Rash (ampicillin/amoxicillin rash): 90–100% of patients given amoxicillin or ampicillin for suspected tonsillitis develop a widespread maculopapular rash — this is NOT penicillin allergy but a hypersensitivity reaction specific to this antibiotic class in EBV infection; must be distinguished from true penicillin allergy (patients can usually receive penicillin again after EBV infection resolves)
- Periorbital oedema: early morning puffiness around eyes — characteristic
- Palatal petechiae: at junction of hard and soft palate — suggestive
- Fatigue: profound; may persist for weeks to months
- Malaise, myalgia, headache

**Complications:**
- Splenic rupture: rare (<0.5%); risk highest in week 2–4; presents as sudden severe left upper quadrant or left shoulder tip pain; haemodynamic instability; 000 emergency
- Airway obstruction: severely enlarged tonsils causing stridor, drooling, inability to swallow; hospital emergency; IV dexamethasone; ENT
- Haematological: thrombocytopenia (common, usually mild and self-limiting); severe haemolytic anaemia (rare); neutropenia
- Neurological: meningitis, encephalitis, Guillain-Barré syndrome — rare
- Chronic EBV: prolonged fatigue >6 months; consider overlap with chronic fatigue syndrome (ME-CFS)

## Assessment Approach

**Diagnosis:**
- Clinical diagnosis in typical presentation (adolescent/young adult, posterior cervical adenopathy, exudative pharyngitis, fever, fatigue, splenomegaly) — high clinical probability
- FBC: lymphocytosis with atypical lymphocytes (activated T-cells) — characteristic; WBC typically elevated (10–20 × 10⁹/L); atypical lymphocytes on film: ≥10% = significant finding
- Heterophile antibody test (Monospot test): rapid in-clinic test; sensitivity 85–90%; specificity >95% in adolescents/adults; may be negative in first week and in children <4 years (poorer sensitivity in young children)
- EBV-specific serology: VCA IgM (acute infection), VCA IgG (past infection), EBNA IgG (develops 4–8 weeks after infection — absence confirms recent primary infection); EA (early antigen): active infection
- CMV serology: if EBV serology negative but clinical picture consistent
- LFTs: hepatitis assessment; ALT/AST elevation common and expected
- FBC + film: if initial Monospot negative but clinical suspicion high; also monitors for cytopenias
- Streptococcal throat swab / rapid antigen test: to exclude co-existent group A strep infection (occurs in ~30%)

**Splenomegaly assessment:**
- Clinical examination: enlarged spleen palpable below left costal margin in ~50% during second week
- Ultrasound: if splenomegaly clinically suspected or before return to contact sport (definitive assessment of size)
- Sports clearance: do NOT return to contact sport until splenomegaly resolved on ultrasound (typically 3–4 weeks minimum; may need 4–6 weeks)

## Key Differentials

- Group A Streptococcal (GAS) tonsillitis: no splenomegaly; no posterior cervical adenopathy; anterior cervical nodes; Monospot negative; positive rapid antigen test; treat with penicillin V
- Cytomegalovirus mononucleosis: clinically identical to EBV; heterophile antibody negative; CMV-specific serology positive; self-limiting; no specific treatment
- HIV primary infection (acute seroconversion): fever, rash, lymphadenopathy; HIV antigen/antibody test (4th generation) is critical — EBV serology negative; see `hiv-monitoring` entry
- Acute toxoplasma: lymphadenopathy; no pharyngitis; Toxoplasma IgM serology
- Viral hepatitis: jaundice + elevated LFTs; HAV IgM, HBsAg, HCV RNA; no pharyngitis
- Lymphoma: persistent painless lymphadenopathy; night sweats; weight loss; heterophile negative; CT and biopsy; haematology urgently
- Diphtheria: rare in Australia (vaccination); grey pseudomembrane; systemic toxicity; notifiable

## Management Principles

**Supportive management (no antiviral treatment — antivirals not effective for IM):**
- Rest: adequate rest in acute phase; fatigue is the limiting factor; school/work absence as needed
- Analgesia: paracetamol + ibuprofen for fever and throat pain
- Fluids: adequate hydration; soft diet if severe pharyngitis
- Duration: acute illness typically 2–4 weeks; fatigue may persist 4–12 weeks; return to normal activity as tolerated

**Corticosteroids (select cases only):**
- Indications: airway compromise from tonsillar hypertrophy; severe thrombocytopenia or haemolytic anaemia; neurological complications
- Dexamethasone IV (hospital) or prednisolone oral (1 mg/kg/day × 5–7 days, then taper)
- NOT routinely recommended for uncomplicated IM: no benefit on fatigue duration; potential adverse effects

**AVOID ampicillin and amoxicillin:**
- Causes 90–100% ampicillin rash in EBV infection
- If streptococcal co-infection confirmed: treat with phenoxymethylpenicillin (penicillin V) or cefalexin (if penicillin allergy)

**Activity restriction:**
- Contact sports and heavy physical activity: avoid for minimum 3–4 weeks from illness onset; until splenomegaly clinically resolved AND (for high-impact sport) ultrasound clearance
- Gentle activity acceptable once acute phase resolves; encourage gradual return
- Driving: advise caution if fatigue severe; not a formal regulatory restriction

**School/university/work:**
- Not contagious once afebrile and feeling well; no specific exclusion period
- May need medical certificate for extended absence (3–6 weeks not uncommon)

**Post-IM fatigue:**
- Common and expected: 10–15% have significant fatigue at 6 months
- Overlap with chronic fatigue syndrome/ME-CFS: if fatigue persists >3 months with post-exertional malaise — see `chronic-fatigue` entry
- Pacing is important: excessive activity can prolong fatigue
- Monitor mood: depression and anxiety common in prolonged illness; PHQ-9; GP Mental Health Care Plan

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Paracetamol** (PBS unrestricted: fever and pain; 1 g QID; safe in mild hepatitis from EBV)
- **Ibuprofen** (PBS unrestricted: fever and pain; 400 mg TDS with food; avoid if significant thrombocytopenia or severe hepatitis)
- **Prednisolone** (PBS unrestricted: acute severe complications only; 1 mg/kg/day; short course 5–7 days)
- **Note:** Do NOT prescribe amoxicillin or ampicillin for pharyngitis in suspected IM — causes severe rash; if streptococcal co-infection: phenoxymethylpenicillin (penicillin V) 500 mg QID × 10 days; or cefalexin if penicillin allergy
- **Note:** There is no PBS-listed antiviral for EBV IM; aciclovir/valaciclovir are not effective for IM despite being EBV antivirals in vitro

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Splenic rupture: sudden severe left upper quadrant pain, shoulder tip pain, haemodynamic instability — surgical emergency
- Airway compromise: stridor, drooling, inability to swallow saliva — IV dexamethasone + ENT emergency
- Severe thrombocytopenia with bleeding

**Urgent hospital assessment:**
- Severe haemolytic anaemia (falling haemoglobin, jaundice, haemoglobinuria)
- Neurological complications (meningitis, encephalitis, Guillain-Barré)
- Inability to maintain hydration orally — IV fluids in hospital
- Clinically severe airway narrowing (not requiring 000 but requiring ED)

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Haematology: unexplained persistent cytopenias after 4 weeks; lymphoma exclusion
- ENT: consideration of tonsillectomy for recurrent or chronic tonsillitis post-IM
- Psychologist: depression or anxiety from prolonged post-IM illness (GP Mental Health Care Plan MBS 2713)

## Telehealth Suitability

Infectious mononucleosis management is partially suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Follow-up after acute diagnosis: fatigue monitoring, activity restriction counselling, return-to-sport planning
- LFT and FBC result review
- Prolonged post-IM fatigue management and counselling
- Mental health review
- Referral coordination

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Acute presentation: physical examination (splenomegaly palpation, lymphadenopathy, tonsillar examination)
- Blood tests: FBC, Monospot, EBV serology, LFTs
- Throat swab for GAS
- Ultrasound for splenomegaly clearance — radiology
