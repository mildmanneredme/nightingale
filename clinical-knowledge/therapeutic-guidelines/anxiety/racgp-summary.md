## Anxiety Disorders Assessment (RACGP)

Anxiety disorders are the most common mental health conditions in Australia, affecting approximately 1 in 4 Australians in their lifetime. They encompass generalised anxiety disorder (GAD), panic disorder, social anxiety disorder (SAD), specific phobias, and health anxiety. Anxiety disorders cause significant disability and frequently coexist with depression. Effective management in primary care includes psychological therapies (first-line), pharmacotherapy, and lifestyle interventions. Telehealth is highly suitable for anxiety assessment and ongoing management. Note: this condition file supplements the broader `mental-health` knowledge base entry and focuses specifically on anxiety disorder presentations distinct from depression.

## Clinical Presentation

**Generalised anxiety disorder (GAD):** Excessive, uncontrollable worry about multiple everyday matters (work, health, finances, family) on more days than not for ≥6 months, associated with ≥3 of: restlessness, fatigue, difficulty concentrating, irritability, muscle tension, sleep disturbance. GAD-7 ≥10 = moderate-to-severe.

**Panic disorder:** Recurrent unexpected panic attacks (discrete episodes of intense fear with ≥4 somatic or cognitive symptoms: palpitations, sweating, trembling, dyspnoea, chest pain, dizziness, derealisation, fear of dying, paraesthesiae), followed by ≥1 month of persistent concern about further attacks or behavioural change (avoidance).

**Social anxiety disorder (SAD):** Marked fear of social situations where scrutiny by others may occur; fear is out of proportion to actual threat; leads to avoidance; impacts occupational and social functioning.

**Health anxiety (illness anxiety / somatic symptom disorder):** Excessive preoccupation with having or developing a serious illness despite reassurance; drives frequent medical consultations, investigations, and avoidance of health-related stimuli.

Physical symptoms of anxiety: palpitations, chest tightness, dyspnoea, dizziness, GI upset, headache — may lead to extensive medical workup before anxiety diagnosis is considered.

## Assessment Approach

Validated screening tools:
- GAD-7 (Generalised Anxiety Disorder 7-item scale): ≥5 mild, ≥10 moderate, ≥15 severe anxiety
- PHQ-4 (combined anxiety + depression 4-item screen) — useful for rapid screening

Mental health assessment: explore symptom onset, triggers, duration, functional impact (work, relationships, daily activities), avoidance behaviours, physical symptoms, substance use, family history, childhood trauma, and suicidal ideation (assess at every consultation — anxiety independently increases suicide risk).

Medical workup to exclude organic cause (as clinically indicated):
- Hyperthyroidism — TSH (palpitations, tremor, weight loss)
- Arrhythmia — ECG if palpitations with cardiac features
- Hypoglycaemia — particularly in diabetic patients
- Phaeochromocytoma — rare; consider if episodic hypertension, headache, diaphoresis
- Substance use/withdrawal — alcohol, caffeine, stimulants, cannabis (cannabis can precipitate and worsen anxiety)
- Medications causing anxiety: corticosteroids, theophylline, pseudoephedrine, SSRIs at initiation (transient activation)

## Key Differentials

- Depression — frequently coexists with anxiety; PHQ-9 to assess; treat both if present
- PTSD — anxiety symptoms following trauma; intrusive memories, hypervigilance, avoidance
- OCD — intrusive thoughts with compulsive rituals; distinguish from GAD worry
- Medical causes of anxiety (hyperthyroidism, cardiac arrhythmia, substance use — see above)
- Adjustment disorder — anxiety in response to identifiable stressor within 3 months; symptoms resolve within 6 months of stressor ending

## Management Principles

**First-line: Psychological therapies**

Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT): most evidence-based psychological treatment for anxiety disorders. Reduces symptoms by identifying and restructuring maladaptive thought patterns and behaviours. Delivered individually or in group format; 6–12 sessions typically. Access via GP mental health care plan (up to 10 sessions per year funded under Medicare; can be extended for complex cases).

Other evidence-based approaches: Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT), Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT), Applied Relaxation, Exposure therapy (for phobias and panic).

Digital mental health: online CBT programs (e.g., This Way Up, MindSpot, Anxiety Australia online programs) are effective for mild-moderate anxiety; accessible and reduce wait times.

**Lifestyle modifications:**
- Regular aerobic exercise (150 min/week) has demonstrated anxiolytic effects comparable to some medications
- Caffeine reduction — significant anxiogenic effect at high doses
- Alcohol reduction — commonly used as anxiolytic; worsens anxiety and depression long-term; disrupts sleep
- Sleep hygiene — anxiety and sleep disturbance are bidirectional; treat insomnia concurrently
- Mindfulness practice — evidence for GAD; accessible via apps (Headspace, Calm, Smiling Mind)

**Pharmacotherapy (adjunct or when psychological therapy not available/declined):**

First-line: SSRIs or SNRIs — effective for all anxiety disorders; full effect takes 4–6 weeks; start at low dose to minimise initial activation (transient worsening of anxiety common in first 1–2 weeks):
- Escitalopram 5–20 mg daily — preferred SSRI (well tolerated, fewer drug interactions)
- Sertraline 25–200 mg daily
- Venlafaxine XR 37.5–225 mg daily (SNRI — also effective for GAD and SAD)
- Duloxetine 30–120 mg daily

Duration: continue for at least 12 months after symptom remission before tapering (higher relapse risk with shorter courses); taper slowly over months.

Short-term anxiolytics (limited use):
- Benzodiazepines: not recommended for long-term management of anxiety; risk of dependence, cognitive impairment, falls; short course (1–2 weeks maximum) may bridge SSRI initiation for severe distress
- Pregabalin 75–300 mg daily (PBS-restricted for GAD): effective for GAD; less dependence risk than benzodiazepines
- Buspirone: modest evidence; non-sedating; useful where benzodiazepine dependence is a concern

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Escitalopram** (PBS restricted: major depressive disorder; off-label for GAD/other anxiety disorders — prescribe with awareness of PBS status)
- **Sertraline** (PBS unrestricted as antidepressant; also indicated for panic disorder and social anxiety disorder under PBS restricted benefit)
- **Venlafaxine XR** (PBS restricted: generalised anxiety disorder, major depressive disorder, social anxiety disorder)
- **Pregabalin** (PBS restricted: generalised anxiety disorder — 75–300 mg/day; also neuropathic pain)
- **Diazepam, oxazepam** (PBS restricted — Schedule 8 in some states; short-term anxiety; dependence risk — limit to 2–4 weeks maximum; taper gradually)
- **Propranolol** (PBS unrestricted — off-label for situational/performance anxiety; not for generalised anxiety)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / mental health emergency:**
- Active suicidal ideation with intent or plan — anxiety is an independent risk factor for suicide; always assess
- Acute psychiatric emergency: agitation, psychosis, severe self-harm, inability to maintain safety

**Urgent in-person or same-day mental health review:**
- Severe anxiety with inability to function (unable to leave home, care for self, eat)
- Comorbid severe depression with suicidal ideation
- Suspected substance intoxication or withdrawal precipitating anxiety crisis

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Psychologist: CBT delivery; 10-session GP mental health care plan (Medicare rebatable); up to 20 sessions for complex cases under additional criteria
- Psychiatrist: complex cases, comorbid personality disorder, refractory anxiety not responding to 2+ SSRI/SNRI trials, bipolar disorder comorbidity, need for specialist pharmacotherapy
- Headspace (for youth 12–25 years): free mental health support service

## Telehealth Suitability

Anxiety disorders are highly suitable for telehealth assessment and management.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- GAD-7 and PHQ-9 screening and scoring
- Mental health assessment and diagnosis
- Mental health care plan creation and GP management plan for psychology referral
- SSRI/SNRI initiation, dose titration, and review
- Psychoeducation about anxiety physiology and CBT principles
- Digital therapy referral (This Way Up, MindSpot)
- Safety planning and suicide risk assessment

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Medical investigations to exclude organic cause (ECG, bloods)
- High-risk presentations where clinical safety assessment requires in-person engagement
- Psychological therapy delivery (usually with psychologist, not GP, via telehealth or in-person)
