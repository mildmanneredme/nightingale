## Heart Failure Assessment (RACGP)

Heart failure (HF) is a clinical syndrome characterised by the heart's inability to meet the body's metabolic demands, or only at the expense of elevated filling pressures. It affects approximately 1–2% of Australians and is associated with significant morbidity and mortality. The most common causes are ischaemic heart disease, hypertension, and cardiomyopathy. HF with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF, LVEF <40%) has the most evidence-based pharmacotherapy. Stable community HF management in GP focuses on medication optimisation, monitoring, and early identification of decompensation. Telehealth is highly suitable for symptom monitoring, weight tracking, and medication review in stable patients.

## Clinical Presentation

Classic symptoms: dyspnoea on exertion, orthopnoea (dyspnoea lying flat), paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea, fatigue, reduced exercise tolerance, ankle oedema.

Physical signs (in-person examination): bilateral basal crepitations, raised JVP, peripheral oedema, third heart sound (S3 gallop), displaced apex beat, ascites in severe cases.

New York Heart Association (NYHA) Functional Classification:
- Class I: no symptoms with ordinary activity
- Class II: slight limitation with ordinary activity; comfortable at rest
- Class III: marked limitation with less than ordinary activity; comfortable at rest
- Class IV: symptoms at rest; any activity causes discomfort

HFrEF vs HFpEF: Heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF, LVEF ≥50%) — same symptoms; management differs (less evidence for mortality benefit from RAAS agents; SGLT2 inhibitors now PBS-listed for HFpEF).

Decompensation triggers: poor medication adherence, dietary sodium/fluid excess, arrhythmias (especially new AF), infection (pneumonia), NSAIDs, myocardial ischaemia.

## Assessment Approach

At every stable HF review:
- Symptom assessment: NYHA class, dyspnoea trajectory, exercise tolerance
- Daily weight monitoring: sudden weight gain >2 kg in 2 days suggests fluid retention — action plan trigger
- Fluid balance: ankle oedema, orthopnoea, breathlessness at rest
- BP and heart rate
- Medication adherence: ACE-I/ARB, beta-blocker, MRA, SGLT2i, diuretic
- Medication side effects: hypotension, dizziness, renal impairment, hyperkalaemia
- Renal function and electrolytes: 1–2 weekly when titrating RAAS agents; then 3–6 monthly when stable
- BNP or NT-proBNP: useful to assess decompensation; not required at every stable review
- Implantable device check: ICD or CRT device follow-up (specialist-managed)

Echocardiogram: baseline at diagnosis; reassess at 3–6 months after optimal medical therapy (OMT) to reassess LVEF (some patients improve with OMT).

## Key Differentials

- Pulmonary disease (COPD, severe asthma) — dyspnoea but different chest findings; spirometry, BNP/NT-proBNP helps differentiate
- Pulmonary embolism — acute dyspnoea, pleuritic chest pain, haemoptysis, tachycardia; D-dimer; CT-PA
- Pleural effusion (malignant, parapneumonic) — unilateral; dullness to percussion
- Bilateral leg oedema without cardiac cause — venous insufficiency, lymphoedema, hypoalbuminaemia (nephrotic syndrome, cirrhosis), medications (CCBs, steroids)
- Anaemia — fatigue, dyspnoea on exertion; no pulmonary oedema; FBC

## Management Principles

**HFrEF (LVEF <40%) — four pillars of therapy (mortality-modifying):**

1. **ACE inhibitor or ARB** (if ACE-I not tolerated): start low, titrate to maximum tolerated dose. Monitor BP, renal function, potassium.
   - ARNi (sacubitril/valsartan — Entresto): superior to ACE-I alone in reducing HF mortality; PBS-listed for stable HFrEF; initiate after ACE-I and beta-blocker optimisation.

2. **Beta-blocker**: carvedilol, bisoprolol, metoprolol succinate — initiate when stable (not acutely decompensated); start low and titrate up.

3. **Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (MRA)**: spironolactone or eplerenone — for NYHA II–IV; monitor potassium and renal function closely (risk of hyperkalaemia with RAAS agents).

4. **SGLT2 inhibitor**: empagliflozin or dapagliflozin — reduce HF hospitalisation and cardiovascular death; PBS-listed for HFrEF; renal protective benefits; can be added to above agents.

**Loop diuretics (frusemide):** Symptom control for fluid overload; titrate to achieve euvolaemia (target weight) but not for mortality benefit. Advise patients on flexible diuretic dosing (self-management action plan).

**Device therapy** (specialist decision): CRT (cardiac resynchronisation therapy) if LBBB and LVEF ≤35%; ICD for primary prevention of sudden cardiac death if LVEF ≤35% despite OMT.

**HFpEF (LVEF ≥50%):** Primarily symptom management — diuretics for fluid overload. SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin) now have PBS listing for HFpEF based on trial evidence. Control of precipitants: hypertension, AF, ischaemia, diabetes, obesity.

**Self-management education:** Daily weight monitoring with action plan (weight gain >2 kg in 2 days → increase diuretic and contact GP); sodium restriction (<2 g/day); fluid restriction in severe HF; daily activity as tolerated (cardiac rehabilitation); influenza and pneumococcal vaccination.

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Ramipril, perindopril** (ACE inhibitors — PBS unrestricted for HF)
- **Candesartan, valsartan** (ARBs — PBS restricted for HFrEF when ACE-I intolerant)
- **Sacubitril/valsartan** (Entresto — PBS restricted: stable HFrEF, LVEF ≤40%, NYHA II–III, on ACE-I or ARB, NT-proBNP ≥600 pg/mL)
- **Carvedilol, bisoprolol, metoprolol succinate** (PBS unrestricted for HF)
- **Spironolactone** (PBS unrestricted — diuretic and MRA for HF; monitor K⁺ and eGFR)
- **Eplerenone** (PBS restricted: post-MI HFrEF)
- **Empagliflozin** (Jardiance — PBS restricted: symptomatic HFrEF with LVEF <40%, or HFpEF with LVEF ≥40%)
- **Dapagliflozin** (Forxiga — PBS restricted: HFrEF; also HFpEF)
- **Frusemide** (PBS unrestricted — loop diuretic for fluid overload)
- **Digoxin** (PBS restricted — adjunct rate control in HF with AF; not for mortality benefit in sinus rhythm)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Acute decompensated HF: sudden severe dyspnoea at rest, SpO₂ <90%, inability to lie flat, acute pulmonary oedema
- Acute chest pain suggesting new MI (precipitant of acute decompensation)
- Syncope or presyncope in HF patient — may indicate arrhythmia (VT) or haemodynamic compromise
- Signs of cardiogenic shock: hypotension, cold peripheries, confusion, oliguria

**Urgent in-person GP review (same day):**
- Weight gain >2 kg over 2 days not responding to diuretic up-titration
- Worsening dyspnoea, orthopnoea, or bilateral oedema
- Significant symptomatic hypotension after medication titration
- Serum potassium >5.5 mmol/L or eGFR fall >30% from baseline on RAAS agents

**Non-urgent cardiology referral:**
- New HF diagnosis — specialist echocardiogram, optimisation of OMT
- Consideration of CRT, ICD, or advanced therapies
- Refractory HF symptoms despite optimal medical therapy
- Heart failure with preserved ejection fraction — diagnostic confirmation and management optimisation

## Telehealth Suitability

Stable, well-compensated chronic HF is highly suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Symptom monitoring: NYHA class, dyspnoea, oedema — patient reports weight, symptoms
- Weight trend review (patient shares home weight diary)
- Medication adherence review and dose adjustment guidance
- Renal function and electrolyte result review and dose adjustments
- Self-management action plan education and reinforcement
- Vaccination review (influenza, pneumococcal)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Auscultation for crackles or third heart sound
- JVP assessment and abdominal examination for hepatomegaly/ascites
- Peripheral oedema grading
- Any episode of decompensation — urgent in-person or ED
