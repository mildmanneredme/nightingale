## Domestic Violence Assessment (RACGP)

Family violence (also called domestic violence, intimate partner violence, or coercive control) is a pattern of behaviour used by one person to control, dominate, or harm another in a family or intimate relationship. It encompasses physical, sexual, emotional/psychological, financial, and technology-facilitated abuse. One in six women and one in sixteen men in Australia have experienced physical or sexual intimate partner violence since the age of 15. Family violence is the leading cause of preventable death, disability, and illness in women aged 18–44 in Australia. GPs have a critical role in: universal screening using the Inquiry approach, responding supportively, safety planning, documentation, mandatory reporting (children at risk), and referral to specialist family violence services. The RACGP "White Book" (5th edition, 2020) is the authoritative Australian GP resource. All patients can be seen safely via telehealth with appropriate modifications; in-person is preferred when possible.

## Clinical Presentation

**Types of family violence:**
- Physical abuse: hitting, slapping, choking, kicking, restraining, hair-pulling; injuries in unusual locations (central body, face, neck); multiple injuries at different stages of healing
- Sexual violence: sexual assault, coercion, reproductive control (preventing contraception or forcing pregnancy)
- Emotional and psychological abuse: controlling behaviour; threats; humiliation; isolation from family and friends; monitoring phone/location; gaslighting
- Coercive control: pattern of ongoing control that strips autonomy; may not involve physical violence but is highly dangerous and recognised in law (criminal offence in QLD, NSW, VIC)
- Financial abuse: controlling access to money; preventing employment; running up debt in partner's name
- Technology-facilitated abuse: monitoring via apps, cameras; sharing intimate images without consent; online harassment
- Stalking: criminal offence in all states; post-separation risk is highest

**Indicators of family violence (clinical red flags — not diagnostic but warrant inquiry):**
- Injuries inconsistent with explanation
- Delay in seeking treatment for injuries
- Partner always present, answers for patient, refuses to leave consultation room
- Depression, anxiety, PTSD with unclear aetiology
- Multiple somatic complaints (headache, pelvic pain, GI symptoms, insomnia) without clear cause
- Frequent presentations with vague symptoms
- Poor adherence to medical treatment ("my partner doesn't want me to take medication")
- Pregnancy: family violence often increases or starts during pregnancy; universal screening in antenatal care
- Postnatal period: high-risk period for violence escalation
- Substance use: often used as coping mechanism for abuse
- Presentations in children with unexplained injuries or behavioural changes

**High-risk features indicating immediate danger:**
- Threats to kill (lethality is significantly increased when perpetrator threatens to kill)
- Recent escalation in severity or frequency of violence
- Weapon use or access to weapons
- Strangulation or choking (strong predictor of subsequent homicide)
- Threats to harm children
- Stalking behaviour
- Perpetrator mental health or substance use issues

## Assessment Approach

**Universal inquiry (RACGP recommended approach — not selective screening):**
- Recommend routine inquiry in adults at preventive health consultations, antenatal visits, and when clinical indicators are present
- Phrase inquiry sensitively: "I ask all my patients about safety at home. Is there anything happening at home that's making you feel unsafe or controlled?"
- Ask in private — never with partner, family members, or older children present
- If interpreter required: use professional interpreter (TIS National 131 450); never use a partner or family member as interpreter — they may be the perpetrator
- Validated tool: HITS (Hurt, Insult, Threaten, Scream) — 4-item brief screening tool; ≥11/16 = likely abuse; can be self-completed in waiting room

**Responding to disclosure:**
- Believe the patient; do not minimise or blame
- Acknowledge: "I'm glad you told me. This is not your fault. You deserve to be safe."
- Explore: type of abuse, duration, children in the household, previous help-seeking, current safety
- Safety assessment: Is the patient safe right now? Does the perpetrator know they are here? Are there children at risk?
- Danger assessment: Use DASH (Domestic Abuse, Stalking and Honour-based Violence Risk Identification) or similar tool
- Patient-centred response: "What would you like to do? I can help with whatever feels right for you."
- Do NOT pressure to leave: leaving is the highest-risk period for homicide; patient must decide timing; leaving safely requires planning

**Documentation:**
- Document thoroughly in own words (quotes helpful): "Patient states 'my husband hits me when he drinks'"
- Document injuries with description, size, location, photographs (with written consent)
- Do not record family violence disclosure in shared areas of medical record visible to perpetrator (e.g., My Health Record) — perpetrator may access; document in progress notes
- Medicolegal report: may be requested by police or court; maintain thorough contemporaneous records

**Mandatory reporting:**
- Children at risk: if children are in the household, mandatory reporting of child abuse or risk of harm applies in all states; threshold varies by state (reasonable belief that child is at risk of significant harm)
- Report to state child protection authorities: FACS (NSW), DHHS (VIC), DCSSDS (QLD), as per local jurisdiction
- Elder abuse: mandatory reporting for supported residential services in some states (VIC, SA); refer to elder abuse helplines

## Key Differentials

- Accidental injury: plausible mechanism; single injury in typical location
- Bleeding disorders: easy bruising; may coexist with abuse; coagulation screen
- Psychiatric illness: can coexist; do not assume mental health explains all symptoms — screen for violence
- Substance use disorder: frequently coexists (abuse victim or perpetrator) — both require concurrent management
- Medical cause of injury (e.g., bruising from medications like aspirin/warfarin): always ask about circumstances of injury

## Management Principles

**RACGP MOVE framework:**
- Make time: create privacy; ask all patients; normalise inquiry
- Offer support: believe; acknowledge; validate the patient's experience and safety concerns
- Validate safety: safety planning; risk assessment; connect to resources
- Empower: patient-centred decisions; information; referrals

**Safety planning:**
- Work with the patient to develop a personalised safety plan
- Safe places to go; essential items to have ready (ID, medications, bank cards, phone, money)
- Code word with trusted friend or family member
- Safe use of phone: delete apps, browser history; use privacy settings; understand digital tracking risk
- Children's school — alert if risk
- Safe storage of evidence (photos, texts, emails — to a trusted person's device or cloud storage)

**Referrals and resources:**
- 1800RESPECT: 1800 737 732 — national domestic violence helpline (24/7); online chat; trauma-informed counsellors; GPs can call for advice on referral options
- DV Connect (QLD): 1800 811 811
- DVLine (WA): 1800 656 463
- Safe Steps (VIC): 1800 015 188
- Domestic Violence Line (NSW): 1800 656 463
- Police: if immediate danger — 000; Family Violence Police Unit in most states
- Family violence services: specialist case management; legal advocacy; refuge placement; financial assistance
- Legal: AVO/DVO/IVO (Apprehended/Domestic/Intervention Violence Orders) — state-specific legal protection; Legal Aid in each state
- Financial independence: Emergency relief funds; Centrelink (if financially dependent)
- Perpetrator programs: respectful relationships programs for perpetrators

**Cultural considerations:**
- Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander: family violence rates disproportionately higher; community factors; refer to culturally safe services (Aboriginal community controlled organisations)
- CALD communities: visa and immigration concerns (may affect willingness to report or leave); FECCA (Federation of Ethnic Communities' Councils of Australia) resources; interpreter services
- LGBTQ+: intimate partner violence occurs in same-sex relationships; may face additional barriers; Rainbow Health Australia resources
- Male victims: less likely to disclose; stigma; access to resources
- Disability: heightened vulnerability; may be dependent on perpetrator for care; NDIS participant safety concerns

**GP self-care:**
- Family violence work is emotionally demanding — develop peer support networks; debrief with trusted colleague or supervisor; access EAP (Employee Assistance Program) if needed

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- There are no specific medications for family violence
- Treat comorbidities arising from family violence: depression (see `depression` entry), anxiety, PTSD (see `ptsd` entry), chronic pain (see `chronic-pain` entry), substance use (see `alcohol-use` entry)
- Emergency contraception: consider if sexual assault disclosed; levonorgestrel (Plan B, Postinor-2 — pharmacy OTC); ulipristal acetate (EllaOne — S4 prescription up to 120 hours); copper IUD most effective (up to 5 days)
- STI prophylaxis: after sexual assault; azithromycin 1 g stat + cefixime 400 mg stat + metronidazole 2 g stat; HIV PEP if high-risk exposure (ED referral within 72 hours)
- Wound care, fracture management, referral for injuries as clinically required

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000:**
- Immediate danger to patient or children — 000 police
- Perpetrator present and patient unable to communicate freely — safety first; police
- Suspected serious injury requiring emergency medical care

**Urgent same-day:**
- High DASH risk score: urgent safety planning; same-day family violence service referral; police notification
- Strangulation / choking: even if appears recovered — significant morbidity; ED assessment recommended (laryngeal injury may not be immediately apparent)
- Child at immediate risk: mandatory report to child protection immediately

**Non-urgent referral:**
- 1800RESPECT: 1800 737 732 — any patient who discloses family violence; can also call for GP advice
- Family violence specialist service: case management, legal advocacy, housing
- Legal Aid: intervention orders, family court matters
- Psychologist: trauma-focused therapy for PTSD from family violence (GP Mental Health Care Plan MBS 2713)
- Financial counsellor: financial abuse recovery
- Child protection: if children at risk — mandatory report

## Telehealth Suitability

Telehealth is possible for family violence care but requires specific safety modifications.

**Suitable for telehealth (with safety checks):**
- Begin any telehealth with safety check: "Are you somewhere private where you can speak freely?"
- 1800RESPECT for patient during telehealth: can text 0458 737 732 if unable to speak
- Safety planning discussion once confirmed private
- Referral coordination
- Mental health review and GP Mental Health Care Plan
- Follow-up after initial disclosure and safety plan established

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Injury documentation and photography (with consent)
- Examination for strangulation or concealed injuries
- STI testing, emergency contraception, HIV PEP (sexual assault)
- Child protection concerns — preferably in person for full assessment
