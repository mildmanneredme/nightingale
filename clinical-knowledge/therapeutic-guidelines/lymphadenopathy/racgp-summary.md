## Lymphadenopathy Assessment (RACGP)

Lymphadenopathy — enlarged lymph nodes (>1 cm in most sites; >1.5 cm inguinal) — is a common presentation in Australian general practice with a wide differential ranging from benign reactive lymphadenopathy (most common) to haematological malignancy or metastatic cancer. The vast majority of lymphadenopathy in general practice is reactive — caused by infection (viral URTI most common) — and resolves spontaneously. However, identifying the minority requiring investigation and urgent referral is a critical GP skill. GPs play a central role in: systematic assessment of lymphadenopathy, distinguishing reactive from pathological nodes, ordering appropriate investigations, and making timely urgent oncology or haematology referrals. The key clinical distinctions are: localised vs generalised, duration, characteristics (hard, fixed, rubbery), and presence of systemic "B symptoms" (fever, night sweats, weight loss). Telehealth has limited utility for lymphadenopathy assessment — physical examination is essential.

## Clinical Presentation

**Normal vs pathological lymph nodes:**
- Normal: <1 cm (axillary and inguinal up to 1.5 cm); soft, mobile, non-tender; may be palpable as part of normal examination
- Pathological size: >1 cm in cervical/axillary/supraclavicular; >1.5 cm inguinal; or any supraclavicular node (always abnormal — high malignancy risk)
- Concerning features: hard consistency, fixed, non-tender, >2 cm, progressive enlargement, associated B symptoms

**Red flags / features suggesting malignancy:**
- Hard, non-tender, matted or fixed to overlying skin or deeper structures
- Supraclavicular location (Virchow's node — left supraclavicular: GI/abdominal malignancy; right: thoracic/lung/mediastinal)
- Progressive enlargement over weeks (failing to regress after 4–6 weeks)
- Size >2 cm
- B symptoms: unexplained fever (>38°C), drenching night sweats, unexplained weight loss >10% in 6 months — associated with lymphoma
- Age >50 years with new lymphadenopathy
- Occupational exposure (asbestos, radiation)
- Mediastinal or intra-abdominal nodes on imaging

**Localised lymphadenopathy — anatomical clues to cause:**
- Cervical nodes: URTI, pharyngitis, dental infection, infectious mononucleosis (EBV), head and neck cancer
- Axillary nodes: local arm/skin infection, breast cancer, melanoma of arm/trunk, cat-scratch disease
- Inguinal nodes: local leg/genital/perianal infection, STIs (syphilis, chlamydia, herpes, LGV), lower limb melanoma, lymphoma
- Submandibular/submental: dental/oral infection, lip/tongue cancer
- Supraclavicular: ALWAYS investigate — GI cancer (left), lung/breast/lymphoma (right and left)

**Generalised lymphadenopathy (≥2 non-contiguous regions):**
- Infectious mononucleosis (EBV): adolescents; fever + cervical + axillary; pharyngitis; fatigue; splenomegaly
- HIV: primary HIV infection (acute seroconversion illness) or AIDS-related lymphadenopathy
- CMV: similar to EBV; mononucleosis syndrome
- Toxoplasmosis: posterior cervical; cat exposure; often self-limiting
- Secondary syphilis: generalised lymphadenopathy + rash; syphilis serology
- Autoimmune: SLE, rheumatoid arthritis, sarcoidosis
- Haematological malignancy: lymphoma (Hodgkin's or non-Hodgkin's), leukaemia, myeloma

## Assessment Approach

**History:**
- Onset and duration: acute (<2 weeks) vs chronic; progressive vs static
- Associated symptoms: fever, night sweats, weight loss (B symptoms); fatigue; pruritus (Hodgkin's)
- Localising symptoms: sore throat, dental pain, skin lesion, breast lump, urological symptoms
- Systemic symptoms: rash, arthralgia (autoimmune, syphilis, viral)
- Exposure history: unprotected sexual intercourse (STIs, HIV, syphilis); tick bite (Lyme disease — not Australian endemic but returned traveller); cat scratch; animal contact (cat-scratch disease — Bartonella)
- Travel: tropical regions (lymphatic filariasis, leishmaniasis, TB)
- Medications: phenytoin, allopurinol, hydralazine — can cause reactive lymphadenopathy
- Smoking and alcohol: head and neck cancer risk
- HIV risk: injecting drug use, unprotected sex, HIV testing

**Physical examination:**
- ALL nodal regions: cervical (anterior, posterior, submental, submandibular, occipital), supraclavicular, axillary, epitrochlear, inguinal
- Node character: size, consistency (soft/rubbery/hard), mobile vs fixed, tender vs non-tender, warmth (overlying erythema)
- Splenomegaly: enlarged spleen suggests systemic illness (EBV, lymphoma, haematological malignancy)
- Hepatomegaly: hepatic involvement
- Oropharynx: tonsils, mouth, teeth — dental/pharyngeal source
- Skin: rash (syphilis, drug, SLE, lymphoma skin involvement)
- Breast: breast cancer screening

**Investigations:**
- FBC with differential: WBC — lymphocytosis (viral), neutrophilia (bacterial); atypical lymphocytes (EBV/CMV); thrombocytopenia (marrow infiltration, autoimmune)
- ESR, CRP: elevated in infection, lymphoma, autoimmune
- EBV serology (Paul-Bunnell / monospot test + EBV VCA IgM/IgG): suspected infectious mononucleosis
- CMV serology: if EBV negative and mononucleosis syndrome
- Toxoplasma serology: generalised lymphadenopathy (especially posterior cervical)
- HIV serology (4th generation Ag/Ab assay): all adults with generalised lymphadenopathy; early HIV lymphadenopathy
- Syphilis serology (TPPA + RPR): generalised or inguinal lymphadenopathy; sexually active
- LFTs: hepatitis, lymphoma with liver involvement; EBV hepatitis
- LDH: elevated in lymphoma and haematological malignancy — important marker
- Uric acid: elevated in haematological malignancy
- Immunoglobulins and electrophoresis: myeloma, immunodeficiency
- CT neck/chest/abdomen/pelvis: staging investigation for suspected lymphoma; also identifies mediastinal/intraabdominal nodes; urgent if B symptoms or concerning clinical features
- Ultrasound local nodes: characterise; assess for necrotic centre (TB, metastatic cancer), doppler pattern
- PET-CT: high sensitivity for lymphoma; haematology/oncology orders

**When to biopsy:**
- Persistent nodes >4–6 weeks without clear cause
- Any supraclavicular node
- Any node with hard, fixed characteristics and no obvious reactive cause
- B symptoms with enlarged nodes
- FNA (fine-needle aspiration): first-line for accessible nodes; cytology; not diagnostic for lymphoma subtype (does not preserve architecture)
- Excisional lymph node biopsy: required for lymphoma diagnosis; refer to surgery/haematology; preserves architecture; diagnostic gold standard

## Key Differentials

- Reactive/infective lymphadenopathy (most common): EBV, URTI, dental infection — self-limiting; <4 weeks; tender; soft
- Lymphoma: Hodgkin's (young adults, rubbery, mediastinal, B symptoms) or Non-Hodgkin's (any age; diverse presentation)
- HIV seroconversion: generalised + fever + rash + pharyngitis + myalgia within 2–4 weeks of exposure
- Metastatic cancer: from head and neck (SCC), thyroid, breast, melanoma, GI, lung
- Sarcoidosis: bilateral hilar adenopathy on CXR; skin lesions; uveitis; ACE level elevated
- Tuberculosis: posterior cervical; history of TB exposure/migration; scrofula (painless matted nodes)
- Cat-scratch disease (Bartonella henselae): axillary or regional from scratch site; cat exposure; self-limiting; doxycycline if severe

## Management Principles

**Observation period for presumed reactive lymphadenopathy:**
- If clear infective cause identified (viral URTI, dental infection, pharyngitis): treat cause; observe for 4–6 weeks
- Advise patient to return if: nodes not resolving, progressively enlarging, new B symptoms
- Do NOT observe without follow-up plan: lymphadenopathy without follow-up is a missed malignancy risk

**Treat underlying cause:**
- Bacterial: antibiotics for dental/throat infection (amoxicillin, cefalexin)
- EBV infectious mononucleosis: supportive (see `infectious-mononucleosis` entry)
- STI: appropriate treatment (see `sexually-transmitted-infections` entry)

**When to refer urgently to haematology or oncology:**
- Any of the following: supraclavicular node (always refer), B symptoms, progressive enlargement >4 weeks without cause, hard/fixed nodes, age >50 with new unexplained adenopathy, associated mediastinal or intraabdominal nodes on imaging
- Suspected lymphoma: urgent haematology referral; CT staging + biopsy
- Suspected metastatic cancer: refer to appropriate specialist (ENT for head/neck primary, oncology)

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **No specific medication for lymphadenopathy per se**
- **Treat underlying cause**: antibiotics for infection; see specific entries for HIV, STIs, TB, lymphoma
- **EBV/viral reactive: no antiviral required**: supportive management; paracetamol for fever; no aspirin (Reye's risk in <18 years); see `infectious-mononucleosis` entry
- **Note:** Empirical antibiotics are NOT indicated for suspected reactive viral lymphadenopathy; only if concurrent bacterial infection identified

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent haematology (2-week wait or sooner):**
- Suspected lymphoma: B symptoms + large nodes; supraclavicular nodes; progressive enlargement; FBC abnormality; elevated LDH; mediastinal widening on CXR
- Suspected leukaemia: FBC abnormalities, blast cells, thrombocytopenia

**Urgent oncology:**
- Suspected metastatic cancer: head and neck primary, thyroid, breast, melanoma with regional adenopathy; 2-week rule cancer pathway

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Haematologist: for excisional biopsy planning; lymphoma staging; haematological malignancy
- ENT: if pharyngeal primary suspected; tonsil biopsy
- Infectious disease: HIV, TB, complex infections

## Telehealth Suitability

Lymphadenopathy requires in-person assessment; result discussion and follow-up planning suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Blood result discussion (FBC, LDH, EBV, HIV serology)
- Imaging result discussion (CT findings)
- Referral coordination (haematology, oncology)
- Reassurance and safety netting for confirmed reactive/infective cause

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Lymph node physical examination: essential — size, consistency, location, fixation; cannot assess via telehealth
- Abdominal examination (splenomegaly)
- Blood tests and imaging arranged in-person
