## Restless Legs Syndrome Assessment (RACGP)

Restless legs syndrome (RLS), also called Willis-Ekbom disease, is a common neurological sensorimotor disorder characterised by an uncomfortable urge to move the legs, typically worse at rest and in the evening or night, and partially or fully relieved by movement. It affects approximately 5–10% of the Australian population to some degree; clinically significant RLS requiring treatment occurs in 2–3%. RLS is frequently underdiagnosed — patients often struggle to describe the sensation and may be attributed to anxiety or neuropathy. It is also a leading cause of insomnia and daytime fatigue. GPs play a central role in: diagnosis using the 4 IRLSSG criteria, excluding and treating secondary causes (iron deficiency, uraemia, pregnancy, medications), initiating pharmacotherapy for moderate-severe symptoms, and monitoring for augmentation (worsening from dopaminergic medications). Telehealth is well-suited to RLS management and medication review.

## Clinical Presentation

**IRLSSG diagnostic criteria (all 4 required):**

1. Urge to move the legs, usually accompanied by or thought to be caused by uncomfortable/unpleasant sensations
2. Urge to move begins or worsens during periods of rest or inactivity (sitting, lying)
3. Urge to move partially or totally relieved by movement (walking, stretching) — for as long as movement continues
4. Urge to move is worse in the evening or night than during the day, OR symptoms only occur in the evening or night

**Key characteristics:**
- Sensation quality: often described as creeping, crawling, tingling, burning, electric, itching, aching — always uncomfortable, never pleasant; difficult to describe precisely
- Location: usually bilateral legs (calves > thighs); can involve arms, less commonly torso
- Timing: almost always circadian — worst in evening; peak between 10pm–4am; less severe in morning
- Relief: movement relieves symptoms immediately while moving; returns on stopping
- Sleep impact: significant insomnia; difficulty falling asleep; nocturnal awakenings
- Impact on quality of life: severe RLS significantly disrupts sleep, daytime function, work, relationships

**Severity (IRLSSG rating scale 0–40):**
- Mild: <10; Moderate: 11–20; Severe: 21–30; Very severe: 31–40
- Treatment usually started at moderate (score ≥11) or when clinically significant

**Associated conditions:**
- Iron deficiency: most important secondary cause; occurs even with normal haemoglobin (tissue iron deficiency); ferritin <75 µg/L — treat with iron
- Pregnancy: 3rd trimester; hormonal changes and iron depletion; usually resolves postpartum
- Chronic kidney disease / uraemia: RLS very common in dialysis patients
- Peripheral neuropathy: overlap; RLS must be distinguished by characteristic relief with movement
- Medications causing/worsening RLS: dopamine antagonists (metoclopramide, prochlorperazine, haloperidol, risperidone, olanzapine), SSRIs, SNRIs, TCAs, antihistamines, tramadol (paradoxically)

**Periodic limb movement disorder (PLMD):**
- Involuntary limb jerks during sleep; different from RLS (patient unaware during sleep; bed partner notices)
- Frequently co-occurs with RLS; not diagnostic of RLS alone
- Confirmed by polysomnography

## Assessment Approach

**Clinical diagnosis — the 4 criteria above are sufficient in typical presentation:**
- No single diagnostic test exists for RLS
- History: use IRLSSG criteria; characterise quality, timing, relief pattern; impact on sleep and function
- Exclude mimics (see key differentials)

**Investigations:**
- Serum ferritin: critical — ferritin <75 µg/L even with normal Hb warrants iron supplementation; target ferritin >75–100 µg/L
- FBC: to assess for iron deficiency anaemia; but ferritin is more sensitive for iron stores
- eGFR: exclude uraemic RLS (common in CKD stage 4–5)
- TSH: hypothyroidism can worsen RLS
- Fasting glucose, HbA1c: peripheral neuropathy from diabetes can overlap
- Vitamin B12, folate: peripheral neuropathy differential
- Medication review: identify any drugs known to cause or worsen RLS

**Polysomnography (sleep study):**
- Not required for clinical diagnosis
- Consider if: complex sleep disorder, suspicion of sleep apnoea, diagnostic uncertainty, treatment-refractory RLS
- Diagnoses PLMD (periodic limb movements ≥15/hour of sleep)

**Specialist referral:**
- Neurologist or sleep medicine physician: diagnostic uncertainty, refractory to first-line treatment, augmentation (see below), complex comorbidities

## Key Differentials

- Peripheral neuropathy: also causes leg discomfort; but relief with movement is not characteristic; neuropathy typically has continuous or positional symptoms rather than circadian circadian circadian circadian pattern; neurological examination, NCS/EMG
- Painful legs and moving toes: rare; painful burning + spontaneous toe movements; no urge to move; neurological
- Nocturnal leg cramps: sudden, severe, involuntary muscle contraction; brief; often calf; resolved by stretching; NOT urge-to-move; NOT circadian
- Venous insufficiency: aching, heaviness in legs; worse when standing or dependent (NOT at rest); no urge to move; responds to elevation; venous duplex if suspected
- Anxiety and psychomotor agitation: akathisia (antipsychotic side effect) — sensation of inner restlessness, need to move entire body; not specifically legs; not circadian; treat by reducing offending medication
- Positional discomfort: leg pain from sitting position — changes position and relieved; no specific circadian pattern
- Fibromyalgia: widespread pain + fatigue; may coexist; no characteristic urge to move at rest

## Management Principles

**Non-pharmacological (first-line for mild RLS):**
- Iron supplementation: if ferritin <75 µg/L, treat regardless of Hb — oral ferrous sulfate 325 mg (or ferrous fumarate, gluconate) daily or every-other-day (alternate-day dosing improves absorption); with vitamin C; recheck ferritin after 3 months; target >100 µg/L; IV iron if malabsorption or intolerance
- Remove/reduce offending medications: metoclopramide, antipsychotics, antidepressants — discuss risks and alternatives with prescribing physician
- Sleep hygiene: consistent sleep schedule; avoid caffeine, alcohol, nicotine — all worsen RLS; sleep restriction therapy
- Leg stretching, walking: before bed; warm/cold leg baths; compression stockings (if venous component)
- Avoid triggers: prolonged inactivity (long flights, cinema), caffeine, alcohol
- Mental engagement: activity during rest periods (handheld games, knitting) — distracts from symptoms

**Pharmacological (moderate-severe RLS; when non-pharmacological insufficient):**

First-line — dopaminergic agents:
- Levodopa/carbidopa (Sinemet): 100/25 mg nocte; short duration of action — best for intermittent symptoms; high augmentation risk with regular use — not recommended as daily regular treatment; use as-needed
- Pramipexole (Sifrol): dopamine agonist; 0.125–0.5 mg 2–3 hours before bed; most commonly used; augmentation risk with prolonged use (30–70% after 2–3 years)
- Rotigotine patch (Neupro): 24-hour transdermal dopamine agonist; lower augmentation risk than oral DA; 1–3 mg/24-hour patch; PBS-listed for Parkinson's — off-label for RLS

Augmentation — the major problem with dopaminergic therapy:
- Symptoms start earlier in the day, spread to arms, become more intense or last longer despite being on medication
- Manage by dose reduction or switching to non-dopaminergic agent
- If augmentation: reduce dopaminergic dose; transition to alpha-2-delta ligand (pregabalin, gabapentin)

Second-line — alpha-2-delta calcium channel ligands (lower augmentation risk):
- Pregabalin 75–300 mg nocte: growing evidence; also treats comorbid pain and anxiety; PBS-listed for neuropathic pain; preferred for patients with coexisting pain or anxiety
- Gabapentin 300–900 mg nocte: similar mechanism; PBS-listed; may cause sedation

Third-line options:
- Opioids (low-dose): oxycodone, codeine — last resort for refractory severe RLS; risk of dependence; short-term; specialist supervision
- Clonazepam 0.5–2 mg nocte: aids sleep; does not treat RLS directly; significant dependency risk; avoid in elderly

**Pregnancy-specific:**
- Iron supplementation first-line (if deficient — almost universal in 3rd trimester)
- Clonazepam: avoid in 1st trimester; use with caution
- Levodopa: safest pharmacological option in pregnancy if iron supplementation insufficient; use lowest effective dose
- Most RLS in pregnancy resolves within weeks of delivery

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Ferrous sulfate 325 mg** (PBS unrestricted: iron deficiency; alternate-day dosing reduces GI side effects and improves absorption; take with vitamin C; take on empty stomach if tolerated; separate from antacids and dairy by 2 hours)
- **Pramipexole (Sifrol)** (PBS restricted: Parkinson's disease; off-label for RLS; 0.125–0.375 mg before bedtime; monitor for augmentation; impulse control disorder risk — gambling, hypersexuality; inform patients)
- **Pregabalin (Lyrica)** (PBS restricted: epilepsy, diabetic peripheral neuropathy, generalised anxiety disorder; off-label for RLS; 75–300 mg nocte; preferred if coexisting anxiety or pain)
- **Levodopa/carbidopa (Sinemet 100/25)** (PBS restricted: Parkinson's disease; off-label for RLS; intermittent/as-needed use only — avoid regular daily use due to augmentation)
- **Gabapentin** (PBS restricted: epilepsy and neuropathic pain; off-label for RLS; 300–900 mg nocte)
- **Note:** Warn patients prescribed pramipexole about impulse control disorders (ICD) — pathological gambling, hypersexuality, binge eating; screen at each visit; cease or reduce if ICD develops; ICD more common with dopamine agonists than with levodopa

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent review:**
- Augmentation (dopamine agonist): symptoms spreading, starting earlier in day, or worsening despite medication — reduce dose and transition to alpha-2-delta ligand; neurology or sleep medicine referral
- Impulse control disorder on pramipexole: gambling, hypersexuality — cease pramipexole

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Neurologist or sleep medicine physician: refractory RLS; severe RLS; augmentation requiring specialist management; diagnostic uncertainty
- Nephrologist: uraemic RLS in CKD — dialysis may improve RLS; EPO treatment helps
- Haematologist: if IV iron required for iron deficiency with malabsorption

## Telehealth Suitability

RLS management is well-suited to telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- IRLSSG criteria confirmation and severity rating
- Medication review and dose titration
- Augmentation monitoring and management
- Iron supplementation monitoring (ferritin results)
- Depression and sleep impact assessment
- Impulse control disorder screening (pramipexole)
- Referral coordination

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Neurological examination (to exclude peripheral neuropathy)
- Blood tests — ferritin, FBC, eGFR, TSH (pathology arranged)
- Polysomnography if sleep study required
