## Unexplained Weight Loss Assessment (RACGP)

Unexplained (involuntary) significant weight loss is defined as loss of ≥5% of body weight over 6–12 months without intentional dietary restriction or increased exercise. It is a non-specific symptom with a broad differential including malignancy, chronic infections, inflammatory conditions, metabolic and endocrine disorders, psychiatric illness, and social determinants (food insecurity, social isolation). The GP's primary role is systematic evaluation to exclude malignancy (present in approximately 25% of referrals for unexplained weight loss) and other serious causes, while also addressing nutritional support and treating underlying conditions. "B symptoms" (unexplained weight loss >10%, night sweats, fever) are a specific constellation suggesting lymphoma or other haematological malignancy. In frail elderly patients, weight loss is often multifactorial (physical, social, psychological) and management requires a comprehensive geriatric assessment approach. Telehealth is suitable for result review and follow-up.

## Clinical Presentation

**Quantifying weight loss:**
- Confirm: objective weight measurements required (not solely patient estimate); compare to previous documented weights in medical record or patient-reported reliable history
- Clinically significant: ≥5% over 6–12 months = requires investigation; ≥10% = particularly concerning
- Document: current BMI; rate of loss (gradual vs rapid)

**Red flag features (B symptoms + malignancy signs):**
- Unexplained fever: recurrent or persistent
- Drenching night sweats: particularly lymphoma
- Haemoptysis (lung cancer)
- Dysphagia (oesophageal/gastric cancer)
- Change in bowel habit; rectal bleeding (colorectal cancer)
- Haematuria (renal/bladder cancer)
- Lymphadenopathy (lymphoma, metastatic malignancy)
- Palpable mass
- Persistent cough; progressive dyspnoea (lung cancer)
- Jaundice; painless (pancreatic cancer, cholangiocarcinoma)

**Metabolic/endocrine causes:**
- Hyperthyroidism: weight loss despite increased appetite; palpitations; tremor; heat intolerance; check TFTs
- Type 1 diabetes (new onset): polyuria; polydipsia; fatigue; BSL + HbA1c
- Adrenal insufficiency: fatigue; postural hypotension; skin pigmentation (Addison's disease); electrolytes
- Diabetes with poor control: caloric loss via glycosuria

**Chronic infection:**
- Tuberculosis: cough; night sweats; haemoptysis; risk factors (overseas birth, close contact); chest X-ray + QuantiFERON
- HIV: risk factors; CD4 count; AIDS-defining illness; HIV antibody
- Chronic hepatitis (B/C): liver disease; risk factors; hepatitis serology
- Subacute bacterial endocarditis: fever; new murmur; IV drug use; echocardiogram

**Gastrointestinal causes:**
- Malabsorption: coeliac disease (check anti-tTG IgA + total IgA); IBD; pancreatic exocrine insufficiency (chronic pancreatitis)
- Dysphagia, early satiety, odynophagia: upper GI malignancy → upper endoscopy
- Steatorrhoea; oily stools; fat malabsorption → faecal elastase; CT abdomen

**Psychiatric and social:**
- Depression: anhedonia; hopelessness; poor appetite; PHQ-9
- Eating disorders: anorexia nervosa; restrictive patterns; see `eating-disorders`
- Dementia: forgetting to eat; see `dementia-assessment`
- Alcohol use disorder: malnutrition; hepatic disease
- Social isolation; financial hardship; food insecurity; inability to shop or cook

**Medications:**
- Weight-neutral to weight-promoting (review all medications): metformin (GI side effects), SSRIs (initial weight loss), GLP-1 agonists (intentional)
- Digoxin toxicity: anorexia; nausea
- Opioids: nausea; constipation; anorexia

## Assessment Approach

**History:**
- Confirm weight loss: objective documentation; patient estimate vs documented weights
- Dietary history: appetite (reduced = organic disease; preserved = malabsorption, hypermetabolic state); food intake; cooking ability
- Review of systems: systematic cancer screening (haemoptysis, dysphagia, haematuria, bowel habit, lymphadenopathy)
- Social history: living situation; social isolation; financial situation; food access; depression screen
- Past medical history: malignancy (recurrence); HIV; chronic disease
- Medications: nausea-inducing; appetite-suppressing; drug interactions
- Travel history: TB exposure regions; endemic infections
- Substance use: alcohol; recreational drugs

**Physical examination:**
- Weight and BMI: document objectively
- General: cachexia (severe muscle wasting); dehydration; jaundice; pallor; lymphadenopathy
- Lymph nodes: cervical, axillary, inguinal, supraclavicular (Virchow's node = abdominal malignancy)
- Abdominal: organomegaly; mass; ascites
- Rectal examination: if gastrointestinal symptoms; rectal mass; occult blood
- Breast examination (women): malignancy
- Thyroid: goitre; nodule; signs of hyperthyroidism
- Mental state: depression; cognitive assessment (elderly)

**Investigations (systematic screen):**
- Baseline: FBC (anaemia, lymphopenia, thrombocytosis), EUC (renal failure), LFTs + albumin (malnutrition, liver disease), BSL + HbA1c (diabetes), TFTs (hyperthyroidism), CRP + ESR (infection, inflammation), LDH (lymphoma, malignancy)
- Cancer screen: PSA (men ≥50), faecal immunochemical test (iFOBT — if due), mammography (women ≥50)
- Coeliac: anti-tTG IgA + total IgA
- HIV: serology (risk factors or no other explanation)
- TB: QuantiFERON-Gold (risk factors) + CXR
- Urinalysis: haematuria; proteinuria; glycosuria
- CXR: lung malignancy; mediastinal lymphadenopathy; TB
- CT chest/abdomen/pelvis (if no cause found after bloods + CXR): occult malignancy; lymphoma staging

**Investigative yield:**
- Malignancy found in 25% of patients referred for unexplained weight loss
- Gastrointestinal causes: most common aetiology after malignancy
- No cause found in 15–25%: careful monitoring; repeat investigations at 6 months; continued surveillance

## Key Differentials

- Malignancy (any): structured cancer screen; CT chest/abdomen/pelvis if bloods negative
- Depression: PHQ-9; screen all patients
- Hyperthyroidism: TFTs; appetite preserved despite weight loss; classic symptoms
- Type 1 diabetes (new onset): polyuria; polydipsia; BSL markedly elevated
- Malabsorption (coeliac, IBD, pancreatic exocrine insufficiency): anti-tTG; faecal elastase; endoscopy
- TB: endemic region or contact; QuantiFERON; CXR
- HIV: risk factors; serology
- Social/food insecurity: assessment; referral to social work; Centrelink; meal services

## Management Principles

**Treat underlying cause:**
- Once identified, targeted treatment of the underlying condition is primary
- Monitor weight after initiating treatment; expected improvement validates diagnosis

**Nutritional support (regardless of cause):**
- Dietitian referral: comprehensive nutritional assessment; high-calorie diet; addressing barriers to eating
- Oral nutritional supplements (ONS): Ensure, Sustagen, Fortisip — calorie-dense supplements between meals; evidence for weight gain and quality of life in malnourished patients
- PBS: no PBS subsidy for ONS in most cases; NDIS or aged care funding may apply
- Treat contributing factors: dental (poor dentition → difficulty eating); dysphagia (speech pathology); depression (antidepressants); pain management

**Frail elderly patients:**
- Multi-factorial assessment: physical, social, psychological; Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment (CGA)
- Address: social isolation; depression; medication review (polypharmacy contributing to anorexia); dental health; meal delivery (Meals on Wheels); community care
- Mirtazapine 7.5–15 mg nocte: stimulates appetite; sedating; useful for concurrent depression + weight loss + insomnia in elderly; discuss geriatrician
- Megestrol acetate: appetite stimulant; evidence in cancer cachexia; thromboembolism risk; specialist prescribing

**Follow-up:**
- Review at 4–6 weeks if cause not found: reassess weight; symptom change; new investigations
- CT chest/abdomen/pelvis: if initial screen negative and weight loss continues; no cause found

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Mirtazapine 7.5–15 mg nocte** (PBS restricted: depression; appetite-stimulating effect; useful in elderly with weight loss + depression; sedation benefit for poor sleep; start low)
- **Oral nutritional supplements (Ensure, Sustagen, Fortisip)**: not PBS-listed; OTC or chemist; recommend between meals not as meal replacement; calorically dense; important in malnutrition
- **Note:** Weight loss associated with GLP-1 agonists (semaglutide, dulaglutide, liraglutide) prescribed for diabetes or obesity management is intentional and should not be investigated as "unexplained" — document clearly in the medical record.

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent investigation (within 2 weeks):**
- Red flag symptoms: haemoptysis, dysphagia, rectal bleeding, haematuria, significant lymphadenopathy, palpable mass → urgent endoscopy, CT, or biopsy as appropriate
- Severe malnutrition with functional decline: hospital or nutrition team consultation
- Suspected lymphoma (B symptoms + lymphadenopathy): CT + haematology referral

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Gastroenterology: dysphagia, change in bowel habit, iron deficiency anaemia → endoscopy
- Haematology/oncology: suspected malignancy on investigations
- Geriatrics: frail elderly with unexplained weight loss + functional decline
- Dietitian: all patients with significant weight loss for nutritional assessment and support
- Social worker: food insecurity, social isolation

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Review of investigation results and management planning
- Dietary advice and nutritional supplement recommendation
- Follow-up for monitoring weight and response to treatment
- Depression screening and mental health management
- Geriatric review and social support planning

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial clinical examination (lymphadenopathy, abdominal mass, organomegaly)
- Blood tests, urine, stool
- Rectal examination and iFOBT collection
