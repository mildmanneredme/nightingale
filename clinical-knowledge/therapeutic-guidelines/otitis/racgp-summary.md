## Otitis Assessment (RACGP)

Ear infections are common GP presentations encompassing acute otitis media (AOM — middle ear infection), otitis media with effusion (OME — glue ear), and otitis externa (OE — outer ear canal infection). AOM predominantly affects children under 5 years, while otitis externa occurs across all age groups and is particularly common in swimmers and those who use hearing aids or earbuds. Most cases of AOM are self-limiting; antibiotics provide modest benefit in select patients. Otitis externa typically responds well to topical treatment. Telehealth assessment is limited by the need for otoscopy; clinical management guidance can be provided remotely in appropriate cases.

## Clinical Presentation

**Acute otitis media (AOM):**
Otalgia (ear pain), pulling at ears in young children, fever, hearing loss, and irritability. Symptoms typically follow an URTI. Otoscopic findings (in-person): erythematous, bulging, opaque tympanic membrane; loss of light reflex; may progress to spontaneous perforation with purulent discharge.

**Otitis media with effusion (OME — "glue ear"):**
Reduced hearing (often detected by parents, teacher, or audiometry); sensation of fullness in ear; no pain or fever. Tympanic membrane appears dull, retracted, or immobile on pneumatic otoscopy. More common in winter, associated with nasal congestion and adenoid hypertrophy.

**Otitis externa (swimmer's ear):**
Ear canal pain (often severe, worse with movement of the pinna or tragus — key diagnostic feature distinguishing from AOM), discharge, pruritus, swelling of canal, and conductive hearing loss if canal obstructed. Predisposing factors: water exposure (swimming, bathing), ear canal trauma (cotton buds), hearing aids, eczema, psoriasis, diabetes (risk of malignant OE).

**Malignant (necrotising) otitis externa:** Severe form in diabetic or immunocompromised patients — spreading infection, granulation tissue, facial nerve palsy; requires urgent hospital admission.

## Assessment Approach

Telehealth limitations: otoscopy cannot be performed via telehealth. History-taking can support management decisions for likely presentations.

History allows differentiation:
- AOM: URTI prodrome, otalgia, fever, young child — consider watchful waiting approach
- Otitis externa: pain worse with pinna movement, recent water exposure, no fever, no preceding URTI — topical treatment appropriate to prescribe remotely
- Otoscopy required for: uncertain diagnosis, persistent or recurrent symptoms, perforation assessment, suspected OME with hearing loss

In children, assess for recurrent AOM (>3 episodes in 6 months or >4 in 12 months) — consider ENT referral for grommets.

Examination required for: malignant otitis externa, cholesteatoma, tympanic membrane perforation.

## Key Differentials

- Referred otalgia — ear pain referred from teeth, TMJ, tonsils, pharynx, or cervical spine; no otoscopic abnormality; consider TMJ dysfunction, dental abscess, tonsillitis
- Ramsay Hunt syndrome — herpes zoster oticus; ear pain, vesicles in ear canal or on pinna, facial nerve palsy; requires urgent antiviral therapy
- Cholesteatoma — chronic painless discharge, hearing loss, possible vertigo; destructive lesion; ENT referral
- Foreign body in ear canal — common in children; in-person removal required

## Management Principles

**Acute otitis media (AOM):**

Watchful waiting (most patients): the majority of AOM resolves spontaneously within 2–3 days. Analgesia (paracetamol, ibuprofen) is the cornerstone of management.

Watchful waiting appropriate for: children ≥2 years with mild-moderate symptoms, no fever >38.5°C, unilateral AOM, no perforation.

Antibiotics appropriate for:
- Children <6 months — all AOM
- Children <2 years with bilateral AOM or perforation
- Severe AOM — fever >38.5°C, severe otalgia, systemically unwell
- Not improved or worsening after 48–72 hours of watchful waiting

Antibiotic of choice for AOM: amoxicillin 25 mg/kg/dose (up to 500 mg) twice daily for 5 days (children) or 500 mg three times daily for 5 days (adults).

Alternative (penicillin allergy — non-immediate): cefalexin. Immediate penicillin allergy: azithromycin or trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole.

**Otitis media with effusion (OME):**
Spontaneous resolution in >90% within 3 months. Watchful waiting for 3 months before considering ENT referral. No antibiotics, antihistamines, or decongestants recommended. ENT referral if persisting >3 months with associated hearing loss.

**Otitis externa (OE):**
Topical treatment is highly effective. Remove debris (microsuction — in-person); keep ear dry.

First-line: combined acidifying/antibiotic/steroid ear drops:
- Ciprofloxacin 0.3% + dexamethasone 0.1% (Cilodex — PBS-listed) — twice daily for 7 days; excellent gram-negative coverage including Pseudomonas
- Acetic acid 2% + hydrocortisone (Kenacomb Otic) — appropriate for mild OE

Analgesia: paracetamol or ibuprofen for pain.

Keep ear dry during treatment — cotton wool with vaseline for showering; avoid swimming until resolved.

For severe swelling occluding canal: ear wick insertion (in-person) to allow drops to reach full length of canal.

**Malignant (necrotising) otitis externa:** IV antibiotics (anti-pseudomonal), surgical debridement — urgent hospital admission.

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Amoxicillin** (PBS unrestricted — for AOM) — 500 mg three times daily (adults); weight-based dosing in children
- **Amoxicillin-clavulanate** (PBS restricted — second-line for AOM not responding to amoxicillin)
- **Ciprofloxacin 0.3% + dexamethasone 0.1% otic drops** (Cilodex — PBS restricted: otitis externa; 4 drops twice daily for 7 days)
- **Framycetin + gramicidin + dexamethasone otic drops** (Sofradex — PBS restricted: otitis externa)
- **Acetic acid 2% ear spray** (Aquaear — OTC; maintenance and mild OE; not PBS-listed)
- **Aluminium acetate ear drops** (Burow's solution — OTC; acidifying agent)
- Oral antibiotics for OE: only if spreading cellulitis (cellulitis of pinna or periauricular skin) — ciprofloxacin 500 mg twice daily covers Pseudomonas (PBS restricted)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Mastoiditis: post-auricular swelling, erythema, tenderness, and protrusion of pinna outward — rare complication of AOM; requires IV antibiotics and ENT review
- Intracranial complication of AOM: severe headache, meningism, facial palsy, confusion
- Malignant otitis externa in diabetic/immunocompromised patient: granulation tissue, facial palsy

**Urgent in-person review (same day):**
- Ramsay Hunt syndrome (herpes zoster oticus): ear pain, vesicles in ear canal, facial nerve palsy — requires antiviral therapy within 72 hours
- Suspected tympanic membrane perforation with significant pain or discharge in a child with fever

**Non-urgent referral:**
- ENT: recurrent AOM (≥3 in 6 months), OME persisting >3 months with hearing loss, cholesteatoma, recurrent/refractory otitis externa, suspected hearing loss

## Telehealth Suitability

Otitis management via telehealth is of moderate suitability due to the diagnostic reliance on otoscopy.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Likely otitis externa — pain with pinna movement, water exposure, no systemic features — prescribe topical treatment
- AOM management advice: analgesia, watchful waiting education, safety netting instructions
- Review of known OME response over time
- Antibiotic prescribing for AOM when clinical features clearly indicate bacterial aetiology

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Otoscopy for any diagnostic uncertainty
- Suspected perforation, mastoiditis, or intracranial complication — direct to ED
- Canal blockage requiring microsuction
- Ear wick insertion for occluded canal
