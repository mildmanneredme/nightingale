## Epistaxis Assessment (RACGP)

Epistaxis (nosebleed) is one of the most common ENT presentations in Australian general practice and emergency departments, affecting approximately 60% of the population at some point. The majority of nosebleeds (90–95%) arise from the anterior nasal septum at Kiesselbach's plexus (Little's area) — superficial anastomosis of multiple arteries accessible for direct pressure and cautery. Posterior epistaxis (5–10%) arises from posterior nasal cavity, tends to affect older patients, is more severe, harder to control, and requires ENT management. Most anterior epistaxis is managed effectively in general practice. Predisposing factors include nasal trauma (nose-picking — most common), dry climate, nasal sprays (corticosteroids, decongestants), anticoagulation/antiplatelet therapy, hypertension, and blood dyscrasias. GPs play a central role in: managing acute epistaxis with direct pressure, anterior rhinoscopy and silver nitrate cautery, addressing predisposing causes, and referring severe or posterior bleeds to ENT. Telehealth has no role in acute management; initial assessment and treatment require in-person attendance.

## Clinical Presentation

**Anterior epistaxis (90–95%):**
- Site: Kiesselbach's plexus (Little's area) — anterior nasal septum, approximately 1 cm from nares
- Presentation: bleeding from one nostril; blood flowing forward out of nose; often self-limiting; history of nose-picking, trauma, nasal spray use
- Typically controlled by direct pressure within 10–15 minutes
- Common age groups: children (nose-picking, trauma) and elderly (dry mucosa, anticoagulants)
- Visible bleeding point on anterior rhinoscopy

**Posterior epistaxis:**
- Site: branches of sphenopalatine artery (posterior nasal cavity)
- Presentation: bleeding from both nostrils; blood flowing down posterior pharynx (can cause vomiting blood); elderly; hypertensive; anticoagulated patients
- Not visible on anterior rhinoscopy
- Does not respond to anterior pressure
- Requires ENT management (posterior pack or embolisation)

**Recurrent epistaxis:**
- Recurrent anterior bleeding: persistent vessels at Little's area requiring cautery
- Hereditary haemorrhagic telangiectasia (HHT/Osler-Weber-Rendu): autosomal dominant; telangiectasias on lips, tongue, nasal mucosa; recurrent severe epistaxis from childhood; family history; ENT specialist
- Nasal tumour: unilateral, persistent, blood-stained nasal discharge + obstruction — must exclude with rhinoscopy and nasal endoscopy by ENT

**Common precipitating factors:**
- Nose picking or nasal trauma: most common cause in children
- Dry environment: low humidity; air conditioning; winter heating
- Nasal corticosteroid sprays: mucosal dryness — direct spray toward lateral wall, not septum; use saline moisturiser
- Anticoagulants: warfarin, apixaban, rivaroxaban — check INR/anticoagulant timing
- Antiplatelets: aspirin, clopidogrel
- Hypertension: associated with epistaxis severity but NOT necessarily causative
- Blood dyscrasias: thrombocytopenia, clotting factor deficiencies
- Nasal polyps, deviated septum, sinusitis

## Assessment Approach

**Acute assessment:**
- Vital signs: BP, HR, SpO2 — hypertension common with acute blood loss; heavy bleeding may cause haemodynamic compromise in elderly
- Estimate blood loss: amount (sheets, bowls), duration of bleeding
- Site: anterior (visible bleeding point) vs posterior (bilateral, posterior pharynx blood)
- Current first aid: has pressure been applied? Correct technique?
- Medications: anticoagulants, antiplatelets, NSAIDs
- Comorbidities: haematological, liver disease, hereditary (HHT)

**Physical examination:**
- Blood pressure: check in all epistaxis patients — hypertension common
- Anterior rhinoscopy: nasal speculum + light; identify site of bleeding (Kiesselbach's plexus anteriorly); look for polyps, foreign body, tumour
- Posterior pharynx: blood trickling down — suggests posterior source

**Investigations (as indicated):**
- FBC: significant blood loss; thrombocytopenia screening
- INR/PT: if on warfarin or liver disease suspected
- Coagulation screen: if bleeding disorder suspected (family history, bruising, unexplained recurrent epistaxis)
- Blood group and hold: if heavy/recurrent bleeding requiring hospital management

## Key Differentials

- Haemoptysis: blood from lungs (coughing up blood); frothy, bright red; respiratory symptoms; distinguish from epistaxis causing swallowed blood presenting as haematemesis
- HHT: telangiectasias on lips, tongue, family history; referral for genetic counselling and ENT specialist
- Nasal tumour: unilateral nasal obstruction + blood-stained discharge → ENT endoscopy; especially >40 years
- Coagulation disorder: multiple bruising sites; joint bleeds; family history; haematology
- Juvenile angiofibroma: adolescent males; recurrent significant unilateral epistaxis; CT nasopharynx; ENT

## Management Principles

**Immediate first aid (educate patient and GP management):**
- Correct technique: sit upright, lean forward (not back — prevents swallowing blood); pinch soft part of nose (lower third — NOT the bridge); continuous firm pressure for 10–15 minutes without releasing
- Breathe through mouth; spit out any blood
- Do NOT pack nostrils with tissue (causes clot disruption); do NOT tilt head back
- Ice pack to bridge of nose: vasoconstriction; adjunct

**Topical vasoconstrictors (first-line alongside pressure):**
- Xylometazoline (Otrivin) or oxymetazoline nasal spray: apply 2–3 sprays to affected nostril; vasoconstriction reduces bleeding within minutes; may use prior to cautery
- Lidocaine spray (Xylocaine): topical anaesthetic prior to examination and cautery; reduces pain and nasal packing discomfort

**Silver nitrate cautery (GP procedure — most effective for anterior epistaxis):**
- Indication: visible bleeding point at Little's area; bleeding controlled or slowed with pressure
- Technique: dry bleeding point with suction or cotton wool; apply silver nitrate stick to bleeding vessel for 5–10 seconds; cauterise surrounding vessels; do NOT cauterise both sides of septum simultaneously (risk of septal perforation — wait minimum 4–6 weeks)
- Silver nitrate sticks: available in general practice; minimal equipment; highly effective
- Post-cautery: apply Vaseline (petroleum jelly) to crusting area to prevent recurrent drying; avoid nose picking
- Success rate: 80–90% for anterior epistaxis

**Nasal packing (when cautery unsuccessful or bleeding point not visible):**
- Anterior nasal packing: Merocel (compressed polymer; expands with moisture) or Rapid Rhino (inflatable) — insert along floor of nose; leave 24–48 hours; antibiotic prophylaxis (amoxicillin-clavulanate or cefalexin) to prevent toxic shock syndrome
- Patient can go home with packing; return in 24–48 hours for removal
- Bismuth iodoform paraffin paste (BIPP) ribbon gauze: older technique; still used; ENT

**Recurrent anterior epistaxis (prevention):**
- Naseptin cream (chlorhexidine + neomycin): apply to Kiesselbach's plexus BD for 4 weeks; reduces nasal colonisation with Staphylococcus (contributor to epistaxis); effective for recurrent anterior bleeds; use on cotton bud; avoid in peanut allergy (arachis oil base — substitute with petroleum jelly + mupirocin)
- Petroleum jelly (Vaseline): apply to anterior septum daily; prevents mucosal drying
- Saline nasal spray: regular humidification; particularly in dry climates and air-conditioned environments
- Nasal corticosteroid spray technique: direct toward lateral wall (not septum); concurrent saline moisturiser

**Anticoagulant-related epistaxis:**
- Do NOT routinely reverse anticoagulation for self-limiting anterior epistaxis — thromboembolism risk outweighs benefit
- Check INR (warfarin): if supratherapeutic, dose adjustment; contact prescribing physician
- Escalate if: recurrent, severe, requiring posterior packing — haematology and ENT collaboration
- Vitamin K for warfarin reversal only if life-threatening bleeding with extreme supratherapeutic INR

**Hypertension in epistaxis:**
- Acute hypertension during epistaxis: usually secondary to pain, anxiety, blood loss (stress response); control epistaxis first; recheck BP once bleeding controlled
- Do NOT aggressively lower BP in the acute setting unless severely elevated (>180/120) with end-organ damage — risks cerebral hypoperfusion
- If hypertension persists after bleeding controlled: review and optimise antihypertensive therapy; see `hypertension` entry

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Xylometazoline (Otrivin) 0.1% nasal spray** (PBS unrestricted: nasal congestion; OTC; vasoconstrictor; use for epistaxis management; maximum 3–5 days continuous use — rebound congestion)
- **Naseptin cream** (PBS restricted: Staphylococcal nasal carriage; OFF-LABEL for recurrent anterior epistaxis; not formally PBS-listed for epistaxis; apply to anterior septum BD × 4 weeks; peanut oil base — check allergy)
- **Amoxicillin-clavulanate (Augmentin Duo)** (PBS unrestricted: antibiotic prophylaxis if nasal packing used; reduces toxic shock risk; standard doses × duration of packing + 2 days; cefalexin alternative if penicillin-sensitive non-anaphylactic)
- **Silver nitrate sticks**: procedure supply; not PBS-funded; available from medical suppliers; minimal cost; most cost-effective treatment for anterior epistaxis
- **Note:** Vitamin K administration for warfarin reversal in epistaxis: only for severe, refractory, haemodynamically significant bleeding and highly supratherapeutic INR; discuss with haematology

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Haemodynamic compromise: shock, tachycardia, hypotension — blood volume resuscitation; transfusion in hospital
- Airway compromise: bilateral posterior bleed causing blood in airway — anaesthetics/ENT emergency
- Failed anterior packing with ongoing bleeding: posterior packing or artery embolisation required

**Urgent ENT:**
- Posterior epistaxis: blood flowing from both nostrils or down posterior pharynx; not controlled with anterior pressure; requires posterior packing or endoscopic/angiographic embolisation
- Failed management with anterior packing × 2: ENT for further management (endoscopic cautery, embolisation)
- Suspected nasal tumour: unilateral obstruction + bleeding — urgent rhinoscopy

**Non-urgent ENT:**
- Recurrent anterior epistaxis refractory to silver nitrate × 2 and topical Naseptin: endoscopic sphenopalatine artery ligation consideration
- HHT: specialist management; laser cautery; nasal embolisation for severe recurrent bleeding
- Chronic recurrent epistaxis with no identifiable Kiesselbach's lesion on rhinoscopy: ENT for endoscopy to exclude posterior or tumour cause

## Telehealth Suitability

Epistaxis acute management requires in-person or emergency care; follow-up and prevention suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Post-acute review: recurrence assessment, prevention strategy
- Counselling on correct first-aid technique for future episodes
- Naseptin prescription and application education
- Anticoagulant management review after epistaxis episode
- Hypertension optimisation following epistaxis

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- All acute epistaxis: anterior rhinoscopy, silver nitrate cautery, nasal packing
- Posterior epistaxis assessment
- Investigation for bleeding disorder (bloods)
- ENT endoscopy for recurrent or unilateral symptoms
