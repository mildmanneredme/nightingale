## Multiple Sclerosis Assessment (RACGP)

Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a chronic immune-mediated inflammatory demyelinating and neurodegenerative disease of the central nervous system (CNS). Australia has one of the highest MS prevalence rates in the world — approximately 33,000 Australians live with MS (prevalence ~130 per 100,000). It predominantly affects women (ratio 3:1) and most commonly presents between 20–40 years of age. MS is the most common cause of non-traumatic neurological disability in young Australians. GPs play a critical role in: recognising early MS presentations and referring urgently for MRI and neurology; shared care of established MS patients (monitoring comorbidities, managing symptoms, coordinating allied health, safety monitoring of disease-modifying therapies); and influencing lifestyle factors that affect prognosis (smoking, exercise, vitamin D). The GP is the primary point of care for the 50–80% of MS time not spent with a neurologist. Telehealth is suitable for ongoing monitoring and medication review.

## Clinical Presentation

**MS phenotypes:**
- Relapsing-remitting MS (RRMS): 85% at diagnosis; discrete relapses (attacks) followed by complete or partial recovery; may convert to secondary progressive
- Secondary progressive MS (SPMS): follows RRMS; gradual disability accumulation with or without superimposed relapses
- Primary progressive MS (PPMS): 15%; gradual disability progression from onset; no relapses; older at onset; harder to treat
- Clinically isolated syndrome (CIS): first demyelinating episode consistent with MS but not yet meeting diagnostic criteria; high conversion to MS if abnormal MRI

**Common presenting symptoms (relapse — NEW neurological symptoms lasting >24 hours, not explained by fever/illness):**

Optic neuritis:
- Unilateral visual loss or blurring; pain with eye movement; reduced colour saturation; afferent pupillary defect (Marcus Gunn pupil); spontaneous recovery over weeks; common first presentation of MS

Spinal cord syndrome (myelitis):
- Paraesthesiae (numbness, tingling) — ascending from feet; often with sensory level
- Lhermitte's sign: electric shock sensation down spine on neck flexion (demyelination of cervical cord)
- Limb weakness: asymmetric; upper motor neuron pattern (spasticity, increased reflexes, upgoing plantar)
- Bladder dysfunction: urgency, frequency, retention; very common
- Bowel: constipation, urgency, incontinence

Brainstem/cerebellar:
- Diplopia: internuclear ophthalmoplegia (INO) — failure of adduction with nystagmus in abducting eye; characteristic of MS
- Vertigo and nausea: acute brainstem demyelination; differentiate from peripheral vertigo
- Dysarthria, dysphagia
- Cerebellar ataxia: intention tremor, gait ataxia, dysmetria

**Ongoing symptoms (outside relapses):**
- Fatigue: most common and debilitating symptom; affects 80%; not proportional to physical disability
- Cognitive impairment: processing speed, working memory, attention — often called "cog fog"
- Depression and anxiety: 2–3× general population; major impact on quality of life
- Spasticity: limb stiffness, spasms, pain
- Pain: neuropathic; musculoskeletal; Lhermitte's
- Heat sensitivity (Uhthoff's phenomenon): transient worsening of existing symptoms with heat/exercise — NOT a relapse

## Assessment Approach

**First presentation (suspected MS relapse or CIS):**
- Urgent neurology referral: any new neurological symptom consistent with CNS demyelination requires same-day to 48-hour neurology contact
- Do NOT start corticosteroids without neurology guidance
- Brain and spinal cord MRI: gadolinium-enhanced — standard investigation; ordered by neurologist or emergency physician
- Urinalysis: exclude UTI triggering pseudo-relapse (Uhthoff's)
- McDonald Criteria: 2017 revision — diagnostic criteria combining clinical + MRI + CSF findings; applied by neurologist

**Established MS monitoring (GP shared care):**

Relapse identification:
- New neurological symptoms lasting >24 hours not explained by infection/fever = potential relapse
- Pseudo-relapse: existing symptoms worsening with infection, fever, heat, fatigue — treat trigger, NOT relapse; no IV steroids
- Relapse management: contact neurologist; IV methylprednisolone 1 g daily × 3–5 days (accelerates recovery, does not improve long-term outcome)

Annual health review:
- Expanded Disability Status Scale (EDSS): neurologist scores 0–10; documents disability progression
- Vaccination: ensure all standard vaccinations up to date (live attenuated vaccines require careful timing with DMTs — discuss with neurologist before administering)
- Vitamin D: maintain 25-OH vitamin D >75 nmol/L — lower vitamin D associated with relapse risk; supplement to target; monitor annually
- Bone density: DEXA every 2 years — corticosteroid exposure + reduced mobility increase osteoporosis risk
- FBC, LFTs, renal function: baseline and monitoring for DMT side effects (depends on specific DMT)
- JC virus antibody titre: for patients on natalizumab (Tysabri) — risk of PML (progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy)

**Comorbidity management:**
- Depression and anxiety screening (PHQ-9, GAD-7): extremely common; treat proactively; SSRIs first-line
- Bladder: urinalysis at each visit; UTIs very common (neurogenic bladder); clean intermittent catheterisation (CIC) if retention; anticholinergics for overactive bladder; urology referral if complex
- Bowel: constipation management (fibre, macrogol, osmotic laxatives)
- Fatigue: exercise; amantadine (off-label); exclude treatable causes (depression, thyroid, anaemia, poor sleep); occupational therapy; fatigue management programs
- Spasticity: physiotherapy; baclofen (PBS-listed); tizanidine; intrathecal baclofen (specialist)
- Pain: neuropathic — amitriptyline, pregabalin, gabapentin; musculoskeletal — physio, NSAIDs

## Key Differentials

- Neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorder (NMOSD): severe optic neuritis + transverse myelitis; anti-AQP4 antibody; different DMTs required — MRI, blood tests, neurology to differentiate
- Clinically isolated syndrome (CIS): single episode; may or may not convert to MS; MRI guides risk stratification
- CNS vasculitis: SLE, Behçet's; multisystem features; ANA, ANCA, complement
- Migraine with aura: visual symptoms; differentiate by pattern; MRI white matter changes can overlap
- B12 deficiency (subacute combined degeneration): dorsal column + corticospinal tract; progressive; B12 level, MRI cervical spine
- Functional neurological disorder: previously hysteria; symptoms inconsistent with neurological disease; no MRI changes; neurology assessment

## Management Principles

**Disease-modifying therapies (DMTs) — neurologist-initiated:**

High-efficacy DMTs (PBS-listed for RRMS):
- Natalizumab (Tysabri): IV infusion monthly; very high efficacy; JC virus antibody monitoring required (PML risk); neurologist-managed
- Ocrelizumab (Ocrevus): IV infusion 6-monthly; B-cell depleting; also for PPMS (first DMT for PPMS); immunisation timing important
- Cladribine (Mavenclad): oral; annual short courses; selective B/T-cell depletion
- Alemtuzumab (Lemtrada): IV; very high efficacy; significant monitoring burden (autoimmune side effects); monthly blood/urine monitoring for 4+ years

Moderate-efficacy DMTs:
- Fingolimod (Gilenya): oral daily; S1P receptor modulator; first-dose cardiac monitoring required; contraindicated in heart disease
- Dimethyl fumarate (Tecfidera): oral twice daily; flushing common initially; PML risk (lymphopenia monitoring)
- Teriflunomide (Aubagio): oral daily; teratogenic — strict contraception required; liver monitoring

Injectables (older, lower-efficacy):
- Interferon beta (Avonex, Betaseron, Rebif): subcutaneous or IM injection; flu-like side effects; less used with availability of oral high-efficacy options
- Glatiramer acetate (Copaxone): subcutaneous daily; injection site reactions

**GP role with established DMT patients:**
- Regular FBC monitoring: depends on DMT — check neurologist protocol
- Manage infections: DMTs are immunosuppressive; treat infections promptly; know when to escalate
- Vaccinations: annual influenza (recommended; killed vaccine safe on all DMTs); pneumococcal; COVID-19 boosters; timing of live vaccines requires neurologist guidance

**Symptom management (GP scope):**
- Fatigue: refer to exercise physiologist (MBS Chronic Disease Management); amantadine 100 mg BD (off-label, not PBS-listed for MS); occupational therapy fatigue management
- Bladder urgency: solifenacin (Vesicare), oxybutynin — anticholinergics; caution in elderly; check for retention before starting; urinalysis at each visit
- Depression: SSRIs (sertraline, escitalopram) — first-line; avoid medications that worsen fatigue (sedating SSRIs)
- Spasticity: baclofen 5–20 mg TDS (PBS unrestricted); physiotherapy; stretching; intrathecal baclofen via neurologist
- Neuropathic pain: amitriptyline 10–75 mg nocte, pregabalin 75–300 mg/day, gabapentin — as per `peripheral-neuropathy` entry
- Constipation: dietary fibre, macrogol (Movicol), adequate hydration

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Baclofen** (PBS unrestricted: muscle spasm; 5–25 mg TDS; titrate slowly; do not stop abruptly — withdrawal seizures; sedation, falls risk in elderly)
- **Amantadine 100 mg BD** (PBS restricted: Parkinson's disease; off-label for MS fatigue; not PBS-listed for MS; private prescription or CDM; discuss with neurologist)
- **Pregabalin / gabapentin** (PBS restricted: neuropathic pain; see `peripheral-neuropathy` entry for dosing)
- **Solifenacin (Vesicare)** (PBS restricted: overactive bladder; check for urinary retention before starting; anticholinergic burden in polypharmacy)
- **DMTs**: ALL initiated by neurologist under PBS Authority (high-cost, complex monitoring requirements); GP manages monitoring blood tests and infections; does NOT initiate or modify DMTs
- **Vitamin D supplements** (PBS unrestricted: 1000–2000 IU/day; target 25-OH vitamin D >75 nmol/L; monitor annually)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Severe acute relapse with rapid neurological deterioration: limb weakness, visual loss, inability to walk — may require urgent IV methylprednisolone in hospital
- Suspected PML (natalizumab complication): subacute cognitive, visual, motor deterioration in JC antibody-positive patient on natalizumab — MRI brain urgently
- Urinary retention: acute retention from neurogenic bladder — catheterisation; urology review

**Urgent neurology (within 24–48 hours):**
- New relapse: any new neurological symptoms lasting >24 hours not explained by fever or infection
- Rapidly progressive disability between clinic appointments
- Suspected treatment failure: ongoing relapses or disability progression on DMT

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Physiotherapist: gait, balance, strength, spasticity
- Occupational therapist: fatigue management, ADL assistance, home modifications
- Psychologist: depression, anxiety, adjustment (GP Mental Health Care Plan)
- Continence nurse or urologist: complex bladder dysfunction
- MS nurse/coordinator: many hospitals have MS nursing support services
- Exercise physiologist: evidence-based exercise for fatigue and function

## Telehealth Suitability

MS ongoing management is suitable for telehealth in stable patients.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Symptom monitoring between neurology appointments
- Medication review and side effect management
- Depression and anxiety monitoring and treatment
- DMT monitoring blood result discussion
- Referral coordination (physio, OT, psychologist)
- Vitamin D and bone density review
- Vaccination planning (timing relative to DMT)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Neurological examination (power, reflexes, coordination, gait, eye movements)
- Blood tests — pathology arranged (DMT monitoring)
- Relapse assessment (new neurological symptoms require in-person exam first)
- Vaccination administration
