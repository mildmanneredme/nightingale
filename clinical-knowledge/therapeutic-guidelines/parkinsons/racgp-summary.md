## Parkinson's Disease Assessment (RACGP)

Parkinson's disease (PD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder characterised by dopaminergic neuronal loss in the substantia nigra, causing the cardinal motor features of bradykinesia, rigidity, and resting tremor. It affects approximately 100,000 Australians, with prevalence increasing with age (1% of those >60 years; 4% of those >80 years). PD has significant non-motor features that are often underrecognised and substantially impact quality of life. Neurologists initiate and manage complex PD therapy; the GP plays a critical role in early recognition and referral, ongoing co-management of non-motor features and comorbidities, medication compliance, and coordination of allied health. Telehealth is suitable for stable Parkinson's monitoring but in-person examination is required for motor assessment.

## Clinical Presentation

**Cardinal motor features (two of four required for clinical diagnosis):**
- Bradykinesia (essential): slowness of movement; reduced amplitude of repetitive movements; micrographia; reduced facial expression (hypomimia); soft voice (hypophonia)
- Resting tremor (4–6 Hz): pill-rolling; disappears with voluntary movement; asymmetric onset (characteristic of PD)
- Rigidity: lead-pipe or cogwheel resistance to passive movement; tested at wrist and elbow
- Postural instability: impaired balance reflexes; falls; later feature; assess with pull test

**Non-motor features (common — often presenting before motor symptoms):**
- REM sleep behaviour disorder (RBD): acting out dreams; often precedes PD by years; highly associated
- Anosmia: loss of sense of smell; early non-motor feature
- Constipation: autonomic dysfunction; often years before diagnosis
- Depression and anxiety: 50% of patients; may predate motor diagnosis
- Cognitive impairment: Parkinson's disease dementia (PDD) in up to 80% over time; mild cognitive impairment earlier
- Autonomic features: orthostatic hypotension; urinary urgency; sexual dysfunction; excessive sweating
- Pain: musculoskeletal, dystonic, central pain — common and underrecognised
- Psychosis (hallucinations): usually visual; commonly dopaminergic medication side effect; may indicate disease progression

**Red flags (atypical — suggest Parkinson's-plus syndromes, not idiopathic PD):**
- Symmetric onset (PD is typically asymmetric)
- Early falls (within 1 year of onset) — suggests PSP (progressive supranuclear palsy)
- Early autonomic failure (severe OH, urinary incontinence within 1 year) — suggests MSA
- Rapid progression
- Poor response to levodopa
- If red flags present — neurology referral essential; brain MRI

## Assessment Approach

**Initial assessment (GP — refer to neurology on suspicion):**
- Movement disorder assessment: observe gait (shuffling, reduced arm swing, festination); handwriting (micrographia); speech (hypophonia, monotone)
- Medication history: dopamine antagonists cause drug-induced parkinsonism (metoclopramide, prochlorperazine, haloperidol, risperidone, quetiapine, olanzapine) — cessation may resolve symptoms
- Screen for drug-induced parkinsonism before labelling idiopathic PD
- Refer to neurology for: new diagnosis confirmation, DaTscan if uncertain, management initiation

**Ongoing GP monitoring:**
- Motor control: patient report of "on/off" fluctuations; wearing off between doses; dyskinesias (involuntary movements — peak-dose levodopa effect)
- Fall risk: STEADI fall risk assessment; home modification; physiotherapy for gait and balance
- Medication timing compliance: levodopa must be taken regularly; off periods due to missed or late doses are a common GP management issue
- Nutrition: protein distribution (large protein meals can impede levodopa absorption — take levodopa 30–60 min before meals)
- Cognitive review: annual MMSE or MoCA; PDD assessment; driving safety review
- Mood: PHQ-9 for depression; GAD-7 for anxiety — treat actively
- Autonomic symptoms: orthostatic hypotension (measure lying/standing BP); bladder symptoms; constipation management
- Driving: AUSTROADS standards — Parkinson's can impair driving; notify licensing authority if unsafe; annual medical fitness to drive assessment

## Key Differentials

- Drug-induced parkinsonism: dopamine antagonist exposure (metoclopramide, antipsychotics); usually symmetric; resolves with cessation; can persist months; commonest mimic
- Essential tremor: action tremor (not resting); head and voice tremor; family history; alcohol-responsive; no bradykinesia or rigidity
- Progressive supranuclear palsy (PSP): early falls; downward gaze palsy; symmetrical; rapid progression; poor levodopa response
- Multiple system atrophy (MSA): early autonomic failure and cerebellar features; poor levodopa response; rapid progression; neurology
- Vascular parkinsonism: lower body parkinsonism; gait disorder; cognitive impairment; brain imaging shows white matter changes; no resting tremor; poor levodopa response
- Alzheimer's disease: dementia before motor features; no resting tremor
- Lewy body dementia (DLB): dementia within 1 year of motor onset; visual hallucinations; fluctuating cognition; RBD; avoid antipsychotics (extreme sensitivity)

## Management Principles

**Neurologist-initiated pharmacotherapy — GP co-manages:**

Levodopa + carbidopa/benserazide (Sinemet, Madopar — cornerstone of PD treatment):
- Most effective symptomatic therapy; gold standard
- Carbidopa/benserazide prevents peripheral conversion of levodopa, reducing nausea and increasing CNS delivery
- Initially start low and titrate slowly; motor complications (wearing off, dyskinesias) develop after years of use
- Modified-release preparations: Sinemet CR, Madopar HBS — used for nocturnal control
- Levodopa-carbidopa intestinal gel (Duodopa): continuous infusion via PEG-J tube for advanced fluctuations — specialist

Dopamine agonists (adjunct or initial monotherapy in younger patients):
- Pramipexole (Sifrol): first-line for younger onset PD (delay levodopa motor complications); nausea, orthostatic hypotension, impulse control disorders (gambling, hypersexuality) — warn patients
- Rotigotine patch: once-daily; useful for consistent delivery; same monitoring as oral dopamine agonists

MAO-B inhibitors:
- Rasagiline, selegiline: mild symptomatic benefit; neuroprotective claims unproven; useful early disease or as adjunct

COMT inhibitors:
- Entacapone (Comtan): extends levodopa effect; used for wearing-off; combined with Sinemet as Stalevo
- Orange urine discolouration — warn patients

**Non-pharmacological (essential — not optional):**
- Physiotherapy: gait training, balance, LSVT BIG (high-amplitude movement therapy); fall prevention — highly effective; refer early
- Speech therapy: LSVT LOUD (Lee Silverman Voice Treatment) — improves hypophonia; commence early
- Occupational therapy: aids, workplace adaptation, driving assessment
- Dietitian: protein distribution; weight management; dysphagia diet if needed
- Parkinson's Australia: nurse specialist, support groups, resources — national organisation
- Exercise: evidence shows exercise slows progression; regular moderate-to-high intensity physical activity recommended by MDS guidelines

**Non-motor management (GP role):**
- Constipation: high-fibre diet, adequate hydration, macrogol; docusate; bisacodyl if needed
- Orthostatic hypotension: compression stockings, increased salt and fluid intake; fludrocortisone or midodrine if severe; avoid triggers (large meals, alcohol, dehydration)
- Depression: SSRIs (sertraline, escitalopram) first-line; TCAs increase fall risk; avoid MAOIs concurrently with selegiline/rasagiline (serotonin syndrome risk)
- Psychosis / hallucinations: first exclude and address infections, dehydration, medication excess; reduce dopaminergic medications cautiously; clozapine (most evidence — requires regular FBC monitoring); quetiapine (less evidence but used); avoid other antipsychotics (worsen parkinsonism, dangerous in DLB)
- Bladder: urgency — oxybutynin (note: anticholinergic load; use with caution in PD due to cognitive effects); mirabegron preferred as less anticholinergic
- Sleep: RBD — clonazepam 0.25–0.5 mg nocte; melatonin 3–12 mg (safer in elderly); safety environment

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Levodopa + carbidopa (Sinemet)** (PBS restricted: Parkinson's disease; authority required; neurologist initiation; titrate dose with neurologist guidance)
- **Levodopa + benserazide (Madopar)** (PBS restricted: Parkinson's disease; alternative to Sinemet)
- **Pramipexole (Sifrol)** (PBS restricted: Parkinson's disease; authority required; warn re impulse control disorders; monitor BP)
- **Rasagiline** (PBS restricted: Parkinson's disease; early monotherapy or adjunct; drug interactions with SSRIs/meperidine — serotonin syndrome risk — check interactions)
- **Entacapone (Comtan)** (PBS restricted: as adjunct to levodopa for wearing off; orange urine)
- **Clozapine** (PBS restricted: psychosis — requires CPMS registration and weekly FBC monitoring for agranulocytosis; use is specialist-managed)
- **Note:** Medication timing is critical in PD; never abruptly cease dopaminergic medication — neuroleptic malignant syndrome-like state (Parkinson's hyperpyrexia syndrome) can occur

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Parkinson's hyperpyrexia syndrome: sudden cessation of dopaminergic medication; fever, rigidity, confusion, autonomic instability — medical emergency; IV dopaminergic rescue required in hospital
- Falls with injury (fractures, head injury) — common complication; arrange urgent assessment
- Aspiration pneumonia: advanced PD with dysphagia; 000 if respiratory compromise

**Urgent neurology review:**
- Sudden worsening of motor function (consider infection, medication change, missed doses as first cause)
- New psychosis in PD — urgent review with neurologist to adjust medications
- Severe dyskinesias or on/off fluctuations requiring medication adjustment
- Suspected deep brain stimulation malfunction (DBS patients) — contact DBS centre

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Neurology: ongoing specialist co-management for all PD patients; consider DBS referral for advanced motor fluctuations
- Physiotherapist: gait, balance, LSVT BIG — routine referral for all
- Speech pathologist: hypophonia, dysphagia — LSVT LOUD
- Occupational therapist: home safety, driving assessment
- Parkinson's Australia: 1800 644 189 — support, education, Parkinson's nurse specialist

## Telehealth Suitability

Parkinson's monitoring is suitable for telehealth in stable patients between specialist reviews.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Non-motor symptom review (mood, sleep, constipation, bladder)
- Medication adherence and timing review
- Results interpretation
- Falls risk counselling and home modification advice
- Driving safety counselling and documentation
- Mental Health Care Plan (psychology referral)
- Referral coordination

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Motor examination (tremor at rest, rigidity, bradykinesia, gait)
- Pull test (postural instability)
- Lying/standing BP for orthostatic hypotension
- Cognitive testing (MMSE, MoCA) — in-person preferred
- Blood pressure monitoring
- Specialist neurologist motor assessment — in-person
