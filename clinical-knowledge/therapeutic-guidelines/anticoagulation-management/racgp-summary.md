## Anticoagulation Management (RACGP)

Anticoagulation therapy is prescribed in general practice primarily for: stroke prevention in atrial fibrillation (AF), treatment and secondary prevention of venous thromboembolism (VTE — DVT and pulmonary embolism), and mechanical heart valve anticoagulation. Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs — apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran, edoxaban) have largely replaced warfarin for most indications due to their predictable pharmacokinetics, no need for routine INR monitoring, fewer drug and food interactions, and equivalent or superior efficacy and safety. Warfarin remains indicated for mechanical heart valves and some other specific situations. GP management of anticoagulation includes prescribing, monitoring, managing bleeding complications, perioperative management, and medication interactions. Telehealth is well-suited for anticoagulation management reviews.

## Clinical Presentation

**Indications for anticoagulation in general practice:**
- AF with elevated stroke risk (CHA₂DS₂-VASc ≥2 in men, ≥3 in women): DOACs first-line (see `atrial-fibrillation` entry)
- VTE treatment: DVT and/or PE — DOACs first-line for most patients (see `thrombosis-dvt` entry)
- VTE secondary prevention: extended anticoagulation after unprovoked or recurrent VTE
- Mechanical heart valves: warfarin only (DOACs are NOT safe in mechanical valves — valve thrombosis)

**Bleeding risk — clinical assessment:**
- HAS-BLED score: Hypertension (SBP >160), Abnormal renal/liver function, Stroke history, Bleeding history, Labile INR, Elderly (≥65), Drugs (antiplatelet/NSAIDs)/alcohol; each = 1 point; ≥3 = high bleeding risk
- Bleeding risk should be assessed but should NOT preclude anticoagulation if benefit (stroke/PE prevention) outweighs risk — most AF patients with high stroke risk AND high bleeding risk still benefit from anticoagulation

## Assessment Approach

**Baseline and ongoing assessment for patients on anticoagulation:**

DOACs (apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran, edoxaban):
- No routine monitoring required (no INR equivalent)
- Annual renal function (eGFR): critical — all DOACs renally cleared to varying degrees; dose adjustment or discontinuation at low eGFR thresholds (drug-specific):
  - Apixaban: dose halve if 2 of 3 (age ≥80, weight ≤60 kg, creatinine ≥133 µmol/L); generally safe to eGFR 15 but dose adjust
  - Rivaroxaban: avoid if eGFR <15; dose adjust at eGFR 15–49 for AF indication
  - Dabigatran: avoid if eGFR <30 in AF; most renally cleared; check at start and annually
  - Edoxaban: avoid if eGFR <15 or >95 mL/min (reduced efficacy at very high eGFR)
- Annual FBC: check for anaemia (may indicate occult bleeding)
- LFTs: baseline; important for liver disease — may affect drug metabolism
- Medication interactions: review at every prescription change

Warfarin:
- INR monitoring: target INR 2.0–3.0 (most indications); 2.5–3.5 (mechanical mitral valve)
- Stable on warfarin: INR every 6–8 weeks
- Unstable INR: INR every 1–2 weeks until stable
- Target INR: check indication carefully — different targets for different valves and conditions
- Warfarin interactions: many — CYP2C9 substrates; antibiotics (azithromycin, fluoroquinolones, metronidazole); amiodarone (major — raises INR markedly); alcohol (variable); dietary vitamin K changes (greens — consistent intake not avoidance is key)
- Patient education: consistent diet, medication changes, alcohol, illness

**Drug interactions with DOACs (clinically significant):**
- P-glycoprotein (P-gp) and CYP3A4 interactions: all DOACs
- Amiodarone: increases all DOAC levels — dose adjustment may be needed
- Rifampicin, carbamazepine, phenytoin, St John's Wort: reduce DOAC levels — avoid combination or switch anticoagulant
- Azole antifungals (fluconazole, itraconazole): increase apixaban and rivaroxaban levels
- Aspirin ± NSAID: significantly increases bleeding risk when added to DOAC — minimise; PPI co-prescription if combined

## Key Differentials

- Anticoagulant overdose vs bleeding: distinguish supratherapeutic anticoagulation from new bleeding cause (peptic ulcer, GI cancer, trauma) — always investigate bleeding source even if INR/DOAC elevated
- Thrombocytopenia: low platelet count worsens bleeding risk; HIT (heparin-induced thrombocytopenia) — rare but serious; specialist involvement
- Liver disease: impairs clotting factors and drug metabolism; warfarin very difficult to manage in liver disease; haematology input for anticoagulation in cirrhosis

## Management Principles

**DOAC selection (AF or VTE indication):**

Apixaban (Eliquis):
- 5 mg twice daily (standard AF dose); 2.5 mg twice daily if dose reduction criteria met (2 of 3: age ≥80, weight ≤60 kg, creatinine ≥133 µmol/L)
- VTE treatment: 10 mg twice daily for 7 days then 5 mg twice daily (no heparin bridge needed)
- Lowest bleeding risk of DOACs (including intracranial — ARISTOTLE, AMPLIFY trials); preferred in elderly and high bleeding risk patients

Rivaroxaban (Xarelto):
- AF: 20 mg once daily with evening meal; 15 mg if eGFR 15–49
- VTE: 15 mg twice daily for 3 weeks with food, then 20 mg once daily
- Once daily (AF) advantageous for adherence; take with food (increases absorption)

Dabigatran (Pradaxa):
- AF: 150 mg twice daily; 110 mg twice daily if ≥80 years, or on verapamil, or high bleeding risk
- More GI side effects; reverse with idarucizumab (Praxbind); most renally eliminated — check eGFR closely

Edoxaban (Lixiana):
- AF: 60 mg once daily; 30 mg if eGFR 15–50, body weight ≤60 kg, or on certain P-gp inhibitors
- Less commonly used in Australian primary care

**Warfarin management:**
- Initiation: loading dose approach (usual daily doses in most patients — avoid excessive loading in elderly)
- INR monitoring: as above; use validated dosing apps or nomogram for dose adjustment
- Target INR 2.0–3.0 for most; 2.5–3.5 for mechanical mitral valve
- Missed dose: if INR within range — take missed dose; if highly elevated INR due to missed doses — seek advice
- High INR management: INR 3.0–4.5 (no bleeding) — reduce or hold dose; monitor; INR 4.5–10 (no major bleeding) — hold warfarin; consider vitamin K 1–2 mg oral; INR >10 or major bleeding — hospital; vitamin K IV ± PCC (prothrombin complex concentrate)

**Perioperative anticoagulation management:**
- Stop DOAC: 1–2 days before low bleeding-risk procedure; 2–4 days before high bleeding-risk surgery (longer for dabigatran and/or reduced eGFR)
- Warfarin: stop 5 days before surgery; bridge with LMWH (e.g., enoxaparin) only if mechanical heart valve, high stroke risk AF, or recent VTE (within 3 months) — most AF patients do NOT require bridging (BRIDGE trial)
- Restart: usually 24–48 hours post-procedure for DOAC; warfarin 1–2 days post-procedure

**Reversal agents (hospital setting):**
- Dabigatran: idarucizumab (Praxbind) — rapid reversal; available in hospitals
- Factor Xa inhibitors (apixaban, rivaroxaban, edoxaban): andexanet alfa (Ondexxya) or PCC (prothrombin complex concentrate — off-label, rapid, available)
- Warfarin: vitamin K IV + PCC; or FFP if PCC unavailable

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Apixaban** (Eliquis — PBS restricted: stroke prevention in AF; treatment and prevention of VTE; authority required; renal dose adjustment criteria apply)
- **Rivaroxaban** (Xarelto — PBS restricted: AF, DVT/PE treatment; authority required; take with evening meal for AF indication)
- **Dabigatran** (Pradaxa — PBS restricted: AF, VTE; authority required; eGFR monitoring critical; idarucizumab reversal agent)
- **Warfarin** (PBS unrestricted — indications including mechanical heart valves, AF, VTE; INR monitoring required; many drug/food interactions)
- **Enoxaparin** (Clexane — PBS restricted: VTE treatment and prevention; DVT treatment bridge; LMWH; dose-adjust at eGFR <30)
- **Vitamin K** (phytomenadione — PBS unrestricted; warfarin reversal; 1–5 mg oral; larger IV doses in hospital for emergency reversal)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Major bleeding on anticoagulation: intracranial (sudden headache, neurological deterioration), GI (haematemesis, melaena, haematochezia with haemodynamic compromise), retroperitoneal, intraocular — reversal agent and resuscitation required
- Suspected stroke/TIA in patient on anticoagulation — emergency imaging (CT brain)
- Warfarin INR >10 or INR >4.5 with significant haemorrhage — vitamin K IV + PCC

**Urgent same-day assessment:**
- Warfarin INR 4.5–10 without bleeding — hold warfarin; consider oral vitamin K 1–2 mg; review in 24 hours
- Unexplained anaemia on anticoagulation (new or progressive) — investigate for occult bleeding (GI most common)
- Suspected new thrombosis (DVT, PE) despite anticoagulation — imaging and haematology/specialist

**Non-urgent management:**
- DOAC to warfarin switch (mechanical valve requirement): overlap with warfarin until INR in range for ≥2 days; then stop DOAC
- Anticoagulation holiday for procedure: coordinate with proceduralist; individualise bridging based on indication and risk

## Telehealth Suitability

Anticoagulation management is highly suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- DOAC monitoring review (renal function results, medication interactions, adherence)
- Warfarin INR result review and dose adjustment counselling
- Perioperative anticoagulation management planning
- Bleeding risk assessment and management planning
- DOAC initiation prescribing for AF or VTE
- Patient education on missed doses, travel, alcohol

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Unexplained bleeding — physical examination
- INR testing — pathology collection
- Blood tests (eGFR, FBC) — pathology
