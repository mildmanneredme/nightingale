## Bell's Palsy Assessment (RACGP)

Bell's palsy is idiopathic acute unilateral peripheral facial nerve (cranial nerve VII) palsy — the most common cause of unilateral facial weakness, accounting for 60–75% of all acute facial palsies. It affects approximately 20–30 per 100,000 population per year in Australia. The aetiology is thought to involve herpes simplex virus (HSV-1) reactivation causing facial nerve inflammation and oedema within the facial canal. Bell's palsy involves all branches of the facial nerve causing both upper and lower facial weakness (key distinction from central/stroke lesions). Most patients recover fully (70–80%) within 3–6 months with appropriate treatment. Prednisolone significantly improves outcomes when started within 72 hours. GPs play a central role in: diagnosing Bell's palsy, excluding central causes (stroke), initiating time-critical corticosteroid treatment, and managing eye care to prevent corneal complications. Telehealth has limited utility for initial assessment of facial weakness — urgent in-person evaluation required.

## Clinical Presentation

**Typical presentation:**
- Sudden onset (hours): unilateral facial weakness developing over hours; may notice on waking
- Upper AND lower face involved: inability to close eye (lagophthalmos), eyebrow drooping, forehead line absence, drooping corner of mouth, difficulty with lip closure
- Ipsilateral symptoms: reduced or absent forehead wrinkling (cannot raise eyebrow); taste disturbance (anterior 2/3 tongue — chorda tympani involvement); hyperacusis (stapedius involvement — sounds louder on affected side); reduced lacrimation; ear pain or discomfort (otalgia)
- Grade (House-Brackmann scale): I = normal; VI = complete paralysis; grade at diagnosis to track recovery

**Key clinical feature distinguishing Bell's palsy from STROKE:**
- Bell's palsy (peripheral CN VII lesion): upper AND lower face affected; CANNOT raise eyebrow; cannot close eye
- Central facial palsy (stroke — UMN lesion): UPPER face SPARED; can wrinkle forehead and close eye on affected side; lower face only weak
- If patient can raise forehead / wrinkle brow on the affected side → CENTRAL lesion → 000 / ED IMMEDIATELY

**Associated features:**
- Herpes zoster oticus (Ramsay Hunt syndrome): facial palsy + vesicles in ear canal (herpes zoster distribution) + sensorineural hearing loss + vertigo; more severe prognosis than Bell's palsy; same treatment but add antiviral; ENT referral

## Assessment Approach

**Urgent assessment on presentation:**
1. Confirm peripheral (LMN) vs central (UMN) facial palsy:
   - Test: ask patient to wrinkle forehead, raise eyebrows, close eyes tightly, smile, show teeth, puff cheeks
   - Peripheral Bell's: ALL movements affected including upper face; cannot close eye; cannot raise brow
   - Central/stroke: forehead and eye closure preserved; lower face weak — emergency
2. Time of onset: <72 hours = time-critical for steroid treatment
3. Examine ear canal: vesicles = Ramsay Hunt syndrome (requires antiviral)
4. Check: other cranial nerve deficits, limb weakness, ataxia — would suggest brainstem lesion, NOT Bell's palsy

**Physical examination:**
- CN VII (all branches): forehead, eye closure, lower face — House-Brackmann grading
- CN V: facial sensation intact (sensation carried by CN V, NOT CN VII — if facial numbness, consider different diagnosis)
- Otoscopy: vesicles in external ear canal (Ramsay Hunt); AOM (rare cause of facial palsy via middle ear infection)
- Other CN: III, VI (eye movements), VIII (hearing, balance) — brainstem lesion if multiple CN involved
- Parotid: parotid mass can compress CN VII — examine parotid gland

**Investigations:**
- Blood tests: NOT routinely required for typical Bell's palsy
- Consider: FBS (diabetes — risk factor); serology if Lyme disease suspected (tick exposure in endemic region — not common in Australia); HIV if risk factors; sarcoid if bilateral or other features
- MRI brain: indicated if atypical features — central signs, multiple CN involvement, very slow progression, recurrent ipsilateral palsy, parotid mass, failure to improve by 3 months; urgent if central signs
- CT brain: only if MRI unavailable and stroke must be excluded urgently

## Key Differentials

- Stroke / TIA (central facial palsy): MOST CRITICAL differential — forehead spared, upper face preserved; 000 immediately; brain CT/MRI urgent
- Ramsay Hunt syndrome: Bell's palsy + herpetic vesicles in ear/mouth + SNHL + vertigo; worse prognosis; add antivirals; ENT
- Lyme disease: tick exposure history; may cause bilateral facial palsy; Lyme serology; not endemic in Australia — consider in returned travellers
- Sarcoidosis: bilateral facial palsy or associated uveitis, parotid enlargement, skin lesions; ACE level, chest X-ray
- Parotid tumour: slow onset; parotid mass palpable; MRI parotid; ENT
- Otitis media / mastoiditis: ear pain + facial palsy; otoscopy; CT temporal bone; ENT emergency
- Cholesteatoma: slowly progressive facial palsy; otoscopy; CT/MRI; ENT
- Acoustic neuroma: usually gradual progressive hearing loss before any facial weakness; MRI IAM
- Guillain-Barré syndrome (GBS): bilateral facial palsy + ascending weakness; areflexia; CSF; 000

## Management Principles

**Corticosteroids (prednisolone) — most important treatment:**
- Start within 72 hours of onset for maximum benefit — time critical
- Prednisolone 50 mg daily for 10 days, then taper (or 1 mg/kg/day, maximum 80 mg, × 10 days)
- Evidence: significantly improves complete recovery rate (NNT ~7 to gain complete recovery at 3 months)
- PBS-listed: PBS unrestricted for short course
- Caution: diabetes (glucose monitoring), hypertension, peptic ulcer (add pantoprazole if high risk), immunosuppression

**Antiviral therapy — adjunct (add to prednisolone, do NOT use alone):**
- Aciclovir or valaciclovir: benefit of adding antivirals to corticosteroids is modest and uncertain for Bell's palsy alone; most Australian and international guidelines recommend prednisolone alone as first-line; antivirals may be added for severe palsy (House-Brackmann V–VI)
- Ramsay Hunt syndrome: antivirals (valaciclovir 1 g TDS × 7–10 days) + prednisolone — both required; antivirals clearly beneficial in Ramsay Hunt (herpes zoster)

**Eye care (critical — prevent corneal injury from lagophthalmos):**
- Artificial tear drops: Systane, Refresh — hourly during waking hours if incomplete eye closure
- Eye lubricant gel (Lacrilube) at night: prevents corneal desiccation overnight
- Eye patch or tape shut overnight: prevent exposure keratopathy if eye cannot close
- Moisture chamber goggles: for outdoor activities or prolonged dryness
- Ophthalmology referral: if any corneal symptoms (pain, redness, visual blurring) — corneal ulcer/keratitis risk
- Sunglasses outdoors: protect against UV and wind

**Physiotherapy:**
- Facial physiotherapy: exercises for nerve recovery; neuromuscular retraining; reduce synkinesis (abnormal facial movements during recovery)
- Refer for physiotherapy if incomplete recovery at 3 months

**Supportive:**
- Analgesia: paracetamol/NSAIDs for otalgia (ear pain)
- Patient reassurance: 70–80% full recovery within 3–6 months; most reassuring in young patients with incomplete palsy and early steroid treatment
- House-Brackmann grading at each review to track recovery

**Prognosis:**
- Good prognosis: incomplete palsy (some voluntary movement), young age, early treatment
- Poorer prognosis: complete palsy, age >60, Ramsay Hunt (30% incomplete recovery), hypertension, diabetes
- Synkinesis: abnormal co-movements during recovery (eye closes when smiling) — physiotherapy helps

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Prednisolone** (PBS unrestricted: inflammatory conditions; 50 mg daily × 10 days then taper; start within 72 hours; add PPI (pantoprazole) if GI risk; monitor glucose in diabetes)
- **Valaciclovir 500 mg** (PBS restricted: herpes zoster; 1 g TDS × 7–10 days for Ramsay Hunt syndrome; add to prednisolone)
- **Artificial tears (Systane, Refresh)** (PBS unrestricted or OTC: lubricant eye drops; prescribe liberally; use hourly during waking hours for incomplete eye closure)
- **Lacrilube eye ointment** (PBS unrestricted: lubricant eye ointment; apply before sleep; prevents nocturnal exposure keratopathy)
- **Pantoprazole 20 mg** (PBS unrestricted: gastric protection; consider with 10-day prednisolone course if peptic ulcer risk or on NSAIDs)
- **Note:** Prednisolone alone is first-line for Bell's palsy; antiviral addition for Bell's palsy remains uncertain from evidence base; antivirals are clearly indicated for Ramsay Hunt syndrome

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Central facial palsy: forehead/upper face spared on affected side — possible stroke; do NOT delay; 000
- GBS (bilateral facial palsy + ascending weakness + areflexia): 000 — respiratory failure risk
- Otogenic facial palsy (mastoiditis, cholesteatoma): ear pain + facial palsy + fever/toxicity — ENT emergency

**Urgent ED or ENT:**
- Mastoiditis or complicated otitis media causing facial palsy: CT temporal bone; IV antibiotics; surgical drainage
- Corneal ulcer or keratitis from exposure: ophthalmology urgently; corneal scarring risk

**Non-urgent ENT/neurology:**
- Failure to improve at 3 months: MRI brain and temporal bone to exclude structural cause; ENT or neurology review
- Recurrent ipsilateral Bell's palsy: must exclude parotid tumour, sarcoidosis, melkersson-rosenthal syndrome; MRI and specialist referral
- Synkinesis: physiotherapy ± botulinum toxin (specialist) for facial re-education

## Telehealth Suitability

Bell's palsy initial assessment requires in-person evaluation; follow-up suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Follow-up monitoring of recovery (progress photos; House-Brackmann verbal assessment)
- Medication compliance check (prednisolone course completion)
- Eye care education and compliance
- Physiotherapy referral and review

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial facial nerve assessment: ESSENTIAL to exclude stroke; cannot safely diagnose via telehealth
- CN examination to exclude central lesion or multiple CN involvement
- Otoscopy (Ramsay Hunt)
- Corneal examination if eye symptoms
- MRI referral for atypical cases
