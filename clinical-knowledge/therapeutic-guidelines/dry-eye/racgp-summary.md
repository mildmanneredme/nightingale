## Dry Eye Assessment (RACGP)

Dry eye disease (DED) is a multifactorial disease of the ocular surface characterised by loss of homeostasis of the tear film, causing ocular symptoms and sometimes visual disturbance. It is one of the most prevalent eye conditions worldwide — affecting approximately 20–30% of adults in Australia, with higher prevalence in women, older adults, and contact lens wearers. DED significantly impairs quality of life through persistent eye discomfort, visual fluctuation, and impact on screen-based work. Two main types: aqueous-deficient dry eye (reduced lacrimal secretion) and evaporative dry eye (increased tear evaporation — meibomian gland dysfunction/MGD is most common cause). GPs play a central role in: diagnosing DED, initiating artificial tear therapy, identifying modifiable risk factors and contributing medications, and referring to optometrist or ophthalmologist for moderate-severe disease. Telehealth is suitable for initial assessment and treatment initiation; slit-lamp examination requires in-person review.

## Clinical Presentation

**Symptoms:**
- Dryness: gritty, sandy, foreign body sensation — most common symptom
- Burning or stinging: particularly in dry/windy environments, air conditioning
- Fluctuating or blurred vision: transiently improves with blinking; worse with reading, screen use, driving
- Paradoxical watering (epiphora): reflex tear overproduction in response to dryness — patients present with "watery eyes" but actually have dry eye
- Light sensitivity (photophobia)
- Eye fatigue, difficulty with sustained reading or computer work
- Worse in: low humidity environments (air conditioning, heated rooms, aeroplanes), wind, sustained visual tasks
- Contact lens intolerance: DED major cause of contact lens discontinuation

**Risk factors:**
- Age: increasing prevalence with age; post-menopausal women particularly affected
- Female sex: hormonal influence on tear composition
- Screen use: reduced blink rate on screens (from 15 blinks/min to 5/min) — major modifiable factor
- Contact lens wear
- Prior eye surgery: LASIK, cataract — transient corneal nerve damage reduces reflex tearing
- Systemic disease: Sjögren's syndrome (sicca syndrome — dry eyes + dry mouth; autoimmune), rheumatoid arthritis, SLE, hypothyroidism, diabetes
- Medications: antihistamines, anticholinergics, antidepressants (TCAs, SSRIs), beta-blockers, diuretics, isotretinoin — all reduce tear production
- Environment: low humidity, air conditioning, smoke
- Nutritional: omega-3 deficiency

## Assessment Approach

**Clinical assessment:**
- OSDI (Ocular Surface Disease Index): 12-item validated questionnaire; scores 0–100; mild 13–22; moderate 23–32; severe >33; useful for severity and monitoring response
- Eye examination:
  - Ocular surface: conjunctival hyperaemia, lid margin irregularity, meibomian gland orifice plugging, blepharitis (anterior or posterior)
  - Tear meniscus height on slit lamp (optometrist/ophthalmologist): reduced (<0.2 mm) = aqueous deficiency
  - TBUT (Tear Break-Up Time): time for first dry spot after blink; <10 seconds = abnormal; <5 seconds = significant — requires slit lamp

**Investigations (if secondary cause suspected):**
- ANA, anti-SSA/SSB (Ro/La): Sjögren's syndrome — dry eyes + dry mouth + salivary gland enlargement + positive antibodies
- TSH: hypothyroidism associated with dry eye
- Blood glucose: diabetes → corneal neuropathy → reduced reflex tearing
- Schirmer's test (optometrist/ophthalmologist): filter paper placed in conjunctival sac × 5 minutes; <10 mm = reduced aqueous production

**Medication review:**
- Identify and consider reducing or switching: antihistamines (cetirizine), TCAs, SSRIs, anticholinergics, beta-blockers, diuretics, isotretinoin

## Key Differentials

- Blepharitis: eyelid margin inflammation; anterior (dandruff-like crusting) or posterior (meibomian gland dysfunction); closely related to evaporative DED; treat underlying blepharitis
- Allergic conjunctivitis: pruritus (itch) predominates; allergic history; seasonal; bilateral; papillary reaction
- Conjunctivitis (infective): discharge; see `conjunctivitis` entry
- Sjögren's syndrome: systemic autoimmune; dry eyes + dry mouth + fatigue; ANA, anti-SSA/SSB; rheumatology
- Corneal erosion or abrasion: acute pain, lacrimation, photophobia; fluorescein staining; urgent ophthalmology
- Superior limbic keratoconjunctivitis: recurrent superior bulbar conjunctival inflammation; thyroid disease association
- Exposure keratopathy: inadequate lid closure; Bell's palsy; orbital disease — cornea exposed

## Management Principles

**Lifestyle modifications:**
- Reduce screen time or increase break frequency: 20-20-20 rule — every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet (6 m) away for 20 seconds; deliberately blink more while on screens
- Environment: humidifier in bedroom and home office; reduce air conditioning direct flow to eyes; wraparound spectacles outdoors in wind
- Dietary omega-3: fatty fish (salmon, mackerel) 2–3× weekly or fish oil supplementation (2–3 g EPA/DHA daily) — evidence for reducing evaporative DED from MGD
- Reduce/cease contact lens wear temporarily during flares

**Artificial tear drops (cornerstone of treatment):**
- Carmellose (carboxymethylcellulose — Celufresh, Celluvisc): preservative-free; viscous; good for MGD; available OTC; PBS-listed
- Hypromellose (Isopto Plain, GenTeal): OTC; mild lubricant; less viscous; 1–2 drops 4–6 hourly
- Polyvinyl alcohol drops: OTC; less viscous
- Preservative-free drops: essential for frequent use (>4–6 times daily) — preserved drops cause preservative-related toxicity with frequent instillation; use multidose preservative-free (Refresh, Systane Ultra preservative-free) or single-dose ampoules
- Viscous gels (Lacrilube, Refresh Celluvisc): at night for overnight lubrication; too blurring for daytime use
- Sodium hyaluronate (Hylo-Fresh, Artelac): high retention; good for moderate DED; preservative-free; twice daily to QDS; evidence superior to conventional drops
- Dosing: mild DED — 2–4 times daily; moderate-severe — every 1–2 hours with preservative-free

**Meibomian gland dysfunction (MGD) / blepharitis treatment:**
- Warm compresses: 5–10 minutes daily; softens meibomian secretions; increase lipid layer
- Lid massage: after warm compress; express meibomian gland secretions
- Lid hygiene (eyelid scrubs): diluted baby shampoo or commercial eyelid wipes (OCuSOFT); BD; reduces bacterial load
- Omega-3 supplementation: addresses MGD specifically; 2–3 g/day fish oil

**Second-line pharmacological options (moderate-severe DED):**
- Topical cyclosporine A 0.1% (Cequa, Restasis): anti-inflammatory; reduces ocular surface inflammation; PBS-listed for moderate-severe DED in Australia; twice daily; improvement at 3 months; persistent use needed; slight stinging on instillation initially
- Topical corticosteroids: short course fluorometholone (FML) for acute inflammatory DED flares; specialist prescription; not long-term (glaucoma, cataract risk)
- Doxycycline oral 50 mg daily × 3 months: anti-inflammatory + improves meibomian gland function; off-label but evidence-based for MGD-related evaporative DED; PBS-unrestricted

**Punctal plugs:**
- Optometrist/ophthalmologist procedure: silicone plugs inserted into lacrimal puncta (lower > upper); reduce tear drainage; retain tears on ocular surface; reversible
- Suitable for aqueous-deficient DED with good response to artificial tears

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Carmellose eye drops (Celufresh)** (PBS restricted: dry eye — listed indication; preservative-free; 1 drop QDS–8× daily; minimal absorption systemically)
- **Sodium hyaluronate (Hylo-Fresh)** (PBS restricted: dry eye; preservative-free multidose bottle; once opened lasts 3–6 months; twice daily initially; titrate to response)
- **Cyclosporine 0.1% eye drops (Cequa)** (PBS restricted: moderate-severe dry eye — specifically listed; twice daily; onset of effect 1–3 months; continuing use required; slight burning initially; not suitable for infective conjunctivitis)
- **Doxycycline 50–100 mg daily** (PBS unrestricted: off-label for MGD/evaporative DED; anti-inflammatory effect on meibomian glands; 3–6 months course; sun sensitivity; avoid in pregnancy)
- **Note:** Preserved eye drops cause preservative (benzalkonium chloride) toxicity with frequent dosing (>4× daily); always prescribe preservative-free formulations when frequent instillation required; this is particularly important for glaucoma patients on preserved eye drops

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent ophthalmology:**
- Corneal ulcer: painful red eye, fluorescein staining positive, visual acuity reduction — immediate ophthalmology
- Severe corneal exposure with keratopathy (lagophthalmos from Bell's palsy, proptosis): urgent ophthalmology
- Vision loss in DED patient: ophthalmology urgently

**Non-urgent optometrist or ophthalmologist:**
- Moderate-severe DED (OSDI ≥23) not responding to 3 months of artificial tears: slit lamp examination, TBUT, Schirmer's test, specialist assessment
- Sjögren's syndrome-related dry eye: co-management with rheumatology
- Contact lens intolerance from DED: optometrist for lens type change or cessation
- Cyclosporine initiation: optometrist or ophthalmologist in Australia typically assess prior to initiation

## Telehealth Suitability

Dry eye management is well-suited to telehealth for initial assessment and ongoing review.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- OSDI questionnaire assessment
- Medication review (identifying contributing drugs)
- Lifestyle and dietary counselling
- Artificial tear drop prescribing and education
- Doxycycline initiation for MGD
- Sjögren's serology ordering
- Referral coordination

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Slit lamp examination (TBUT, tear meniscus, corneal staining)
- Schirmer's test
- Punctal plug insertion (optometrist/ophthalmologist)
- Fluorescein staining for corneal erosion assessment
