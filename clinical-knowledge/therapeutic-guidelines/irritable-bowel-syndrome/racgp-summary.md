## Irritable Bowel Syndrome Assessment (RACGP)

Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) is a common functional bowel disorder characterised by recurrent abdominal pain associated with defaecation or a change in bowel habit, in the absence of organic pathology. It affects approximately 10–15% of Australians and is one of the most common GI presentations in general practice. IBS is diagnosed clinically using the Rome IV criteria after excluding red flag conditions. Management is multimodal — dietary, psychological, and pharmacological. Telehealth is highly suitable for IBS assessment and management.

## Clinical Presentation

Rome IV diagnostic criteria: recurrent abdominal pain on average ≥1 day/week in the last 3 months, associated with ≥2 of:
- Related to defaecation (pain improves or worsens with defaecation)
- Associated with a change in stool frequency
- Associated with a change in stool form (appearance)

Symptoms must have onset ≥6 months ago with current criteria met for ≥3 months.

IBS subtypes:
- IBS-C (constipation predominant): hard/lumpy stools >25% of bowel motions
- IBS-D (diarrhoea predominant): loose/watery stools >25%
- IBS-M (mixed): both hard and loose stools
- IBS-U (unclassified)

Associated symptoms: bloating, abdominal distension, urgency, sensation of incomplete evacuation, mucus in stool. Symptoms often worse with stress, dietary triggers, and menstruation.

Post-infectious IBS: onset following acute gastroenteritis in approximately 10% of cases; often IBS-D subtype.

## Assessment Approach

IBS is a positive clinical diagnosis based on Rome IV criteria in the absence of red flag features — not a diagnosis of exclusion requiring extensive investigation in typical presentations.

Minimal investigations for typical IBS in <50 year old with no red flags:
- FBC — exclude anaemia
- CRP / ESR — exclude systemic inflammation
- Coeliac serology (tTG-IgA + IgA) — coeliac disease mimics IBS and is common
- Thyroid function (TSH) — if IBS-D or IBS-C
- Consider: stool MCS/ova and parasites if post-travel or post-infectious history

Red flag features requiring further investigation (colonoscopy, imaging):
- Age >50 years with new symptoms
- Rectal bleeding
- Unexplained weight loss
- Nocturnal symptoms waking patient from sleep
- Fever
- Family history of colorectal cancer, IBD, or coeliac disease
- Positive faecal occult blood test
- Anaemia on FBC

Consider faecal calprotectin: elevated in IBD (>200 µg/g) vs normal in functional IBS — useful when IBD is suspected but colonoscopy is being avoided.

## Key Differentials

- Coeliac disease — may present identically to IBS-D; check tTG-IgA; requires duodenal biopsy on gluten-containing diet
- Inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn's, ulcerative colitis) — bloody diarrhoea, weight loss, systemic features; elevated CRP/calprotectin; colonoscopy
- Colorectal cancer — age >50, weight loss, rectal bleeding, change in bowel habit; colonoscopy
- Microscopic colitis — chronic watery diarrhoea, often post-NSAID or PPI use; colonoscopy with biopsies
- Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO) — may coexist with IBS; consider if bloating and IBS-D symptoms
- Lactose intolerance / fructose malabsorption — overlap with IBS; dietary exclusion or breath testing

## Management Principles

**Patient education:** Explain the gut-brain axis; reassure that IBS is a genuine disorder without structural damage; address anxiety about cancer or serious disease; emphasise that IBS is manageable but not curable.

**Dietary approaches:**

Low FODMAP diet: most evidence-based dietary intervention for IBS; reduces fermentable carbohydrates (Fermentable Oligosaccharides, Disaccharides, Monosaccharides, And Polyols); significantly reduces symptoms in ~50–75% of patients. Conducted in 3 phases (restriction, reintroduction, personalisation). Refer to dietitian with IBS/Low FODMAP training (Medicare CDM referral applicable); Monash University FODMAP App (smartphone resource).

General dietary advice: regular meals, avoid large meals, reduce insoluble fibre (bran) if IBS-C with bloating, increase soluble fibre (psyllium). Reduce alcohol and caffeine. Keep a food and symptom diary.

**Psychological approaches:** Gut-directed hypnotherapy and CBT have strong evidence for IBS; refer to psychologist or gastroenterological psychologist. Mental health care plan applicable.

**Pharmacotherapy (symptom-specific):**

For IBS-C:
- Psyllium husk (Metamucil) — soluble fibre; reduces stool hardness; take with adequate water
- Osmotic laxatives: macrogol (Movicol, Osmolax) — safe long-term use for constipation in IBS-C
- Linaclotide (Constella) — PBS-listed for moderate-severe IBS-C not responding to laxatives; reduces visceral hypersensitivity

For IBS-D:
- Loperamide 2 mg PRN — reduces stool frequency; not for continuous use
- Antispasmodics: hyoscine butylbromide (Buscopan — OTC; PBS-listed), mebeverine — for abdominal cramp/spasm

For pain and bloating:
- Antispasmodics: hyoscine, mebeverine, peppermint oil (OTC)
- Low-dose tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline 10–25 mg nocte): central pain modulation; useful for IBS with significant pain; titrate slowly

For IBS with comorbid anxiety/depression:
- SSRIs: more useful for IBS-D subtype; modest evidence; use if comorbid depression/anxiety

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Linaclotide** (Constella — PBS restricted: moderate-to-severe IBS-C after inadequate response to ≥2 laxatives; 290 mcg daily before breakfast)
- **Hyoscine butylbromide** (Buscopan — PBS restricted: antispasmodic; also OTC) — 10–20 mg three times daily
- **Macrogol** (Movicol, Osmolax — PBS unrestricted for constipation)
- **Psyllium husk** (Metamucil — OTC; not PBS-listed)
- **Amitriptyline** (PBS unrestricted — used at sub-antidepressant doses for IBS pain; start 10–25 mg nocte)
- **Loperamide** (PBS unrestricted — for IBS-D symptom control)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000:**
- Acute severe abdominal pain with guarding or rigidity — surgical emergency; IBS does not cause peritonism

**Urgent in-person GP or gastroenterology:**
- Any red flag features (rectal bleeding, weight loss, fever, nocturnal symptoms, new symptoms >50 years) — requires colonoscopy referral
- Sudden significant change in IBS symptom pattern — warrants re-evaluation

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Gastroenterology: diagnostic uncertainty, red flag features, refractory symptoms, consideration of colonoscopy
- Dietitian: low FODMAP dietary guidance (Medicare CDM referral; strong evidence base)
- Psychologist: gut-directed hypnotherapy or CBT for IBS (Medicare mental health care plan)

## Telehealth Suitability

IBS is highly suitable for telehealth management.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Symptom assessment and Rome IV diagnosis confirmation
- Dietary education and low FODMAP initiation guidance
- Pharmacotherapy initiation and review
- Referral coordination (dietitian, psychologist, gastroenterology)
- Mental health co-assessment (PHQ-9, GAD-7 — anxiety and depression common in IBS)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Any red flag feature requiring physical examination or colonoscopy
- Assessment of acute severe abdominal pain (surgical emergency cannot be ruled out via telehealth)
