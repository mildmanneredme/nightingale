## Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia Assessment (RACGP)

Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) is the non-malignant enlargement of the prostate gland causing lower urinary tract symptoms (LUTS). It is the most common benign tumour in men, affecting over 50% of men aged >50 years and rising to 80–90% by age 80. BPH-related LUTS significantly impacts quality of life. Management ranges from watchful waiting for mild symptoms to pharmacotherapy and surgical intervention for moderate-severe or refractory symptoms. A critical aspect of assessment is distinguishing benign BPH from prostate cancer. Telehealth is suitable for initial symptom assessment and medication review; physical examination and investigations require in-person components.

## Clinical Presentation

**LUTS classification:**
- Storage (irritative) symptoms: urinary frequency (>8 micturitions/day), urgency, urgency incontinence, nocturia (≥2 voids/night), dysuria
- Voiding (obstructive) symptoms: weak stream, hesitancy, intermittent stream, straining to void, incomplete bladder emptying, dribbling
- Post-micturition symptoms: post-void dribble, sensation of incomplete emptying

**IPSS (International Prostate Symptom Score):** 7-item validated questionnaire; scores 0–7 mild, 8–19 moderate, 20–35 severe; standard tool for BPH assessment and monitoring.

**Complications of BPH (indicating need for surgical referral):**
- Acute urinary retention (AUR) — inability to void despite full bladder; painful; catheterisation required
- Chronic urinary retention — painless, large residual volume; overflow incontinence
- Recurrent UTIs — from incomplete bladder emptying
- Bladder stones
- Upper tract dilatation and renal impairment from chronic obstruction

**Differential red flags requiring urgent assessment:**
- Haematuria — prostate cancer, bladder cancer, renal cell carcinoma must be excluded
- Weight loss, bone pain, elevated PSA — prostate cancer
- Neurological symptoms (e.g., saddle anaesthesia, lower limb weakness) — neurogenic bladder, spinal cord pathology

## Assessment Approach

**Initial assessment:**
- IPSS scoring — quantify symptom severity and impact on quality of life
- Medical history: previous urinary surgery, urethral instrumentation, neurological conditions (MS, Parkinson's, spinal injury), diabetes (autonomic neuropathy), medications causing urinary symptoms (alpha-blockers as treatment; anticholinergics, decongestants as cause)
- Urinary diary: frequency-volume chart — useful for distinguishing polyuria from storage symptoms and nocturia from nocturnal polyuria
- Examination: abdominal examination (bladder distension); digital rectal examination (DRE) — assess prostate size, consistency, tenderness, nodules

**Investigations:**
- Urinalysis: MSU — exclude UTI, haematuria
- PSA (prostate-specific antigen): offered after informed discussion of benefits and harms; screen for prostate cancer; PSA >4 ng/mL warrants urology referral; PSA increases with BPH (larger gland = higher PSA) and acute infection; age-specific reference ranges apply
- Renal function (UEC, creatinine): assess for obstructive uropathy
- Post-void residual (PVR) bladder scan: can be arranged at radiology or urology; PVR >200 mL suggests significant outlet obstruction
- Uroflowmetry: peak flow rate (Qmax) <10 mL/s suggests obstruction; often arranged at urology

## Key Differentials

- Prostate cancer — DRE (nodule, asymmetry, induration), PSA; urology referral for suspected cancer
- Overactive bladder (OAB) — predominantly storage symptoms (urgency, frequency, urgency incontinence) without significant voiding symptoms; no prostate enlargement on DRE; treated differently (anticholinergics, mirabegron)
- Urethral stricture — voiding symptoms in younger men, history of STI, urethral trauma or catheterisation; urology assessment
- Neurogenic bladder — spinal cord pathology, MS, Parkinson's, diabetes; neurological features present
- UTI — frequency, dysuria, urgency; MSU; treat and reassess
- Bladder cancer — haematuria; urgent cystoscopy referral

## Management Principles

**Watchful waiting (mild symptoms, IPSS ≤7, no complications):**
- Lifestyle modifications: limit evening fluid intake (especially caffeine and alcohol — diuretic and irritant effects), void on a timed schedule, double voiding technique
- Annual review with IPSS and PSA

**Pharmacotherapy:**

Alpha-1-adrenergic blockers (first-line for voiding symptoms, moderate-severe IPSS):
- Tamsulosin 0.4 mg daily — highly selective for prostatic alpha-1 receptors; minimal blood pressure effect; most commonly used; onset of action days to weeks
- Silodosin 8 mg daily — highly selective; retrograde ejaculation common side effect
- Alfuzosin 10 mg daily — less retrograde ejaculation than tamsulosin
- Side effects (all): retrograde ejaculation (especially tamsulosin), postural hypotension, dizziness; intraoperative floppy iris syndrome if cataract surgery planned — advise ophthalmologist

5-alpha reductase inhibitors (5ARIs) — for large prostates (>30–40 mL), high PSA, or combination therapy:
- Finasteride 5 mg daily — reduces DHT; reduces prostate size over 6 months; most benefit in large glands; reduces PSA by ~50% (double PSA to estimate true value); risk of sexual side effects (reduced libido, ED, ejaculatory dysfunction); breast tenderness; persist after cessation (post-finasteride syndrome — controversial but documented)
- Dutasteride 0.5 mg daily — similar to finasteride; blocks both isoforms of 5-alpha reductase

Combination therapy (alpha-blocker + 5ARI): superior to monotherapy for large prostates and moderate-severe symptoms; reduces risk of AUR and need for surgery over time.

Anticholinergics / beta-3 agonists (for storage symptoms + OAB component):
- Solifenacin, oxybutynin, mirabegron — for urgency and frequency component; use with caution if significant voiding symptoms and high PVR (risk of AUR)
- Mirabegron (beta-3 agonist) — safer than anticholinergics if bladder outlet obstruction component present; less dry mouth and constipation

**Surgical management (urology referral):**
- TURP (transurethral resection of prostate): gold standard for significant obstruction; very effective; retrograde ejaculation in 75% post-procedure
- Laser prostatectomy (GreenLight, HoLEP): alternatives to TURP with lower bleeding risk
- Minimally invasive options: Urolift (prostatic urethral lift), Rezum (steam treatment) — day procedures; less effect on ejaculation; suitable for smaller prostates

**Indications for urological referral:**
- Haematuria (urgent — exclude cancer)
- Acute urinary retention
- IPSS ≥20 or bothersome moderate symptoms not responding to pharmacotherapy
- PSA elevation requiring prostate biopsy decision
- Suspected prostate cancer
- Recurrent UTIs
- Renal impairment from outlet obstruction

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Tamsulosin** (Flomaxtra, Minipress SR — PBS restricted: LUTS/BPH; 0.4 mg daily; first-line alpha-blocker; warn about intraoperative floppy iris syndrome before cataract surgery)
- **Alfuzosin** (PBS restricted — BPH; 10 mg daily XL)
- **Finasteride** (Proscar — PBS restricted: symptomatic BPH; 5 mg daily; onset 6 months; reduces PSA 50%)
- **Dutasteride** (Avodart — PBS restricted: BPH with enlarged prostate; 0.5 mg daily)
- **Dutasteride/tamsulosin** (Duodart — PBS restricted: combination for enlarged prostate)
- **Solifenacin** (Vesicare — PBS restricted: overactive bladder; useful for storage symptom component in BPH)
- **Mirabegron** (Betmiga — PBS restricted: overactive bladder; beta-3 agonist; safer in BPH than anticholinergics)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Acute urinary retention — inability to void, severe suprapubic pain, bladder palpable on examination; urethral catheterisation required
- Urosepsis — UTI with systemic features (fever, hypotension, tachycardia, rigors) — IV antibiotics

**Urgent referral (within days):**
- Haematuria (frank haematuria or persistent microscopic) — urgent urology for cystoscopy and upper tract imaging to exclude urothelial cancer
- High PVR with renal impairment — obstructive uropathy; urology urgently

**Non-urgent urology referral:**
- Moderate-severe LUTS (IPSS ≥8) not responding to pharmacotherapy after 3–6 months
- PSA elevation requiring biopsy decision
- Recurrent UTIs in setting of BPH
- Patient requesting surgical treatment for symptom relief

## Telehealth Suitability

BPH assessment is moderately suited to telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- IPSS scoring and symptom review
- Medication prescribing and review (tamsulosin, finasteride, dutasteride)
- Side effect monitoring and medication switching
- PSA result interpretation and referral coordination
- Lifestyle advice (fluid management, dietary, caffeine/alcohol reduction)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Digital rectal examination (DRE) — prostate assessment; baseline before treatment
- Post-void residual bladder scan — radiology arrangement
- Haematuria assessment — physical examination, urine dipstick, urgent imaging
- Urethral catheterisation for acute retention
