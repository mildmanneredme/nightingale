## Drug Allergy Assessment (RACGP)

Drug allergy (immunologically mediated hypersensitivity) is distinct from adverse drug reactions (ADRs) and drug intolerance. Approximately 10–15% of patients self-report penicillin allergy, but formal allergy evaluation confirms true penicillin allergy in <10% of these patients. This "penicillin allergy overdiagnosis" has major public health consequences: unnecessary use of broader-spectrum antibiotics (vancomycin, carbapenems), increased antimicrobial resistance, prolonged hospital admissions, and higher costs. The GP's critical roles are: distinguish true immunological drug allergy from intolerance and ADR, accurately document drug allergy in the medical record (drug, reaction type, date, management), manage acute hypersensitivity reactions, and facilitate de-labelling of likely incorrect penicillin allergy labels through allergy testing or structured risk assessment. ASCIA provides patient and clinician resources at allergy.org.au. Telehealth is suitable for review of allergy history and prescribing alternatives.

## Clinical Presentation

**Classification of adverse drug reactions:**
- Type A (predictable — 80%): dose-dependent; known pharmacological effect; examples: NSAID gastropathy, opioid constipation, ACE inhibitor cough — NOT allergy
- Type B (unpredictable — 20%): includes immunological (allergy) and non-immunological hypersensitivity

**Drug hypersensitivity classification (Gell and Coombs):**
- Type I (IgE-mediated / immediate): within 1–6 hours of drug exposure; urticaria, angioedema, bronchospasm, anaphylaxis; most clinically important; requires allergy testing (skin testing ± oral challenge)
- Type II (cytotoxic): haemolytic anaemia (penicillin), thrombocytopenia; days to weeks
- Type III (immune complex): serum sickness; vasculitis; 1–3 weeks
- Type IV (delayed/cell-mediated): maculopapular exanthem (most common delayed reaction); contact dermatitis; Stevens-Johnson syndrome; DRESS (Drug Reaction with Eosinophilia and Systemic Symptoms); typically 1–3 weeks after exposure

**Immediate reactions (Type I — IgE-mediated):**
- Onset: within 1 hour of drug; usually within 30 minutes IV; up to 6 hours oral
- Symptoms: urticaria (hives), pruritus, angioedema, rhinitis, bronchospasm, hypotension, anaphylaxis
- Most common drugs: penicillins, cephalosporins, sulfonamides, NSAIDs, muscle relaxants, contrast dye
- Risk factors: prior anaphylaxis, IgE to drug, atopy

**Delayed reactions (Type IV):**
- Maculopapular exanthem: most common; rash 1–2 weeks after starting drug; symmetrical; trunk and limbs; non-urticarial; amoxicillin rash in EBV infection (NOT true allergy)
- DRESS (Drug Reaction with Eosinophilia and Systemic Symptoms): severe; fever + rash + lymphadenopathy + organ involvement (liver, kidney, lungs) + eosinophilia; onset 2–8 weeks; carbamazepine, phenytoin, allopurinol, sulfonamides; MEDICAL EMERGENCY → hospital
- Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) / Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN): severe mucocutaneous reaction; target lesions; skin peeling; mucosal involvement; allopurinol, carbamazepine, sulfonamides; life-threatening → 000 + hospital
- AGEP (Acute Generalised Exanthematous Pustulosis): pustular rash; fever; aminopenicillins, macrolides; usually resolves on stopping drug

**Penicillin allergy (common and important):**
- Most common reported drug allergy
- True IgE-mediated penicillin allergy: anaphylaxis, urticaria, angioedema within 1 hour of penicillin
- NOT penicillin allergy: GI side effects (nausea, diarrhoea), mild rash appearing >72 hours into course (likely viral), amoxicillin rash with EBV (NOT allergy)
- Cross-reactivity with cephalosporins: <2% in true penicillin allergy (historical 10% figure is incorrect); can usually use cephalosporins with monitoring
- Allergy label removal (de-labelling): structured risk assessment or allergy testing; many patients with historical "penicillin allergy" can safely receive penicillin

**ACE inhibitor cough and angioedema:**
- ACE inhibitor cough: bradykinin-mediated; NOT IgE-mediated; affects 5–20% of patients; persistent dry cough
- Management: switch to ARB (no cough); NOT an allergy — document as "ACE inhibitor intolerance — cough"
- ACE inhibitor angioedema: bradykinin-mediated; NOT histamine; antihistamines and adrenaline often ineffective; airway risk; switch to ARB; AVOID ALL ACE inhibitors
- NSAID hypersensitivity: cross-reactive COX-1 inhibition → urticaria/angio-oedema; or aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease (asthma); often non-IgE mechanism

## Assessment Approach

**History (structured allergy assessment):**
- Drug name (generic and brand)
- Date of reaction
- Reaction type: symptoms, timing (immediate vs delayed), severity (mild rash vs anaphylaxis)
- Prior exposures and tolerance (same drug or cross-reactive drug tolerated since?)
- Concurrent illness (viral infection — amoxicillin rash in EBV)
- Treatment required (antihistamine, adrenaline, hospital)
- Outcome (resolved spontaneously, required ICU)

**Clinical assessment of current reaction:**
- Vital signs: hypotension + tachycardia = anaphylaxis; immediate adrenaline
- Skin: urticaria, morbilliform rash, target lesions, bullae, mucosal involvement
- Respiratory: wheeze, stridor
- Severity grading: mild (localised rash) → moderate (urticaria + one other system) → severe (anaphylaxis)

**Investigations (limited role in acute assessment):**
- Serum tryptase: within 1–4 hours of acute anaphylaxis; elevated supports mast cell degranulation (IgE-mediated); baseline level for comparison
- Eosinophil count + LFTs: DRESS (eosinophilia + hepatitis)
- Skin testing + specific IgE: allergy clinic; diagnoses IgE-mediated penicillin allergy; confirms or de-labels
- Patch testing: delayed contact dermatitis

**Documentation (critical):**
- Document clearly: drug name (generic), class (betalactam, sulfonamide), reaction type, date, severity, investigations, management
- Incorrect or vague documentation causes treatment difficulties; avoid "penicillin allergy — unknown" without further history

## Key Differentials

- Drug intolerance/ADR: predictable, pharmacological; no immunological mechanism; not an allergy (document separately)
- Drug-drug interaction causing side effects: not allergy
- Viral exanthem: maculopapular rash during viral infection + antibiotic co-administration; amoxicillin + EBV; NOT penicillin allergy
- Urticaria from another cause: food, viral, idiopathic; temporal relationship to drug not always causal
- Erythema multiforme: target lesions; post-infectious (HSV most common); distinguish from SJS
- DRESS vs drug reaction vs infectious mono: liver function tests; FBC; EBV/CMV serology

## Management Principles

**Acute anaphylaxis — immediate management:**
- Adrenaline (epinephrine) IM IMMEDIATELY: adult 0.5 mg (0.5 mL of 1:1000); child 0.01 mg/kg (max 0.5 mg); anterolateral mid-thigh; repeat every 5 minutes if no improvement
- Call 000; lay patient flat (Trendelenburg if hypotensive); airway management
- IV access; IV normal saline (large volume fluid for hypotension)
- Antihistamine (promethazine or chlorphenamine IM) + IV hydrocortisone: ADJUNCT ONLY; do NOT delay adrenaline; second-line agents
- EpiPen prescription: all patients with anaphylaxis; train patient and family in use
- Carry adrenaline at all times; ASCIA anaphylaxis action plan; MedicAlert bracelet
- See `anaphylaxis-management`

**SJS/TEN / DRESS — immediate hospital:**
- Stop offending drug immediately
- 000 / emergency hospital; intensive care; dermatology; ophthalmology
- IVIG (SJS/TEN); systemic corticosteroids (DRESS — debated); supportive wound care; fluid management

**Penicillin allergy de-labelling:**
- Low-risk historical label (rash only, >10 years ago, no anaphylaxis): structured risk assessment; oral amoxicillin challenge in supervised setting (GP office or allergy clinic) with 30-minute observation
- High-risk (anaphylaxis, recent reaction, SJS, DRESS): allergy clinic referral for formal skin testing ± oral challenge
- ASCIA De-labelling Penicillin Allergy resources: allergy.org.au — clinical tools for GPs
- After negative challenge/skin test: document "penicillin allergy — de-labelled (date); skin test/challenge negative"

**Safe alternative prescribing:**
- Penicillin allergy (unconfirmed, mild): cefalexin (cephalosporin; <2% cross-reactivity); use with monitoring
- Penicillin allergy (anaphylaxis confirmed): azithromycin/clindamycin (macrolide or lincosamide); avoid all betalactams
- NSAIDs: switch to paracetamol or to COX-2 selective NSAID (celecoxib) — less likely to trigger hypersensitivity

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Adrenaline 0.3 mg EpiPen (EpiPen)** (PBS restricted: anaphylaxis; adults and children ≥30 kg; prescribe 2 EpiPens; train in self-administration; ensure ASCIA action plan completed)
- **Adrenaline 0.15 mg EpiPen Junior (EpiPen Jr)** (PBS restricted: children 10–20 kg; two pens prescribed; train parents and school)
- **Promethazine 25 mg IM (Phenergan)** (PBS unrestricted: adjunct antihistamine for allergic reactions; not primary treatment; do NOT use without adrenaline for anaphylaxis)
- **Note:** Codeine allergy label in some patients may reflect opioid-class side effects (nausea, itch from histamine release — not IgE) rather than true allergy; document carefully and consider safe alternative opioids if analgesia needed.

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000:**
- Anaphylaxis: adrenaline immediately then 000
- SJS/TEN: mucocutaneous blistering, mucosal involvement, skin sloughing → 000
- DRESS with organ involvement: fever + rash + eosinophilia + elevated LFTs or renal impairment → hospital

**Urgent allergy clinic referral:**
- History consistent with IgE-mediated penicillin allergy (anaphylaxis): allergy testing
- Frequent antibiotic courses needed and allergy label preventing access to first-line antibiotics: allergy de-labelling

**Non-urgent:**
- Historical penicillin allergy label requiring clarification: ASCIA risk assessment; consider supervised oral challenge or allergy clinic
- Recurrent urticaria of uncertain cause: allergy clinic

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Review of allergy history and documentation
- Safe antibiotic prescribing in patient with recorded drug allergy
- ASCIA anaphylaxis action plan review
- EpiPen training follow-up and prescription renewal
- De-labelling risk stratification and allergy clinic referral

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Acute allergic reaction: clinical assessment, SpO₂, vital signs
- Oral drug challenge for de-labelling: supervised setting with adrenaline available
- Skin testing (allergy clinic)
