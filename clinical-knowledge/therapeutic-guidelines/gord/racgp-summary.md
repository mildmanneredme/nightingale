## GORD Assessment (RACGP)

Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) is one of the most common conditions in general practice, affecting up to 20% of Australian adults. It results from abnormal reflux of gastric contents into the oesophagus, causing troublesome symptoms and/or complications. The majority of GORD can be managed empirically in primary care without endoscopy. Telehealth is well-suited to symptom review, PPI management, and lifestyle counselling.

## Clinical Presentation

Typical symptoms: heartburn (burning sensation rising from stomach or lower chest), regurgitation (effortless return of gastric contents to mouth/throat), particularly postprandial and nocturnal.

Atypical symptoms: chronic cough, hoarse voice, globus sensation, laryngitis, non-cardiac chest pain, asthma exacerbation. Consider GORD as contributing factor in unexplained chronic cough or poorly controlled asthma.

Aggravating factors: large meals, fatty or spicy food, caffeine, alcohol, chocolate, mint, smoking, obesity, pregnancy, supine position after eating, and medications (NSAIDs, calcium channel blockers, bisphosphonates, nitrates).

A clinical diagnosis based on typical symptoms is appropriate in most cases. Endoscopy is not required for uncomplicated GORD without alarm features.

## Assessment Approach

Symptom frequency and severity: assess impact on quality of life, duration, and response to previous antacid/PPI therapy.

Screen for alarm features (see Escalation Triggers) which require urgent endoscopy referral.

Assess BMI — obesity is a major risk factor and weight loss improves symptoms.

Review medications: NSAIDs, aspirin, calcium channel blockers, nitrates, bisphosphonates, and theophylline can worsen GORD.

Differentiate from: peptic ulcer disease (epigastric pain, often improves with eating), functional dyspepsia (epigastric discomfort, bloating, early satiety without heartburn), cardiac chest pain (needs exclusion if atypical).

H. pylori testing: consider if dyspepsia is predominant or ulcer disease is suspected — test and treat with urea breath test (Medicare-rebatable in appropriate clinical context).

## Key Differentials

- Peptic ulcer disease — epigastric pain, often with nausea, H. pylori association, NSAIDs
- Functional dyspepsia — overlaps with GORD; bloating, early satiety, epigastric discomfort
- Oesophageal motility disorders — dysphagia with solids and liquids, regurgitation of undigested food
- Cardiac chest pain — atypical GORD can mimic angina; ECG and troponin if any doubt
- Barrett's oesophagus — complication of long-standing GORD; requires endoscopic surveillance
- Eosinophilic oesophagitis — dysphagia, food impaction, atopy history; increasingly recognised

## Management Principles

**Step 1 — Lifestyle modification:**
- Weight loss if BMI ≥25 kg/m²
- Elevate head of bed 15–20 cm (not just extra pillows)
- Avoid eating within 2–3 hours of lying down
- Reduce trigger foods: caffeine, alcohol, fatty/spicy food, chocolate, mint
- Smoking cessation
- Avoid tight clothing

**Step 2 — Antacids / alginate-antacid combinations:**
- Useful for mild, infrequent symptoms
- Gaviscon (alginate-antacid): OTC; effective for postprandial symptoms and nocturnal regurgitation

**Step 3 — Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs):**
- First-line for moderate-to-severe or frequent GORD
- Give 30 minutes before first meal of the day for optimal efficacy
- Initial trial: 4–8 weeks at standard dose
- Responders: step-down to lowest effective dose; on-demand PPI is appropriate for many patients
- Long-term PPI: reassess indication annually. Consider risks: hypomagnesaemia, B12 deficiency, C. diff risk (in hospitalised patients), potential CKD association with prolonged use at high doses

**H2 receptor antagonists:** Alternative for mild symptoms; less effective than PPIs for erosive oesophagitis.

**Surgical referral:** Laparoscopic fundoplication for patients with confirmed GORD on pH monitoring who are young, prefer to avoid long-term PPI, or have large hiatus hernia with mechanical symptoms — refer to upper GI surgeon.

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Omeprazole** (PBS unrestricted — initial treatment of peptic conditions; also OTC available) — 20 mg daily before breakfast
- **Pantoprazole** (PBS unrestricted) — 40 mg daily; once-daily standard dose
- **Esomeprazole** (PBS — restricted for erosive oesophagitis confirmed on endoscopy; 20 mg unrestricted for symptom control)
- **Rabeprazole, lansoprazole** (PBS — restricted for endoscopy-confirmed erosive oesophagitis)
- **Ranitidine** (withdrawn from market in 2020 due to NDMA contamination)
- **Famotidine** (H2 antagonist — available OTC; PBS-listed for peptic ulcer)
- **Gaviscon** (alginate-antacid — OTC; not PBS-listed)
- For H. pylori eradication (if co-existing): standard triple therapy (PPI + amoxicillin + clarithromycin for 7 days) — all PBS-listed

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent endoscopy referral (within 2 weeks via gastroenterologist):**
- Dysphagia (difficulty swallowing) — alarm symptom requiring urgent investigation
- Odynophagia (painful swallowing)
- Unexplained weight loss (>5% over 3 months)
- Persistent vomiting
- Haematemesis or melaena
- Iron deficiency anaemia without explanation
- Palpable epigastric mass
- Age >50 years with new-onset dyspepsia without prior investigation

**Urgent in-person GP or ED (same day):**
- Severe vomiting with haematemesis — possible active upper GI bleed (call 000 if haemodynamically unstable)
- Chest pain — exclude cardiac cause urgently; if any doubt, call 000

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Gastroenterology: GORD not responding to 8 weeks of high-dose PPI; suspected Barrett's oesophagus surveillance; diagnostic uncertainty
- Upper GI surgery: consideration for anti-reflux surgery

## Telehealth Suitability

GORD management is highly suitable for telehealth for established patients.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Symptom review and response to PPI therapy
- PPI dose adjustment and step-down management
- Lifestyle modification counselling
- Assessment of alarm feature absence (symptom history)
- Review of medication interactions contributing to GORD

**Requires in-person or specialist assessment:**
- Any alarm feature present (dysphagia, weight loss, haematemesis, anaemia) — requires in-person assessment and urgent endoscopy referral
- Atypical chest pain where cardiac cause not yet excluded
- Consideration of anti-reflux surgery
