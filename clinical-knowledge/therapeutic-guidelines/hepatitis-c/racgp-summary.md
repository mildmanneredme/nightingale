## Hepatitis C Assessment (RACGP)

Hepatitis C virus (HCV) infection is a blood-borne viral infection affecting approximately 117,000 Australians (prevalence ~0.7% of adults). Australia has been a world leader in HCV elimination — since March 2016, highly effective direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) have been listed on the PBS with unrestricted access, achieving cure rates >95% in 8–12 weeks. GPs can now initiate, prescribe, and manage HCV treatment without specialist co-prescription, making this a primary care intervention. HCV is transmitted predominantly via injecting drug use (70–80% of new infections), blood transfusion prior to 1990, tattooing/piercing with unsterile equipment, and occupational needlestick. Perinatal and sexual transmission occurs but is less common. Most HCV-infected individuals are asymptomatic; untreated chronic HCV causes progressive liver fibrosis, cirrhosis, hepatocellular carcinoma, and death. GPs play a central role in: testing, diagnosis, treatment initiation and monitoring, and harm reduction. Telehealth is well-suited to HCV management.

## Clinical Presentation

**Acute HCV infection (20–30% symptomatic):**
- Incubation: 2–26 weeks after exposure
- Symptoms (if present): fatigue, jaundice, nausea, right upper quadrant discomfort, dark urine
- 20–40% spontaneously clear; 60–80% develop chronic infection
- Spontaneous clearance more likely: younger age, female, acute symptomatic illness, favourable IL28B genotype

**Chronic HCV infection (>6 months):**
- Mostly asymptomatic for decades — major cause of missed diagnosis
- Symptoms: fatigue (most common); right upper quadrant discomfort; mild jaundice; pruritus; myalgia
- Extrahepatic manifestations: mixed cryoglobulinaemia (vasculitis, arthralgias, renal disease), membranoproliferative GN, non-Hodgkin's lymphoma, type 2 diabetes, lichen planus
- Progressive fibrosis: F0 (no fibrosis) → F4 (cirrhosis); over 20–30 years; accelerated by: alcohol, obesity, HIV coinfection, male sex

**HCV genotypes (affects treatment selection):**
- Genotype 1 (most common globally and in Australia): 55% of Australian cases
- Genotype 3: 30% of Australian cases; associated with faster fibrosis progression and steatosis
- Pan-genotypic DAAs now available — genotype testing less critical but may inform regimen choice

**At-risk populations to screen:**
- People who inject or have injected drugs (current or past): highest risk group in Australia
- Born in high-prevalence country: Egypt, Pakistan, Italy, parts of Asia
- Prior blood transfusion or organ transplant before 1990 (Australia screening began 1990)
- Healthcare workers with occupational exposure history
- Tattooing/piercing in unsterile environments
- People in prison or correctional facilities
- Children born to HCV-positive mothers
- Men who have sex with men (MSM) with HIV

## Assessment Approach

**Testing pathway:**

Step 1 — HCV antibody test (anti-HCV):
- Serological test; detects prior exposure; becomes positive 8–12 weeks after infection
- Positive = past or current infection; negative = no current or past infection (if exposure <8 weeks, repeat in 12 weeks)
- Does NOT confirm current active infection

Step 2 — If anti-HCV positive → HCV RNA (PCR):
- Detectable from 1–2 weeks post-exposure
- Positive HCV RNA = active (chronic) infection requiring treatment
- Negative HCV RNA + positive antibody = spontaneous clearance or successful treatment (assess SVR)

**Baseline investigations before treatment:**
- LFTs (AST, ALT, GGT, ALP, bilirubin): assess liver inflammation; elevated in active hepatitis
- FBC: thrombocytopenia and anaemia suggest advanced fibrosis/cirrhosis
- INR, albumin: markers of synthetic liver function; impaired in cirrhosis
- eGFR and creatinine: renal function — affects some DAA dosing (sofosbuvir in severe CKD)
- HCV genotype: genotype 1 vs 3 vs other; helps select DAA regimen (less critical with pan-genotypic agents)
- HCV quantitative RNA (viral load): not required to treat but useful baseline and for monitoring SVR
- APRI score or FIB-4 index: calculated from AST, ALT, platelets, age — non-invasive fibrosis assessment; identifies cirrhosis risk; FIB-4 >3.25 = high probability of cirrhosis
- Fibroscan (transient elastography): liver stiffness measurement; gold standard non-invasive fibrosis staging; referral to gastroenterology/radiology; required if cirrhosis staging needed
- HIV serology: all HCV patients (HIV coinfection common in PWID; affects treatment and urgency)
- Hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg): HBV coinfection — reactivation risk with DAA treatment
- Hepatitis B vaccination: if HBV non-immune (check HBsAb)

## Key Differentials

- Hepatitis B (HBV): similar risk factors; HBsAg, HBV DNA; different treatment; chronic HBV coinfection affects HCV treatment planning
- Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD): common; elevated LFTs; ultrasound; no HCV antibody
- Alcohol-related liver disease: history; GGT elevated; ALT, AST
- Drug-induced liver injury: medication review
- Autoimmune hepatitis: young women; ANA, anti-SMA; elevated immunoglobulins
- Haemochromatosis: iron overload; elevated ferritin, transferrin saturation; HFE gene

## Management Principles

**Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) — PBS-listed, GP-prescribable:**

Sofosbuvir/velpatasvir (Epclusa):
- Pan-genotypic (all genotypes 1–6): standard regimen for most Australians
- 400 mg/100 mg once daily × 12 weeks
- Cure rate: >95% (SVR12 — sustained virological response 12 weeks post-treatment)
- PBS-listed: unrestricted — any GP can prescribe; no specialist co-prescription required
- Caution: eGFR <30 — reduced dose or alternative; not recommended in Child-Pugh C cirrhosis without specialist
- Drug interactions: check at hep-druginteractions.org (University of Liverpool database) — interactions with rifampicin, carbamazepine, St John's Wort, amiodarone

Glecaprevir/pibrentasvir (Maviret):
- Pan-genotypic: 8-week regimen for treatment-naive non-cirrhotic patients; 12 weeks for cirrhosis
- 300 mg/120 mg once daily
- Preferred for CKD (renally excreted — no dose adjustment); preferred for shorter treatment duration in suitable patients
- PBS-listed: unrestricted; GP-prescribable
- Drug interactions: check hep-druginteractions.org; some interactions with statins

Sofosbuvir/ledipasvir (Harvoni): genotype 1 and 4; older regimen; less commonly used now with pan-genotypic options

**Treatment monitoring:**
- Baseline ALT, FBC, renal function: before starting
- HCV RNA at 12 weeks post-treatment completion (SVR12): confirms cure
- If SVR12 positive (undetectable HCV RNA): CURED — document; no further HCV monitoring required (reinfection remains possible)
- If SVR12 negative: not cured; repeat treatment or specialist referral
- LFTs during treatment: not routinely required for uncomplicated cases
- HIV/HBV monitoring: if coinfection

**Cirrhosis management (if F3–F4 fibrosis or cirrhosis):**
- Treat HCV — reduces portal hypertension and hepatocellular carcinoma risk even in cirrhosis
- Post-SVR: 6-monthly liver ultrasound and AFP (alpha-fetoprotein) — hepatocellular carcinoma surveillance continues even after HCV cure in cirrhotic patients
- Gastroscopy: variceal surveillance — refer gastroenterologist
- Fibroscan or gastroenterology review for staging and ongoing monitoring
- Alcohol abstinence: crucial even after HCV cure in cirrhosis

**Harm reduction (concurrent with treatment):**
- Needle and syringe programs (NSPs): prevent reinfection; freely available in all Australian states
- Opioid pharmacotherapy: buprenorphine/methadone — PWID should be on OPT if dependent; treat opioid dependence alongside HCV
- Reinfection: HCV cure does not prevent reinfection; ongoing NSP use and risk reduction counselling required; retest 12-monthly if ongoing risk

**HBV activation risk:**
- HBV reactivation during HCV DAA treatment: occurs rarely in HBsAg-positive patients; screen ALL HCV patients for HBsAg; if positive — gastroenterology/hepatology co-management; consider prophylactic tenofovir or entecavir

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Sofosbuvir/velpatasvir (Epclusa) 400/100 mg** (PBS unrestricted: HCV treatment; all genotypes; 12 weeks; GP prescribable without specialist; once daily; check drug interactions; eGFR <30 — specialist advice)
- **Glecaprevir/pibrentasvir (Maviret) 100/40 mg × 3 tablets daily** (PBS unrestricted: HCV treatment; pan-genotypic; 8 weeks non-cirrhotic treatment-naive; 12 weeks with cirrhosis; preferred in CKD)
- **Note:** All DAAs are now unrestricted on PBS — no authority prescription or specialist letter required; any GP can initiate, prescribe and manage HCV treatment; this is a deliberate policy to maximise access and contribute to Australia's HCV elimination goals
- **Note:** Drug interactions are the major prescribing risk — check hep-druginteractions.org before prescribing; key interactions: rifampicin (contraindicated), carbamazepine (contraindicated), amiodarone (sofosbuvir-containing regimens — bradycardia risk), rosuvastatin dose reduction (glecaprevir/pibrentasvir)
- **Hepatitis B vaccination**: PBS-listed; offer to all HCV patients not immune to HBV

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent hepatology/gastroenterology:**
- Decompensated cirrhosis: ascites, variceal bleeding, hepatic encephalopathy, jaundice in known cirrhotic — specialist co-management of DAA treatment; hospital if acute decompensation
- Child-Pugh B/C cirrhosis: DAA treatment requires specialist oversight
- HBV/HCV coinfection: specialist management to prevent HBV reactivation
- Suspected hepatocellular carcinoma: rising AFP + new liver lesion on ultrasound — urgent hepatology/oncology

**Non-urgent specialist referral:**
- Fibroscan: before treatment if cirrhosis staging required; most patients can be managed without specialist
- Gastroenterologist: decompensated cirrhosis; post-SVR surveillance in cirrhosis; variceal screening

## Telehealth Suitability

HCV management is highly suitable for telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- HCV testing discussion and result counselling
- DAA treatment initiation, education, and prescribing
- Drug interaction review
- Treatment monitoring
- SVR12 result discussion and documentation of cure
- Harm reduction counselling
- Referral coordination

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial physical examination (assess for stigmata of chronic liver disease)
- Blood tests — pathology arranged (HCV RNA, LFTs, FBC, eGFR)
- Fibroscan — radiology or gastroenterology
- Hepatitis B vaccination administration
