## Male Hypogonadism Assessment (RACGP)

Male hypogonadism is the failure of the testes to produce adequate levels of testosterone (androgen deficiency) and/or spermatozoa. It is classified as primary (testicular failure — hypergonadotrophic hypogonadism: elevated LH/FSH) or secondary (hypothalamic-pituitary dysfunction — hypogonadotrophic hypogonadism: low or inappropriately normal LH/FSH). Late-onset hypogonadism (LOH) refers to testosterone deficiency arising in ageing men — controversial diagnosis; symptoms non-specific; prevalence increases with age, obesity, metabolic syndrome, and chronic illness. CRITICAL: testosterone should only be measured in patients with symptoms and signs of deficiency — screening of asymptomatic men is NOT recommended. TRT (testosterone replacement therapy) should NOT be initiated without biochemical confirmation on two separate morning fasting samples. TRT is contraindicated in men desiring fertility (suppresses spermatogenesis). Prostate cancer and haematocrit monitoring are mandatory. Telehealth is suitable for ongoing management after initial assessment.

## Clinical Presentation

**Symptoms of androgen deficiency:**
- Reduced libido (most specific symptom): decreased sexual interest; reduced sexual thoughts/fantasies
- Erectile dysfunction: contributes; but ED is multifactorial
- Fatigue, lethargy, reduced energy; poor exercise tolerance
- Reduced muscle mass and strength
- Increased adiposity; metabolic syndrome association
- Low mood; reduced concentration; poor memory
- Reduced body and facial hair; reduced shaving frequency
- Hot flushes and sweats (hypogonadism-specific, especially in acute/severe deficiency)
- Reduced testicular volume; gynaecomastia (if elevated oestradiol relative to testosterone)

**Specific syndromes:**
- Klinefelter syndrome (47,XXY): primary hypogonadism; small firm testes; gynaecomastia; azoospermia; tall stature; learning difficulties; diagnose by karyotype
- Kallmann syndrome: hypogonadotrophic hypogonadism + anosmia; GnRH deficiency; congenital
- Haemochromatosis: iron deposition in pituitary/testes; secondary hypogonadism; transferrin saturation + ferritin
- Pituitary tumour (prolactinoma): most common pituitary tumour; high prolactin suppresses GnRH pulsatility; secondary hypogonadism; headache; visual field defect
- Opioid-induced hypogonadism: suppression of hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis by opioids; common in chronic opioid users; check LH/FSH (low = central suppression)

## Assessment Approach

**Indications for testosterone measurement (symptoms + signs required):**
- Low libido, erectile dysfunction, or sexual dysfunction PLUS at least one other symptom (fatigue, reduced muscle, mood change)
- Infertility workup
- Osteoporosis without clear cause
- History suggesting specific syndrome (Klinefelter, Kallmann, pituitary disease)
- Patients on opioids with sexual dysfunction symptoms

**Biochemical diagnosis — MANDATORY criteria:**
- Two separate fasting morning samples (7–11 AM): testosterone follows circadian rhythm; morning levels peak
- Total testosterone: reference ranges vary by laboratory; generally hypogonadism defined as total testosterone <8 nmol/L (symptomatic patients); 8–12 nmol/L is a "grey zone" where clinical judgement and free testosterone needed
- Free testosterone (calculated or measured): more clinically relevant when SHBG is altered (elevated SHBG in obesity, aging — lowers free T; low SHBG in obesity — elevates free T)
- LH and FSH: differentiate primary (↑LH/FSH) from secondary (↓ or inappropriately normal LH/FSH)
- Prolactin: exclude prolactinoma (secondary hypogonadism); elevated prolactin → pituitary MRI
- SHBG: influences interpretation of total testosterone
- FBC: haematocrit baseline before TRT (polycythaemia is main haematological risk of TRT)
- PSA: baseline before TRT in men ≥40 years; TRT is contraindicated if PSA >4 ng/mL without prostate cancer workup
- TFTs, LFTs: exclude systemic causes

**Pituitary MRI:**
- Indicated if: secondary hypogonadism (low LH/FSH) confirmed; hyperprolactinaemia; headache/visual symptoms; to exclude pituitary tumour

## Key Differentials

- Erectile dysfunction (primary): multifactorial; vascular, neurological, psychological; testosterone normal; see `erectile-dysfunction`
- Depression: overlap of symptoms; PHQ-9; may require concurrent treatment
- Obstructive sleep apnoea: fatigue, reduced libido; overlapping with hypogonadism; STOP-BANG questionnaire; refer for sleep study
- Hypothyroidism: fatigue, cognitive impairment, weight gain; TFTs
- Prolactinoma: secondary hypogonadism; prolactin elevated → pituitary MRI; cabergoline treatment
- Age-related changes: symptoms of "male menopause" — some symptoms are normal ageing; diagnosis requires biochemical deficiency + symptoms

## Management Principles

**Lifestyle modification (first-line for late-onset hypogonadism / borderline testosterone):**
- Weight loss: 10% body weight reduction increases testosterone by approximately 2–3 nmol/L; most effective intervention for obese men with low testosterone
- Exercise: resistance training + aerobic exercise; increases testosterone; improves body composition
- Alcohol reduction; smoking cessation
- Treat OSA (CPAP): may improve testosterone
- Trial lifestyle modification for 3–6 months before TRT in men with total testosterone 8–12 nmol/L

**Testosterone Replacement Therapy (TRT):**

Indications:
- Total testosterone consistently <8 nmol/L on two morning fasting samples + symptoms of androgen deficiency
- Symptomatic hypogonadism with confirmed biochemical deficiency

Contraindications:
- Active prostate cancer: ABSOLUTE contraindication
- Haematocrit >50% (polycythaemia risk)
- Severe untreated OSA (worsens OSA)
- Desire for fertility: TRT suppresses spermatogenesis; use clomiphene or gonadotrophins to stimulate endogenous testosterone for fertility

Formulations available in Australia:
- Testosterone undecanoate gel (Testogel, AndroForte 5%): apply to upper arm, shoulder or abdomen daily; do NOT apply to scrotum; wash hands; avoid skin contact with women or children until dry (transfer risk); PBS-restricted
- Testosterone undecanoate capsules (Andriol Testocaps) 40 mg: oral; poor bioavailability; take with fatty meal; 3–4 capsules daily; PBS-listed but not preferred formulation
- Testosterone undecanoate IM injection (Reandron 1000 mg/4 mL): 10–14 weekly injection; administered by GP; stable levels; preferred for adherence; PBS-restricted
- Testosterone implants (pellets): 6-monthly; subcutaneous; specialist (endocrinology/urology); consistent levels; not widely used in Australia

Monitoring:
- Testosterone level: 3–6 months after initiation; target mid-normal range (12–25 nmol/L); adjust formulation/dose
- PSA: 3–6 months after initiation; then annually; new prostate cancer concern if rise >1 ng/mL in 12 months → urology
- Haematocrit (FBC): 3–6 months; target <50%; phlebotomy or dose reduction if elevated; risk of polycythaemia
- Lipids and blood glucose: metabolic monitoring; TRT may improve metabolic profile in hypogonadal men
- Bone density: baseline and monitoring if osteoporosis present

**Hyperprolactinaemia / prolactinoma:**
- Dopamine agonist (cabergoline 0.25–1.5 mg twice weekly): first-line; shrinks most prolactinomas; restores gonadotrophin axis; PBS-listed for hyperprolactinaemia; normalises testosterone in secondary hypogonadism
- Surgery: macroadenoma not responding to cabergoline or causing visual compromise; neurosurgery (transsphenoidal)

**Infertility with hypogonadism:**
- Clomiphene citrate 25–50 mg daily (off-label): stimulates LH/FSH secretion; increases endogenous testosterone; preserves fertility; specialist (endocrinology/andrology)
- Gonadotrophin therapy (FSH + hCG injections): for secondary hypogonadism with infertility; specialist; expensive; PBS Authority

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Testosterone undecanoate gel (Testogel 50 mg)** (PBS restricted: androgen deficiency in men; Authority required; requires biochemical diagnosis on two fasting morning samples; apply daily; counsel on transfer risk)
- **Testosterone undecanoate 1000 mg/4 mL IM (Reandron)** (PBS restricted: androgen deficiency; 10–14 weekly injection; administered by GP or nurse; check haematocrit and PSA before each injection)
- **Cabergoline 0.5 mg (Dostinex)** (PBS restricted: hyperprolactinaemia; dopamine agonist; effective for prolactinoma; take with food; nausea initially; compulsive behaviours rare but document counselling)
- **Note:** Testosterone is a Schedule 4 controlled drug. Prescriptions must comply with state drug regulations (e.g., some states require special prescription pads for Schedule 8 – check local regulations). TRT should not be initiated in men with PSA >4 ng/mL without prior urology assessment. PSA monitoring is mandatory.

## Escalation Triggers

**Urgent:**
- Visual field defect + secondary hypogonadism: pituitary macroadenoma; urgent pituitary MRI; neurosurgery
- Haematocrit >54% on TRT: stop TRT; urgent haematology; manage polycythaemia

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Endocrinology: secondary hypogonadism (pituitary evaluation needed); Klinefelter syndrome; complex cases; infertility management
- Urology/andrology: testicular pathology; infertility; TRT initiation if PSA borderline
- Haematology: polycythaemia complicating TRT

## Telehealth Suitability

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- TRT monitoring: review testosterone levels, haematocrit, PSA results
- Dose adjustment and formulation discussion
- Symptom assessment and lifestyle review
- Follow-up after prolactinoma/endocrine treatment

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial presentation: physical examination (testicular volume, gynaecomastia, virilisation), blood tests
- TRT injection administration (Reandron)
- New visual symptoms or headache (pituitary tumour)
