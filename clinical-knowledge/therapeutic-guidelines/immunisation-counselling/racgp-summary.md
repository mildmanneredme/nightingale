## Immunisation Counselling Assessment (RACGP)

Immunisation is the single most cost-effective public health intervention after clean water. The National Immunisation Program (NIP) provides free vaccines to all Australians at key life stages, with additional provisions for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples, immunocompromised patients, and other at-risk groups. Australia has high childhood immunisation coverage (>95% for most vaccines), but significant gaps remain in adolescent, adult, and catch-up vaccination. GPs play a central role in: delivering NIP vaccines, identifying and addressing vaccination gaps (using the Australian Immunisation Register — AIR), counselling vaccine-hesitant patients, managing adverse events following immunisation (AEFI), and providing special population immunisation advice (immunocompromised, pregnancy, travel — see `pre-travel-consultation` entry). Vaccine hesitancy is an important challenge requiring a respectful, evidence-based, patient-centred approach.

## Clinical Presentation

**Australian Immunisation Register (AIR):**
- National register recording all vaccines given in Australia (from birth)
- GP access via HPOS (Health Provider Online Services) or practice management software integration
- Check AIR at every preventive health opportunity — identifies gaps and overdue vaccines
- Patients can view their own record via Medicare online account or My Health Record

**NIP Schedule — key life stage vaccines (as of 2023):**

Infants and children (free for all):
- 6 weeks: Infanrix Hexa (DTaP-IPV-Hib-HBV) + Prevenar 13 (PCV13) + RotaTeq (rotavirus oral)
- 4 months: Infanrix Hexa + Prevenar 13 + RotaTeq
- 6 months: Infanrix Hexa + Prevenar 13
- 12 months: MMRV (measles-mumps-rubella-varicella) or MMR + varicella; Nimenrix (MenACWY); Hiberix (Hib booster); hepatitis A (for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children)
- 18 months: MMR + varicella booster (if not received MMRV at 12 months) or MMRV booster; dTpa booster
- 4 years: MMRV or MMR + varicella; dTpa booster; IPV (combined)
- 10–15 years (school-based): dTpa; MenACWY; HPV (Gardasil 9 — 2-dose schedule)

Adults and older:
- Influenza: annual NIP-funded for ≥65 years, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander ≥6 months, pregnant women, chronic disease, immunocompromised, NDIS participants; others: recommended but not NIP-funded (private)
- Pneumococcal: Prevenar 13 (PCV13) + Pneumovax 23 (23PPV) for ≥70 years (NIP-funded); catch-up available
- Zoster (Shingrix — recombinant zoster vaccine, RZV): NIP-funded for adults ≥65 (≥50 for immunocompromised); 2-dose schedule (0 and 2–6 months); superior to old live zoster vaccine (Zostavax — no longer recommended)
- COVID-19: boosters per current ATAGI schedule; funded boosters for aged care residents, immunocompromised, ≥65, and others per current guidance; refer to ATAGI website for current recommendations (frequently updated)

Pregnancy (NIP-funded):
- Influenza: inactivated; any trimester; ideally before flu season
- dTpa (pertussis): 20–32 weeks (ideally 28 weeks) every pregnancy; maternal antibody transfer to infant
- COVID-19: recommended in pregnancy; mRNA vaccines safe; ATAGI guidance

Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples (additional NIP vaccines):
- Hepatitis A: 12–18 months and 18 months – 4 years (catch-up to age 9)
- Pneumococcal: enhanced schedule from 6 weeks (4 doses PCV13 in infancy; Pneumovax 23 at 4 years); 65 years
- Annual influenza: from 6 months
- Additional meningococcal coverage

Special populations (at-risk NIP vaccines):
- Hepatitis B: infants (birth dose + 6-week-6-month course); catch-up in unvaccinated adults in high-risk groups (HBV-negative healthcare workers, prisoners, sexual partners of HBsAg+, IDU, MSM)
- Hepatitis A: adults with chronic liver disease, MSM, IDU, travellers, healthcare workers
- Meningococcal B (Bexsero): recommended for immunocompromised, asplenia, complement deficiency; not NIP-funded for most adults — private script
- Yellow fever, typhoid, hepatitis A/B combination, malaria: travel-specific — see `pre-travel-consultation` entry

## Assessment Approach

**Vaccination history review:**
- Check AIR at every preventive health opportunity
- Ask about: childhood vaccines (particularly if born overseas), catch-up vaccines, occupational vaccines (healthcare workers — check hepatitis B, influenza; farmers/vets — Q fever), travel vaccines
- Document AIR-confirmed vaccines in medical record

**Catch-up vaccination:**
- Australian Immunisation Handbook provides catch-up schedules for all vaccines
- ASCIA NIP catch-up calculator: ascia.org.au (immunocompromised patients)
- Missed childhood vaccines: catch up as per handbook schedule; most vaccines can be given at any age
- Interrupted schedule: can resume where left off for most vaccines (no need to restart course)

**Adverse events following immunisation (AEFI) assessment:**
- Common (expected, not concerning): injection site redness/swelling/tenderness, low-grade fever, fatigue, irritability (infants) — reassure; paracetamol for comfort
- Moderate (monitor): fever >39°C for >24 hours; extensive injection site reaction
- Reportable AEFIs: anaphylaxis (rare; 1–2 per million doses), febrile convulsion, severe allergic reaction, intussusception (rotavirus — rare)
- All significant AEFIs should be reported to the state/territory health authority; SAEFVIC (Victoria) or equivalent
- 15-minute wait post-vaccination: mandatory in clinic for anaphylaxis observation; stock adrenaline (EpiPen) and know how to manage anaphylaxis

**Vaccine-hesitant patients:**
- Approach: listen and explore concerns without judgment; acknowledge concerns as understandable
- Motivational interviewing techniques: open questions; explore ambivalence; affirm autonomy
- Common concerns and responses:
  - "Vaccines cause autism": MMR-autism claim is fraudulent; retracted; 20+ studies with millions of children show no link
  - "Too many vaccines at once": immune system handles thousands of antigens daily; simultaneous vaccines safe and effective
  - "Natural immunity is better": for some diseases (chickenpox, measles) natural infection does provide immunity, but at the cost of disease risk and potential serious complications; vaccine immunity is safer
  - "I don't trust [pharma/government]": acknowledging distrust; focus on evidence and clinician relationship; offer to answer specific questions over time
- SHARE approach (Sharing accurate information, Hearing concerns, Addressing questions, Reminding of vaccine-preventable disease severity, Explaining evidence)
- Document discussion and patient's decision; offer to revisit

## Key Differentials

- Immunocompromised patient: assess carefully before live vaccines — immunocompromised patients must NOT receive live vaccines (MMR, MMRV, oral typhoid, yellow fever, live attenuated influenza, live zoster Zostavax); use inactivated alternatives (Shingrix, inactivated influenza, Prevenar 13, Pneumovax 23)
- Egg allergy: influenza vaccines manufactured in eggs; anaphylaxis to egg is NOT an absolute contraindication — most patients with egg allergy can receive influenza vaccine in clinic with 30-minute observation; refer to allergy clinic if prior anaphylaxis to egg; MMR (also egg-based process) does not cross-react with egg — safe in egg allergy
- Febrile illness: minor illness (sniffly nose) is NOT a contraindication to vaccination; defer only if moderate-severe acute febrile illness

## Management Principles

**Vaccine administration:**
- Correct route, site, and dose per Australian Immunisation Handbook
- Most vaccines: intramuscular (IM) — anterolateral thigh in infants; deltoid in adults
- Subcutaneous (SC): MMR/MMRV, some other live vaccines
- Oral: rotavirus (RotaTeq)
- Intranasal: FluMist (live attenuated influenza — not recommended for immunocompromised)
- Cold chain: vaccines must be stored at 2–8°C at all times; cold chain breach = report and seek ATAGI advice before use
- Record in AIR: mandatory for NIP vaccines; record all vaccines administered

**No-fault compensation scheme:**
- AVCS (Australian Vaccine Injury Compensation Scheme — from 2021): available for serious, confirmed AEFIs from NIP-funded vaccines; no-fault; GPs may provide supporting documentation

**Catch-up immunisation for under-vaccinated or overseas-born children:**
- Check AIR on enrolment at GP; refer to catch-up schedule in Handbook
- Schools require vaccination records for enrolment in some states; No Jab No Pay (Centrelink family payments conditional on vaccination)

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS / NIP)

- **NIP-funded vaccines**: administered free at GP; claim via AIR; no PBS item number needed; separate MBS item numbers for consultation and vaccine administration (MBS items 10981–10996)
- **Influenza vaccine (inactivated — Fluzone, Vaxigrip, FluQuadri)**: NIP-funded for ≥65, pregnant, chronic disease, immunocompromised, NDIS; private prescription for others (~$25–30 per dose at pharmacy)
- **Shingrix (RZV — recombinant zoster vaccine)**: NIP-funded for ≥65 (2023); ≥50 for immunocompromised (special access); 2-dose schedule (0 and 2–6 months apart)
- **Gardasil 9 (HPV)**: NIP-funded for school Year 7 program (12–13 years); funded catch-up to age 25 for eligible unvaccinated; private for ≥26 years if desired
- **Note:** Importation of some vaccines (e.g., Zostavax — no longer recommended; some travel vaccines): check TGA access for specific imported vaccines; most travel vaccines available through specialised travel clinics or GP

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Anaphylaxis post-vaccination: maintain 15-minute wait period; EpiPen immediately; 000; lie patient flat with legs elevated; see `anaphylaxis-management` entry
- Serious febrile convulsion post-vaccination — if prolonged (>5 minutes): 000

**Urgent review:**
- Suspected intussusception in infant after rotavirus vaccine: inconsolable crying, vomiting, blood in stool — ED immediately
- Serious AEFI: report to state health authority and SAEFVIC or equivalent

**Non-urgent:**
- Allergy clinic: prior anaphylaxis to vaccine component; complex allergy history; desensitisation assessment
- ATAGI guidance: current recommendations at immunise.health.gov.au — frequently updated, especially COVID-19 guidance

## Telehealth Suitability

Immunisation counselling is suitable for telehealth; vaccine administration is in-person.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- AIR review and vaccination history assessment
- Vaccine-hesitant patient counselling
- Vaccine scheduling and planning
- Immunocompromised patient vaccine review (which vaccines are safe)
- Pregnancy vaccine counselling (influenza, dTpa, COVID-19)
- Catch-up schedule planning
- AEFI assessment after vaccination (if minor)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- All vaccine administration (in-person; 15-minute post-vaccine observation required)
- Moderate-severe AEFI requiring physical assessment
- Cold chain breach assessment if vaccine mishandled
