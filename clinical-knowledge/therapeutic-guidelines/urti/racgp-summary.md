## URTI Assessment (RACGP)

Most upper respiratory tract infections (URTIs) are viral and self-limiting. Antibiotics are not indicated for viral URTI and their inappropriate use contributes to antimicrobial resistance. Symptoms typically resolve within 7–10 days without specific treatment.

## Common presentations

Symptoms commonly associated with viral URTI include sore throat, runny nose (rhinorrhoea), nasal congestion, mild fever (typically below 38.5°C), cough, and general malaise. Differentiation from bacterial causes (such as Group A Streptococcus pharyngitis) is important and may require clinical assessment or rapid antigen testing.

Features suggesting bacterial infection that may warrant antibiotic consideration include high fever (above 38.5°C), significant tonsillar exudate, tender anterior cervical lymphadenopathy, and absence of cough (Centor/McIsaac criteria). These assessments require GP clinical judgement and, where appropriate, in-person examination.

## Self-care recommendations

Recommended self-care for uncomplicated viral URTI includes:
- Adequate rest and hydration
- Paracetamol or ibuprofen (as appropriate) for symptomatic relief of fever and pain — refer to NPS MedicineWise dosing guidance
- Saline nasal irrigation for nasal congestion
- Throat lozenges or warm honey and lemon for sore throat (adults and children over 12 months)
- Avoiding smoking and second-hand smoke exposure

## Escalation triggers

Patients should be advised to seek urgent medical attention if they experience:
- High fever (above 39°C) not responding to paracetamol
- Difficulty breathing or swallowing
- Neck stiffness or severe headache (possible meningitis)
- Symptoms persisting beyond 10–14 days or worsening after initial improvement
- Symptoms of peritonsillar abscess (muffled voice, drooling, trismus)

Call 000 or go to the nearest emergency department immediately for: severe difficulty breathing, inability to swallow, or rapidly spreading neck swelling.

## Antibiotic stewardship note

Antibiotics should not be prescribed for confirmed viral URTI. Delayed prescribing strategies may be appropriate in select cases following GP clinical assessment. Patient education on the viral nature of most URTIs and the risks of unnecessary antibiotic use is recommended.
