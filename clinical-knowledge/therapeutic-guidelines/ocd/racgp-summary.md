## OCD Assessment (RACGP)

Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) is a common, chronic, and often severely disabling mental health condition characterised by intrusive, unwanted obsessions (recurrent thoughts, images, or urges) and compulsions (repetitive behaviours or mental acts performed to reduce anxiety). OCD affects approximately 2–3% of Australians at some point in their lifetime. It is often underdiagnosed and under-recognised — average delay between symptom onset and diagnosis is 11 years. OCD is classified separately from anxiety disorders in DSM-5. GPs play a central role in: recognising OCD (especially atypical presentations), assessing severity, initiating SSRI treatment, providing cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) referral (specifically Exposure and Response Prevention — ERP), and arranging psychiatry or specialist psychology referral for moderate-severe disease. Telehealth is well-suited to OCD monitoring and treatment review.

## Clinical Presentation

**Obsessions (intrusive unwanted thoughts, images, urges — involuntary):**
- Contamination: fears of germs, illness, dirt contaminating self or others
- Harm: fears of harming self or others (without intention to act)
- Symmetry/order: need for things to be "just right"; distress when asymmetric
- Unwanted sexual or violent thoughts: intrusive — egodystonic (patient knows these are wrong/unwanted)
- Religious/moral (scrupulosity): excessive guilt; fear of moral transgression; blasphemous thoughts
- Somatic: preoccupation with body sensations, illness (overlaps with health anxiety)

**Compulsions (behaviours or mental acts performed to reduce obsession-related distress):**
- Checking: repeatedly checking locks, stove, light switches; reassurance seeking
- Washing/cleaning: excessive handwashing, showering, cleaning; raw/cracked hands
- Ordering/arranging: arranging objects until "right"; repetitive touching
- Mental rituals: counting, praying, repeating words silently; neutralising unwanted thoughts
- Seeking reassurance: from family, doctors, friends — excessive reassurance-seeking is a compulsion
- Avoiding triggers: avoiding situations that trigger obsessions (avoidance maintains OCD)

**Key diagnostic criteria (DSM-5):**
- Presence of obsessions AND/OR compulsions
- Obsessions/compulsions are time-consuming (≥1 hour/day) OR cause significant distress or functional impairment
- Not better explained by substance or medical condition
- Insight specifier: good/fair insight; poor insight; absent insight/delusional (important for treatment)

**OCD severity (Y-BOCS — Yale-Brown Obsessive Compulsive Scale):**
- Self-report or clinician-administered; 0–40; mild 8–15, moderate 16–23, severe 24–31, extreme 32–40
- Y-BOCS-II is the updated version; monitors treatment response

**OCD presentations that GP must recognise:**
- OCD with primarily mental rituals: not visible compulsions; may appear as severe health anxiety or intrusive thoughts
- Pure O (primarily obsessions): patient believes they are "going crazy" from intrusive thoughts — educate that intrusive thoughts are normal; it is the response (neutralising) that maintains OCD
- Body dysmorphic disorder (BDD): excessive preoccupation with perceived physical defect; overlaps with OCD; similar treatment
- Tic-related OCD: co-existing tics; different treatment considerations; psychiatry

## Assessment Approach

**Screening (GP):**
- Two-question OCD screen:
  1. "Do you have unwanted thoughts that keep coming back and that you find hard to stop?"
  2. "Do you find yourself doing repetitive things over and over (washing hands, checking, counting)?"
  Positive response → OCD more detailed assessment

**OCD assessment:**
- Y-BOCS-II or OCI-R (OCD Inventory Revised — self-report): quantify severity; monitor response
- Content of obsessions and compulsions: understand themes to match to appropriate treatment
- Functional impact: work, relationships, daily function; hours per day occupied with OCD
- Avoidance behaviours: how much is patient avoiding triggers?
- Duration and onset
- Comorbidities: depression (very common — up to 67% lifetime comorbidity), anxiety, tics, substance use
- Risk: suicidality assessment — OCD significantly increases suicide risk; PHQ-9 and direct inquiry
- Insight: does patient recognise obsessions as irrational? (relevant for treatment approach)

**Investigations:**
- No specific investigations for OCD
- If medical causes suspected: TSH (OCD-like symptoms in hyperthyroidism), neurological assessment if sudden onset (PANDAS — paediatric acute-onset neuropsychiatric syndrome associated with streptococcal infection — paediatric psychiatry)

## Key Differentials

- Generalised anxiety disorder (GAD): worry about real-life concerns (not intrusive irrational obsessions); no compulsions; see `anxiety` entry
- Health anxiety (illness anxiety disorder): excessive worry about illness; medical reassurance-seeking is similar to OCD compulsion; overlap; CBT treats both
- Depression with rumination: repeated negative thoughts; not egodystonic intrusions; no compulsions; PHQ-9
- Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD): intrusive memories of specific trauma; avoidance; hyperarousal; see `ptsd` entry
- Schizophrenia: psychotic features; intrusive thoughts with lack of insight (ego-syntonic); hallucinations; distinguish carefully; psychiatry
- Tic disorders / Tourette's: repetitive movements/vocalisations; tics are brief involuntary motor/vocal; often co-exists with OCD
- ADHD: intrusive thoughts from poor attention regulation; different treatment; overlap possible

## Management Principles

**Psychoeducation (first step in all cases):**
- Explain OCD model: obsessions trigger anxiety → compulsions temporarily reduce anxiety → relief reinforces the loop → obsessions return and strengthen
- Compulsions MAINTAIN OCD: explain why compulsions must be reduced, not just obsessions
- OCD is NOT caused by weakness; it is a brain disorder; treatment is evidence-based
- OCD Australia (ocdaustralia.com): patient education resources; GP tool

**Cognitive behavioural therapy — Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP):**
- GOLD STANDARD psychological treatment for OCD: stronger evidence than any medication
- ERP: deliberately face feared situations (exposure) WITHOUT performing compulsions (response prevention); anxiety habituates; fear reduces; OCD loses power
- Requires trained clinical psychologist specialising in OCD: referral via GP Mental Health Care Plan (MBS 2713) — 10 psychology sessions per year
- Digital CBT: NOCD (telehealth OCD specialist therapy platform); useful waitlist option; OCD-specific
- Note: generic CBT without ERP component is significantly less effective for OCD

**SSRI pharmacotherapy (moderate-severe OCD, or mild OCD not responding to CBT alone):**

First-line SSRIs:
- Sertraline (Zoloft) 50–200 mg daily: most used; PBS-restricted for depression/anxiety (off-label for OCD at GP level; psychiatrist may list for OCD indication)
- Fluvoxamine (Luvox) 100–300 mg daily: PBS-listed specifically for OCD in Australia; first-choice if insurance/PBS coverage important; BD dosing; more drug interactions (CYP1A2)
- Fluoxetine (Prozac) 20–80 mg daily: PBS-restricted; long half-life; once weekly dosing possible
- Escitalopram 10–20 mg daily: commonly used; well-tolerated

Key prescribing points for OCD:
- Higher doses than depression often required: sertraline up to 200 mg/day; fluvoxamine up to 300 mg/day
- Longer response time: 8–12 weeks for OCD vs 4–6 weeks for depression; counsel patience
- Partial response: increase dose before declaring failure; maximum tolerated dose before switching
- Maintenance: continue for minimum 12 months after response; 2+ years if severe; indefinite in recurrent or severe OCD

**Second-line and augmentation (specialist territory):**
- Clomipramine (TCA): older; most evidence of any drug for OCD; anticholinergic side effects; cardiac monitoring (QTc); specialist initiation; if SSRIs failed
- SSRI + antipsychotic augmentation: risperidone, aripiprazole; when partial SSRI response; psychiatrist-initiated
- SSRI + clomipramine combination: complex interactions; psychiatrist-managed only

**Comorbidity management:**
- Depression: treat concurrent with OCD; SSRI often addresses both; monitor suicidality
- Anxiety: ERP for OCD usually reduces comorbid anxiety; avoid benzodiazepines — not effective for OCD; suppress exposure exercises
- Substance use: address separately; may be coping mechanism for OCD anxiety

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Fluvoxamine (Luvox) 50/100 mg** (PBS restricted: OCD — specifically listed indication; 100–300 mg daily in 2 divided doses; CYP1A2 inhibitor — interactions with caffeine, clozapine, theophylline, warfarin, olanzapine; start 50 mg BD; titrate over 4 weeks to response)
- **Sertraline (Zoloft) 50/100 mg** (PBS restricted: depression, social anxiety disorder; OCD is off-label at GP prescribing but widely used; 50–200 mg daily; start 50 mg; increase to 100–200 mg for OCD)
- **Fluoxetine 20 mg** (PBS restricted: depression; off-label for OCD; 20–80 mg daily; long half-life — fewer discontinuation symptoms)
- **Note:** OCD typically requires higher SSRI doses and longer treatment duration than depression; counsel patients on 8–12 week wait for response; do not abandon treatment prematurely
- **Clomipramine**: PBS restricted for depression and OCD; significant anticholinergic and cardiac side effects; cardiological review before use; typically specialist-initiated for OCD

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate:**
- Active suicidal ideation with plan: OCD has substantially elevated suicide risk — same-day psychiatric review

**Urgent psychiatry:**
- Severe OCD (Y-BOCS ≥24): significant functional impairment; hospitalisation if unable to function
- OCD with poor insight or delusional intensity: requires careful distinction from psychosis; psychiatry
- SSRI failure after adequate trial: complex psychopharmacology; clomipramine or augmentation

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Psychologist (ERP-trained): all OCD cases — GP Mental Health Care Plan (MBS 2713); first-line treatment
- Psychiatrist: moderate-severe OCD; SSRI failure; diagnostic complexity; augmentation strategies
- OCD Australia: patient education and support; ocdaustralia.com

## Telehealth Suitability

OCD monitoring and ongoing management are well-suited to telehealth.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- OCD assessment (Y-BOCS) and monitoring
- SSRI initiation, dose adjustment, and review
- Psychoeducation and ERP concept explanation
- PHQ-9 depression screening and monitoring
- Referral coordination (psychologist, psychiatry)
- Safety assessment between face-to-face reviews

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- Initial comprehensive mental health assessment
- Severe OCD requiring psychiatric evaluation
- Physical examination if medical cause suspected (PANDAS)
