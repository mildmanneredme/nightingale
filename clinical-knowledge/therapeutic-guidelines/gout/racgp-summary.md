## Gout Assessment (RACGP)

Gout is the most common inflammatory arthritis in Australia, affecting approximately 3–5% of Australian adults and rising with age. It is caused by hyperuricaemia (elevated serum urate) leading to monosodium urate (MSU) crystal deposition in joints and periarticular tissues. Gout typically presents as acute flares of severe joint pain and inflammation, most commonly in the first metatarsophalangeal (MTP) joint (podagra). Without adequate urate-lowering therapy (ULT), gout progresses to chronic tophaceous gout with persistent joint damage and tophi formation. Gout is highly associated with metabolic syndrome, cardiovascular disease, hypertension, diabetes, and chronic kidney disease. Telehealth is well-suited for gout management, acute flare treatment prescribing, and ULT initiation and monitoring.

## Clinical Presentation

**Acute gout flare:**
- Typically sudden onset, reaching maximum intensity within 12–24 hours
- Classic: exquisite pain, swelling, erythema (redness), warmth and tenderness of affected joint — often so painful that bed sheets cannot be tolerated on the joint
- First MTP joint (podagra): most common (50% of first attacks); also ankle, midfoot, knee, wrist, finger, elbow
- Fever and systemic upset common in severe attacks
- Attacks are self-limiting (7–14 days untreated); recur with increasing frequency without ULT
- Triggering factors: excess purine intake (red meat, seafood, fructose, beer), dehydration, illness, trauma, surgery, rapid changes in urate level (starting or stopping ULT)

**Intercritical period:** Symptom-free between attacks initially; in chronic untreated gout, joint damage and tophi develop.

**Chronic tophaceous gout:** Deposits of MSU crystals under skin (tophi — palpable firm deposits; common sites: ear helix, olecranon bursa, Achilles tendon, MTP joints); bony erosions on X-ray; joint deformity; may occur in any stage if hyperuricaemia is chronic.

**Risk factors:**
- Hyperuricaemia: serum urate >360 µmol/L (women), >420 µmol/L (men)
- Male sex; post-menopausal women
- Metabolic syndrome, obesity, type 2 diabetes
- Hypertension — especially thiazide diuretics (raise urate), also loop diuretics
- Chronic kidney disease (CKD) — impaired urate excretion
- Diet: red meat, seafood, alcohol (especially beer), high-fructose foods
- Medications: low-dose aspirin, cyclosporine, tacrolimus
- Family history

## Assessment Approach

**Diagnosis:**
- Clinical diagnosis in classic presentation (acute podagra in middle-aged to elderly man, hyperuricaemia, rapid onset to maximum intensity): diagnostic confidence high
- Synovial fluid analysis: gold standard — MSU crystals (needle-shaped, negatively birefringent under polarised microscopy) from aspirated joint; differentiates from septic arthritis and pseudogout (calcium pyrophosphate crystals)
- Note: serum urate may be NORMAL during acute attack (mobilisation from joints can temporarily lower serum urate); do not use normal urate to exclude gout during acute flare

**Investigations:**
- Serum urate: during intercritical period, not acute flare (may be falsely low); target <360 µmol/L on ULT
- Renal function (UEC, creatinine, eGFR): CKD common comorbidity; allopurinol dosing dependent on eGFR
- FBC, LFTs: baseline before ULT
- Joint X-ray: in chronic gout — bony erosions with overhanging edge ("rat bite" erosions); normal in early disease
- Musculoskeletal ultrasound or dual-energy CT: can demonstrate MSU crystal deposits; useful when diagnosis uncertain or prior to joint aspiration; increasing availability
- Fasting glucose, lipid panel: metabolic syndrome assessment
- Urine urate/creatinine ratio: if considering uricosuric therapy

## Key Differentials

- Septic arthritis — must be excluded in acute monoarthritis; fever, elevated WBC and CRP; requires joint aspiration and synovial fluid culture; joint aspiration is therapeutic and diagnostic; do not delay empiric antibiotics if septic arthritis likely — call 000 or ED for systemically unwell patient with hot joint
- Pseudogout (calcium pyrophosphate deposition — CPPD): clinically similar to gout; crystal-positive joint aspiration (calcium pyrophosphate, rhomboid-shaped, positively birefringent); associated with older age, hyperparathyroidism, haemochromatosis; treatment similar to acute gout but no ULT
- Reactive arthritis — post-infectious; asymmetric; urogenital or enteric trigger; no hyperuricaemia
- Rheumatoid arthritis — polyarticular, small joints, morning stiffness, seronegative or RF/CCP positive; subacute onset
- Osteoarthritis — chronic, degenerative; no acute inflammatory flares; normal urate
- Trauma or fracture — history; X-ray
- Bursitis / cellulitis — warmth, erythema; distinguish by careful examination and aspiration if uncertain

## Management Principles

**Acute gout flare treatment:**

NSAIDs (first-line if no contraindication):
- Naproxen 500 mg twice daily, or indomethacin 50 mg three times daily for 5–7 days; full anti-inflammatory dose required — analgesic dose insufficient
- Avoid in CKD (eGFR <30), heart failure, anticoagulant use, active peptic ulcer; add PPI gastroprotection if risk factors present
- Colchicine (first-line alternative or adjunct):
- Low-dose: colchicine 1 mg immediately, then 0.5 mg 1 hour later (total 1.5 mg on day 1); continue 0.5 mg twice daily for 7–10 days; superior tolerability to high-dose; avoid in severe renal or hepatic impairment; interactions with statins and cyclosporine (myopathy risk)
- Previous standard was higher dose (0.5 mg hourly until relief or diarrhoea) — now replaced by low-dose regimen
- Prednisolone (when NSAIDs and colchicine contraindicated — CKD, warfarin, elderly):
- 30–40 mg daily for 5 days then cease or short taper; highly effective; use when other options unavailable
- Joint aspiration with corticosteroid injection: for single joint flare; rapid relief; requires in-person procedure

**Urate-lowering therapy (ULT) — prevent recurrence and progression:**

Indications for ULT (should be offered to patients with):
- ≥2 flares per year
- Tophi (any)
- Erosive disease on imaging
- CKD stage 3+ with gout
- Comorbid urolithiasis
- First attack in younger patient (age <40) with high urate or strong family history

Allopurinol (xanthine oxidase inhibitor — first-line ULT):
- Start at 50–100 mg daily (lower starting dose in CKD — reduces hypersensitivity risk)
- Titrate monthly by 50–100 mg increments to achieve target serum urate <360 µmol/L (or <300 µmol/L in tophaceous gout)
- Maximum dose: 900 mg/day (dose-reduced in CKD — consult product information)
- Start during stable intercritical period (not during acute flare unless flare already being treated); starting ULT during flare can prolong/precipitate flare
- Prophylactic co-prescription of colchicine 0.5 mg daily (or NSAID) for first 3–6 months of ULT — prevents mobilisation flares
- HLA-B*5801 testing: Asian populations (Han Chinese, Thai, Korean) have higher risk of severe allopurinol hypersensitivity syndrome (DRESS, TEN/SJS) — consider testing before starting; rheumatology or dermatology guidance

Febuxostat:
- 40–80 mg daily; alternative to allopurinol; useful in allopurinol intolerance or moderate CKD; cardiovascular safety concerns (FDA warning — may increase cardiovascular events vs allopurinol); use with caution in established CVD; PBS-restricted in Australia

**Lifestyle modifications (important adjunct, not sufficient alone):**
- Reduce purine-rich foods: red meat, organ meats, seafood (especially anchovies, sardines, mussels)
- Reduce alcohol: particularly beer (highest purine content); spirits less impact
- Avoid high-fructose drinks (soft drinks, fruit juices)
- Maintain hydration: 2 L water daily — increases renal urate excretion
- Weight loss: hyperuricaemia often improves with weight loss
- Review medications: switch thiazide diuretic if possible (use ARB — losartan has mild uricosuric effect and can replace thiazide; discuss with prescriber of original medication)

## Prescribing Considerations (AU PBS)

- **Colchicine** (PBS unrestricted — acute gout flare; 1 mg immediately then 0.5 mg 1 hour later; 0.5 mg twice daily for flare and prophylaxis during ULT initiation; dose-reduce in CKD; interactions with statins)
- **Naproxen** (PBS unrestricted — anti-inflammatory for acute gout; 500 mg twice daily; add PPI if gastric risk)
- **Indomethacin** (PBS unrestricted — acute gout; 50 mg three times daily; higher GI and renal risk than naproxen)
- **Allopurinol** (PBS restricted: gout with ≥2 attacks per year or tophaceous gout; start 50–100 mg daily, titrate monthly; target urate <360 µmol/L; authority required for PBS subsidy)
- **Febuxostat** (Adenuric — PBS restricted: gout in allopurinol intolerance; 40–80 mg daily; cardiovascular caution)
- **Prednisolone** (PBS unrestricted — short course for acute gout when NSAIDs/colchicine contraindicated; 30–40 mg daily for 5 days)

## Escalation Triggers

**Immediate 000 / ED:**
- Suspected septic arthritis — hot, swollen joint with systemic sepsis features (high fever, rigors, haemodynamic instability); requires emergency joint aspiration and IV antibiotics
- Allopurinol hypersensitivity syndrome (DRESS/TEN): fever + diffuse rash + mucositis + eosinophilia within weeks of starting allopurinol — stop allopurinol immediately; hospital admission

**Urgent in-person assessment:**
- Single hot swollen joint with fever — cannot exclude septic arthritis without aspiration; in-person assessment and possible aspiration required; septic arthritis is an orthopaedic emergency
- New tophi with functional impairment — rheumatology referral

**Non-urgent referral:**
- Rheumatology: refractory gout despite optimised ULT, HLA-B*5801 testing guidance, complex tophaceous gout, suspected alternative crystal arthropathy (CPPD, apatite)
- Dietitian: dietary modification support for metabolic syndrome and gout
- Nephrologist: concurrent CKD stage 4–5 management (allopurinol dosing complex, ULT target selection)

## Telehealth Suitability

Gout management is well suited to telehealth for most presentations.

**Suitable for telehealth:**
- Acute flare: history assessment and colchicine/NSAID/prednisolone prescribing (if diagnosis established and septic arthritis excluded)
- ULT initiation counselling and allopurinol prescribing
- Urate monitoring result review and dose titration
- Lifestyle counselling (dietary, alcohol, hydration)
- Medication review (identify offending drugs — thiazides, aspirin)

**Requires in-person assessment:**
- First presentation with hot swollen joint — septic arthritis must be excluded; joint aspiration if uncertain
- Joint aspiration for diagnosis or corticosteroid injection
- Serum urate, renal function blood tests — pathology arranged
